### Title
**Why Do You Weigh Less on the Moon? — Gravity, Mass, and the Force That Keeps You Grounded**

### Overview
You weigh less on the Moon because weight is not just about YOU. Weight is the force of gravity pulling on your mass, and that force depends on the mass of the planet or moon you're standing on. The Moon has much less mass than Earth, so its gravitational pull is weaker, making you weigh less. Your ... Students build a cause-and-effect model exploring how planet mass and distance from center interact with gravitational pull, object weight, and jump height in this system.

### NGSS Standard
**MS-PS2-4, MS-ESS1-2**: Construct and present arguments using evidence to support the claim that gravitational interactions are attractive and depend on the masses of interacting objects. Develop and use a model to describe the role of gravity in the motions within galaxies and the solar system.

### Learning Objectives
- Model how gravitational pull depends on the mass of the planet and the distance from its center
- Explain the difference between mass and weight using gravitational interactions
- Predict how object weight and jump height change on different planets and moons
- Describe how gravity governs motion within the solar system and beyond

### Component List (Starting Model: 5 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Planet Mass | External | The total amount of matter that makes up the planet or moon |
| Distance from Center | External | How far an object is from the center of the planet or moon's mass |
| Gravitational Pull | Internal | The strength of the attractive force exerted by the planet or moon on objects at its surface |
| Object Weight | Internal | The downward force experienced by an object due to gravitational pull |
| Jump Height | Internal | How high a person can jump, which increases when gravitational pull decreases |

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format. Each item is three-dimensional, assessing a Science and Engineering Practice (SEP), Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and Crosscutting Concept (CCC) simultaneously, aligned to MS-PS2-4, MS-ESS1-2.

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.1 (Determine components of a system) + DCI + CCC4 (Describe a system in terms of its components)

A scientist is studying why do you weigh less on the moon?. She identifies these components: Planet Mass, Distance from Center, Gravitational Pull, Object Weight, Jump Height. She sorts them into external (things we set or cannot control) and internal (things that change based on other factors).

Which list correctly sorts the components?

A. External: Planet Mass, Gravitational Pull — Internal: Distance from Center, Object Weight, Jump Height
B. External: Planet Mass, Distance from Center — Internal: Gravitational Pull, Object Weight, Jump Height
C. External: Gravitational Pull, Object Weight — Internal: Planet Mass, Distance from Center, Jump Height
D. External: Jump Height, Planet Mass — Internal: Distance from Center, Gravitational Pull, Object Weight

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Planet Mass and Distance from Center are external because they represent conditions that drive the system. Gravitational Pull, Object Weight, and Jump Height are internal because they change based on other factors in the system.

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships among components) + DCI + CCC4 (Describe components and interactions)

An astronaut weighs 180 pounds on Earth. On the Moon, where gravity is about 1/6 of Earth's, what would the astronaut weigh, and would their mass change?

A. Planet Mass has no effect on Gravitational Pull
B. As Planet Mass increases, Gravitational Pull increases
C. As Planet Mass decreases, Gravitational Pull increases
D. Gravitational Pull controls Planet Mass, not the other way around

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Greater planet mass means stronger gravitational pull. A more massive planet attracts objects with more force.
