## Platform

### Title
**We Built a Better Dog (And Maybe a Mistake) — How Humans Hack Evolution Through Selective Breeding**

### Overview
Humans have been modifying organisms through selective breeding for over 10,000 years — long before we understood genetics. From wolves to dogs, from wild grass to corn, artificial selection has shaped the living world to meet human needs. Students investigate the driving question: How have humans changed plants and animals to suit our needs — and at what cost? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

### Learning Objectives
- Explain how artificial selection differs from natural selection
- Model how selection pressure affects trait frequency over generations
- Predict unintended consequences of extreme selective breeding
- Evaluate ethical implications of genetic modification technologies

LESSON PURPOSE:
Humans have been modifying organisms through selective breeding for over 10,000 years — long before we understood genetics. From wolves to dogs, from wild grass to corn, artificial selection has shaped the living world to meet human needs.

NGSS ALIGNMENT:
MS-LS4-5: Gather and synthesize information about technologies that have changed the way humans influence the inheritance of desired traits in organisms.

• Crosscutting Concept: Cause and Effect
  Students identify that human selection pressure CAUSES changes in trait frequency, with both intended effects (desired traits) and unintended consequences (reduced diversity).

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• From Wolves to Dogs:
  All domestic dogs (Canis lupus familiaris) descended from wolves through at least 15,000 years of selective breeding. Early humans selected wolves that were less aggressive and more cooperative. Over centuries, specialized breeding created everything from tiny Chihuahuas to massive Great Danes — all from the same ancestor.

• Artificial vs. Natural Selection:
  Both processes work through the same mechanism: variation exists, some individuals reproduce more than others, traits are inherited. The difference is WHO selects. In natural selection, the environment determines fitness. In artificial selection, HUMANS choose which traits to favor — often for our benefit rather than the organism's.

• The Cost of Extreme Breeding:
  Extreme selective breeding reduces genetic diversity, causing problems. Purebred English Bulldogs have such flat faces they can barely breathe. German Shepherds have sloped backs causing hip dysplasia. Cavalier King Charles Spaniels often have skulls too small for their brains. These are direct consequences of breeding for appearance over health.

• Modern Genetic Technologies:
  Today, selective breeding has been supplemented by genetic modification (GMOs), gene editing (CRISPR), and cloning. These technologies can achieve in one generation what would take decades of selective breeding. They raise important ethical questions about how far humans should go in modifying other organisms.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Selective breeding is the same as genetic modification"
  Reality: Selective breeding uses natural reproduction — choosing which organisms mate based on their traits. Genetic modification directly alters DNA in a lab. Selective breeding has been done for 10,000+ years; GMOs are a modern technology. Both change organisms, but through very different methods.
  Strategy: Analogy: Selective breeding is like choosing which songs to play on a playlist. GMO is like remixing the songs in a studio.

• "Artificial selection is unnatural"
  Reality: Humans have been selectively breeding organisms for over 10,000 years — longer than recorded history. Every fruit, vegetable, and domestic animal has been modified by humans. The 'natural' versions of corn, bananas, and watermelon look nothing like what we eat today.
  Strategy: Show images of wild banana (tiny, full of seeds) vs. modern banana. Wild corn (teosinte) vs. sweet corn. We changed these.

• "More selection is always better"
  Reality: Extreme selection creates a dangerous trade-off. While the desired trait increases, genetic diversity crashes. Low diversity makes populations vulnerable to disease epidemics, environmental changes, and inherited disorders. The Irish Potato Famine killed over a million people because all the potatoes were genetically identical.
  Strategy: Show the bulldog breathing problem. Ask: We got the flat face we wanted. Was it worth this cost?

ANSWER KEY:
Big Question: How have humans changed plants and animals to suit our needs — and at what cost?
Answer: Humans change organisms through selective breeding — choosing individuals with desired traits to reproduce generation after generation. It works, but there's a hidden cost: extreme selection reduces genetic diversity, making populations vulnerable to disease and creating health problems. Purebred bulldogs can barely breathe because we bred them for flat faces!

Simulation Answers:
• Extreme Purebred Scenario: When Selection Pressure is maximized, Desired Trait Frequency rises rapidly — the trait becomes nearly universal within a few generations. But Genetic Diversity crashes. With fewer genetic variants, the population becomes vulnerable to diseases, environmental changes, and inherited health problems.
• Wild Population Scenario: With no Selection Pressure, Desired Trait Frequency stays at natural levels (no change in any specific direction). Genetic Diversity remains high — the population has maximum variation, making it resilient and adaptable to environmental challenges.

Reflection Exemplars:
• Q: How is artificial selection like natural selection?
  A: Both work through the same mechanism: (1) Variation exists in the population, (2) Some individuals reproduce more, (3) Traits are inherited by offspring. The only difference is WHO decides which traits are 'fit.' In natural selection, the environment decides. In artificial selection, humans decide.
• Q: Why do purebred dogs have more health problems?
  A: Extreme selective breeding dramatically reduces genetic diversity. When only dogs with specific appearance traits are allowed to breed (generation after generation), harmful recessive genes become concentrated. English Bulldogs were bred for flat faces, creating breathing problems. German Shepherds were bred for sloped backs, causing hip dysplasia. The 'desired' appearance comes at the cost of health.

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2 (Developing and Using Models) + DCI LS4.B (Natural selection and artificial selection) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

In a computational model of selective breeding, a student sets the selection pressure slider to maximum for a single trait: short legs in dogs. The model runs for 20 simulated generations. Two graphs update in real time. Graph 1 shows 'Desired Trait Frequency' rising sharply from 10 percent to 95 percent over the 20 generations. Graph 2 shows 'Genetic Diversity Index' dropping sharply from 100 percent to 15 percent over the same period. The student notices that as the desired trait increases, genetic diversity crashes.

What does the model reveal about the relationship between extreme selection pressure and genetic diversity?

A. Genetic diversity increases as the desired trait becomes more common because the population is improving
B. Genetic diversity remains unchanged because selective breeding only affects the one trait being selected
C. Genetic diversity and trait frequency are unrelated variables that change independently
D. Genetic diversity crashes because only organisms with the desired trait are allowed to reproduce, drastically shrinking the gene pool

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: The model reveals a critical trade-off. When only dogs with short legs are allowed to breed, all the genetic variation carried by dogs without short legs is eliminated from the population. Each generation, the gene pool gets smaller. After 20 generations of extreme selection, 85 percent of the original genetic diversity has been lost. If you chose A, the graph clearly shows diversity dropping as the trait increases. Selecting for one trait removes individuals (and their genes) that do not have that trait. If you chose B, when you prevent most of the population from reproducing, you eliminate not just the unwanted trait but all the other genetic variation those individuals carried. If you chose C, the model shows a clear inverse relationship: as trait frequency rises, diversity falls. They are directly linked through the selection process.

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 6 (Constructing Explanations) + DCI LS4.B (Unintended consequences of artificial selection) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

English Bulldogs were selectively bred for over 200 years to achieve their signature flat face (brachycephalic skull). Breeders consistently chose the flattest-faced dogs for reproduction and excluded dogs with longer snouts. Today, the breed standard requires an extremely flat face. However, veterinary data shows that 70 percent of English Bulldogs suffer from Brachycephalic Obstructive Airway Syndrome (BOAS), which causes chronic difficulty breathing, overheating, sleep apnea, and reduced life expectancy (average 8 years compared to 12 years for mixed-breed dogs of similar size).

What does the English Bulldog example demonstrate about selective breeding?

A. Selective breeding can successfully achieve a desired cosmetic trait but may cause serious unintended health consequences when breeders focus on appearance without considering overall organism function
B. The breathing problems are unrelated to the flat face and are caused by a virus that only affects Bulldogs
C. Only natural selection can produce healthy organisms because artificial selection always fails
D. Selective breeding always produces the healthiest possible animals because breeders know best

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: The Bulldog case is a textbook example of unintended consequences. Breeders achieved their goal (flat face), but the same skull changes that flattened the face also compressed the airways, soft palate, and nasal passages. The trait breeders wanted and the health problem are physically connected. If you chose D, the 70 percent BOAS rate and 4-year reduction in lifespan directly contradict the claim that selective breeding always produces healthy animals. If you chose B, BOAS is a structural problem caused by the skull shape, not a virus. The compressed airways are a direct physical consequence of the flat skull that breeders selected for. If you chose C, artificial selection is a powerful tool when used responsibly (crop improvement, disease-resistant livestock). The problem is not artificial selection itself, but narrowly focusing on appearance without considering health.

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 7 (Engaging in Argument from Evidence) + DCI LS4.B (Genetic diversity and population resilience) + CCC7 (Stability and Change)

A conservation biologist compares two populations of the same salmon species. Population A has high genetic diversity with many different allele variants. Population B has very low genetic diversity because it was bred in a hatchery from only 10 original fish. Both populations face a new waterborne pathogen that has never affected this species before. After one year of exposure, 60 percent of Population A survives, while only 5 percent of Population B survives.

Why might a population with high genetic diversity be more resilient against new threats than one with low diversity?

A. High genetic diversity means all organisms are identical, which creates uniform resistance to diseases
B. With more genetic variation, the population is more likely to contain some individuals with natural resistance to the new pathogen, allowing them to survive and reproduce
C. Genetic diversity makes individual organisms physically larger and therefore harder for pathogens to kill
D. Genetic diversity causes organisms to reproduce faster, replacing dead individuals more quickly

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Genetic diversity acts as biological insurance. In a diverse population, different individuals carry different immune system variants. When a new pathogen appears, some individuals are likely to have genetic resistance by chance. In Population A, enough resistant individuals survived (60 percent) to sustain the population. In Population B, with only 10 founders' genes, the chance of any individual having resistance was extremely low. If you chose C, genetic diversity affects the range of traits in a population, not the size of individuals. Resistance to pathogens comes from immune system variation, not body size. If you chose D, genetic diversity does not affect reproduction rate. It affects the variety of traits available for selection when new challenges arise. If you chose A, high genetic diversity means organisms are genetically different from each other, not identical. This variation is precisely what provides resilience.

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 6 (Constructing Explanations) + DCI LS4.B (Historical consequences of low genetic diversity) + CCC7 (Stability and Change — vulnerability from uniformity)

During the 1840s, Ireland depended heavily on potatoes as its primary food source. Nearly all Irish potatoes were a single variety called the Lumper, genetically almost identical to each other because they were reproduced by cloning (planting pieces of existing potatoes rather than growing from seed). In 1845, a water mold pathogen called Phytophthora infestans arrived from the Americas. Within two years, it destroyed nearly the entire Irish potato crop. Over one million people died and another million emigrated.

How does the selective breeding model help explain why the Irish Potato Famine was so devastating?

A. The potatoes were never selectively bred, so they had no defenses against any disease
B. Irish farmers grew a population of genetically near-identical potatoes, so when a pathogen could infect one plant, it could infect virtually all of them. There was no genetic variation for resistance.
C. The pathogen was too powerful for any potato variety to resist, regardless of genetic diversity
D. The famine was caused entirely by cold weather and had nothing to do with genetics or breeding practices

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: The Lumper potato was essentially a monoculture clone army. Every plant had the same genes, the same immune system, and the same vulnerabilities. When Phytophthora infestans found a way to infect one plant, it could infect them all. This is the real-world catastrophic consequence of the diversity crash the model predicts. If you chose A, the Lumper was actually the product of selection for high yield and easy growing. The problem was not lack of breeding but lack of genetic diversity in the breeding population. If you chose C, Peru (where potatoes originated) had thousands of genetically diverse potato varieties, many of which had natural resistance to the blight. Diversity would have saved the crop. If you chose D, while weather affected the severity, the fundamental cause was genetic uniformity. The pathogen destroyed the crop because every plant was equally vulnerable.

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 7 (Engaging in Argument from Evidence) + DCI LS4.B (Comparing natural and artificial selection) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A student creates a comparison table of natural selection and artificial selection. Both processes start with genetic variation in a population. In natural selection, the environment determines which traits help survival and reproduction. In artificial selection, humans choose which traits to favor. Natural selection operates over thousands to millions of years. Artificial selection can produce dramatic changes in just decades. However, natural selection tends to maintain genetic diversity because many different traits can help survival, while artificial selection for a single trait tends to reduce diversity.

Based on the comparison, why does artificial selection often reduce genetic diversity more rapidly than natural selection?

A. There is no real difference in how the two processes affect genetic diversity
B. Natural selection always increases diversity, while artificial selection always decreases it without exception
C. In artificial selection, humans typically select for one or a few specific traits and exclude all organisms without those traits from breeding, eliminating far more genetic variation per generation than natural selection, which favors many survival-related traits simultaneously
D. Artificial selection is weaker than natural selection and cannot maintain diversity

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: Natural selection operates on many traits simultaneously (disease resistance, foraging ability, predator avoidance, reproductive success), so many different genetic combinations can be 'fit.' Artificial selection typically targets one or a few traits and removes all organisms that lack them, creating a much narrower genetic bottleneck per generation. If you chose D, artificial selection is actually faster and more intense than natural selection because humans can prevent all 'undesirable' individuals from reproducing, something natural selection rarely achieves. If you chose B, the relationship is not absolute. Well-managed artificial selection programs can maintain diversity, and natural selection can reduce diversity in small populations (genetic drift). If you chose A, the comparison data clearly shows different outcomes: natural selection maintains broader diversity because it selects on many traits, while artificial selection on a single trait creates a bottleneck.
