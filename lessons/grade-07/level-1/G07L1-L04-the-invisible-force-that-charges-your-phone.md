## Platform

### Title
**The Invisible Force That Charges Your Phone — How Electricity and Magnetism Shape Our World**

### Overview
Electromagnetism is one of the four fundamental forces of nature and the foundation of modern technology. Every electric motor, generator, speaker, and MRI machine relies on the connection between electricity and magnetism discovered in the 1800s. Students investigate the driving question: How can a force you can't see, smell, or touch move objects and transfer energy? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

### NGSS Standard
**MS-PS2-3, MS-PS2-5**: Ask questions about data to determine the factors that affect the strength of electric and magnetic forces. Conduct an investigation and evaluate the experimental design to provide evidence that fields exist between objects exerting forces on each other even though the objects are not in contact.

### Learning Objectives
- Explain how electric current flowing through a wire creates a magnetic field
- Model the factors that affect electromagnet strength — current, coils, and core material
- Predict how changing variables affects the strength of an electromagnetic force
- Design experiments to test how invisible fields exert real forces on objects

LESSON PURPOSE:
Electromagnetism is one of the four fundamental forces of nature and the foundation of modern technology. Every electric motor, generator, speaker, and MRI machine relies on the connection between electricity and magnetism discovered in the 1800s.

NGSS ALIGNMENT:
MS-PS2-3, MS-PS2-5: Ask questions about data to determine the factors that affect the strength of electric and magnetic forces. THREE-DIMENSIONAL LEARNING:
• Science Practice: Asking Questions and Planning Investigations
  Students ask questions about data and design investigations to determine factors affecting electromagnetic force strength.
• Disciplinary Core Idea: PS2.B Types of Interactions
  Electric and magnetic forces between a pair of objects do not require contact. The sizes of the forces in each situation depend on the properties of the objects and their distances apart.
• Crosscutting Concept: Cause and Effect
  Students identify that specific changes in current and coils CAUSE predictable changes in magnetic field strength and lifting capacity.

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• Electricity Creates Magnetism:
  In 1820, Hans Christian Oersted discovered that electric current creates a magnetic field around a wire. This was revolutionary — it showed that electricity and magnetism are not separate forces but aspects of the same phenomenon. Every wire carrying current is a tiny magnet.

• How Electromagnets Work:
  Wrapping current-carrying wire into a coil concentrates the magnetic field. Each loop of wire adds its field to the next, creating a much stronger combined field. Adding an iron core further amplifies the effect because iron is easily magnetized, aligning its internal magnetic domains with the coil's field.

• Factors Affecting Strength:
  Three main factors control electromagnet strength: (1) Current — more current means a stronger field; (2) Number of coils — more wraps concentrate the field; (3) Core material — ferromagnetic materials like iron amplify the field. These factors combine to determine the total electromagnetic force.

• Real-World Applications:
  Electromagnets are everywhere: electric motors (fans, cars, power tools), generators (power plants), speakers (phones, headphones), MRI machines (hospitals), maglev trains, and junkyard cranes. The ability to turn magnetism on and off with a switch makes electromagnets far more versatile than permanent magnets.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Magnets are magic"
  Reality: Magnetism is a fundamental force of nature with well-understood physics. It results from the motion of electric charges at the atomic level. Every magnet works because of electron behavior.
  Strategy: Build an electromagnet. Turn current on = magnet. Turn off = not a magnet. It's physics, not magic.

• "Electricity and magnetism are unrelated"
  Reality: Electricity and magnetism are two aspects of the SAME force — electromagnetism. Moving charges create magnetic fields, and moving magnets create electric current. They are fundamentally linked.
  Strategy: Show Oersted's experiment: a compass needle deflects when current flows through a nearby wire.

• "Bigger magnets are always stronger"
  Reality: Size alone doesn't determine magnet strength. An electromagnet's strength depends on current, number of coils, and core material — not just physical size. A small, well-designed electromagnet can be stronger than a large one.
  Strategy: Compare a large weak magnet to a small powerful one. Measure lifting capacity of each.

FACILITATION TIPS:
• Step 1: Let students explore the interface. Don't over-explain.
  Let them discover. Circulate and support, don't lecture.
• Step 2: Ask "When this goes up, what happens to that?" to
  guide positive/negative relationship decisions. Let students debate.
• Step 3: Give time for students to "break" the model — turn
  things on/off and observe. This is where real insight happens.
• Step 4: Don't give answers. Ask questions. Let curiosity drive
  the research. Celebrate when students' additions don't work as
  expected — that's authentic science.

ANSWER KEY:
Big Question: How can a force you can't see, smell, or touch move objects and transfer energy?
Answer: Invisible electromagnetic forces work because moving electric charges (current) create magnetic fields around them. By wrapping wire into coils and increasing current, we concentrate this invisible force into something strong enough to lift cars in junkyards — all from a force you can't see!

Simulation Answers:
• Maximum Coils Scenario: When Number of Coil Wraps is maximized with moderate current, Magnetic Field Strength increases significantly. Lifting Capacity rises proportionally. Each additional coil contributes to the total field strength.
• Full Power Scenario: When both Electric Current AND Coil Wraps are at maximum, Magnetic Field Strength reaches its peak. The combined effect is greater than either variable alone — they multiply rather than simply add. This demonstrates why real electromagnets optimize BOTH variables.

Reflection Exemplars:
• Q: Why are electromagnets more useful than permanent magnets?
  A: Electromagnets can be turned ON and OFF with a switch — permanent magnets can't. This is critical for applications like junkyard cranes (pick up, move, drop), MRI machines (controlled scanning), electric motors (alternating field creates rotation), and speakers (rapidly changing field creates sound waves).
• Q: How does your phone use electromagnetic forces?
  A: Your phone's speaker uses an electromagnet to vibrate a diaphragm (sound). The vibration motor is a tiny electric motor (electromagnetic rotation). Wireless charging uses electromagnetic induction. The cellular antenna sends and receives electromagnetic waves. The touchscreen uses electric fields to detect your finger.

Key Concepts:
• Magnetic Domains: Inside ferromagnetic materials like iron, groups of atoms create tiny magnetic regions called domains. An external field aligns these domains, amplifying the effect.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format. Each item is three-dimensional, assessing a Science and Engineering Practice (SEP), Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and Crosscutting Concept (CCC) simultaneously, aligned to MS-PS2-3, MS-PS2-5.

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 3 (Planning and Carrying Out Investigations) + DCI PS2.B (Types of interactions — electromagnetic forces) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A student builds an electromagnet by wrapping 50 turns of copper wire around an iron nail and connecting the wire to a 1.5-volt battery. The electromagnet picks up 8 small paper clips. The student wants to make the electromagnet stronger and considers four possible modifications to the design.

Which modification would most likely increase the number of paper clips the electromagnet can lift?

A. Using a longer nail but keeping the same 50 turns of wire spread over its length
B. Moving the electromagnet farther away from the paper clips before turning it on
C. Wrapping 100 turns of wire around the nail instead of 50
D. Replacing the iron nail with a wooden dowel of the same size

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: Doubling the number of coil wraps concentrates and amplifies the magnetic field because each loop of current-carrying wire contributes its own magnetic field, and these fields add together. More loops means a stronger combined field. If you chose D, an iron core is essential because iron is ferromagnetic and amplifies the magnetic field dramatically. A wooden dowel is not magnetic and would produce a much weaker electromagnet. If you chose A, spreading the same number of wraps over a longer nail actually decreases field strength at each point because the turns are farther apart and their fields do not reinforce each other as strongly. If you chose B, magnetic field strength decreases with distance. Moving the electromagnet farther away would reduce the force on the paper clips, not increase it.

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 4 (Analyzing and Interpreting Data) + DCI PS2.B (Factors affecting electromagnetic force) + CCC3 (Scale, Proportion, and Quantity)

In a computational model of an electromagnet, a student tests the effects of two variables: electric current (amps) and number of coil wraps. She records these results in a data table. With 1 amp and 50 wraps, the model shows a magnetic field strength of 100 units. With 2 amps and 50 wraps, it shows 200 units. With 1 amp and 100 wraps, it shows 200 units. With 2 amps and 100 wraps, it shows 400 units. She notices that changing just one variable doubles the field, but changing both quadruples it.

What does the data pattern demonstrate about how current and coil wraps affect magnetic field strength?

A. Magnetic field strength has a maximum limit that cannot be exceeded regardless of current or coils
B. Only the number of coil wraps matters; current has no real effect on the field
C. Electric current cancels out the effect of additional coils, so only one variable should be changed
D. The two variables have a combined multiplicative effect, so increasing both together produces a much stronger field than changing either one alone

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: The data shows a clear multiplicative relationship. Doubling either variable alone doubles the output (100 to 200), but doubling both together quadruples it (100 to 400). This is because magnetic field strength is proportional to the product of current times the number of turns. If you chose B, the data clearly shows that increasing current from 1 to 2 amps doubled the field strength at both 50 wraps (100 to 200) and 100 wraps (200 to 400). Current clearly matters. If you chose A, the data shows no evidence of a maximum limit. Each increase in current or coils produced a proportional increase in field strength. If you chose C, the data shows the opposite: current and coils reinforce each other. When both are doubled, the result is four times stronger, not weaker.

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 6 (Constructing Explanations) + DCI PS2.B (Electromagnetic force at a distance) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A student holds a strong bar magnet near a compass and observes the compass needle swing to point toward the magnet instead of north. She moves the magnet farther away and watches the needle slowly return toward north. She repeats this at several distances and records how much the needle deflects at each distance. At 2 centimeters the needle points directly at the magnet. At 5 centimeters it deflects 45 degrees. At 10 centimeters it deflects only 10 degrees. At 20 centimeters the needle barely moves.

Which conclusion is best supported by the student's data?

A. Magnetic force and gravitational force are identical, which is why both act at a distance
B. The compass needle moves because air molecules carry the magnetic force from the magnet to the needle
C. Magnetic field strength decreases rapidly with distance, but the field extends beyond the magnet's surface and acts on objects without physical contact
D. The magnet only produces a field when it is touching the compass needle

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: The data shows that the magnetic field is strongest close to the magnet and weakens rapidly with distance, but it still exerts a measurable force even at 10 centimeters away. This demonstrates that magnetic fields extend through space and exert force without physical contact, which is the defining property of a field force. If you chose D, the data shows the needle deflecting at every distance tested, even 10 centimeters away. The field clearly extends far beyond the magnet's surface. If you chose B, magnetic fields propagate through vacuum and do not require air or any medium. Magnets work in outer space where there are no air molecules. If you chose A, while both magnetic and gravitational forces act at a distance, they are fundamentally different forces with different properties. Gravity only attracts, while magnetism can attract or repel.

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 6 (Constructing Explanations) + DCI PS2.B (Controllability of electromagnetic force) + CCC4 (Systems and System Models)

A junkyard uses a massive electromagnet suspended from a crane to sort metal scrap. The operator turns the electromagnet on to pick up a car, moves the crane over a pile, and turns the electromagnet off to drop the car. A seventh-grader watching this asks: 'Why don't they just use a permanent magnet? It would never run out of battery.' The junkyard owner explains that an electromagnet is essential for this job.

Which explanation best describes why an electromagnet is more useful than a permanent magnet for the junkyard crane?

A. Electromagnets are always cheaper to manufacture than permanent magnets
B. Electromagnets can be turned on and off with a switch, and their strength can be adjusted by changing the current, giving the operator precise control over when objects are picked up and released
C. Electromagnets are lighter than permanent magnets, making the crane easier to operate
D. Permanent magnets do not work on metal objects, while electromagnets do

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: The key advantage of an electromagnet is controllability. The operator needs to turn the magnetic force on to grab metal and off to release it. A permanent magnet cannot be switched off, so the car could never be dropped. Additionally, adjusting the current lets the operator control how strong the grip is. If you chose A, cost is not the primary advantage. Many industrial electromagnets are actually more expensive than permanent magnets because they require electrical infrastructure. If you chose D, permanent magnets absolutely work on ferromagnetic metals like iron and steel. Both types of magnets attract the same materials. If you chose C, industrial electromagnets are typically much heavier than comparable permanent magnets because they contain large coils of wire and iron cores.

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 7 (Engaging in Argument from Evidence) + DCI PS2.B (Evidence for invisible forces) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A student's electromagnet model shows that increasing electric current from 0 to 3 amps causes the number of paper clips lifted to increase from 0 to 24 in a proportional relationship. When the current is turned off, all paper clips fall. The student also tests distance: at 1 centimeter the electromagnet lifts 24 clips, at 3 centimeters it lifts 8, and at 5 centimeters it lifts only 2. A classmate argues: 'You cannot prove invisible forces are real because you cannot see them.'

Using the model's data, which argument best demonstrates that the invisible magnetic force is real?

A. The model shows that changing the current produces predictable, measurable changes in lifting capacity without physical contact between the electromagnet and the clips, which is evidence of a real force acting through an invisible field
B. Invisible forces cannot be proven real, so the classmate is correct and we should accept that
C. The invisible force is real because the textbook says it is, and textbooks are always correct
D. The magnetic force is actually visible if you sprinkle iron filings around the magnet, so it is not really invisible

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: The model provides quantitative evidence: a systematic relationship between current and lifting capacity, and between distance and force. The force acts without contact, and its effects are predictable and repeatable. In science, a force is proven real not by seeing it directly, but by observing its measurable, reproducible effects. If you chose C, authority is not evidence. In science, claims must be supported by data and observations, not by appeals to authority. If you chose D, iron filings show the pattern of the field, not the force itself. More importantly, the argument should focus on the measurable effects (lifting capacity) as evidence, not on making the field visible. If you chose B, science regularly studies invisible phenomena (gravity, radio waves, atoms) by measuring their effects. The inability to see something directly does not mean it cannot be proven real.
