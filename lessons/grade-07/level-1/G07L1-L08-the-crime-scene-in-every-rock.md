## Platform

### Title
**The Crime Scene in Every Rock — Reading Earth's History One Layer at a Time**

### Overview
Earth is 4.6 billion years old, and its history is written in stone — literally. Rock strata (layers) form a record of environmental conditions, life forms, and geological events that have shaped our planet over deep time. Students investigate the driving question: How can a single rock tell you what happened on Earth millions of years ago? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

### NGSS Standard
**MS-ESS1-4**: Construct a scientific explanation based on evidence from rock strata for how the geologic time scale is used to organize Earth's 4.6-billion-year-old history.

### Learning Objectives
- Explain how rock strata form layers that represent different time periods
- Model the relationship between sedimentation, erosion, and fossil preservation
- Use the principle of superposition to determine relative age of rock layers
- Reconstruct past environments using evidence found in rock layers

### Component List (Starting Model: 3-4 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Sedimentation Rate | External | How quickly new layers of sediment are deposited by water, wind, or volcanic activity |
| Erosion Rate | External | How quickly existing rock layers are worn away by water, wind, ice, or chemical processes |
| Rock Layer Thickness | Internal | How thick the accumulated rock strata become over geological time |
| Fossil Preservation Quality | Internal | How well organisms are preserved in the rock — depends on burial speed and conditions |

LESSON PURPOSE:
Earth is 4.6 billion years old, and its history is written in stone — literally. Rock strata (layers) form a record of environmental conditions, life forms, and geological events that have shaped our planet over deep time.

NGSS ALIGNMENT:
MS-ESS1-4: Construct a scientific explanation based on evidence from rock strata for how the geologic time scale is used to organize Earth's 4.6-billion-year-old history.

THREE-DIMENSIONAL LEARNING:
• Science Practice: Constructing Explanations
  Students construct scientific explanations using evidence from rock strata to explain how geologists organize Earth's history.
• Disciplinary Core Idea: ESS1.C The History of Planet Earth
  Rock strata and the fossil record can be used as evidence to organize the relative occurrence of major historical events in Earth's history.
• Crosscutting Concept: Scale, Proportion, and Quantity
  Students work with vast timescales (millions to billions of years) and understand how thin layers of rock can represent enormous spans of geological time.

LEVER FRAMEWORK:
• Locate: Students identify sedimentation rate, erosion rate, rock layer thickness, and fossil preservation quality as components of the geological record system.
• Establish: Students determine that sedimentation builds layers while erosion destroys them, and that rapid burial improves fossil preservation.
• Visualize: Students build a model showing how sedimentation and erosion interact to create (or destroy) the rock record.
• Evaluate: Students run rapid deposition, heavy erosion, and balanced scenarios to observe different geological outcomes.
• Revise: Students add tectonic activity or sea level change to explore how geological forces alter the record after formation.

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• How Rock Layers Form:
  Sedimentary rock forms when particles (sand, mud, shells, organic material) settle and accumulate in layers. Over millions of years, pressure from overlying layers compresses and cements these sediments into solid rock. Each layer represents a specific time period and environment.

• Principle of Superposition:
  In undisturbed rock sequences, the oldest layers are at the bottom and the youngest are at the top. This principle, established by Nicolas Steno in 1669, allows geologists to determine the RELATIVE age of rock layers and the fossils they contain without needing absolute dates.

• Fossils as Evidence:
  Fossils are the preserved remains or traces of ancient organisms. They form when organisms are rapidly buried in sediment before decomposition. Index fossils — from organisms that lived for a short time but across a wide geographic area — are especially useful for dating and correlating rock layers across distant locations.

• Gaps in the Record:
  Unconformities are gaps in the rock record where erosion has removed layers or deposition didn't occur. These represent missing time — potentially millions of years of history erased. Geologists identify three types: angular unconformities, disconformities, and nonconformities.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Rocks don't change"
  Reality: Rocks are constantly being created, destroyed, and recycled through the rock cycle. Sedimentary rocks can be metamorphosed by heat and pressure, melted into magma, or eroded into sediment that forms new rock. Earth's surface is dynamic.
  Strategy: Show images of the same location millions of years apart. Ask: Does this look like nothing changed?

• "Fossils are just pretty rocks"
  Reality: Fossils are preserved evidence of ancient life — they contain critical information about what organisms existed, what the environment was like, and how life has changed over time. Each fossil is a data point in the story of evolution.
  Strategy: Give students real or replica fossils. Ask: What can you learn about this organism and its world from just this rock?

• "The Earth has always looked like it does now"
  Reality: Earth's surface has changed dramatically over 4.6 billion years. Continents have moved, oceans have appeared and disappeared, mountains have risen and eroded away. The landscape you see today is just a snapshot of an ever-changing planet.
  Strategy: Show a time-lapse animation of continental drift from Pangaea to present. Earth never stays the same.

  things on/off and observe. This is where real insight happens.
• Step 4: Don't give answers. Ask questions. Let curiosity drive
  the research. Celebrate when students' additions don't work as
  expected — that's authentic science.

ANSWER KEY:
Big Question: How can a single rock tell you what happened on Earth millions of years ago?
Answer: Rocks are history books written in stone. Each layer records the environment when it formed — ocean sediments contain marine fossils, desert layers contain sand dunes, volcanic layers contain ash. By reading layers from bottom to top (superposition), we can reconstruct millions of years of Earth's story!

Simulation Answers:
• Rapid Deposition Scenario: When Sedimentation Rate is maximized (simulating a flood or volcanic ash fall), Rock Layer Thickness increases rapidly. Fossil Preservation Quality is excellent because organisms are buried quickly before they can decompose. This is why many of the best fossil sites are locations of ancient floods or volcanic events.
• Heavy Erosion Scenario: When Erosion Rate dominates, Rock Layer Thickness decreases as layers are worn away. Fossil Preservation Quality drops because exposed fossils are destroyed by weathering. Heavy erosion creates unconformities — gaps where millions of years of history are simply MISSING from the record.

Reflection Exemplars:
• Q: Why is the fossil record incomplete?
  A: Three main reasons: (1) Most organisms decompose before fossilization — you need rapid burial in the right conditions. (2) Erosion destroys existing fossils and rock layers. (3) Tectonic activity can metamorphose or melt fossil-bearing rocks. Scientists estimate we've found less than 1% of all species that have ever lived.
• Q: How do marine fossils end up on mountaintops?
  A: Millions of years ago, those rocks formed at the bottom of an ancient ocean. Marine organisms died and were buried in ocean sediment. Over millions of years, tectonic forces uplifted those ocean-floor rocks thousands of feet into the air, creating mountains. The fossils traveled with the rock from ocean floor to mountaintop.

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 6 (Constructing Explanations) + DCI ESS1.C (History of Earth — rock strata as evidence) + CCC1 (Patterns)

A geologist examines a cliff face in Utah and documents five horizontal rock layers. Layer 1 (bottom, oldest) is limestone containing marine fossils. Layer 2 is sandstone with fossilized footprints of land animals. Layer 3 is shale containing fern leaf impressions. Layer 4 is limestone again with different marine fossils than Layer 1. Layer 5 (top, youngest) is sandstone with no fossils. The geologist notes that the principle of superposition tells us that in undisturbed layers, the bottom layer is the oldest.

What environmental history does this rock sequence reveal about this location?

A. All five layers formed simultaneously during a single catastrophic flood event
B. The layers are in random order and do not tell us anything about environmental history
C. The area was covered by ocean, then became land, then developed forests, then was flooded by ocean again, then became dry land. The environment changed multiple times over millions of years.
D. The area has always been dry land because sandstone only forms in deserts

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: Reading bottom to top using superposition: Layer 1 (marine limestone) indicates ocean. Layer 2 (sandstone with land tracks) indicates the sea retreated and land appeared. Layer 3 (shale with ferns) indicates a forested wetland. Layer 4 (marine limestone again) indicates the ocean returned. Layer 5 (barren sandstone) indicates dry land returned. This records millions of years of environmental change. If you chose D, the limestone layers with marine fossils clearly indicate ocean coverage at those times. Sandstone can form in many environments, not just deserts. If you chose A, the different fossil types in different layers (marine, land animals, plants, different marine species) indicate formation over different time periods with different environments. If you chose B, the principle of superposition gives the layers a clear chronological order from oldest (bottom) to youngest (top), telling a coherent environmental story.

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 2 (Developing and Using Models) + DCI ESS1.C (Fossil preservation and sedimentation rate) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

In the rock strata model, a student adjusts the sedimentation rate slider to maximum, simulating a sudden flood event that rapidly buries the area in thick sediment. The model tracks both sedimentation rate and fossil preservation quality. Before the flood simulation, the model shows a fish dying on the surface and slowly decomposing over several weeks with very poor fossil preservation. During the flood simulation, the same fish is buried within hours under thick sediment, and the model shows excellent fossil preservation.

What does the model predict about the relationship between rapid burial and fossil preservation quality?

A. No fossils can form during flood events because moving water prevents sedimentation
B. Rapid burial destroys all organisms, making fossil preservation impossible
C. Rapid burial produces excellent fossil preservation because organisms are covered before decomposition and scavengers can break them down
D. Fossil preservation quality depends only on the type of organism, not on how quickly it is buried

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: Rapid burial is one of the most important factors in fossil preservation. When an organism is quickly covered by sediment, it is protected from oxygen, bacteria, and scavengers that would otherwise decompose it. Many of the world's most spectacular fossil sites (like the Burgess Shale) formed during rapid deposition events. If you chose B, while extreme forces can damage organisms, the primary effect of rapid burial is protection from decomposition. Many exquisitely preserved fossils formed during flood events. If you chose D, while organism type matters (hard shells preserve better than soft tissue), burial speed is a critical factor. Even soft-bodied organisms can be preserved if buried rapidly enough. If you chose A, floods are actually excellent at depositing thick layers of sediment very quickly. Flood deposits are some of the most fossil-rich layers in the geological record.

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 4 (Analyzing and Interpreting Data) + DCI ESS1.C (Interpreting environmental change from strata) + CCC1 (Patterns)

A paleontologist studying a canyon in Arizona finds an unusual rock sequence. Layer A (bottom) contains abundant plant fossils and coal deposits. Layer B (middle) contains marine fossils including shells, coral fragments, and shark teeth. Layer C (top) contains plant fossils and freshwater fish. Between layers A and B, there is a sharp boundary with no gradual transition. The paleontologist measures the thickness of each layer and notes that Layer B is much thinner than layers A and C.

What is the best interpretation of the marine fossil layer (B) sitting between two land-based layers?

A. The marine and plant fossils formed at the same time but were sorted into different layers by gravity
B. The area was initially land with forests, then sea levels rose and ocean covered the area long enough to deposit marine sediment, then sea levels dropped and land returned
C. The rock layers were flipped upside down by an earthquake, putting marine fossils in the middle
D. Ancient people collected marine shells and scattered them between the plant layers

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: The sequence tells a clear story of environmental change: forests (Layer A) were submerged when sea levels rose, marine organisms lived and died in the ocean that covered the area (Layer B), and then the sea retreated and terrestrial life returned (Layer C). The thin marine layer suggests the ocean coverage was relatively brief. If you chose D, there is no evidence of human activity in these ancient rock layers, and the fossils are embedded within the rock, not scattered on a surface. If you chose A, fossils form within the sediment of their living environment. Marine fossils form in marine sediment, not in terrestrial sediment that was somehow rearranged. If you chose C, if the layers were inverted, the superposition principle would not apply, but there are geological indicators (cross-bedding, fossil orientation) that confirm these layers are right-side up.

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 6 (Constructing Explanations) + DCI ESS1.C (Unconformities and missing time) + CCC7 (Stability and Change)

While mapping rock layers in Nevada, a geologist discovers an unconformity: a gap in the rock record where expected layers are missing. Below the gap, she finds 300-million-year-old marine limestone. Above the gap, she finds 100-million-year-old sandstone. This means 200 million years of geological history are unrepresented at this location. Nearby sites have complete records showing that during those 200 million years, the area experienced multiple environmental changes including tropical forests, deserts, and shallow seas.

What does the unconformity represent, and what most likely caused the 200-million-year gap?

A. A gap where rock layers were deposited but later eroded away, or where conditions prevented deposition from occurring, leaving 200 million years of Earth's history unrecorded at this location
B. Nothing happened at this location for 200 million years, so no rocks formed
C. A layer of unusual rare minerals that formed during those 200 million years
D. A boundary between igneous and metamorphic rock that has nothing to do with time

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: An unconformity is a gap in the geological record representing lost time. Rock layers that once existed were eroded away by wind, water, or ice, or conditions at the location prevented new sediment from being deposited. The nearby sites prove that geological events did occur during those 200 million years; the evidence was simply destroyed at this particular location. If you chose C, unconformities are gaps (absences of rock), not unusual rock types. They represent missing pages in Earth's history book. If you chose D, unconformities can occur between any rock types and specifically represent missing time, not just a rock type boundary. If you chose B, the nearby sites with complete records prove that geological processes were active during those 200 million years. The gap exists because evidence was removed by erosion, not because nothing happened.

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 2 (Developing and Using Models) + DCI ESS1.C (Erosion vs. deposition in the rock record) + CCC7 (Stability and Change — competing processes)

In the rock strata model, a student sets the erosion rate higher than the sedimentation rate and runs the simulation for 50 million simulated years. The model shows existing rock layers being progressively worn away from the top down. Fossils embedded in those layers are destroyed as the rock containing them erodes. The rock record at that location becomes thinner and thinner. The student then reverses the settings, making sedimentation rate higher than erosion rate, and watches new layers begin to build up on top of the eroded surface.

Based on the model, what happens to the geological record at a location where erosion dominates for an extended period?

A. Rock layers continue building normally because erosion only affects surface soil, not deeper rock
B. Erosion creates new, better-preserved fossils by exposing them to fresh air
C. The rock layers become stronger and more resistant to future erosion as they are compressed
D. Rock layers are progressively worn away from the top, destroying the fossils within them and creating a gap in the geological record at that location

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: When erosion exceeds deposition, existing rock layers are worn away from the top down. Each layer that erodes takes its fossil record with it, permanently destroying that evidence. This is how unconformities form: periods of erosion remove rock and create gaps in the record. Erosion acts as a thief, stealing pages from Earth's history book. If you chose A, erosion absolutely affects deep rock layers given enough time. Canyons like the Grand Canyon prove that erosion can cut through hundreds of meters of rock. If you chose B, while erosion can expose fossils temporarily, continued erosion eventually destroys them entirely. Exposed fossils weather and crumble without protective rock around them. If you chose C, erosion weakens and removes rock; it does not strengthen it. Compression from burial (lithification) strengthens rock, but erosion removes the overlying layers needed for compression.
