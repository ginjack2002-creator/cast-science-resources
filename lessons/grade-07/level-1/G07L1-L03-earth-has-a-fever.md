## Platform

### Title
**Earth Has a Fever — Why 2 Degrees Could Change Everything**

### Overview
Climate change is one of the defining challenges of the 21st century. Understanding the science behind it — greenhouse gases, feedback loops, and cascading effects — is essential for making informed decisions about our planet's future. Students investigate the driving question: If Earth's temperature rises by just 2 degrees Celsius, why could it change everything? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

### NGSS Standard
**MS-ESS3-5**: Ask questions to clarify evidence of the factors that have caused the rise in global temperatures over the past century.

### Learning Objectives
- Explain how the greenhouse effect traps heat in Earth's atmosphere
- Model how increasing CO2 concentration affects global temperature and ice coverage
- Predict cascade effects of rising temperatures on sea level and weather patterns
- Evaluate human contributions to climate change using model evidence

LESSON PURPOSE:
Climate change is one of the defining challenges of the 21st century. Understanding the science behind it — greenhouse gases, feedback loops, and cascading effects — is essential for making informed decisions about our planet's future.

NGSS ALIGNMENT:
MS-ESS3-5: Ask questions to clarify evidence of the factors that have caused the rise in global temperatures over the past century.

THREE-DIMENSIONAL LEARNING:

• Disciplinary Core Idea: ESS3.D Global Climate Change
  Human activities have significantly altered the biosphere, sometimes damaging or destroying natural habitats and causing extinction of species.
• Crosscutting Concept: Stability and Change
  Students analyze how small changes in CO2 concentration create large, cascading changes in global climate systems.

LEVER FRAMEWORK:
• Locate: Students identify CO2 concentration, solar energy, global temperature, and ice sheet coverage as key components of the climate system.
• Establish: Students determine that CO2 positively affects temperature, which negatively affects ice coverage, creating a cascade of effects.
• Visualize: Students build a climate model showing how greenhouse gas increases drive temperature and ice changes.
• Evaluate: Students run pre-industrial, current trajectory, and emission reduction scenarios to compare outcomes.
• Revise: Students add methane emissions or albedo effect to explore feedback loops in the climate system.

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• The Greenhouse Effect:
  Certain gases in Earth's atmosphere (CO2, methane, water vapor) allow sunlight to pass through but trap heat radiating back from Earth's surface. This natural greenhouse effect keeps Earth habitable — without it, average temperature would be -18°C. The problem is that human activities are dramatically increasing greenhouse gas concentrations.

• CO2 and Temperature:
  Atmospheric CO2 has increased from 280 ppm (pre-industrial) to over 420 ppm today — a 50% increase. Ice core data shows that CO2 and temperature have moved together for 800,000 years. The current rate of CO2 increase is unprecedented in the geological record.

• Feedback Loops:
  Climate change involves powerful feedback loops. As temperatures rise, ice melts, exposing dark ocean and land that absorb MORE heat (ice-albedo feedback). Warming permafrost releases stored methane, causing MORE warming. These positive feedbacks can accelerate change beyond what CO2 alone would cause.

• Impacts of 2°C Warming:
  At 2°C above pre-industrial levels: sea levels could rise 0.5m+ (flooding coastal cities), extreme weather events become more frequent, coral reefs face mass extinction, agricultural zones shift, and water scarcity affects billions. The difference between 1.5°C and 2°C is enormous in terms of human impact.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Weather and climate are the same thing"
  Reality: Weather is short-term, local conditions (today's temperature). Climate is long-term, global patterns (average temperature over decades). A cold day doesn't disprove climate change — it's the long-term TREND that matters.
  Strategy: Show a graph of daily temperatures (noisy) vs. the 30-year average trend (clearly rising). Ask: Which tells the real story?

• "The greenhouse effect is bad"
  Reality: The NATURAL greenhouse effect is essential — without it, Earth would be frozen. The problem is that humans have ENHANCED it by adding excess CO2, trapping more heat than the system can handle.
  Strategy: Ask: Is a blanket bad? No — but if you pile on 10 blankets in summer, you'll overheat. Same concept.

• "Scientists disagree about climate change"
  Reality: Over 97% of climate scientists agree that human activities are causing global warming. The scientific consensus is overwhelming. Disagreement exists about exact timelines and solutions, not about whether it's happening.
  Strategy: Show the 97% statistic visually. Compare to: 97% of doctors agree smoking causes cancer — would you argue?

FACILITATION TIPS:
• Step 1: Let students explore the interface. Don't over-explain.
  Let them discover. Circulate and support, don't lecture.
• Step 2: Ask "When this goes up, what happens to that?" to
  guide positive/negative relationship decisions. Let students debate.
• Step 3: Give time for students to "break" the model — turn
  things on/off and observe. This is where real insight happens.
• Step 4: Don't give answers. Ask questions. Let curiosity drive
  the research. Celebrate when students' additions don't work as
  expected — that's authentic science.

ANSWER KEY:
Big Question: If Earth's temperature rises by just 2 degrees Celsius, why could it change everything?
Answer: Even 2°C of warming matters enormously because Earth's climate system has feedback loops. Rising CO2 traps heat, which melts ice, which exposes dark ocean that absorbs MORE heat, causing MORE melting. Small temperature changes cascade into massive effects on sea level, weather, and ecosystems!

Simulation Answers:
• Current Trajectory Scenario: When CO2 Concentration is locked at high levels, Average Global Temperature rises steadily. Ice Sheet Coverage decreases as temperatures climb. The model shows that sustained high CO2 leads to continuous warming and ice loss — matching real-world observations.
• Emission Reduction Scenario: When CO2 is reduced to moderate levels, temperature still rises but much more slowly. Ice Sheet Coverage decreases less dramatically. The key insight: even reduced emissions don't immediately reverse warming, but they significantly slow the rate of change.

Reflection Exemplars:
• Q: Why is 2°C dangerous if daily temps vary more?
  A: Daily temperature variations are LOCAL and TEMPORARY. A 2°C rise in AVERAGE GLOBAL temperature means the entire planet — every ocean, every continent, every ecosystem — is warmer all the time. It shifts weather patterns, melts ice caps permanently, raises sea levels globally, and disrupts ecosystems that evolved for specific temperature ranges.
• Q: How does ice melting create a feedback loop?
  A: Ice is white and reflects sunlight (high albedo). When ice melts, it exposes dark ocean water that ABSORBS heat instead. This absorbed heat raises temperatures further, melting MORE ice, exposing MORE dark water, absorbing MORE heat. The original warming AMPLIFIES itself — that's a positive feedback loop.

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format. Each item is three-dimensional, assessing a Science and Engineering Practice (SEP), Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and Crosscutting Concept (CCC) simultaneously, aligned to MS-ESS3-5.

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2 (Developing and Using Models) + DCI ESS3.D (Human activities and global warming) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A climate model shows the relationship between carbon dioxide concentration and global average temperature over the past 800,000 years. The data reveals a consistent pattern: when CO2 levels rise, global temperatures rise within a few hundred years, and when CO2 levels fall, temperatures follow. Pre-industrial CO2 levels were approximately 280 parts per million (ppm). A student sets the model's CO2 slider to the current level of 420 ppm and runs a simulation forward 100 years.

Based on the model's established pattern, what cascade of effects does increasing CO2 from 280 to 420 ppm predict?

A. Temperature decreases because more CO2 means more plants absorbing heat
B. Ice sheets grow thicker because warmer air carries more moisture and dumps more snow at the poles
C. Temperature stays exactly the same because Earth's systems are perfectly self-correcting
D. Temperature rises, which melts ice sheets, exposing dark ocean surfaces that absorb more heat, creating a feedback loop

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: More CO2 traps more heat through the greenhouse effect, raising temperatures. Higher temperatures melt reflective ice sheets, exposing dark ocean water that absorbs even more solar radiation, which raises temperatures further. This positive feedback loop (the ice-albedo effect) accelerates warming beyond what CO2 alone would cause. If you chose A, while plants do absorb CO2, the current rate of CO2 emissions far exceeds what plants can absorb, and deforestation is reducing that capacity. If you chose C, Earth's climate systems have tipping points and are not perfectly balanced at all CO2 levels. The 800,000-year data proves that CO2 changes drive temperature changes. If you chose B, while warmer air does hold more moisture, the net effect of warming is overwhelmingly ice loss, not ice gain. Any increased snowfall is far outweighed by accelerated melting.

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 7 (Engaging in Argument from Evidence) + DCI ESS3.D (Weather vs. climate) + CCC3 (Scale, Proportion, and Quantity)

During a particularly cold week in January, with temperatures dropping to minus 15 degrees Fahrenheit, a classmate posts on social media: 'It is literally freezing outside. Where is this global warming everyone keeps talking about? Climate change is clearly not real.' The post gets many likes and shares from people who agree. Meanwhile, NASA data shows that the previous year was the hottest on record globally, continuing a trend of rising average temperatures over the past 50 years.

Which response uses the best scientific reasoning to address the classmate's claim?

A. Climate change only happens in tropical regions, so cold weather in January is expected and irrelevant
B. The classmate is correct because personal experience of cold weather disproves climate data
C. Last week's cold snap is weather, which describes short-term, local conditions. Climate change refers to long-term shifts in global average temperatures measured over decades, and one cold week does not contradict a 50-year warming trend
D. The cold week proves that climate change has reversed and global temperatures are now dropping

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: Weather and climate operate on fundamentally different scales. Weather is what happens in one place over days or weeks. Climate is the average pattern across the entire planet over decades or longer. A single cold week in one city tells us nothing about the global average temperature trend, just as one losing game does not mean a team has a losing season. If you chose B, personal experience of local weather cannot override global data collected from thousands of stations, satellites, and ocean buoys over decades. If you chose A, climate change affects the entire planet, not just tropical regions. Arctic regions are actually warming faster than anywhere else. If you chose D, a single cold week cannot reverse a decades-long global trend. Global average temperatures continued to rise even during that cold week because other parts of the planet were warmer than average.

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 2 (Developing and Using Models) + DCI ESS3.D (Feedback loops in climate) + CCC4 (Systems and System Models)

A student explores the ice-albedo feedback loop in a climate model. The model shows these relationships: warming temperatures melt ice, melting ice exposes dark ocean, dark ocean absorbs more solar radiation, absorbed radiation raises temperatures, and higher temperatures melt more ice. The student labels this a 'positive feedback loop' in her lab notebook. Her lab partner objects, saying 'positive' means 'good for the environment,' so this cannot be positive since it accelerates warming.

Which explanation best clarifies why the ice-albedo effect is called a 'positive' feedback loop?

A. It is called positive because melting ice has a positive impact on ocean ecosystems by adding fresh water
B. It is called positive because adding more ice to the poles increases Earth's reflectivity and cools the planet
C. In science, 'positive feedback' means the output amplifies the original change. Warming causes melting, which causes more warming, which causes more melting. The cycle reinforces itself.
D. The term 'positive' is outdated and scientists no longer use it to describe feedback loops

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: In science, 'positive feedback' does not mean 'good.' It means the output of a process amplifies the original input. Warming melts ice, exposing dark surfaces that absorb more heat, causing more warming. Each cycle makes the next cycle stronger. This is the opposite of negative feedback, which stabilizes a system. If you chose A, fresh water from melting ice actually disrupts ocean circulation and harms many marine ecosystems. The term 'positive' does not refer to environmental benefit. If you chose B, this describes a negative feedback scenario (cooling reinforces cooling), not the ice-albedo positive feedback loop described in the stimulus. If you chose D, positive feedback is a current, widely-used scientific term in climate science, ecology, physiology, and engineering.

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 4 (Analyzing and Interpreting Data) + DCI ESS3.D (Comparing emission scenarios) + CCC3 (Scale, Proportion, and Quantity)

A climate model allows students to compare two scenarios over the next 80 years: Scenario A (current trajectory) keeps emissions increasing at today's rate, while Scenario B (emission reduction) cuts emissions by 50 percent by 2050 and reaches net zero by 2070. The model outputs show that Scenario A predicts a 3.5 degree Celsius temperature rise and 1.1 meters of sea level rise by 2100. Scenario B predicts a 1.5 degree Celsius rise and 0.4 meters of sea level rise by 2100. A student notices that even Scenario B shows continued warming.

Based on the model data, what is the key difference between the two scenarios?

A. Emission reduction significantly slows the rate of warming and limits sea level rise, though warming does not stop immediately because CO2 already in the atmosphere continues trapping heat
B. Emission reduction immediately reverses warming and restores all polar ice sheets to their original size
C. The current trajectory cools the planet because more CO2 stimulates plant growth that absorbs all excess heat
D. Both scenarios produce identical outcomes because CO2 has no measurable effect on temperature

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: The model shows that reducing emissions does not instantly fix the problem because CO2 persists in the atmosphere for centuries. However, Scenario B produces dramatically less warming (1.5 vs. 3.5 degrees C) and sea level rise (0.4 vs. 1.1 meters). The difference between these scenarios affects billions of people and countless ecosystems. If you chose B, the climate system has enormous inertia. CO2 already emitted will continue warming the planet for decades even after emissions stop. Reversal takes centuries. If you chose D, the model clearly shows very different outcomes: a 2-degree difference in temperature and a 0.7-meter difference in sea level rise, which contradicts the claim that CO2 has no effect. If you chose C, while plants absorb some CO2, current emissions are roughly double what Earth's natural systems can absorb. More CO2 does not cool the planet.

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 6 (Constructing Explanations) + DCI ESS3.D (Human impact on climate systems) + CCC4 (Systems and System Models)

A coastal city is planning for the future. Climate models predict that sea levels will rise 0.3 to 1.0 meters by 2100, depending on emission scenarios. The city council reviews three proposals: (1) build a sea wall high enough to block the maximum predicted rise, (2) restore wetlands and mangrove forests along the coast to absorb wave energy and storm surge, and (3) do nothing and hope the predictions are wrong. An engineer explains that the sea wall costs 500 million dollars but protects against flooding, while the wetland restoration costs 50 million dollars and also absorbs CO2, filters water, and supports biodiversity.

Based on the climate model and systems thinking, which approach provides the most comprehensive long-term solution?

A. Build only the sea wall because engineered solutions are always better than natural ones
B. Restore wetlands because they address multiple system components simultaneously: absorbing CO2, reducing storm surge, filtering water, and supporting biodiversity, while also being more cost-effective
C. Do nothing, because climate models are always wrong and sea levels will not actually rise
D. Combine the sea wall and wetland restoration, but the question only asks for one approach

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Wetland restoration is a systems-level solution that addresses multiple interconnected components: it reduces atmospheric CO2 (addressing the cause), absorbs storm surge (addressing the effect), filters water, supports biodiversity, and costs one-tenth as much as the sea wall. Systems thinking values solutions that improve multiple parts of the system simultaneously. If you chose C, ignoring model predictions is not a scientific approach. Climate models have accurately predicted warming trends for decades. Doing nothing puts millions of people at risk. If you chose A, engineered solutions like sea walls address only one symptom (flooding) without addressing the underlying cause or providing additional ecosystem benefits. If you chose D, while a combined approach could be effective, the question asks which single approach is most comprehensive, and wetland restoration addresses more system components per dollar invested.
