## Platform

### Title
**Why You Can't Slide Forever — The Hidden Energy Transfers That Stop Everything**

### Overview
Energy is one of the most fundamental concepts in all of science. The law of conservation of energy states that energy cannot be created or destroyed — only transferred or transformed. Understanding energy transfer is key to everything from designing brakes to building power plants. Students investigate the driving question: Why does every moving thing eventually stop — and where does the energy go? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

### NGSS Standard
**MS-PS3-5**: Construct, use, and present arguments to support the claim that when the kinetic energy of an object changes, energy is transferred to or from the object.

### Learning Objectives
- Explain the relationship between speed, mass, and kinetic energy
- Model how friction transfers kinetic energy into thermal energy
- Predict how surface type affects the rate of energy transfer
- Construct arguments about energy conservation using model evidence

LESSON PURPOSE:
Energy is one of the most fundamental concepts in all of science. The law of conservation of energy states that energy cannot be created or destroyed — only transferred or transformed. Understanding energy transfer is key to everything from designing brakes to building power plants.

NGSS ALIGNMENT:
MS-PS3-5: Construct, use, and present arguments to support the claim that when the kinetic energy of an object changes, energy is transferred to or from the object.

THREE-DIMENSIONAL LEARNING:
• Science Practice: Engaging in Argument from Evidence
  Students construct and present arguments using model evidence that energy is transferred when kinetic energy changes.
• Disciplinary Core Idea: PS3.B Conservation of Energy and Energy Transfer
  When the motion energy of an object changes, there is inevitably some other change in energy at the same time — energy is conserved.
• Crosscutting Concept: Energy and Matter
  Students track energy as it transfers from kinetic to thermal form, demonstrating that energy is conserved even as it changes form.

LEVER FRAMEWORK:

• Visualize: Students build a model showing how kinetic energy is converted to thermal energy through friction.
• Evaluate: Students run ice, concrete, and carpet scenarios to compare energy transfer rates on different surfaces.
• Revise: Students add air resistance or mass to explore more complex energy transfer dynamics.

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• Kinetic Energy:
  Kinetic energy is the energy of motion, calculated as KE = ½mv². This means kinetic energy depends on both mass and speed — but speed has a SQUARED effect. Doubling the mass doubles the energy, but doubling the speed QUADRUPLES it. This is why high-speed crashes are so much more destructive.

• Friction and Heat:
  Friction is a force that opposes motion between surfaces in contact. It converts kinetic energy into thermal energy (heat) through microscopic interactions between surface irregularities. This is why brakes get hot, why rubbing your hands warms them, and why meteors burn up entering the atmosphere.

• Conservation of Energy:
  The total energy in a closed system remains constant. When a sliding object stops, its kinetic energy hasn't disappeared — it has been transformed into thermal energy (heat in the surfaces and surrounding air). If you could measure all the heat produced, it would exactly equal the kinetic energy lost.

• Real-World Applications:
  Engineers use energy transfer principles everywhere: car brakes convert kinetic to thermal energy (disc brakes can reach 300°C), regenerative braking in electric cars converts kinetic back to electrical energy, and heat shields on spacecraft manage the enormous thermal energy from atmospheric friction.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Energy disappears when objects stop"
  Reality: Energy is NEVER created or destroyed — this is the law of conservation of energy. When a ball stops rolling, its kinetic energy has been converted to thermal energy (heat) in the surface and surrounding air.
  Strategy: Rub your hands vigorously. The motion stops, but you feel HEAT. That's the energy — it transformed.

• "Friction only slows things down"
  Reality: Friction also enables motion! Without friction, you couldn't walk (feet would slip), cars couldn't drive (tires wouldn't grip), and you couldn't hold anything. Friction is essential for controlled movement.
  Strategy: Try to walk on ice. Friction is what you MISS when it's gone.

• "Heavier objects always stop faster"
  Reality: Heavier objects have MORE kinetic energy at the same speed (KE = ½mv²), so they actually require MORE friction force to stop. This is why loaded trucks need longer stopping distances than empty ones.
  Strategy: Push a heavy book and a light book at the same speed. The heavy one slides farther because it has more KE to convert.

• Step 1: Let students explore the interface. Don't over-explain.
  Let them discover. Circulate and support, don't lecture.
• Step 2: Ask "When this goes up, what happens to that?" to
  guide positive/negative relationship decisions. Let students debate.
• Step 3: Give time for students to "break" the model — turn
  things on/off and observe. This is where real insight happens.
• Step 4: Don't give answers. Ask questions. Let curiosity drive
  the research. Celebrate when students' additions don't work as
  expected — that's authentic science.

ANSWER KEY:
Big Question: Why does every moving thing eventually stop — and where does the energy go?
Answer: Nothing ever slides forever because friction converts kinetic energy (motion) into thermal energy (heat). The energy doesn't disappear — it transforms. Rub your hands together and feel the heat — that's kinetic energy becoming thermal energy right in your palms! Energy is always conserved.

Simulation Answers:
• Ice vs. Concrete Scenario: On ice (low friction), Kinetic Energy decreases very slowly and the object slides a long distance. On concrete (high friction), Kinetic Energy drops rapidly. In both cases, Thermal Energy Output accounts for the lost kinetic energy — confirming energy conservation.
• Speed Comparison Scenario: When Initial Speed is doubled, Kinetic Energy quadruples (KE = ½mv²). The object takes much longer to stop because there's four times as much energy to convert to heat. This is why stopping distance increases dramatically at higher speeds — and why speed limits save lives.

Reflection Exemplars:
• Q: Where does all the energy go when a car brakes?
  A: When a car brakes, the brake pads press against the rotors, creating friction. The car's kinetic energy is converted into thermal energy — the brakes can reach 300°C or more. Some energy also goes into sound (squealing) and deforming the brake pad material. The total energy is conserved.
• Q: Why do space probes move forever?
  A: In space, there's essentially no friction — no air, no surface contact. Without friction, there's nothing to convert kinetic energy into another form. So objects in space keep their kinetic energy and continue moving at the same speed indefinitely. This is Newton's First Law in action.

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2 (Developing and Using Models) + DCI PS3.B (Conservation of energy and energy transfer) + CCC5 (Energy and Matter)

In an energy model, a student launches a toy car at the same initial speed across three different surfaces: ice, concrete, and carpet. The model tracks the car's kinetic energy over time. On ice, the car travels 20 meters before stopping. On concrete, it travels 5 meters. On carpet, it travels only 1.5 meters. The model also displays a thermal energy reading for each surface, which increases as the car slows down.

On which surface does the car's kinetic energy last the longest, and why?

A. All three surfaces produce the same result because the car started at the same speed on each one
B. Ice, because its low friction converts kinetic energy to thermal energy very slowly, allowing the car to retain motion energy longer
C. Carpet, because rough surfaces generate additional kinetic energy that keeps the car moving
D. Concrete, because harder surfaces are more durable and preserve the car's speed

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Ice has the lowest friction coefficient of the three surfaces, so it converts kinetic energy to thermal energy at the slowest rate. The car retains its kinetic energy longest on ice and slides the farthest (20 meters). On carpet, high friction rapidly converts kinetic energy to heat, stopping the car in just 1.5 meters. If you chose C, rough surfaces increase friction, which removes kinetic energy faster. Surfaces cannot generate new kinetic energy. If you chose D, surface hardness and durability are not what determines how long kinetic energy lasts. Friction is the key factor. If you chose A, the same initial speed means the same initial kinetic energy, but different friction levels cause the energy to be converted to heat at different rates, producing very different distances.

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 7 (Engaging in Argument from Evidence) + DCI PS3.B (Conservation of energy) + CCC5 (Energy and Matter — energy is conserved)

A student rolls a ball across the classroom floor and watches it slow down and eventually stop. She writes in her lab notebook: 'When the ball stopped, the energy was used up. The kinetic energy no longer exists because the ball is not moving anymore.' Her teacher asks the class to evaluate this claim using the law of conservation of energy, which states that energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed from one form to another.

Using the conservation of energy, what is scientifically incorrect about the student's claim?

A. Nothing is incorrect. The student accurately described how energy works when objects stop moving.
B. The energy returned to the person who originally pushed the ball, restoring their muscle energy.
C. The energy was not used up. It was stored invisibly inside the ball and will make it roll again later.
D. Energy is never destroyed. The ball's kinetic energy was transformed into thermal energy (heat) in the ball and floor surfaces through friction. The total energy in the system remains the same.

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: The law of conservation of energy is absolute: energy cannot be created or destroyed. When the ball stops, every joule of kinetic energy has been converted into thermal energy through friction. The ball, the floor, and the air are all slightly warmer. If you could measure all the thermal energy produced, it would exactly equal the kinetic energy the ball started with. If you chose A, the claim that energy is 'used up' violates the conservation of energy, one of the most fundamental laws in physics. If you chose C, the energy is not stored in the ball. It has been transferred to the surfaces as heat and dispersed. The ball will not spontaneously roll again. If you chose B, energy does not return to its source. Once kinetic energy is converted to thermal energy through friction, it disperses into the environment as heat.

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 5 (Using Mathematics and Computational Thinking) + DCI PS3.B (Relationship between speed and kinetic energy) + CCC3 (Scale, Proportion, and Quantity)

The energy model shows that kinetic energy follows the formula KE = 1/2 times mass times velocity squared. A student tests this by calculating KE for a 1-kilogram ball at different speeds. At 10 meters per second, KE equals 50 joules. At 20 meters per second (double the speed), KE equals 200 joules (four times the energy). At 30 meters per second (triple the speed), KE equals 450 joules (nine times the energy). The student then applies this to car safety and stopping distances.

What practical implication does the velocity-squared relationship have for vehicle safety?

A. Faster vehicles are lighter because kinetic energy replaces some of the vehicle's mass
B. Doubling speed simply doubles stopping distance in a perfectly proportional relationship
C. Stopping distance increases dramatically at higher speeds because the brakes must convert much more kinetic energy into heat, and that energy grows with the square of speed
D. Speed has no meaningful effect on stopping distance because brakes always apply the same force

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: Because KE increases with the square of velocity, doubling your speed quadruples the kinetic energy that brakes must convert to heat. This means stopping distance roughly quadruples when speed doubles. A car going 60 mph needs approximately four times the stopping distance of one going 30 mph. This is why speed limits exist and why speeding is so dangerous. If you chose A, kinetic energy and mass are independent properties. Adding speed does not reduce mass. If you chose D, brakes apply a roughly constant friction force, but with four times the energy at double speed, it takes four times the distance to stop. If you chose B, the relationship is quadratic, not linear. Doubling speed quadruples (not doubles) the kinetic energy and approximately quadruples the stopping distance.

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 6 (Constructing Explanations) + DCI PS3.B (Energy transfer in designed systems) + CCC5 (Energy and Matter — energy flows)

An automotive engineer is designing brakes for a new electric vehicle. Traditional friction brakes convert kinetic energy into thermal energy (heat) that is wasted. The engineer proposes regenerative brakes instead, which use electric generators connected to the wheels. When the driver brakes, the generators convert the car's kinetic energy back into electrical energy, which recharges the battery. The engineer's data shows that regenerative braking can recover up to 70 percent of the car's kinetic energy, while traditional brakes recover 0 percent.

Based on the energy model and conservation of energy, why is the regenerative braking system more energy-efficient?

A. Regenerative brakes convert kinetic energy into useful electrical energy stored in the battery, rather than wasting it all as thermal energy dissipated into the environment
B. Regenerative brakes create new energy that did not exist before, giving the car free electricity
C. Regenerative brakes destroy the kinetic energy completely, which is more efficient than converting it to heat
D. Regenerative brakes increase friction to maximum levels, which makes the car stop faster

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: Regenerative braking captures kinetic energy and transforms it into electrical energy that can be used again, instead of letting it all become waste heat. This is more efficient because the energy stays within the vehicle's system in a usable form. Conservation of energy means you cannot create new energy, but you can choose what form it takes. If you chose B, regenerative brakes do not create energy. They convert existing kinetic energy into electrical energy. Conservation of energy prohibits creating energy from nothing. If you chose C, energy cannot be destroyed. Both braking systems convert kinetic energy into another form. The difference is what form: useful electricity vs. wasted heat. If you chose D, regenerative braking is not about friction levels. It is about what happens to the kinetic energy: converting it to electricity (useful) rather than heat (wasted).

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 2 (Developing and Using Models) + DCI PS3.B (Energy in real-world scenarios) + CCC5 (Energy and Matter — tracking energy transformations)

A student uses the energy model to trace what happens to the energy when she rubs her hands together vigorously for 10 seconds. The model shows: chemical energy stored in food is converted to kinetic energy in her arm muscles, kinetic energy of moving hands is converted to thermal energy through friction between skin surfaces, and a small amount of kinetic energy is converted to sound energy (the rubbing sound). She measures a 2-degree temperature increase in her palms.

If the student could perfectly account for all energy in this system, what would she find?

A. The total energy in the system decreased because friction destroyed some of the energy
B. The thermal energy gained by her hands plus the sound energy produced would exactly equal the chemical energy her muscles consumed, confirming that total energy is conserved
C. The energy simply disappeared into the air and cannot be tracked or measured
D. More energy was created by friction than her muscles originally provided, giving her hands extra heat

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Conservation of energy requires that every joule of chemical energy consumed by her muscles can be accounted for as thermal energy (heat in her palms and the air) plus sound energy plus any remaining kinetic energy. No energy is lost; it just changes form. The total before equals the total after. If you chose A, conservation of energy means energy is never destroyed. Friction converts energy from one form to another but does not eliminate it. If you chose D, friction cannot create energy. It can only convert kinetic energy into thermal and sound energy. The output can never exceed the input. If you chose C, all energy can be tracked and measured. The thermal energy is measurable with a thermometer, and the sound energy is measurable with a microphone. Nothing disappears; it transforms.
