## Platform

### Title
**Blood Moon: When the Sky Turns Red — The Cosmic Dance of Earth, Moon, and Sun**

### Overview
The Earth-Moon-Sun system creates some of the most dramatic natural phenomena visible from Earth — lunar phases, eclipses, and tides. Understanding these patterns requires modeling how gravity, orbital mechanics, and light interact across vast distances. Students investigate the driving question: Why does the Moon change shape every night and sometimes turn blood red? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

### NGSS Standard
**MS-ESS1-1, MS-ESS1-2**: Develop and use a model of the Earth-Sun-Moon system to describe the cyclic patterns of lunar phases, eclipses of the Sun and Moon, and seasons. Develop and use a model to describe the role of gravity in the motions within galaxies and the solar system.

### Learning Objectives
- Model how the Moon's orbital position determines the phase we see from Earth
- Explain why lunar eclipses turn the Moon red instead of making it disappear
- Predict when eclipses occur based on the alignment of Earth, Moon, and Sun
- Describe how gravity keeps the Earth-Moon-Sun system in constant motion

LESSON PURPOSE:
The Earth-Moon-Sun system creates some of the most dramatic natural phenomena visible from Earth — lunar phases, eclipses, and tides. Understanding these patterns requires modeling how gravity, orbital mechanics, and light interact across vast distances.

NGSS ALIGNMENT:

LEVER FRAMEWORK:

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• Lunar Phases:
  The Moon completes one orbit around Earth every 29.5 days. As it orbits, the angle between Sun, Moon, and Earth changes, illuminating different portions of the Moon's surface. The Moon doesn't produce light — it reflects sunlight. New moon occurs when the Moon is between Earth and Sun, and full moon when Earth is between Sun and Moon.

• Lunar Eclipses:
  A lunar eclipse occurs when Earth passes directly between the Sun and Moon, casting its shadow on the Moon. The Moon turns red because Earth's atmosphere refracts red wavelengths of sunlight into the shadow. Eclipses don't happen monthly because the Moon's orbit is tilted 5.1 degrees relative to Earth's orbital plane.

• Gravity and Orbits:
  Gravity is the attractive force between all objects with mass. The Moon orbits Earth because gravity provides the centripetal force for orbital motion. The same force creates tides — the Moon's gravity pulls on Earth's oceans, creating bulges on opposite sides of the planet.

• Scale and Distance:
  The Moon is about 384,400 km from Earth. Light from the Sun takes about 8 minutes to reach Earth and an additional 1.3 seconds to reach the Moon. The Moon's diameter is about 1/4 of Earth's, making accurate physical models challenging to build.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "The Moon makes its own light"
  Reality: The Moon has no light source — it reflects sunlight. What we call moonlight is actually reflected sunlight. The Moon's brightness depends entirely on its position relative to the Sun.
  Strategy: Shine a flashlight on a ball in a dark room. Ask: Is the ball producing light or reflecting it?

• "Earth's shadow causes Moon phases"
  Reality: Moon phases are caused by the ANGLE at which we see the Moon's sunlit side, not by Earth's shadow. Earth's shadow only affects the Moon during the rare event of a lunar eclipse.
  Strategy: Use a ball and flashlight. Show that turning the ball creates phases without any shadow from a third object.

• "The Moon changes shape"
  Reality: The Moon doesn't physically change — it's always a sphere. What changes is how much of its sunlit side we can see from Earth as it orbits. We're seeing different ANGLES, not different shapes.
  Strategy: Hold a ball under a light and walk around it. Notice you see different amounts of the lit side.

FACILITATION TIPS:
• Step 1: Let students explore the interface. Don't over-explain.
  Let them discover. Circulate and support, don't lecture.
• Step 2: Ask "When this goes up, what happens to that?" to
  guide positive/negative relationship decisions. Let students debate.
• Step 3: Give time for students to "break" the model — turn
  things on/off and observe. This is where real insight happens.
• Step 4: Don't give answers. Ask questions. Let curiosity drive
  the research. Celebrate when students' additions don't work as
  expected — that's authentic science.

ANSWER KEY:
Big Question: Why does the Moon change shape every night and sometimes turn blood red?
Answer: The Moon appears to change shape because we see different amounts of its sunlit side as it orbits Earth. During a lunar eclipse, Earth blocks direct sunlight but our atmosphere bends red wavelengths into the shadow — painting the Moon blood red!

Simulation Answers:
• Full Moon Scenario: When Moon Position is on the opposite side of Earth from the Sun, Visible Moon Phase is at maximum. We see the Moon's entire sunlit face. Earth's Shadow is minimal because the Moon is not aligned with the shadow.
• Lunar Eclipse Scenario: When Moon Position aligns exactly behind Earth relative to the Sun, Earth's Shadow engulfs the Moon. Visible Moon Phase drops significantly, and the Moon turns red because Earth's atmosphere bends red light into the shadow zone.

Reflection Exemplars:
• Q: Why don't we get an eclipse every month?
  A: The Moon's orbit is tilted about 5 degrees relative to Earth's orbit around the Sun. Most months, the Moon passes above or below Earth's shadow. Eclipses only happen when the Moon crosses the plane of Earth's orbit at exactly the right time — about 2-4 times per year.
• Q: Why does the Moon turn red?
  A: Earth's atmosphere acts like a lens, bending sunlight around our planet. Blue and green wavelengths scatter away (same reason our sky is blue), but red wavelengths bend enough to reach the Moon inside the shadow. It's essentially projecting all of Earth's sunsets onto the Moon simultaneously.

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format. Each item is three-dimensional, assessing a Science and Engineering Practice (SEP), Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and Crosscutting Concept (CCC) simultaneously, aligned to MS-ESS1-1, MS-ESS1-2.

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2 (Developing and Using Models) + DCI ESS1.A (The Universe and Its Stars — Earth-Moon-Sun system patterns) + CCC1 (Patterns)

A seventh-grade class is studying the Earth-Moon-Sun system. Their teacher projects a diagram showing the Moon in four different positions as it orbits Earth, with sunlight coming from the right side. At Position 1, the Moon is between Earth and the Sun. At Position 2, the Moon is to the left of Earth. At Position 3, the Moon is on the far side of Earth from the Sun. At Position 4, the Moon is to the right of Earth. The teacher asks students to predict what the Moon looks like from Earth at each position.

At Position 1, the Moon is directly between Earth and the Sun. Which prediction best explains what an observer on Earth would see?

A. A full Moon, because the Moon is closest to the Sun and fully illuminated
B. A new Moon, because the sunlit side of the Moon faces away from Earth
C. A half Moon, because exactly half of the Moon is always lit by the Sun
D. A lunar eclipse, because the Moon is in Earth's shadow

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: When the Moon is between Earth and the Sun, the sunlit half faces away from Earth and the dark half faces toward us, making the Moon invisible. This is a new Moon. If you chose A, you confused the Moon's proximity to the Sun with how much of its lit surface faces Earth. Being closer to the Sun does not mean we see more light. If you chose D, lunar eclipses occur when the Moon passes through Earth's shadow, which happens at Position 3 (full Moon), not Position 1. If you chose C, a half Moon (quarter phase) occurs at Positions 2 or 4, not when the Moon is directly between Earth and the Sun.

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 2 (Developing and Using Models) + DCI ESS1.A (Patterns of motion in the Earth-Moon-Sun system) + CCC1 (Patterns — cyclic patterns of lunar phases)

A student builds a physical model using a lamp (Sun), a basketball (Earth), and a tennis ball (Moon). She moves the tennis ball in a circle around the basketball while observing how much of the tennis ball's lit surface she can see from behind the basketball. She notices a repeating cycle: no light visible, a sliver of light on the right, half lit on the right, fully lit, half lit on the left, a sliver on the left, then back to no light.

The student claims her model proves that Moon phases are caused by Earth's shadow falling on the Moon. Why is this claim incorrect?

A. Earth's shadow does cause phases, but only during certain months of the year
B. Earth's shadow causes phases only during the full Moon, not during other phases
C. Moon phases are caused by the changing angle between the Sun, Moon, and Earth as the Moon orbits, not by Earth's shadow
D. The model is too small to accurately represent shadows in space

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: Moon phases result from viewing different amounts of the Moon's sunlit half as the angle between Sun, Moon, and Earth changes during the Moon's orbit. Earth's shadow is only involved during the rare event of a lunar eclipse, not the monthly phase cycle. If you chose A, Earth's shadow has nothing to do with the regular monthly phase cycle in any season. If you chose D, size does not affect the fundamental geometry of the model; the angles and lighting still demonstrate the correct principle. If you chose B, Earth's shadow only falls on the Moon during a lunar eclipse, which is a separate and rare event, not part of the regular phase cycle.

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 2 (Developing and Using Models) + DCI ESS1.A (Role of gravity in orbital motions) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

During a lunar eclipse, the Moon enters Earth's shadow and turns a deep red-orange color instead of disappearing completely. A student examines data from three recent lunar eclipses and notices that the shade of red varies: one was bright copper, another was dark brown, and a third was nearly invisible. Scientists report that the color differences correlate with the amount of dust and aerosols in Earth's atmosphere at the time of each eclipse.

Which explanation best accounts for why the Moon turns red during a lunar eclipse rather than going completely dark?

A. Earth's atmosphere bends red wavelengths of sunlight into the shadow, while shorter wavelengths scatter away
B. The Sun emits only red light during eclipse events because its energy output decreases
C. Mars reflects red light onto the Moon whenever the three planets align during an eclipse
D. The Moon's surface minerals glow red when they cool rapidly in the shadow

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: Earth's atmosphere acts like a lens, refracting sunlight around the planet and into the shadow zone. Blue and green wavelengths scatter away (the same reason our sky is blue), but longer red wavelengths bend enough to reach the Moon. This is essentially all of Earth's sunsets projected onto the Moon at once. If you chose D, the Moon's minerals do not emit light; the Moon has no internal energy source that would cause glowing. If you chose C, Mars is far too distant and dim to illuminate the Moon, and planetary alignment is not required for a lunar eclipse. If you chose B, the Sun's energy output does not change during an eclipse; the eclipse is caused by Earth blocking sunlight, not by changes in the Sun.

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 6 (Constructing Explanations) + DCI ESS1.A (Orbital tilt and eclipse frequency) + CCC1 (Patterns — why eclipses are rare)

A student looks at a calendar and counts that there are approximately 12 full Moons per year. She reasons that since a lunar eclipse happens when the Moon is full and passes through Earth's shadow, there should be 12 lunar eclipses per year. However, NASA data shows that total lunar eclipses occur only about once every 2.5 years on average for any given location on Earth. The student's teacher tells her that the Moon's orbital plane is tilted about 5 degrees relative to Earth's orbital plane around the Sun.

Based on the teacher's information, why do total lunar eclipses occur so rarely despite monthly full Moons?

A. Earth's shadow is too small to cover the Moon during most full Moons
B. Lunar eclipses can only happen during winter months when Earth is closest to the Sun
C. The Sun moves out of alignment with Earth and the Moon after each eclipse, preventing another from occurring
D. The 5-degree tilt means the Moon usually passes above or below Earth's shadow at full Moon, and alignment only occurs when the Moon crosses Earth's orbital plane at the right time

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: Because the Moon's orbit is tilted about 5 degrees relative to Earth's orbit around the Sun, the Moon usually passes slightly above or below Earth's shadow during full Moon. A lunar eclipse only occurs when the full Moon happens at the same time the Moon crosses the plane of Earth's orbit (called a node). If you chose A, Earth's shadow is actually much larger than the Moon at the Moon's distance, so size is not the issue. If you chose C, the Sun does not move out of alignment; the issue is the Moon's tilted orbit causing it to miss the shadow. If you chose B, eclipses can happen in any month and any season; they are determined by orbital geometry, not by Earth's distance from the Sun.

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 2 (Developing and Using Models) + DCI ESS1.A (Gravity and orbital motions) + CCC4 (Systems and System Models)

Scientists use computer models to predict the exact date, time, and duration of future eclipses hundreds of years in advance. These models track the gravitational interactions between the Sun, Earth, and Moon, including the Moon's 5-degree orbital tilt, the slow wobble (precession) of the Moon's orbit over 18.6 years, and the slightly elliptical shape of both orbits. A student asks her teacher: 'If we removed gravity from the model, what would happen to the predictions?'

Based on how the model works, what would happen if gravity were removed from the Earth-Moon-Sun system?

A. Phases would continue but eclipses would stop because gravity only matters during eclipses
B. The Moon would continue orbiting Earth but at a slower speed, producing fewer eclipses
C. The Moon would fall directly into the Sun because there would be nothing to hold it near Earth
D. The Moon and Earth would travel in straight lines away from each other, and no phases, eclipses, or tides would occur

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: Gravity is the force that keeps the Moon in orbit around Earth and Earth in orbit around the Sun. Without gravity, both objects would travel in straight lines (Newton's First Law) and move apart. No orbits means no changing angles, so there would be no phases, no eclipses, and no tidal effects. The entire system of patterns depends on gravitational orbits. If you chose B, without gravity, the Moon would not orbit at all, not just orbit more slowly. If you chose A, gravity is responsible for all orbital motion, not just eclipses. Without gravity, there are no orbits and therefore no phases either. If you chose C, without gravity, no object would be attracted to any other. The Moon would not fall into the Sun because falling requires gravitational attraction.
