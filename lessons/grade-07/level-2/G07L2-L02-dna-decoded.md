## Platform

### Title
**DNA Decoded — The Blueprint of Life and What Makes You, You**

### Overview
Students model how parent gene variation and environmental conditions interact to produce diverse offspring with varying survival advantages. By building a cause-and-effect model of genetics, gene expression, and trait variation, students discover why siblings look different despite sharing parents, why genetic diversity matters for species survival, and the critical difference between sexual and asexual reproduction.

### Cover Image
[Dramatic 3D visualization of a DNA double helix unraveling with colorful base pairs glowing, transitioning into diverse human silhouettes, deep blue and purple tones, cinematic science illustration]

### Grade
7th

### NGSS Standard
**MS-LS3-1, MS-LS3-2**: Develop and use a model to describe why structural changes to genes (mutations) located on chromosomes may affect proteins and may result in harmful, beneficial, or neutral effects to the structure and function of the organism. Develop and use a model to describe why asexual reproduction results in offspring with identical genetic information and sexual reproduction results in offspring with genetic variation.

### Learning Objectives
- Model how parent gene variation produces diverse offspring through sexual reproduction
- Explain how environmental conditions influence which genes are expressed
- Predict how trait variation affects survival advantage in different environments
- Describe the difference between genetic variation from sexual reproduction and identical copies from asexual reproduction

### Component List (Starting Model: 5 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Parent Gene Variation | External | The diversity of alleles and genetic material contributed by both parents |
| Environmental Conditions | External | External factors like nutrition, climate, and lifestyle that affect gene expression |
| Gene Expression Level | Internal | Which genes are actively being turned on or off in the organism's cells |
| Trait Variation in Offspring | Internal | The visible and functional differences between siblings and within populations |
| Survival Advantage | Internal | Whether an organism's particular combination of traits helps it survive in its current environment |

```
Scenario one: High genetic diversity. Set Parent Gene Variation

offspring are basically clones. Now shift the environment.

can't handle the new conditions, NONE of them can. Survival
Advantage drops across the board. The entire population could
collapse.

This is why biodiversity matters. This is why sexual reproduction
exists despite being slower than cloning. Variation is a species'
insurance policy.
```

## Step 4: REVISE & EXTEND — Play, Research, Expand

### Text Editor

```
SET THE CONDITIONS — REFINE YOUR MODEL

Your model shows a relationship between Parent Gene Variation and Trait Variation,
but right now that relationship applies ALL the time. In the
real system, Parent Gene Variation only affects Trait Variation when Gene Expression Level is active.

This is called a CONDITIONAL RELATIONSHIP — the connection
between two components depends on a third component's state.

Task A: SET THE CONDITION
   • Click on the connection arrow from Parent Gene Variation to Trait Variation
   • In the toolbar, click "Conditions"
   • In the conditions panel, set: IF Gene Expression Level is ON
   • Click "Save Conditions"

Task B: VERIFY THE MODEL
   • Run the simulation with Gene Expression Level OFF and Parent Gene Variation ON
   • Trait Variation should NOT respond to Parent Gene Variation alone
   • Now turn Gene Expression Level ON — Parent Gene Variation's effect on Trait Variation activates
   • This matches how the real system behaves

Your model now captures the conditional logic of the real system.
Not all relationships are always active — some depend on conditions.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
```

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format, aligned to MS-LS3-1 and MS-LS3-2.

---

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.1 (Develop and use models) + DCI LS3.A (Inheritance of Traits) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A student is modeling how parents pass traits to offspring. She sorts five components into external inputs and internal responses: Parent Gene Variation, Environmental Conditions, Gene Expression Level, Trait Variation in Offspring, and Survival Advantage.

Which list correctly sorts these components?

A. External: Trait Variation, Survival Advantage — Internal: Parent Gene Variation, Environmental Conditions, Gene Expression Level
B. External: Parent Gene Variation, Gene Expression Level — Internal: Environmental Conditions, Trait Variation, Survival Advantage
C. External: Parent Gene Variation, Survival Advantage — Internal: Environmental Conditions, Gene Expression Level, Trait Variation
D. External: Parent Gene Variation, Environmental Conditions — Internal: Gene Expression Level, Trait Variation, Survival Advantage

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: Parent Gene Variation and Environmental Conditions are inputs that determine the starting conditions. Gene Expression Level, Trait Variation, and Survival Advantage are outputs that respond automatically. If you chose B, Gene Expression Level is not something we directly control; it responds to genetic and environmental inputs. If you chose A, Trait Variation and Survival Advantage are outcomes, not inputs. If you chose C, Survival Advantage is determined by the interaction of traits and environment, not set as an input.
---

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Develop and use models) + DCI LS3.B (Variation of Traits) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A farmer grows two fields of wheat. Field A uses seeds from many different parent plants (high genetic diversity). Field B uses seeds that are all genetically identical clones. A new fungal disease arrives that neither field has encountered before.

Based on a genetics model, which prediction is best supported?

A. Both fields will be equally affected because the disease is new to both.
B. Field A will likely have some surviving plants because genetic variation means some may have natural resistance, while Field B may lose all plants because they are equally vulnerable.
C. Field A will lose more plants because having different genes means more confusion in how plants respond.
D. Field B will survive better because identical plants can coordinate their immune response.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Genetic variation is a population's insurance policy. In Field A, different plants have different alleles, so some may happen to have genes that resist the fungus. In Field B, all plants are identical, so if one is vulnerable, they all are. If you chose A, genetic diversity creates different susceptibilities. If you chose D, plants don't coordinate immune responses, and identical genetics means identical vulnerability. If you chose C, genetic diversity creates OPTIONS, not confusion.
---

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Develop and use models) + DCI LS3.A (Inheritance of Traits) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

Identical twins are separated at birth and raised in different environments. Twin A grows up in a nutrition-rich environment with regular exercise. Twin B grows up with limited nutrition and high stress. As adults, they look noticeably different despite having identical DNA.

Which explanation best describes why identical DNA produced different outcomes?

A. Environmental conditions influenced which genes were expressed in each twin, causing different traits to develop despite the same genetic blueprint.
B. Twin A received better genes from the environment, while Twin B's genes were damaged.
C. Twin B's DNA mutated over time due to the stressful environment, creating different genes.
D. The twins' DNA was not actually identical because all humans have completely unique DNA.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: Epigenetics shows that environment can turn genes on or off without changing the DNA sequence itself. Nutrition, stress, exercise, and other factors influence gene expression. Same blueprint, different building conditions, different results. If you chose C, normal environmental stress doesn't cause DNA mutations. If you chose B, environments don't give or damage genes; they influence which existing genes are active. If you chose D, identical twins DO have identical DNA from the same fertilized egg.
---

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Develop and use models) + DCI LS3.B (Variation of Traits) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A student runs three scenarios in a genetics model. In Scenario 1 (high parent variation, stable environment), trait variation is high and survival advantage is high. In Scenario 2 (high parent variation, changing environment), trait variation stays high and survival advantage decreases slightly. In Scenario 3 (low parent variation, changing environment), trait variation is low and survival advantage drops dramatically.

Which conclusion is best supported by comparing all three scenarios?

A. High parent variation guarantees survival regardless of environmental conditions.
B. Environmental change always causes populations to go extinct.
C. Low parent variation only matters when the environment is stable.
D. Populations with more genetic variation are better equipped to survive environmental changes because they have a wider range of traits to draw from.

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: Comparing Scenarios 2 and 3 is key. Both face environmental change, but the high-variation population (Scenario 2) maintains better survival because some individuals already have traits suited to new conditions. The low-variation population (Scenario 3) collapses because all individuals are equally vulnerable. If you chose B, Scenario 2 shows survival is possible with sufficient variation. If you chose A, Scenario 2 shows that even high variation results in some survival decrease. If you chose C, low variation is most dangerous when the environment changes, not when it's stable.
---

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Develop and use models) + DCI LS3.B (Variation of Traits) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A conservation biologist discovers that a population of endangered pandas has very low genetic variation because the population has been small and isolated for many generations. Climate change is expected to alter the panda's habitat over the next 50 years.

Based on the genetics model, which action would most effectively improve the panda population's chances of surviving environmental change?

A. Wait for natural mutations to create the needed genetic variation over time.
B. Move all pandas to a zoo where the environment is controlled and never changes.
C. Introduce pandas from a different geographic population to increase genetic diversity through interbreeding.
D. Feed the existing pandas better food to help their genes adapt to the changing climate.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: The model shows that genetic variation is essential for surviving environmental change. Introducing individuals from another population brings new alleles, increasing the genetic diversity available for natural selection. If you chose B, a controlled zoo prevents evolution and doesn't solve the underlying genetic diversity problem. If you chose D, better nutrition doesn't change DNA or create new alleles. If you chose A, mutations occur randomly and rarely; with a small population and rapid environmental change, waiting is too risky.
