## Platform

### Title
**Ocean Currents and Climate — The Hidden Rivers That Control Our Weather**

### Overview
Students model how temperature differences between the equator and poles drive ocean circulation, distributing heat globally and stabilizing regional climates. By building a cause-and-effect model of temperature gradients, wind patterns, current speed, heat distribution, and climate stability, students discover why Europe is warmer than expected for its latitude and what could happen if ocean currents weaken.

### Cover Image
[Dramatic aerial view of ocean currents visualized as glowing blue and red streams flowing across a dark ocean, warm red currents moving north and cold blue currents moving south, cinematic satellite perspective]

### Grade
7th

### NGSS Standard
**MS-ESS2-6, MS-ESS3-5**: Develop and use a model to describe how unequal heating and rotation of the Earth cause patterns of atmospheric and oceanic circulation that determine regional climates. Ask questions to clarify evidence of the factors that have caused the rise in global temperatures over the past century.

### Learning Objectives
- Model how temperature differences between the equator and poles drive ocean circulation
- Explain how wind patterns and ocean currents work together to distribute heat globally
- Predict how disruptions to ocean currents could affect regional climate stability
- Describe why Europe is warmer than expected for its latitude due to ocean heat transport

### Component List (Starting Model: 5 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Temperature Difference (Equator vs Poles) | External | The heat gradient between tropical and polar regions that drives circulation |
| Wind Patterns | External | Surface winds that push ocean water and create surface currents |
| Ocean Current Speed | Internal | How fast water circulates through the global conveyor belt system |
| Heat Distribution | Internal | How evenly thermal energy is spread across the planet by ocean circulation |
| Regional Climate Stability | Internal | How predictable and moderate weather patterns are in coastal areas |

## Step 2: ESTABLISH — Connect the Relationships

### Text Editor

```
TIME TO MAP THE CONVEYOR BELT

Those five components form a cascade. Temperature differences
drive currents, currents distribute heat, and heat distribution
determines climate. Let's trace the chain.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Task A: OPEN CONNECTION MODE

Task B: DRAW YOUR RELATIONSHIPS
• Temperature Difference → Ocean Current Speed
• Wind Patterns → Ocean Current Speed
• Ocean Current Speed → Heat Distribution
• Heat Distribution → Regional Climate Stability
• Heat Distribution → Temperature Difference

Task C: SET POSITIVE OR NEGATIVE

  ○ Temperature Difference → Ocean Current Speed = POSITIVE (+)
    Bigger gradient = faster circulation

  ○ Wind Patterns → Ocean Current Speed = POSITIVE (+)
    Stronger winds = faster surface currents

  ○ Ocean Current Speed → Heat Distribution = POSITIVE (+)
    Faster currents = more even heat spread

  ○ Heat Distribution → Regional Climate Stability = POSITIVE (+)
    More even heat = more stable weather

  ○ Heat Distribution → Temperature Difference = NEGATIVE (−)
    As currents spread heat, they REDUCE the gradient that drives them.
    This is a self-regulating feedback loop!

Task D: CHECK YOUR MODEL
• 5 arrows total
• 4 positive, 1 negative
• Notice the feedback loop: heat distribution reduces the
  temperature difference that drives currents!

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

The negative feedback loop is the key insight. The ocean
regulates itself: currents spread heat, which reduces the
gradient, which slows currents. It's self-balancing.
```

### Video Script

```
"Your pieces are placed. Now let's trace the invisible rivers.

Temperature Difference to Ocean Current Speed. When the
temperature gap between the equator and poles gets bigger,
does water move faster or slower?

Faster! A bigger gradient creates a stronger density difference.
Hot water is less dense, cold water is more dense. That
density difference drives circulation. POSITIVE.

Wind Patterns to Ocean Current Speed. Stronger surface winds
push more water. Also POSITIVE.

Ocean Current Speed to Heat Distribution. Faster currents
carry more tropical heat to higher latitudes. The heat spreads
more evenly. POSITIVE.

Heat Distribution to Regional Climate Stability. When heat is
distributed more evenly, coastal regions get more moderate and
predictable weather. POSITIVE.

Now the fascinating one. Heat Distribution back to Temperature
Difference. When currents successfully spread heat from equator
to poles, what happens to the temperature gap?

It SHRINKS. The currents are reducing the very thing that drives
them. That's a NEGATIVE relationship. And it's a feedback loop.

This is why the ocean is self-regulating. Currents spread heat,
which reduces the gradient, which slows currents slightly, which
lets the gradient rebuild. Balance.

But what happens when we mess with that balance?

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

### Video Script

```
"The conveyor belt is wired. Let's turn it on.

Scenario one: Normal circulation. Moderate temperature difference,
normal winds. This is Earth right now.

Watch the graphs. Ocean Current Speed is steady. Heat Distribution
is even. Warm water from the tropics reaches higher latitudes.
Regional Climate Stability is high. London has mild winters.
California has cool summers. The system works.

Scenario two: crank up the temperature gradient. Maximum
difference between equator and poles.

Current speed increases. The conveyor belt runs faster. More
heat gets transported. Distribution evens out further.

Now scenario three: this is the one scientists worry about.
REDUCE the temperature gradient. This simulates what happens
when polar ice melts. All that fresh, cold water dilutes the
salty polar waters. The density difference weakens.

Watch Ocean Current Speed. It drops. The conveyor belt slows.

Heat Distribution becomes uneven. Tropical regions keep their
heat. But northern regions that relied on warm currents? They
lose their heat delivery.

Regional Climate Stability drops. And here's the paradox:
even though the GLOBE is warming, parts of Europe could get
COLDER because the Gulf Stream isn't delivering tropical heat
anymore.

That's not science fiction. Scientists have measured the
Atlantic conveyor belt slowing by about 15% since the
mid-20th century.

Your model just showed why that matters.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

```
Your model shows a relationship between Wind Patterns and Ocean Current Speed,
but right now that relationship applies ALL the time. In the
real system, Wind Patterns only affects Ocean Current Speed when Temperature Difference is active.

This is called a CONDITIONAL RELATIONSHIP — the connection
between two components depends on a third component's state.

Task A: SET THE CONDITION
   • Click on the connection arrow from Wind Patterns to Ocean Current Speed
   • In the toolbar, click "Conditions"
   • In the conditions panel, set: IF Temperature Difference is ON
   • Click "Save Conditions"

Task B: VERIFY THE MODEL
   • Run the simulation with Temperature Difference OFF and Wind Patterns ON
   • Ocean Current Speed should NOT respond to Wind Patterns alone
   • Now turn Temperature Difference ON — Wind Patterns's effect on Ocean Current Speed activates
   • This matches how the real system behaves
```

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score. Aligned to MS-ESS2-6 and MS-ESS3-5.

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.1 + DCI ESS2.C + CCC5 (Energy and Matter)

A student builds a model of ocean circulation with five components: Temperature Difference, Wind Patterns, Ocean Current Speed, Heat Distribution, and Regional Climate Stability.

Which correctly sorts external inputs from internal responses?

A. External: Temperature Difference, Ocean Current Speed — Internal: Wind Patterns, Heat Distribution, Climate Stability
B. External: Heat Distribution, Wind Patterns — Internal: Temperature Difference, Ocean Current Speed, Climate Stability
C. External: Temperature Difference, Wind Patterns — Internal: Ocean Current Speed, Heat Distribution, Climate Stability
D. External: Temperature Difference, Climate Stability — Internal: Wind Patterns, Ocean Current Speed, Heat Distribution

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: Temperature Difference and Wind Patterns are the driving forces that students control. The other three respond automatically to those inputs.
---

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 + DCI ESS2.C + CCC5

In an ocean circulation model, a student draws an arrow from Heat Distribution back to Temperature Difference and labels it negative. A classmate disagrees, saying distributing heat should increase the temperature difference.

Which explanation best supports the negative relationship?

A. Heat distribution creates new heat at the poles, increasing the temperature gap.
B. As currents carry tropical heat poleward, the poles warm up while the equator cools slightly, reducing the temperature difference between them.
C. The relationship should be positive because more heat always means higher temperatures everywhere.
D. Heat distribution causes polar ice to melt, which increases the temperature difference.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: This is a self-regulating feedback loop. Currents deliver equatorial heat to polar regions, warming the poles and slightly cooling the equator. This reduces the gradient that drives the currents, creating a self-balancing system.
---

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 + DCI ESS2.C + CCC5

London, England, and Goose Bay, Canada, are at nearly the same latitude. Yet London's average winter temperature is about 10 degrees Celsius warmer.

Which factor best explains this difference?

A. Canada is at a higher elevation than England, making it naturally colder.
B. England is surrounded by land that retains heat better than water.
C. London receives more direct sunlight because it is closer to the equator.
D. The Gulf Stream carries warm water from the tropics across the Atlantic, heating the air that blows across England.

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: The Gulf Stream is a powerful warm ocean current that delivers enormous amounts of tropical heat to northwestern Europe. This ocean heat transport is the primary reason London has mild winters while Goose Bay at the same latitude has harsh ones.
---

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 + DCI ESS3.D + CCC5

Scientists monitoring the Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC) report that it has slowed by approximately 15% since the mid-20th century. Global temperatures have been rising during this same period due to increased greenhouse gases.

Which prediction is best supported by an ocean circulation model?

A. The AMOC slowdown will have no effect on regional climates because wind patterns are more important than ocean currents.
B. If the AMOC continues to weaken, Europe could experience colder winters even as global average temperatures continue to rise, because the Gulf Stream delivers less tropical heat.
C. The AMOC slowdown will cause global temperatures to drop, reversing global warming.
D. The AMOC will speed up as global temperatures rise because warmer water moves faster.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: The model shows that ocean currents distribute heat. If the AMOC weakens, less tropical heat reaches Europe, causing regional cooling even while the globe warms overall. This is the paradox of ocean current disruption under climate change.
---

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 + DCI ESS2.C + CCC5

A climate scientist wants to understand how melting Arctic ice could affect ocean circulation. The model shows: Temperature Difference drives Ocean Current Speed (positive), Ocean Current Speed drives Heat Distribution (positive), and Heat Distribution drives Climate Stability (positive).

If melting ice reduces the temperature difference between equator and poles, what cascade of effects would the model predict?

A. Reduced temperature difference → slower ocean currents → less heat distribution → lower climate stability
B. Reduced temperature difference → faster ocean currents → uneven heat distribution → lower climate stability
C. Reduced temperature difference → faster ocean currents → more heat distribution → higher climate stability
D. Reduced temperature difference → no change in ocean currents → no change in climate stability

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: Following the model's positive relationships in reverse: reducing the input (temperature difference) reduces each downstream component. Slower currents mean less heat delivery, which means less stable regional climates. This cascade is exactly what scientists fear from accelerating polar ice melt.
