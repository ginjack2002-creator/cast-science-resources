## Platform

### Title
**Evolution in Action — Why Biodiversity Is Nature's Insurance Policy**

### Overview
Students model how environmental change creates selection pressure that favors certain traits over others, driving adaptation and evolution. By building a cause-and-effect model of environmental change rate, population size, genetic variation, selection pressure, and adaptation rate, students discover why large, diverse populations survive environmental shifts while small, uniform ones face extinction.

### Cover Image
[Dramatic split image showing identical organisms on one side gradually transitioning to diverse adapted forms on the other, showing speciation over time, natural history illustration style]

### Grade
7th

### NGSS Standard
**MS-LS4-1, MS-LS4-4**: Analyze and interpret data for patterns in the fossil record that document the existence, diversity, extinction, and change of life forms throughout the history of life on Earth. Construct an explanation based on evidence that describes how genetic variations of traits in a population increase some individuals' probability of surviving and reproducing in a specific environment.

### Learning Objectives
- Model how environmental change creates selection pressure that favors certain traits
- Explain why larger populations with more genetic variation adapt faster than small, uniform ones
- Predict extinction risk based on population size, genetic variation, and rate of environmental change
- Construct explanations for how natural selection drives adaptation over many generations

### Component List (Starting Model: 5 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Environmental Change Rate | External | How quickly the habitat is changing in temperature, food availability, or other conditions |
| Population Size | External | The total number of individuals in the population |
| Genetic Variation | Internal | The diversity of traits and alleles present within the population |
| Selection Pressure | Internal | How strongly the current environment favors certain traits over others |
| Adaptation Rate | Internal | How quickly favorable traits spread through the population via reproduction |

## Step 2: ESTABLISH — Connect the Relationships

### Text Editor

```
TIME TO WIRE NATURAL SELECTION

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Task A: OPEN CONNECTION MODE

Task B: DRAW YOUR RELATIONSHIPS
• Environmental Change Rate → Selection Pressure
• Population Size → Genetic Variation
• Genetic Variation → Adaptation Rate
• Selection Pressure → Adaptation Rate
• Adaptation Rate → Population Size

Task C: SET POSITIVE OR NEGATIVE

  ○ Environmental Change Rate → Selection Pressure = POSITIVE (+)
    Faster change = stronger pressure to adapt

  ○ Population Size → Genetic Variation = POSITIVE (+)
    More individuals = more trait diversity

  ○ Genetic Variation → Adaptation Rate = POSITIVE (+)
    More trait options = faster adaptation

  ○ Selection Pressure → Adaptation Rate = POSITIVE (+)
    Stronger pressure = faster spread of favorable traits

  ○ Adaptation Rate → Population Size = POSITIVE (+)
    Successful adaptation = more survivors = bigger population
    (positive feedback loop!)

Task D: CHECK YOUR MODEL
• 5 arrows, all POSITIVE
• Notice the feedback loop: adaptation increases population,
  which increases variation, which increases adaptation capacity

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

All positive relationships, but the system isn't always positive.
When environmental change is faster than adaptation, the spiral
goes the OTHER way: population shrinks, variation drops,
adaptation slows, extinction approaches.
```

### Video Script

```
"Let's wire the forces of evolution.

Environmental Change Rate to Selection Pressure. When the
environment changes fast, the gap between current traits and
needed traits widens. Pressure intensifies. POSITIVE.

Population Size to Genetic Variation. More individuals means
more random mutations and recombinations. More people means
more trait options. POSITIVE.

Genetic Variation to Adaptation Rate. If you have lots of
different traits in your population, some of them might already
match the new conditions. More options, faster adaptation.
POSITIVE.

Selection Pressure to Adaptation Rate. Stronger pressure means
organisms with the right traits survive much better than those
without. The advantage is bigger, so favorable traits spread
faster. POSITIVE.

Adaptation Rate to Population Size. When a population adapts
successfully, more individuals survive and reproduce. The
population grows. POSITIVE.

And there's your feedback loop. Bigger population means more
variation, which means better adaptation, which means bigger
population.

But here's the dark side. If environmental change outpaces
adaptation, the spiral reverses. Population shrinks. Variation
drops. Adaptation slows. Population shrinks more. That's the
extinction death spiral.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

## Step 4: REVISE & EXTEND — Play, Research, Expand

### Text Editor

```
SET THE CONDITIONS — REFINE YOUR MODEL

Your model shows a relationship between Genetic Variation and Adaptation Rate,
but right now that relationship applies ALL the time. In the
real system, Genetic Variation only affects Adaptation Rate when Selection Pressure is active.

This is called a CONDITIONAL RELATIONSHIP — the connection
between two components depends on a third component's state.

Task A: SET THE CONDITION
   • Click on the connection arrow from Genetic Variation to Adaptation Rate
   • In the toolbar, click "Conditions"
   • In the conditions panel, set: IF Selection Pressure is ON
   • Click "Save Conditions"

Task B: VERIFY THE MODEL
   • Run the simulation with Selection Pressure OFF and Genetic Variation ON
   • Adaptation Rate should NOT respond to Genetic Variation alone
   • Now turn Selection Pressure ON — Genetic Variation's effect on Adaptation Rate activates
   • This matches how the real system behaves

Task C: SET ADDITIONAL CONDITION
   • Click on the arrow from Selection Pressure to Adaptation Rate
   • Click "Conditions" and set: IF Genetic Variation is ON
   • Save — Selection Pressure now only affects Adaptation Rate when
     Genetic Variation is active

Your model now captures the conditional logic of the real system.
Not all relationships are always active — some depend on conditions.
```

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 6.1 + DCI LS4.B + CCC2

A student's evolution model shows that environmental change rate drives selection pressure (positive), and selection pressure drives adaptation rate (positive). But the model also shows that adaptation rate depends on genetic variation (positive).

If a population has strong selection pressure but almost no genetic variation, what will happen?

A. The population will adapt slowly or not at all because there are no alternative traits for selection to favor.
B. The population will develop new mutations to meet the challenge.
C. The population will migrate to a better environment instead of adapting.
D. The population will adapt quickly because strong pressure forces rapid change.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: Natural selection can only act on variation that already exists. Strong pressure without options is like having a test with no correct answers available. The population needs raw material (genetic variation) for selection to work on.
