## Platform

### Title
**The Invisible War — Your Body's Army Against Invaders**

### Overview
Students model how the immune system detects and responds to pathogen invasions. By building a cause-and-effect model of pathogen load, vaccine status, white blood cell response, antibody production, and infection severity, students discover how the body's interacting subsystems fight infection. This lesson connects directly to understanding vaccines, immune memory, and why some people get sicker than others.

### NGSS Standard
**MS-LS1-3, MS-LS1-8**: Use argument supported by evidence for how the body is a system of interacting subsystems composed of groups of cells. Gather and synthesize information that sensory receptors respond to stimuli by sending messages to the brain for immediate behavior or storage as memories.

### Learning Objectives
- Model how the immune system detects and responds to pathogen invasions
- Explain how vaccines train the immune system to produce antibodies before infection
- Predict infection severity based on pathogen load and immune system preparedness
- Describe how white blood cells and antibodies work together as interacting subsystems

### Component List (Starting Model: 5 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Pathogen Load | External | Amount of bacteria or virus entering the body at one time |
| Vaccine Status | External | Whether the immune system has been previously trained to recognize this pathogen |
| White Blood Cell Response | Internal | How quickly and strongly immune cells activate to fight the invasion |
| Antibody Production | Internal | The rate at which targeted pathogen-fighting proteins are created |
| Infection Severity | Internal | How sick the person gets based on pathogen spread vs. immune response |

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
Your body is constantly under attack from pathogens, bacteria,
viruses, and other microorganisms that can cause disease. The
immune system is a complex network of cells, tissues, and organs
that work together to defend the body, acting as interacting
subsystems within the larger body system.

First Line of Defense: The body's first defenses are physical
barriers: skin blocks most pathogens, mucus traps them in the
nose and throat, and stomach acid destroys many that are
swallowed. These are non-specific defenses.

White Blood Cell Response: When pathogens breach physical
barriers, white blood cells respond first. Neutrophils arrive
within hours, engulfing and destroying invaders. Macrophages
follow, cleaning up debris and presenting pathogen fragments
to trigger a targeted response.

Antibody Production: B-cells produce antibodies: Y-shaped
proteins that lock onto specific pathogen molecules called
antigens. The first encounter takes 7-10 days. This delay
is why new infections make you sick.

Immune Memory and Vaccines: After fighting an infection, the
immune system creates memory cells. If the same pathogen
returns, antibody production begins within hours. Vaccines
exploit this by introducing harmless versions of pathogens.

LEVER FRAMEWORK:
• L (Locate): Students identify pathogen load, vaccine status,
  white blood cell response, antibody production, and infection
  severity as key components.
• E (Establish): Students determine that pathogen load drives
  infection severity positively while vaccine status and immune
  responses reduce it through negative relationships.
• V (Visualize): Students build a model showing how immune
  subsystems respond with and without prior vaccination.
• Ev (Evaluate): Students run vaccinated, unvaccinated, and
  low-exposure scenarios to compare immune response timelines.
• R (Revise): Students add memory cell formation or fever
  response to explore adaptation over time.

SLIDE-BY-SLIDE FACILITATION:
Slide 1: "Right now, inside your body, there's a war happening.
And you're winning." Show dramatic immune cell imagery.

Slide 2: Have students read objectives. Pre-teach 'pathogen'
and 'antibody.'

Slide 3: Quick poll: Who has been sick in the last year? What
happened? How did you recover?

Slide 4: Preview each LEVER step. Emphasize SUBSYSTEMS working
together.

Slide 5: Guide sorting of external vs. internal components.
Discuss why vaccine status is an input.

Slide 6: Help students identify positive and negative
relationships. Trace the cascade.

Slide 7: Students predict first, then run simulations. Compare
the vaccinated and unvaccinated graphs side by side.

Slide 8: Lead discussion about immune memory. Connect to
real-world vaccination data.

Slide 9: Groups design public health campaigns using model
evidence.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Vaccines give you the disease" — Vaccines contain weakened,
  inactivated, or partial versions of pathogens that CANNOT
  cause the full disease. Side effects (sore arm, low fever)
  are the immune system RESPONDING, not the person getting sick.
  Analogy: A fire drill doesn't burn down the school.

• "Antibiotics kill viruses" — Antibiotics only work against
  bacteria. Viruses are not cells. That's why doctors don't
  prescribe antibiotics for the flu.

• "If you're healthy, you don't need vaccines" — Even healthy
  people can get severely ill. Vaccines also protect vulnerable
  people around you through herd immunity.

DISCUSSION PROMPTS:
• "Why does it take several days to recover from a new illness
   but your body fights it faster the second time?"
• "How does your model show that vaccines don't prevent exposure
   but do prevent severe illness?"
• "Why is herd immunity important for people who can't be
   vaccinated?"

ASSESSMENT:
• Can student explain the cause-effect chain verbally?
• Does student's model show correct positive/negative relationships?
• Did student add at least one researched component?

DIFFERENTIATION:
Support: Provide a simplified diagram of immune response timeline;
use colored beads to physically model pathogens (red), white blood
cells (white), and antibodies (yellow); sentence frames: "When
pathogen load increases, __ happens because __"

Challenge: Research how autoimmune diseases occur when the immune
system attacks the body's own cells; investigate how HIV targets
white blood cells; add fever response component

ELL: Provide visual vocabulary cards with immune system diagrams;
pair with English-proficient partner

CROSS-CURRICULAR:
• Math: Graph antibody production rates over time for vaccinated
  vs. unvaccinated individuals and calculate the difference
• ELA: Write a persuasive essay using model evidence to explain
  how vaccines work to a skeptical audience
• Social Studies: Research the history of vaccination from Edward
  Jenner's smallpox vaccine to modern mRNA technology

EXTENSION CONNECTIONS:
• Body systems unit — immune as interacting subsystems
• Cell biology — white blood cells as specialized cells
• Genetics — how antibody diversity is generated

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format. Each item is three-dimensional, assessing a Science and Engineering Practice (SEP), Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and Crosscutting Concept (CCC) simultaneously, aligned to MS-LS1-3 and MS-LS1-8.

---

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 7.1 (Engage in argument from evidence) + DCI LS1.A.3 (Body as interacting subsystems) + CCC4 (Systems and System Models)

A student is building a model of the immune system. She identifies five components: Pathogen Load, Vaccine Status, White Blood Cell Response, Antibody Production, and Infection Severity. She needs to sort them into external inputs (things she controls) and internal responses (things that change automatically).

Which list correctly sorts the components of the immune system model?

A. External: Infection Severity, Vaccine Status — Internal: Pathogen Load, White Blood Cell Response, Antibody Production
B. External: Pathogen Load, White Blood Cell Response — Internal: Vaccine Status, Antibody Production, Infection Severity
C. External: Antibody Production, Pathogen Load — Internal: White Blood Cell Response, Vaccine Status, Infection Severity
D. External: Pathogen Load, Vaccine Status — Internal: White Blood Cell Response, Antibody Production, Infection Severity

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: Pathogen Load (how much virus enters) and Vaccine Status (whether the person was vaccinated) are inputs that can be set before the simulation runs, making them external. White Blood Cell Response, Antibody Production, and Infection Severity all change automatically based on those inputs, making them internal. If you chose B, White Blood Cell Response is not something we control; it responds automatically to pathogen detection. If you chose A, Infection Severity is the output of the system, not an input. If you chose C, Antibody Production is an automatic immune response, not an input we set.
---

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 7.1 (Engage in argument from evidence) + DCI LS1.A.3 (Interacting subsystems) + CCC4 (Systems and System Models)

In an immune system model, a student draws an arrow from White Blood Cell Response to Infection Severity and labels it as a negative relationship. Another student says the relationship should be positive because white blood cells are responding TO the infection.

Which explanation best supports why the relationship between White Blood Cell Response and Infection Severity is negative?

A. When white blood cells increase, they cause inflammation, which makes the person feel sicker.
B. When white blood cells respond more strongly, they destroy more pathogens, reducing how severe the infection becomes.
C. White blood cells produce antibodies, which increase infection severity by causing immune reactions.
D. When infection severity increases, white blood cells increase, so they must have a positive relationship.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: A negative relationship means when one goes up, the other goes down. A stronger white blood cell response destroys more pathogens early, directly reducing infection severity. If you chose A, while inflammation can cause discomfort, the primary function of white blood cells is to REDUCE infection, not worsen it. If you chose D, this describes the reverse relationship (infection affecting WBCs), not the direction being asked about (WBCs affecting infection). If you chose C, white blood cells and antibody-producing B-cells are different types of immune cells with different functions.

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 7.1 (Engage in argument from evidence) + DCI LS1.A.3 (Interacting subsystems) + CCC4 (Systems and System Models)

A student runs two simulations of an immune system model. In both simulations, Pathogen Load is set to high. In Simulation A, Vaccine Status is off. In Simulation B, Vaccine Status is on. The student observes that Infection Severity is much lower in Simulation B.

Based on the model, which explanation best describes why the vaccinated person (Simulation B) had lower infection severity?

A. The vaccine increased the person's pathogen load tolerance, allowing them to handle more pathogens without getting sick.
B. The vaccine destroyed the pathogens before they entered the body, so there was nothing to fight.
C. The vaccinated person had fewer white blood cells, so the immune response was weaker and caused less inflammation.
D. The vaccine pre-trained memory cells, so antibody production began immediately instead of taking days, neutralizing pathogens before they could multiply.

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: Vaccines work by training the immune system in advance. Memory cells store the blueprint for specific antibodies. When the real pathogen arrives, antibody production begins within hours instead of the 7-10 days needed for a first encounter. This speed difference is what prevents severe illness. If you chose B, vaccines don't destroy pathogens outside the body; they prepare the immune system to respond faster inside the body. If you chose C, vaccinated people don't have fewer white blood cells; they have pre-existing memory cells that accelerate the targeted response. If you chose A, vaccines don't change tolerance; they change the SPEED of immune response.

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 7.1 (Engage in argument from evidence) + DCI LS1.A.3 (Interacting subsystems) + CCC4 (Systems and System Models)

A doctor tracks two patients exposed to the same virus on the same day. Patient X was vaccinated six months ago. Patient Y was never vaccinated. On Day 1, both patients have the same pathogen load. By Day 3, Patient X shows no symptoms while Patient Y has a high fever and body aches. By Day 7, Patient Y's antibody levels finally rise and symptoms begin to improve.

Which observation best supports the claim that the immune system's subsystems interact differently in vaccinated vs. unvaccinated individuals?

A. Both patients had the same pathogen load, proving that the virus affects everyone equally regardless of vaccination.
B. Patient Y's fever on Day 3 shows that the immune system was not working at all until Day 7.
C. Patient X's immediate antibody response (from pre-existing memory cells) neutralized the virus before symptoms developed, while Patient Y's delayed antibody response allowed the virus to multiply and cause illness.
D. Patient X had no symptoms because the vaccine prevented the virus from entering the body.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: The key difference is timing. Patient X's immune system already had memory cells from the vaccine, so antibody production started immediately. Patient Y's immune system had to identify the pathogen, design antibodies, and begin mass production, a process that took 7 days. During those 7 days, the virus multiplied and caused symptoms. If you chose D, the vaccine doesn't prevent viral entry; it prepares a faster immune response. If you chose B, Patient Y's fever IS the immune system working (inflammation is a defense mechanism), not evidence it wasn't working. If you chose A, the same pathogen load produced very different outcomes, proving that internal immune response matters.

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 7.1 (Engage in argument from evidence) + DCI LS1.A.3 (Interacting subsystems) + CCC4 (Systems and System Models)

A public health department wants to protect a community from a new virus. They know from immune system models that: higher pathogen load increases infection severity (positive relationship), vaccination increases antibody production speed (positive relationship), and faster antibody production decreases infection severity (negative relationship).

Based on the model, which strategy would most effectively reduce infection severity across the community?

A. Vaccinate as many people as possible so their immune systems can produce antibodies quickly upon exposure.
B. Tell people to avoid going outside so they are never exposed to any pathogens.
C. Develop stronger antibiotics to kill the virus once people are already sick.
D. Focus only on treating the sickest patients and let the virus spread naturally.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: The model shows that vaccination dramatically increases the speed of antibody production, which directly reduces infection severity through a negative relationship. Vaccinating the community gives each person's immune system a head start against the virus. If you chose B, complete avoidance is impractical and doesn't prepare the immune system for eventual exposure. If you chose C, antibiotics don't work against viruses; they only target bacteria. If you chose D, letting the virus spread uncontrolled means unvaccinated people face severe infections, overwhelming healthcare systems.
---
