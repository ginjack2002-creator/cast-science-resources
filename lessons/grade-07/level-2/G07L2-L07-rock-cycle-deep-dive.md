## Platform

### Title
**Rock Cycle Deep Dive — How Earth Recycles Itself Over Millions of Years**

### Overview
Students model how heat, pressure, and weathering drive the continuous transformation of rock types through the rock cycle. By building a cause-and-effect model of heat and pressure, weathering rate, igneous rock formation, sedimentary layers, and metamorphic transformation, students discover that the rock cycle has no beginning or end, that Earth constantly creates and destroys rocks, and that sedimentary rock layers provide a timeline of our planet's 4.6-billion-year history.

### Grade
7th

### NGSS Standard
**MS-ESS2-1, MS-ESS1-4**: Develop a model to describe the cycling of Earth's materials and the flow of energy that drives this process. Construct a scientific explanation based on evidence from rock strata for how the geologic time scale is used to organize Earth's 4.6-billion-year-old history.

### Learning Objectives
- Model how heat, pressure, and weathering drive the continuous transformation of rock types
- Explain how the three rock types (igneous, sedimentary, metamorphic) are connected through the rock cycle
- Predict which rock type dominates under different geological conditions
- Describe how rock strata and the rock cycle help scientists organize Earth's geologic history

### Component List (Starting Model: 5 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Heat & Pressure (Depth) | External | The intensity of thermal and compressive conditions deep underground |
| Weathering Rate | External | How rapidly rocks are broken down by physical, chemical, and biological processes at the surface |
| Igneous Rock Formation | Internal | The rate at which magma cools and solidifies into new igneous rock |
| Sedimentary Layers | Internal | The accumulation of eroded rock fragments that compact into sedimentary rock over time |
| Metamorphic Transformation | Internal | The rate at which existing rocks are changed by heat and pressure into metamorphic rock |

### Text Editor

```

The rock cycle is unique: it has NO starting point. Any rock

• Heat & Pressure → Metamorphic Transformation
• Heat & Pressure → Igneous Rock Formation
• Weathering Rate → Sedimentary Layers
• Sedimentary Layers → Metamorphic Transformation
• Weathering Rate → Igneous Rock Formation

Task C: SET POSITIVE OR NEGATIVE

  ○ Heat & Pressure → Metamorphic Transformation = POSITIVE (+)
    More heat/pressure = more metamorphic rock formed

  ○ Heat & Pressure → Igneous Rock Formation = POSITIVE (+)
    Extreme heat melts rock to magma, which cools into igneous

  ○ Weathering Rate → Sedimentary Layers = POSITIVE (+)
    More weathering = more sediment = more sedimentary rock

  ○ Sedimentary Layers → Metamorphic Transformation = POSITIVE (+)
    As layers pile up, weight creates pressure that transforms
    deeper layers into metamorphic rock

  ○ Weathering Rate → Igneous Rock Formation = NEGATIVE (−)
    High weathering erodes exposed igneous rock at the surface,
    breaking it down into sediment

Task D: CHECK YOUR MODEL
• 5 arrows total
• 4 positive, 1 negative

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

The cycle connects everything. Underground processes build
igneous and metamorphic rock. Surface processes build
sedimentary rock. And the cycle never stops as long as Earth
has energy.
```

### Video Script

```
"Let's wire Earth's recycling system.

Heat and Pressure to Metamorphic Transformation. When
conditions get hot and compressed underground, rocks change.
Their minerals rearrange. Limestone becomes marble. Shale
becomes slate. More heat and pressure means more metamorphism.
POSITIVE.

Heat and Pressure to Igneous Rock Formation. If it gets hot
enough, rock doesn't just change. It MELTS. That magma
eventually cools and solidifies into brand new igneous rock.
Granite underground. Basalt at the surface. POSITIVE.

Weathering Rate to Sedimentary Layers. At the surface, wind
and water and ice break rocks apart. Those fragments get
transported, deposited, buried, and compressed into
sedimentary rock. Sandstone. Limestone. Shale. More weathering
means more sediment means more sedimentary rock. POSITIVE.

Here's a fascinating one. Sedimentary Layers to Metamorphic
Transformation. As sedimentary layers pile up, the weight of
upper layers creates intense pressure on the deeper layers.
If buried deep enough, that pressure plus heat transforms them
into metamorphic rock. The surface process feeds the underground
process. POSITIVE.

And the negative: Weathering Rate to Igneous Rock Formation.
Weathering DESTROYS exposed igneous rock at the surface,
breaking it into sediment. NEGATIVE.

Look at your model. Underground builds igneous and metamorphic.
Surface builds sedimentary. And they feed into each other.
That's why it's a CYCLE, not a chain. No starting point. No
ending point. Just continuous transformation.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

```
This is called a CONDITIONAL RELATIONSHIP — the connection
between two components depends on a third component's state.

Task A: SET THE CONDITION

   • This matches how the real system behaves
```

### Video Script

```
"Your rock cycle model works. Underground forces build igneous
and metamorphic rock. Surface forces build sedimentary rock.
The cycle maintains all three through continuous transformation.

But there's more to explore.

Tectonic Plate Movement. Plates push rock into the mantle
through subduction, where it gets metamorphosed or melted.
Volcanic activity and mountain building bring deep rock back
to the surface where weathering begins again. Plate tectonics
is the engine that connects the deep and surface parts of the
rock cycle.

Fossil Preservation. When organisms die and are buried rapidly
in sediment, they can become fossilized. These fossils are
found ONLY in sedimentary rock, which is why sedimentary
layers are Earth's history book. The Law of Superposition tells
us older layers are on the bottom, younger on top.

Here's a mind-bending question: What would happen if Earth's
internal heat died out completely? Mars gives us the answer.
Its core cooled billions of years ago. No internal heat means
no melting, no metamorphism, no plate tectonics. Only
weathering remains. Mars's rock cycle essentially stopped.

Research one component. Add it. See how the full picture of
Earth's recycling emerges.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

```
heat (radioactive decay, residual formation heat) drives melting,
metamorphism, and plate tectonics. Solar energy drives weathering

metamorphic) continuously transform. Sedimentary strata follow
the Law of Superposition. The geologic time scale organizes 4.6
billion years of history.
```

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.1 + DCI ESS2.A + CCC5 (Energy and Matter)

A student builds a rock cycle model with five components: Heat & Pressure, Weathering Rate, Igneous Rock Formation, Sedimentary Layers, and Metamorphic Transformation.

Which correctly sorts external driving forces from internal rock-forming processes?

A. External: Sedimentary Layers, Weathering Rate — Internal: Heat & Pressure, Igneous Rock Formation, Metamorphic Transformation
B. External: Heat & Pressure, Igneous Rock Formation — Internal: Weathering Rate, Sedimentary Layers, Metamorphic Transformation
C. External: Heat & Pressure, Weathering Rate — Internal: Igneous Rock Formation, Sedimentary Layers, Metamorphic Transformation
D. External: Heat & Pressure, Metamorphic Transformation — Internal: Weathering Rate, Igneous Rock Formation, Sedimentary Layers

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: Heat & Pressure and Weathering Rate are the driving forces that power the rock cycle. The three rock-forming processes (igneous, sedimentary, metamorphic) are the internal responses to those forces.
---

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 + DCI ESS2.A + CCC5

A geologist finds a rock that was once sandstone (sedimentary) but now has a crystalline structure with folded layers.

Which process most likely caused this transformation, and what type of rock is it now?

A. Heat and pressure transformed it into a metamorphic rock without melting it.
B. Weathering turned it into a different sedimentary rock.
C. Erosion reshaped it but it remained sedimentary.
D. It melted and cooled into an igneous rock.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: Crystalline structure with folded layers indicates metamorphic transformation. The sandstone was buried deep enough for heat and pressure to change its mineral structure without completely melting it. If it had melted, it would have become magma and then cooled into igneous rock.
---

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 + DCI ESS2.A + CCC5

In a rock cycle model, the student notices that increasing Weathering Rate has a NEGATIVE relationship with Igneous Rock Formation. A classmate says the relationship should be positive because weathering creates more material.

Which explanation best supports why this relationship is negative?

A. Igneous rocks are too hard to be weathered, so the relationship doesn't exist.
B. Weathering at the surface breaks down and erodes exposed igneous rock, converting it to sediment and reducing the amount of intact igneous rock.
C. Weathering prevents magma from reaching the surface.
D. Weathering destroys all rock types equally, including igneous, so there is less igneous rock.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Weathering is a destructive surface process. When igneous rock (like granite or basalt) is exposed at the surface, weathering breaks it down into sediment fragments. This reduces the amount of intact igneous rock. The sediment eventually becomes sedimentary rock, continuing the cycle.
---

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 6.1 (Construct explanations) + DCI ESS1.C + CCC5

Scientists find fossils of marine organisms in sedimentary rock layers on top of a mountain, 3,000 meters above sea level. The rock layers follow the Law of Superposition (older layers on bottom, younger on top).

Which explanation best accounts for marine fossils at high elevation?

A. The organisms are actually land animals that happen to look like marine creatures.
B. The fossils were carried to the mountain top by ancient floods.
C. The sedimentary rock formed on an ancient ocean floor when marine organisms were buried in sediment. Over millions of years, tectonic forces uplifted the seafloor rock to mountain height, and the fossils were preserved within it.
D. The organisms climbed the mountain and died at the top.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: The rock cycle and plate tectonics explain this. Marine organisms died and were buried in ocean floor sediment, becoming fossils in sedimentary rock. Tectonic forces (plate collisions, mountain building) then uplifted this seafloor rock to mountain elevations over millions of years. The Himalayas, for example, contain fossils of marine organisms from the ancient Tethys Sea.
---

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 + DCI ESS2.A + CCC5

A student wants to explain how a single piece of granite (igneous rock) could eventually become sandstone (sedimentary rock) and then marble (metamorphic rock).

Which sequence of rock cycle processes correctly describes this transformation?

A. Granite melts into magma, which cools into sandstone, which is compressed into marble.
B. Granite is heated until it becomes marble, which is then weathered into sandstone.
C. Granite absorbs water and swells into sandstone, which then crystallizes into marble.
D. Granite is weathered into sand-sized fragments, which are deposited and compressed into sandstone. The sandstone is buried deep and subjected to heat and pressure, transforming it into marble.

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: This traces the correct rock cycle path. Granite (igneous) at the surface is broken down by weathering into sediment. Those sand-sized fragments are transported, deposited, and compressed over time into sandstone (sedimentary). If that sandstone is buried deep enough, heat and pressure transform it into marble (metamorphic). Each step requires specific conditions and processes.
