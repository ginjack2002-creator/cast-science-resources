### Title
**Why Are Cheetahs Losing Their Speed? — Genetic Diversity, Habitat Loss, and the Future of the Fastest Animal**

### Overview
Cheetahs are losing their speed because they've lost their genetic diversity. About 10,000 years ago, cheetah populations crashed to just a few individuals (a genetic bottleneck). The survivors were so closely related that today's cheetahs are almost genetic clones of each other. With so little gene... Students build a cause-and-effect model exploring how population size and habitat fragmentation interact with genetic diversity, trait variation, and species fitness in this system.

### NGSS Standard
**MS-LS4-6, MS-LS4-4**: Use mathematical representations to support explanations of how natural selection may lead to increases and decreases of specific traits in populations over time. Construct an explanation based on evidence that describes how genetic variations of traits in a population increase some individuals' probability of surviving and reproducing in a specific environment.

### Learning Objectives
- Model how habitat fragmentation reduces population size and genetic diversity
- Explain how low genetic diversity reduces trait variation and species fitness
- Predict how small, isolated populations lose adaptive traits over time
- Describe how natural selection depends on genetic variation within a population

|-----------|------|-------------------|

| Trait Variation | Internal | The range of differences in physical traits like speed, size, and disease resistance among cheetahs |
| Species Fitness | Internal | The overall ability of the cheetah population to survive, reproduce, and adapt to challenges |

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format. Each item is three-dimensional, assessing a Science and Engineering Practice (SEP), Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and Crosscutting Concept (CCC) simultaneously, aligned to MS-LS4-6, MS-LS4-4.

---

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.1 (Determine components of a system) + DCI + CCC4 (Describe a system in terms of its components)

A scientist is studying why are cheetahs losing their speed?. She identifies these components: Population Size, Habitat Fragmentation, Genetic Diversity, Trait Variation, Species Fitness. She sorts them into external (things we set or cannot control) and internal (things that change based on other factors).

Which list correctly sorts the components?

A. External: Species Fitness, Population Size — Internal: Habitat Fragmentation, Genetic Diversity, Trait Variation
B. External: Population Size, Genetic Diversity — Internal: Habitat Fragmentation, Trait Variation, Species Fitness
C. External: Genetic Diversity, Trait Variation — Internal: Population Size, Habitat Fragmentation, Species Fitness
D. External: Population Size, Habitat Fragmentation — Internal: Genetic Diversity, Trait Variation, Species Fitness

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: Population Size and Habitat Fragmentation are external because they represent conditions that drive the system. Genetic Diversity, Trait Variation, and Species Fitness are internal because they change based on other factors in the system.
---

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships among components) + DCI + CCC4 (Describe components and interactions)

A wildlife biologist discovers that a population of 50 isolated cheetahs has much less genetic diversity than a connected population of 500 cheetahs. Which factor best explains this difference?

A. Genetic Diversity controls Population Size, not the other way around
B. As Population Size increases, Genetic Diversity increases
C. Population Size has no effect on Genetic Diversity
D. As Population Size decreases, Genetic Diversity increases

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Larger populations maintain more genetic diversity because there are more individuals carrying different alleles. Small populations lose rare alleles
