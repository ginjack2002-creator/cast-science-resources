## Platform

### Title
**Electromagnetic Spectrum — The Invisible Waves That Run Your World**

### Overview
Students model the relationship between wave frequency, energy, and penetration depth across the electromagnetic spectrum. By building a cause-and-effect model of wave frequency, amplitude, energy, penetration depth, and detection sensitivity, students discover why different types of electromagnetic waves are used for different purposes and why visible light is just a tiny fraction of all the waves surrounding us.

### Cover Image
[Dramatic visualization of the electromagnetic spectrum showing colorful waves transitioning from long red radio waves through visible light to short purple gamma rays, with real-world applications at each range, dark background with glowing waves]

### Grade
7th

### NGSS Standard
**MS-PS4-2, MS-PS4-3**: Develop and use a model to describe that waves are reflected, absorbed, or transmitted through various materials. Integrate qualitative scientific and technical information to support the claim that digitized signals are a more reliable way to encode and transmit information than analog signals.

### Learning Objectives
- Model the relationship between wave frequency, energy, and penetration depth across the electromagnetic spectrum
- Explain why different types of electromagnetic waves are used for different purposes based on their properties
- Predict how waves interact with different materials based on frequency and energy
- Describe how digital signals use electromagnetic waves to transmit information reliably

### Component List (Starting Model: 5 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Wave Frequency | External | How fast the electromagnetic wave oscillates, determining its position on the spectrum |
| Wave Amplitude | External | How strong or tall the wave is, related to the intensity of the signal |
| Wave Energy | Internal | The amount of energy carried by the wave, determined by frequency and amplitude |
| Penetration Depth | Internal | How far the wave passes through different types of matter |
| Detection Sensitivity | Internal | Whether human senses or specific instruments can detect the wave |

Task B: DRAW YOUR RELATIONSHIPS
• Wave Frequency → Wave Energy
• Wave Amplitude → Wave Energy
• Wave Energy → Penetration Depth
• Wave Frequency → Detection Sensitivity
• Wave Amplitude → Detection Sensitivity

Task C: SET POSITIVE OR NEGATIVE

  ○ Wave Frequency → Wave Energy = POSITIVE (+)
    Higher frequency = more energy per photon (gamma rays are
    dangerous, radio waves are harmless)

  ○ Wave Amplitude → Wave Energy = POSITIVE (+)
    Bigger amplitude = more intense wave = more total energy

  ○ Wave Energy → Penetration Depth = POSITIVE (+)
    Higher energy waves penetrate deeper into materials

  ○ Wave Frequency → Detection Sensitivity = POSITIVE/NEGATIVE (+/−)
    Human eyes detect only the visible range. Below and above
    that range, waves are invisible to us.

  ○ Wave Amplitude → Detection Sensitivity = POSITIVE (+)
    Stronger signals are easier to detect

Task D: CHECK YOUR MODEL
• 5 arrows total
• This maps how wave properties determine behavior!

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

The key pattern: frequency determines energy, energy determines
what the wave can pass through. That's why X-rays see through
flesh but radio waves pass through walls.
```

### Video Script

```
"Let's wire the spectrum.

Wave Frequency to Wave Energy. When frequency goes up, does
energy go up or down? UP. Higher frequency waves carry more
energy per photon. That's why gamma rays can damage cells but
radio waves are completely harmless. POSITIVE.

Wave Amplitude to Wave Energy. Bigger amplitude means a more
intense signal. A bright light has more energy than a dim one
at the same frequency. POSITIVE.

Wave Energy to Penetration Depth. Higher energy waves can
push deeper into materials. X-rays have enough energy to pass
through your skin and muscle, but not your dense bones. Radio
waves pass through walls because walls can't absorb their
particular energy. POSITIVE.

Wave Frequency to Detection Sensitivity. This one is complex.
Your eyes detect only a narrow frequency range: visible light.
Below that (infrared, radio) you're blind. Above that
(ultraviolet, X-rays) you're also blind. Human detection peaks
in the visible range and drops to zero everywhere else.

Wave Amplitude to Detection Sensitivity. A louder signal is
easier to pick up. A brighter light is easier to see. POSITIVE.

The pattern is clear: frequency drives energy, energy drives
penetration. Different frequencies interact with matter
differently. And THAT is why we use different parts of the
spectrum for different technologies.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"

Scenario one: radio waves. Frequency very low, moderate

Wave Energy is low. These waves don't carry much punch. But
Penetration Depth is interesting: radio waves pass through
walls, through your body, through most everyday materials.

Detection Sensitivity? Zero for human eyes. You can't see
radio waves. That's why your WiFi works but you can't see
the signal. You need a special antenna.

the tiny sliver of the spectrum your eyes evolved to detect.

atmosphere.

Energy jump.

but get absorbed by bone and metal (high density). That
differential penetration creates the X-ray image your doctor
reads.

Detection? Again zero for human eyes. But X-rays expose
photographic film and electronic sensors, revealing what's
invisible to us.

The model reveals the pattern: frequency determines energy,
energy determines interaction with matter. Each part of the
spectrum has its purpose.

SET THE CONDITIONS — REFINE YOUR MODEL

Your model shows a relationship between Wave Frequency and Wave Energy,
but right now that relationship applies ALL the time. In the
real system, Wave Frequency only affects Wave Energy when Wave Amplitude is active.

This is called a CONDITIONAL RELATIONSHIP — the connection
between two components depends on a third component's state.

Task A: SET THE CONDITION
   • Click on the connection arrow from Wave Frequency to Wave Energy
   • In the toolbar, click "Conditions"
   • In the conditions panel, set: IF Wave Amplitude is ON
   • Click "Save Conditions"

Task B: VERIFY THE MODEL
   • Run the simulation with Wave Amplitude OFF and Wave Frequency ON
   • Wave Energy should NOT respond to Wave Frequency alone
   • Now turn Wave Amplitude ON — Wave Frequency's effect on Wave Energy activates
   • This matches how the real system behaves

Task C: SET ADDITIONAL CONDITION
   • Click on the arrow from Wave Amplitude to Wave Energy
   • Click "Conditions" and set: IF Wave Frequency is ON
   • Save — Wave Amplitude now only affects Wave Energy when
     Wave Frequency is active

Your model now captures the conditional logic of the real system.

They differ in frequency/wavelength. Higher frequency = shorter
wavelength = more energy. Visible light is the atmospheric
transmission window matching solar peak output. Digital signals
encode as discrete pulses (1/0), more noise-resistant than
continuous analog signals.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Light and radio are completely different" — Same type of wave,
  different frequencies. All travel at light speed.
• "Higher frequency always dangerous" — Depends on energy AND
  intensity. Low-intensity X-ray is safer than high-intensity radio.
• "Walls block all waves" — Walls block visible light but radio
  passes through (WiFi works!). Metal blocks radio (elevator signal).

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.1 + DCI PS4.B + CCC6 (Structure and Function)

Which correctly sorts wave model components into external inputs and internal responses?

A. External: Wave Frequency, Wave Energy — Internal: Wave Amplitude, Penetration Depth, Detection Sensitivity
B. External: Wave Frequency, Detection Sensitivity — Internal: Wave Amplitude, Wave Energy, Penetration Depth
C. External: Wave Frequency, Wave Amplitude — Internal: Wave Energy, Penetration Depth, Detection Sensitivity
D. External: Penetration Depth, Wave Frequency — Internal: Wave Amplitude, Wave Energy, Detection Sensitivity

Correct Answer: C

---

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 + DCI PS4.B + CCC6

A hospital uses X-rays to image a patient's broken arm. The X-ray image shows bones clearly but soft tissue appears transparent.

Which explanation best describes why X-rays pass through soft tissue but are stopped by bone?

A. X-rays have high energy due to their high frequency, which allows them to penetrate low-density soft tissue. Bone is denser and absorbs more of the X-ray energy, creating contrast.
B. Soft tissue is transparent to all types of electromagnetic waves.
C. X-rays only travel in straight lines, and bones block their path like a wall.
D. X-rays are attracted to calcium in bones but repelled by soft tissue.

Correct Answer: A

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 + DCI PS4.B + CCC6

A student notices that WiFi works through walls but visible light does not. Both are electromagnetic waves.

Which property explains why radio waves (WiFi) pass through walls while visible light is blocked?

A. Radio waves are faster than visible light.
B. Radio waves have lower frequency and interact differently with wall materials than higher-frequency visible light, which is absorbed or reflected by the wall's molecular structure.
C. Walls have special filters that block only visible light.
D. Radio waves are smaller than visible light and can fit between wall molecules.

Correct Answer: B

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 8.1 + DCI PS4.C + CCC6

A telecommunications company is choosing between analog and digital signal transmission for a new system. Analog signals vary continuously (smooth wave). Digital signals encode information as discrete pulses (1s and 0s).

Which advantage of digital signals is best supported by wave science?

A. Digital signals can be perfectly regenerated because any noise distortion doesn't change whether a pulse reads as 1 or 0, while analog noise permanently degrades the signal.
B. Digital signals don't use electromagnetic waves, so they avoid interference.
C. Digital signals travel faster than analog signals through the same medium.
D. Digital signals use less energy than analog signals.

Correct Answer: A

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 + DCI PS4.B + CCC6

A scientist is designing a system to see through thick fog. Visible light is scattered and blocked by fog droplets. The scientist considers using waves from a different part of the electromagnetic spectrum.

Based on wave-matter interaction principles, which type of wave would most effectively penetrate fog?

A. Gamma rays, because they have the highest penetration depth of all EM waves.
B. Ultraviolet waves, because they have higher energy than visible light.
C. Infrared waves only work in total darkness, so they cannot help in fog.
D. Radio waves (radar), because their longer wavelengths are not scattered by small fog droplets the way shorter visible light wavelengths are.

Correct Answer: D
