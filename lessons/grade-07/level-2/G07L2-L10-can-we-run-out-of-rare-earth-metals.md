## Platform

### Title
**Can We Run Out of Rare Earth Metals? — Mining, Recycling, and the Hidden Cost of Modern Technology**

### Overview
We probably won't literally run out of rare earth metals, but we could face severe shortages that disrupt technology production worldwide. The minerals exist in Earth's crust, but the easy-to-mine deposits are being depleted. Right now, we recycle less than 1% of rare earths from old electronics. If... Students build a cause-and-effect model exploring how mining rate and recycling rate interact with available supply, market price, and substitution incentive in this system.

[A dramatic split image showing a rare earth mine with massive excavation equipment on one side and a pile of discarded smartphones and electronics on the other, highlighting the connection between mining and e-waste, cinematic industrial photography]

7th

### NGSS Standard
**MS-ESS3-1, MS-ESS3-4**: Construct a scientific explanation based on evidence for how the uneven distributions of Earth's mineral, energy, and groundwater resources are the result of past and current geoscience processes. Construct an argument supported by evidence for how increases in human population and per-capita consumption of natural resources impact Earth's systems.

### Learning Objectives
- Model how mining rate and recycling rate interact to determine the available supply of rare earth metals
- Explain how supply and demand dynamics drive market price and substitution incentives
- Predict how changes in resource consumption and recycling affect long-term supply sustainability
- Describe how human activity impacts Earth's finite mineral resources

### Component List (Starting Model: 5 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Mining Rate | External | The rate at which rare earth metals are extracted from underground deposits |
| Recycling Rate | External | The percentage of rare earth metals recovered from discarded electronics and products |
| Available Supply | Internal | The total amount of rare earth metals accessible for manufacturing and technology production |
| Market Price | Internal | The cost per unit of rare earth metals, driven by the balance of supply and demand |
| Substitution Incentive | Internal | The economic pressure to develop alternative materials that can replace rare earth metals |

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format. Each item is three-dimensional, assessing a Science and Engineering Practice (SEP), Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and Crosscutting Concept (CCC) simultaneously, aligned to MS-ESS3-1, MS-ESS3-4.

---

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.1 (Determine components of a system) + DCI + CCC4 (Describe a system in terms of its components)

A scientist is studying can we run out of rare earth metals?. She identifies these components: Mining Rate, Recycling Rate, Available Supply, Market Price, Substitution Incentive. She sorts them into external (things we set or cannot control) and internal (things that change based on other factors).

Which list correctly sorts the components?

A. External: Mining Rate, Available Supply — Internal: Recycling Rate, Market Price, Substitution Incentive
B. External: Available Supply, Market Price — Internal: Mining Rate, Recycling Rate, Substitution Incentive
C. External: Substitution Incentive, Mining Rate — Internal: Recycling Rate, Available Supply, Market Price
D. External: Mining Rate, Recycling Rate — Internal: Available Supply, Market Price, Substitution Incentive

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: Mining Rate and Recycling Rate are external because they represent conditions that drive the system. Available Supply, Market Price, and Substitution Incentive are internal because they change based on other factors in the system.

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships among components) + DCI + CCC4 (Describe components and interactions)

A country that depends on imported rare earth metals for its technology industry faces a supply disruption. According to the supply-price-substitution model, what would happen first?

A. Available Supply controls Mining Rate, not the other way around
B. As Mining Rate increases, Available Supply increases
C. Mining Rate has no effect on Available Supply
D. As Mining Rate decreases, Available Supply increases

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Higher mining rates extract more rare earth metals from the ground, increasing the available supply for manufacturing.
