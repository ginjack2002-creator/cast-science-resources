## Platform

### Title
**Newton's Laws — The Three Rules That Govern Every Motion in the Universe**

### Overview
Students model how applied force, mass, and friction interact to determine an object's acceleration. By building a cause-and-effect model of force, mass, friction, net force, and acceleration, students discover why pushing a full shopping cart is harder than pushing an empty one and why F = ma explains every motion they have ever seen.

### Cover Image
[Dramatic freeze-frame action shot of a bowling ball mid-collision with pins, showing motion blur and force vectors, dynamic lighting against a dark background, cinematic sports physics]

### Grade
7th

### NGSS Standard
**MS-PS2-1, MS-PS2-2**: Apply Newton's Third Law to design a solution to a problem involving the motion of two colliding objects. Plan an investigation to provide evidence that the change in an object's motion depends on the sum of the forces acting on the object and the mass of the object.

### Learning Objectives
- Model how applied force, mass, and friction interact to determine an object's acceleration
- Explain why heavier objects require more force to achieve the same acceleration as lighter ones
- Predict how net force determines whether an object speeds up, slows down, or stays at rest
- Design investigations demonstrating that F = ma through controlled variable experiments

### Component List (Starting Model: 5 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Applied Force | External | The push or pull exerted on the object by an external source |
| Object Mass | External | How much matter the object contains, determining its resistance to acceleration |
| Acceleration | Internal | How quickly the object's speed changes in response to net force |
| Friction Force | Internal | The resistance force from the surface that opposes the object's motion |
| Net Force | Internal | The total unbalanced force remaining after friction is subtracted from applied force |

```
Task B: DRAW YOUR RELATIONSHIPS
• Applied Force → Net Force
• Friction Force → Net Force
• Net Force → Acceleration
• Object Mass → Acceleration
• Acceleration → Friction Force

Task C: SET POSITIVE OR NEGATIVE

  ○ Applied Force → Net Force = POSITIVE (+)
    More push = more net force available

  ○ Friction Force → Net Force = NEGATIVE (−)
    Higher friction SUBTRACTS from applied force

  ○ Net Force → Acceleration = POSITIVE (+)
    More net force = more acceleration (F = ma!)

  ○ Object Mass → Acceleration = NEGATIVE (−)
    More mass = LESS acceleration for same force (a = F/m!)

  ○ Acceleration → Friction Force = POSITIVE (+)
    Faster motion increases friction between surfaces

Task D: CHECK YOUR MODEL
• 5 arrows total
• 3 positive, 2 negative
• This IS Newton's Second Law as a system!

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

drives acceleration. Mass resists it. Friction fights it.
```

### Video Script

```
"Let's wire Newton's Laws into your model.

Applied Force to Net Force. More push means more total force.
POSITIVE.

Friction Force to Net Force. Friction OPPOSES motion. It
subtracts from your push. NEGATIVE. If you push with 10
Newtons and friction takes 3, your net force is only 7.

Net Force to Acceleration. This is Newton's Second Law.
More net force means more acceleration. POSITIVE. F = ma.

Now the critical one. Object Mass to Acceleration. When you
push a heavy object with the same force, does it accelerate
more or less?

LESS. Way less. That's the 'inversely proportional' part.
Doubling the mass HALVES the acceleration. NEGATIVE.

This is why pushing a full cart is harder. Same force, more
mass, less acceleration. F = ma solved it.

One more: Acceleration to Friction Force. As an object moves
faster, friction between surfaces increases. POSITIVE.

Look at your model. Net Force is where the battle happens.
Applied Force pushes it up. Friction pushes it down. Whatever
net force remains determines acceleration, but mass decides
how much that net force actually accomplishes.

That's physics. That's Newton. That's everything.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

```
too. On a rough enough surface, friction could equal your
push, and net force becomes ZERO. No acceleration at all.
The object doesn't move.

You just proved Newton's Laws. Every time you push something,
throw something, or ride something, these exact relationships
are at work.
```

```
• "Heavier objects fall faster" — In vacuum, all objects fall

• "Action-reaction forces cancel out" — They act on DIFFERENT
  objects, so they never cancel.
```

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 3.1 (Plan investigations) + DCI PS2.A + CCC7 (Stability and Change)

A student sorts five components of a force and motion model: Applied Force, Object Mass, Acceleration, Friction Force, and Net Force.

Which correctly identifies the external inputs and internal responses?

A. External: Net Force, Applied Force — Internal: Object Mass, Acceleration, Friction Force
B. External: Applied Force, Friction Force — Internal: Object Mass, Acceleration, Net Force
C. External: Applied Force, Object Mass — Internal: Acceleration, Friction Force, Net Force
D. External: Applied Force, Acceleration — Internal: Object Mass, Friction, Net Force

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: Applied Force (how hard you push) and Object Mass (how heavy it is) are inputs students control. Acceleration, Friction Force, and Net Force are determined by physical laws based on those inputs.
---

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 3.1 + DCI PS2.A + CCC7

Two identical forces are applied to two objects. Object A has a mass of 2 kg. Object B has a mass of 8 kg. Both experience the same friction.

Which statement correctly compares their accelerations?

A. Both objects accelerate at the same rate because the same force is applied.
B. Object A accelerates slower because lighter objects have less inertia to overcome.
C. Object A accelerates 4 times faster than Object B because acceleration is inversely proportional to mass.
D. Object B accelerates faster because more mass means more momentum.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: Newton's Second Law: a = F/m. With the same net force, dividing by 4 times the mass gives 1/4 the acceleration. Object A (2 kg) accelerates 4 times faster than Object B (8 kg).
---

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 3.1 + DCI PS2.A + CCC7

A student pushes a box across three different surfaces with the same force. On Surface 1 (smooth tile), the box accelerates quickly. On Surface 2 (carpet), it accelerates slowly. On Surface 3 (rough concrete), it barely moves.

What best explains these different outcomes?

A. Different surfaces provide different amounts of friction, which changes the net force and therefore the acceleration.
B. The student pushes harder on smooth surfaces because they are easier.
C. Gravity is different on each surface, affecting how fast the box moves.
D. The mass of the box changes on each surface.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: The applied force and mass are constant. What changes is friction. Smooth tile has low friction (high net force, high acceleration). Rough concrete has high friction (low net force, minimal acceleration). Net Force = Applied Force minus Friction.
---

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 3.4 (Plan investigations) + DCI PS2.A + CCC7

A loaded truck (mass: 5000 kg) and an empty car (mass: 1000 kg) are both traveling at 60 km/h. Both slam on their brakes, which provide the same friction force.

Which vehicle takes longer to stop, and why?

A. Both stop at the same time because they experience the same brake force.
B. The truck takes longer because it is moving faster than the car.
C. The car takes longer because it is lighter and has less friction with the road.
D. The truck takes longer because its greater mass means the same braking force produces less deceleration (a = F/m), requiring more time and distance to stop.

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: Both travel at the same speed and experience the same braking force. But the truck's mass is 5 times greater. By a = F/m, the truck's deceleration is 1/5th that of the car. Less deceleration means more time and distance to stop. This is why loaded trucks have longer stopping distances and why following distance matters.
---

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 3.4 + DCI PS2.A + CCC7

An engineer is designing a safer vehicle. Based on Newton's Laws, she knows that in a collision, passengers continue moving forward at the vehicle's speed (First Law), and the force they experience depends on how quickly they decelerate (Second Law).

Which design feature best applies Newton's Laws to reduce injury?

A. Make the vehicle heavier so it has more momentum and pushes through obstacles.
B. Add crumple zones that collapse gradually, extending the time of impact and reducing the peak force on passengers.
C. Remove seatbelts so passengers can brace themselves with their arms.
D. Make the vehicle rigid so it doesn't deform during a collision.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Newton's Second Law: F = ma, which can be rewritten as F = m(change in velocity / time). By extending the time of deceleration (crumple zones collapse gradually), the peak force is reduced. Same impulse, longer time, lower force. This is the fundamental principle behind crumple zones, airbags, and padded dashboards.
