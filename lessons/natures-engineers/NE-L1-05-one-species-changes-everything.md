## Overview

### Big Question
**Can removing just ONE species really destroy an entire ecosystem?**

### NGSS Standards
**MS-LS2-4, HS-LS2-6**: Construct an argument supported by empirical evidence that changes to physical or biological components of an ecosystem affect populations. Evaluate claims, evidence, and reasoning that the complex interactions in ecosystems maintain relatively consistent numbers and types of organisms in stable conditions, but changing conditions may result in a new ecosystem.

### Learning Objectives
- Explain what a keystone species is and why some species matter more than others
- Model how keystone species population affects habitat quality, food availability, and biodiversity
- Identify feedback loops where outputs feed back into the system
- Predict what happens to an ecosystem when its keystone species is removed

---

## Vocabulary

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Keystone Species** | A species that has an outsized impact on its ecosystem — remove it and everything changes |
| **Biodiversity** | The variety of different species living in an ecosystem |
| **Feedback Loop** | When the output of a system circles back and affects its own input — creating a cycle |
| **Cascade Effect** | When one change triggers a chain of changes throughout a system |

---

## Model Components

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Keystone Species Population | External | How many of the key species (like beavers) are present in the ecosystem |
| Habitat Quality | Internal | Overall health of the ecosystem — water, soil, shelter, nesting sites |
| Food Availability | Internal | How much food the ecosystem produces for all species |
| Number of Other Species | Internal | How many different species can survive in the ecosystem |

---

## Think About It

> When keystone species population increases, what happens to habitat quality? And when habitat quality increases, what happens to the number of other species? Does anything loop back?

---

## Relationships

- **Keystone Species Population to Habitat Quality** = POSITIVE (+)
  More keystone organisms (like beavers) create and maintain habitat — dams create ponds, dig channels, flood areas that become wetlands. Their physical activity directly improves habitat quality.

- **Habitat Quality to Food Availability** = POSITIVE (+)
  Better habitat supports more plant growth, more insects, more small animals — all of which are food for other species. A healthy wetland produces far more food than a dry streambed.

- **Food Availability to Number of Other Species** = POSITIVE (+)
  More available food supports more species. When food is abundant, specialists and generalists alike can find niches. When food is scarce, only the toughest generalists survive.

- **Number of Other Species to Habitat Quality** = POSITIVE (+) — FEEDBACK LOOP
  More species improve habitat quality — plants stabilize soil, predators control herbivore overgrazing, decomposers recycle nutrients. Biodiversity itself is a habitat-building force. This creates a REINFORCING LOOP.


---

## Scenarios

**Scenario 1: Thriving Colony**
- Settings: High keystone population
- Prediction Question: What do you predict will happen to biodiversity when the keystone species is thriving?

**Scenario 2: Species Removed**
- Settings: Zero keystone population
- Prediction Question: If we remove the keystone species entirely, what do you predict happens to all three internal components?

**Scenario 3: Recovery**
- Settings: Gradually increasing from zero
- Prediction Question: When we bring the keystone species back, will the ecosystem recover immediately or slowly?


---

## Key Discoveries

- One species can support an entire ecosystem — losing it causes a cascade of decline
- Habitat quality, food availability, and biodiversity are all connected in a chain
- There is a FEEDBACK LOOP: more species improve habitat quality, which supports even more species
- Recovery takes time — ecosystems do not bounce back instantly when the keystone returns

---

## Answer to Big Question

YES — removing just one keystone species can transform an entire ecosystem! When beavers are removed, their dams decay, ponds drain, wetlands dry up, and hundreds of species lose their habitat. But here is the hopeful part: when keystone species are brought back, they restart the chain. The key is the FEEDBACK LOOP — once the ecosystem starts recovering, it builds on itself, getting healthier and healthier over time.

## STEM Challenge: Jenga Ecosystem

**Mission:** Play a modified Jenga game where each block represents a species. Discover which removals cause the tower to collapse and which ones the ecosystem can survive.

**Scenario:** A conservation agency needs to understand which species are most critical to protect. Your team will simulate species removal using a Jenga tower and identify which species, if removed, cause the ecosystem to crash.

**Guiding Questions:**
- Which block (species) caused the tower to fall? Why was that one so important?
- Could the tower survive losing some species but not others?
- How is this game similar to what happens in a real ecosystem?

---

## Game: Jenga Ecosystem

**Type:** Modified Board Game

Label Jenga blocks as different species (beaver, frog, dragonfly, willow tree, trout, etc.). One block is marked KEYSTONE. Students take turns removing species. The game plays normally until someone removes the keystone block — then they must also remove 3 additional blocks immediately (cascade collapse). Visceral demonstration of keystone species importance.

**Materials:**
- Jenga set
- Species label stickers (pre-printed)
- Gold star sticker for KEYSTONE block
- Ecosystem web reference card
- Data recording sheets

**Learning Connection:** Students physically experience the difference between removing a regular species (tower wobbles) and removing the keystone (cascade collapse). Makes the abstract concept visceral.

---

## STEAM Activity: Restoration Case Study Data Lab

**Type:** Data Analysis + Visualization

Students receive real datasets from actual beaver reintroduction projects (Methow Valley WA, River Otter UK, Yellowstone). They graph species counts, water quality, and vegetation cover over time before and after reintroduction. Compare real data to model predictions.

**Materials:**
- Printed datasets from real reintroduction projects
- Graph paper or graphing software
- Colored pencils
- Calculators
- Comparison worksheet

**Learning Connection:** Students see that their simple 4-component model actually predicts real-world outcomes — the cascade and feedback loop appear in actual data from real restoration projects.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format. Each item is three-dimensional, assessing a Science and Engineering Practice (SEP), Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and Crosscutting Concept (CCC) simultaneously, aligned to MS-LS2-4, HS-LS2-6.

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.1 (Determine components of a system) + DCI LS2.C.1 (Describe how ecosystem interactions maintain stability) + CCC4 (Describe a system in terms of its components)

Scientists studying a wetland ecosystem build a model with four components: keystone species population, habitat quality, food availability, and the number of other species (biodiversity). They classify some components as external inputs that the scientists can set and others as internal outputs that the model calculates.

Which classification is correct?

A. External: Keystone Species Population, Habitat Quality — Internal: Food Availability, Number of Other Species
B. External: Food Availability, Number of Other Species — Internal: Keystone Species Population, Habitat Quality
C. All four components are external inputs that the scientists control directly.
D. External: Keystone Species Population — Internal: Habitat Quality, Food Availability, Number of Other Species

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: Keystone species population is the only external input that the scientists set. Habitat quality, food availability, and number of other species are all internal components that change based on how the keystone species affects the system. They are outputs that cascade from the input. If you chose A, habitat quality is not an external input. It is determined by how the keystone species modifies the environment. If you chose B, food availability and biodiversity are results of the system, not inputs that scientists set. If you chose C, only the keystone species population is set directly. The other three are calculated by the model.

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships among components) + DCI LS2.C.1 (Explain interactions that maintain ecosystem stability) + CCC2 (Use cause and effect to explain events)

A model of a wetland ecosystem shows a chain of positive relationships: keystone species population positively affects habitat quality, habitat quality positively affects food availability, and food availability positively affects the number of other species. The model also shows a feedback loop where number of other species positively affects habitat quality.

A student claims that the feedback loop means the ecosystem will grow forever without any limit. Why is this claim incorrect?

A. The feedback loop is reinforcing, but real ecosystems have limits like space, water, and nutrients that eventually slow growth.
B. The feedback loop is negative, so it actually slows growth automatically.
C. The claim is actually correct. Feedback loops always cause unlimited growth.
D. Feedback loops do not exist in real ecosystems, only in computer models.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: While the feedback loop between biodiversity and habitat quality is reinforcing (more of one leads to more of the other), real ecosystems have carrying capacities. Space, water, nutrients, and sunlight are all limited. The reinforcing loop accelerates growth but cannot push past the physical limits of the environment. If you chose C, no real system grows forever. All ecosystems have resource limits that eventually constrain growth. If you chose B, the model describes a positive (reinforcing) feedback loop, not a negative one. More species improve habitat, which supports more species. If you chose D, feedback loops exist in all ecosystems. They are fundamental to how ecosystems self-regulate.

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to predict events) + DCI LS2.A.1 (Describe how organisms interact) + CCC4 (Describe interactions within a system)

Scientists tracked a wetland over four years. In Year 1, beavers (the keystone species) were present and the ecosystem had high habitat quality, high food availability, and 47 species. In Year 2, hunters removed all the beavers. By Year 3, habitat quality had dropped to medium, food availability had dropped to low, and only 28 species remained. By Year 4, habitat quality was low, food was very low, and only 12 species remained.

Based on the model, what caused the cascade of decline from Year 2 to Year 4?

A. Hunters removed all the other species along with the beavers.
B. Removing the keystone species broke the first link in the chain, causing habitat to decline, then food, then biodiversity, each step triggering the next.
C. The 47 species competed with each other until most were eliminated.
D. A drought reduced water levels, killing species directly.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: The model shows a cascade chain: keystone species affects habitat, habitat affects food, food affects biodiversity. Removing the beavers broke the first link. Without beavers maintaining dams and ponds, habitat quality declined. Lower habitat quality meant less food. Less food meant fewer species could survive. Each step caused the next. If you chose D, the data does not mention a drought. The cause was beaver removal, and the model traces the cascade from that single change. If you chose A, the question states only beavers were removed. The other species declined because of the cascade effect, not direct removal. If you chose C, competition existed before the beavers were removed too. The decline was caused by losing the keystone species, not by sudden competition.

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 6.1.1 (Construct an argument using evidence) + DCI LS2.C.1 (Evaluate how changes affect populations) + CCC7 (Describe stability and change in a system)

Two students debate whether a damaged ecosystem can recover if the keystone species is reintroduced. Student A says the ecosystem should bounce back immediately once beavers return. Student B says recovery will take time because each step in the cascade must rebuild in order.

The model shows that when beavers are reintroduced at Year 5, habitat quality reaches medium by Year 6, food availability reaches medium by Year 7, and species count reaches 30 by Year 8. Full recovery to 47 species does not happen until Year 10.

Which student's argument is best supported by the data?

A. Neither student, because the ecosystem never fully recovered.
B. Student A, because the ecosystem started recovering as soon as beavers returned.
C. Student B, because the data shows that recovery took five years and each component rebuilt in sequence: habitat first, then food, then biodiversity.
D. Both students are equally correct because some things recovered quickly and some slowly.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: The data clearly supports Student B. Recovery took five full years (Year 5 to Year 10), and each component rebuilt in order: habitat quality improved first, then food availability caught up, then species returned. The cascade works in reverse during recovery, with each step depending on the one before it. If you chose B, while the ecosystem did start recovering, it did not bounce back "immediately." It took five years for full recovery, with each step lagging behind the previous one. If you chose A, the data shows full recovery (47 species) was reached by Year 10. The ecosystem did fully recover. If you chose D, the key insight is the sequential nature of recovery, which specifically supports Student B's argument.

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to predict events) + DCI LS2.C.1 (Predict effects of ecosystem changes) + CCC7 (Use stability and change to predict outcomes)

A conservation group wants to protect a wetland that currently has a healthy beaver population, high biodiversity, and an active feedback loop between species diversity and habitat quality. The group has two options: Option 1 is to focus resources on protecting the beavers from hunting and habitat loss. Option 2 is to ignore the beavers and focus only on planting more native plants to improve habitat quality directly.

Based on the model, which option is more likely to maintain the ecosystem long-term?

A. Both options are equally effective because the feedback loop will maintain the system regardless.
B. Neither option will work because ecosystems always eventually collapse.
C. Option 1, because the model shows that the keystone species is the first link in the cascade chain, and protecting it sustains the entire system including the reinforcing feedback loop.
D. Option 2, because plants are more important than beavers for habitat quality.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: The model shows the keystone species is the first link in the cascade. Protecting beavers sustains the chain from keystone population to habitat quality to food availability to biodiversity. The reinforcing feedback loop between biodiversity and habitat quality amplifies the benefits. If beavers are lost, the entire cascade collapses and the feedback loop cannot sustain itself alone. If you chose D, while plants help, the model shows that beavers are the primary driver of habitat quality through their dam-building activity. Plants alone cannot replicate what beavers do. If you chose A, the feedback loop is reinforcing but requires the keystone species to sustain the initial input. Without beavers, the loop weakens and eventually collapses. If you chose B, well-managed ecosystems with keystone species present can remain healthy indefinitely.
