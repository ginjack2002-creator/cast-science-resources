## Platform

### Title
**The Carbon Cycle: Where Does Your Breath Go? — How Carbon Travels Between Air, Plants, Soil, and Back Again**

### Overview
Students model where the carbon in their breath goes and how it comes back. By building a cause-and-effect model of CO2, photosynthesis, decomposition, and soil carbon storage, students discover that carbon moves in a continuous cycle between the atmosphere, living organisms, and the ground. This lesson serves as a hook for deeper inquiry into matter cycles, ecosystems, and climate science.

### Cover Image
[A photorealistic, cinematic image of a lush green forest with visible CO2 molecules floating in the air, sunlight streaming through tree canopy, showing the invisible exchange of carbon between trees and atmosphere, ultra-crisp detail]

### Grade
5th

### NGSS Standard
**5-LS1-1, 5-LS2-1**: Develop a model to describe the movement of matter among plants, animals, decomposers, and the environment. Support an argument that plants get the materials they need for growth chiefly from air and water.

### Learning Objectives
- Trace carbon as it moves between the atmosphere, plants, soil, and back
- Model how CO2 levels and sunlight affect photosynthesis rate
- Explain the role of decomposition in releasing stored carbon
- Predict what happens to atmospheric CO2 when photosynthesis stops

### Component List (Starting Model: 5 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| CO2 in Atmosphere | External | The amount of carbon dioxide gas in the air |
| Sunlight Intensity | External | The amount of solar energy reaching plants |
| Photosynthesis Rate | Internal | How fast plants absorb CO2 and convert it to sugar |
| Decomposition Rate | Internal | How fast dead organisms are broken down, releasing carbon |
| Soil Carbon Storage | Internal | The amount of carbon locked in the ground from dead plants |

## Step 2: ESTABLISH — Connect the Relationships

### Text Editor

```
TIME TO DRAW THE INVISIBLE LINES

Those five components don't just sit there — they AFFECT each other.
When one changes, others change too. Let's map those connections.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Task A: OPEN CONNECTION MODE
• Click the "Connect" icon in the TOP LEFT corner of your screen
• Your cursor is now ready to draw relationship arrows

Task B: DRAW YOUR RELATIONSHIPS
• Click on "CO2 in Atmosphere" and drag an arrow to "Photosynthesis Rate"
• Click on "Sunlight Intensity" and drag an arrow to "Photosynthesis Rate"
• Click on "Photosynthesis Rate" and drag an arrow to "CO2 in Atmosphere"
• Click on "Decomposition Rate" and drag an arrow to "CO2 in Atmosphere"
• Click on "Photosynthesis Rate" and drag an arrow to "Soil Carbon Storage"

Task C: SET POSITIVE OR NEGATIVE
• Look at the +/- toggle in the TOP LEFT corner
• For each connection, ask: "When this goes UP, does the other go UP or DOWN?"

  ○ CO2 in Atmosphere → Photosynthesis Rate = POSITIVE (+)
    More CO2 gives plants more raw material for photosynthesis.

  ○ Sunlight Intensity → Photosynthesis Rate = POSITIVE (+)
    More sunlight provides more energy for plants to photosynthesize.

  ○ Photosynthesis Rate → CO2 in Atmosphere = NEGATIVE (−)
    Faster photosynthesis removes more CO2 from the air.

  ○ Decomposition Rate → CO2 in Atmosphere = POSITIVE (+)
    Faster decomposition releases more stored carbon back as CO2.

  ○ Photosynthesis Rate → Soil Carbon Storage = POSITIVE (+)
    More photosynthesis creates more plant matter that eventually stores carbon in soil.

Task D: CHECK YOUR MODEL
• You should have 5 arrows total
• 1 negative relationship, 4 positive relationships
• Notice the LOOP: CO2 feeds photosynthesis, which reduces CO2

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Notice the feedback loop? Photosynthesis REMOVES CO2 from the air.
Decomposition ADDS it back. That's the cycle!
```

### Video Script

```
"You've built the carbon cycle. You've wired the connections. Now
let's flip the switch and see if it works like the real world.

Click the 'Play' button in the top left. Watch the graph panel.

First, let's see the normal carbon cycle. Set CO2 and Sunlight
to moderate levels. Let it run. Watch the balance — photosynthesis
pulls CO2 down, decomposition pushes it back up. The cycle flows.

Now let's test something dramatic. Lock Sunlight Intensity to zero.
Total darkness. What happens?

Watch Photosynthesis Rate. It crashes to zero. Without sunlight,
plants can't photosynthesize. They can't pull CO2 from the air.

But look at Decomposition Rate. It's STILL going. Dead organisms
are still being broken down, still releasing CO2.

So what happens to CO2 in Atmosphere? It RISES. Carbon is being
released by decomposition, but nothing is removing it. The filter
is turned off.

This is exactly what scientists worry about when forests are cut
down. Fewer trees means less photosynthesis. Less photosynthesis
means CO2 builds up.

Now try the High CO2 scenario. Set CO2 to 90%. Watch what plants
do — Photosynthesis Rate increases because there's more CO2 for
plants to absorb. It's a negative feedback loop. More CO2 means
faster photosynthesis, which pulls CO2 back down.

The carbon cycle is self-regulating, but it has limits.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

### Teacher Guide

```
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
TEACHER GUIDE: The Carbon Cycle: Where Does Your Breath Go?
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

LESSON PURPOSE:
This lesson serves as a HOOK for matter cycling inquiry. Students
build a carbon cycle model with feedback loops, then identify
gaps that drive further research. It connects to climate science,
ecosystems, and conservation.

NGSS ALIGNMENT:
5-LS1-1: Support an argument that plants get the materials they
need for growth chiefly from air and water.
5-LS2-1: Develop a model to describe the movement of matter among
plants, animals, decomposers, and the environment.

THREE DIMENSIONS:
• Science Practice: Developing and Using Models — Students develop
  a model showing how carbon cycles between atmosphere, plants,
  soil, and back.
• Disciplinary Core Idea: LS2.B Cycles of Matter and Energy
  Transfer — Matter cycles between living and nonliving parts of
  an ecosystem through processes like photosynthesis and decomposition.
• Crosscutting Concept: Systems and System Models — Students model
  the carbon cycle as a system with feedback loops where outputs
  become inputs.

PACING GUIDE:
• Step 1 (Locate): 8-10 minutes
• Step 2 (Establish): 8-10 minutes
• Step 3 (Visualize): 10-12 minutes
• Step 4 (Extend): 10-15 minutes
• Total: 40-45 minutes (or split across 2 days)

LEVER FRAMEWORK:
• L (Locate): Students identify CO2 in Atmosphere, Sunlight
  Intensity, Photosynthesis Rate, Decomposition Rate, and Soil
  Carbon Storage as the five system components.
• E (Establish): Students determine that CO2 and Sunlight
  positively affect Photosynthesis, that Photosynthesis negatively
  affects CO2 (removes it), and that Decomposition positively
  affects CO2 (releases it).
• V (Visualize): Students build the carbon cycle model showing
  feedback loops where photosynthesis removes CO2 and decomposition
  adds it back.
• Ev (Evaluate): Students run the Sunlight Shutdown scenario and
  observe CO2 building up when photosynthesis stops, then test the
  High CO2 scenario to see plants respond by photosynthesizing faster.
• R (Revise): Students add Ocean Absorption or Fossil Fuel Burning
  to explore how humans and oceans affect the carbon cycle.

SLIDE-BY-SLIDE FACILITATION:
• Slide 1 (Cover, 2 min): Say "Every breath you take is part of
  a giant cycle. Where does it go?" Do: Have students breathe out
  onto their hands and ask: Where does that CO2 go?
• Slide 2 (Learning Objectives, 2 min): Say "Today we trace carbon
  through the entire cycle." Do: Have students read objectives aloud
  and circle unfamiliar words.
• Slide 3 (Big Question, 2 min): Say "This is the mystery we will
  solve with our model." Do: Quick-write: students write their best
  guess in 30 seconds.
• Slide 4 (LEVER Framework, 2 min): Say "We will use all five LEVER
  steps to build and test our carbon cycle model." Do: Briefly review
  each step with the class.
• Slide 5 (Activity 1, 8 min): Say "Our model has five parts. Two
  are external. Three are internal." Do: Students sort component cards
  into external and internal categories.
• Slide 6 (Activity 2, 8 min): Say "Notice that photosynthesis has
  a NEGATIVE effect on CO2 — it removes carbon from the air. This
  creates a feedback loop." Do: Guide students to draw all five
  relationship arrows in ModelIt.
• Slide 7 (Activity 3, 10 min): Say "What happens when we take away
  sunlight? Let's test it." Do: Students run Sunlight Shutdown
  scenario and record observations.
• Slide 8 (Discoveries, 5 min): Say "Carbon moves in a loop, not
  a straight line. That's what makes it a cycle." Do: Class
  discussion: How is this different from an energy chain?
• Slide 9 (STEM Challenge, 10 min): Say "Now use what you learned
  to design a carbon-capturing schoolyard." Do: Distribute design
  materials, circulate to assist groups.

PRE-LESSON PREP:
□ Test ModelIt access on student devices
□ Have images of forests and decomposing leaves ready
□ Review vocabulary: carbon dioxide, photosynthesis, decomposition

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Plants get carbon from soil" — Plants get carbon from CO2 in the
   AIR through photosynthesis, not from the soil. Soil provides
   minerals and water. Ask: If plants got carbon from soil, why do
   they have leaves reaching toward sunlight?
• "Carbon is only in living things" — Carbon exists in the atmosphere
   (CO2), in oceans (dissolved CO2), in rocks (limestone), and in
   fossil fuels. Living things are just one stop on the cycle.
   Show students a piece of chalk (calcium carbonate) and explain
   it contains carbon.
• "Decomposition is bad" — Decomposition is essential for the carbon
   cycle. Without it, dead matter would pile up and nutrients would
   never return to the soil for new plants. Ask: What would happen
   to a forest if nothing ever decomposed?

FACILITATION TIPS:
• Step 1: Let students explore the interface. Don't over-explain.
• Step 2: Ask "When this goes up, what happens to that?" to
   guide positive/negative relationship decisions.
• Step 3: Give time for students to "break" the model — turn
   things on/off and observe. This is where insight happens.
• Step 4: Don't give answers. Ask questions. "What about oceans?"
   "Where do humans fit?" Let curiosity drive.

DISCUSSION PROMPTS:
• "Why is the carbon cycle called a cycle and not a chain?"
• "How are forests sometimes called 'the lungs of the Earth'?"
• "What would happen to CO2 levels if all decomposition suddenly stopped?"
• "How does understanding the carbon cycle help us think about
   climate change?"

EXTENSION CONNECTIONS:
• Ecosystem unit → Decomposers as part of food web
• Climate science → Human CO2 emissions and carbon imbalance
• Water cycle → Comparing matter cycles in Earth systems

DIFFERENTIATION:
Support: Provide a visual diagram of the carbon cycle with labeled
arrows; use sentence frames: 'When CO2 goes up, photosynthesis goes
__ because...'; pair students for model building so stronger modelers
can guide peers
Challenge: Research how much carbon a single tree captures per year;
add ocean absorption as a component; compare carbon cycles in
forests, grasslands, and deserts
ELL: Provide visual vocabulary cards; pair with English-proficient
partner

CROSS-CURRICULAR CONNECTIONS:
• Math: Calculate how many trees would be needed to offset the
  school's annual CO2 emissions
• ELA: Write a narrative following a single carbon atom through
  three full cycles
• Social Studies: Research how deforestation in the Amazon affects
  the global carbon cycle

ASSESSMENT:
• Can student trace carbon through the full cycle verbally?
• Does student's model show correct positive/negative relationships?
• Can student explain the feedback loop between CO2 and photosynthesis?
• Did student add at least one researched component?
```

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format. Each item is three-dimensional, assessing a Science and Engineering Practice (SEP), Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and Crosscutting Concept (CCC) simultaneously, aligned to 5-LS1-1 and 5-LS2-1.

---

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.1 (Determine components of a system) + DCI LS2.B (Cycles of Matter) + CCC4 (Describe a system in terms of its components)

A scientist is studying the carbon cycle. She makes a list of things that affect how carbon moves through the environment: CO2 in the atmosphere, sunlight intensity, photosynthesis rate, decomposition rate, and soil carbon storage. She sorts them into two groups: things she can adjust in her experiment (external) and things that respond to conditions (internal).

Which list correctly sorts the components of the carbon cycle system?

A. External: CO2 in Atmosphere, Photosynthesis Rate — Internal: Sunlight Intensity, Decomposition Rate, Soil Carbon Storage
B. External: CO2 in Atmosphere, Sunlight Intensity — Internal: Photosynthesis Rate, Decomposition Rate, Soil Carbon Storage
C. External: Decomposition Rate, Sunlight Intensity — Internal: CO2 in Atmosphere, Photosynthesis Rate, Soil Carbon Storage
D. External: CO2 in Atmosphere, Soil Carbon Storage — Internal: Sunlight Intensity, Photosynthesis Rate, Decomposition Rate

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: CO2 in Atmosphere and Sunlight Intensity are external because they represent environmental conditions that scientists can set or adjust. Photosynthesis Rate, Decomposition Rate, and Soil Carbon Storage are internal because they respond to and are determined by the external conditions. If you chose A, Photosynthesis Rate is not external — it changes based on how much CO2 and sunlight are available. If you chose C, Decomposition Rate responds to conditions; it is not something you set from outside the system. If you chose D, Soil Carbon Storage is a result of the system, not an input you control.

---

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships among components) + DCI LS2.B (Cycles of Matter) + CCC4 (Describe components and interactions)

In a carbon cycle model, a student draws an arrow from Photosynthesis Rate to CO2 in Atmosphere and labels it as a negative relationship. This means that when photosynthesis increases, CO2 in the atmosphere decreases. Another student disagrees and says the arrow should be positive.

Which explanation best supports why the relationship between Photosynthesis Rate and CO2 in Atmosphere is negative?

A. When photosynthesis increases, more plants grow, so there are more leaves to produce CO2.
B. When photosynthesis increases, plants absorb more CO2 from the air, reducing the amount in the atmosphere.
C. When photosynthesis increases, decomposition also increases, releasing more CO2.
D. When photosynthesis increases, sunlight is used up, so there is less energy for CO2 production.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: A negative relationship means that when one component goes up, the other goes down. During photosynthesis, plants absorb CO2 from the atmosphere and convert it into glucose. More photosynthesis means more CO2 is removed from the air, so atmospheric CO2 decreases. If you chose A, leaves do not produce CO2 during photosynthesis — they absorb it. CO2 is released during respiration and decomposition, not photosynthesis. If you chose C, decomposition is a separate process; increased photosynthesis does not directly increase decomposition. If you chose D, sunlight is not "used up" in a way that affects CO2 production.

---

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships among components) + DCI LS1.C (Organization for Matter and Energy Flow) + CCC4 (Describe interactions within a system)

A student builds a model of the carbon cycle. The model shows that more CO2 leads to faster photosynthesis (positive relationship), faster photosynthesis reduces CO2 (negative relationship), and decomposition adds CO2 back to the atmosphere (positive relationship). The student blocks all sunlight in the model to test what happens.

Based on the model, what would most likely happen when sunlight drops to zero?

A. CO2 in Atmosphere would decrease because plants would stop releasing CO2.
B. Photosynthesis would increase because plants need less sunlight in the dark.
C. CO2 in Atmosphere would increase because decomposition continues but photosynthesis stops.
D. Decomposition would stop because it depends on sunlight to break down dead matter.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: Without sunlight, photosynthesis cannot occur, so plants stop absorbing CO2 from the atmosphere. However, decomposition does not require sunlight — bacteria and fungi continue breaking down dead matter, releasing CO2 into the air. With CO2 being added (by decomposition) but not removed (because photosynthesis stopped), CO2 levels rise. If you chose A, plants absorb CO2 during photosynthesis; stopping photosynthesis means LESS CO2 is removed, not less released. If you chose B, photosynthesis requires sunlight — no sunlight means no photosynthesis. If you chose D, decomposition does not depend on sunlight. Bacteria and fungi decompose matter in dark environments like soil.

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to explain/predict events) + DCI LS2.B (Cycles of Matter) + CCC4 (Describe components and interactions)

Scientists measured CO2 levels and photosynthesis rates in a forest over four seasons. In spring, CO2 was moderate, sunlight was increasing, and photosynthesis rate was rising. In summer, sunlight was high, photosynthesis was at its peak, and CO2 was at its lowest. In fall, sunlight decreased, photosynthesis slowed, and CO2 started rising. In winter, sunlight was very low, photosynthesis was minimal, and CO2 was at its highest because decomposition continued.

Which observation best supports the idea that photosynthesis and decomposition have opposite effects on atmospheric CO2?

A. In spring, both sunlight and photosynthesis were increasing.
B. In summer, photosynthesis was highest and CO2 was lowest, but in winter, photosynthesis was minimal and CO2 was highest because decomposition continued releasing carbon.
C. In fall, sunlight decreased and photosynthesis slowed.
D. CO2 levels changed across all four seasons.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Comparing summer and winter reveals the opposite effects. In summer, high photosynthesis removes CO2 from the air, pulling levels down. In winter, photosynthesis is minimal but decomposition continues, adding CO2 without anything removing it. This contrast shows that photosynthesis reduces CO2 (negative effect) while decomposition increases it (positive effect). If you chose A, this shows sunlight driving photosynthesis but does not address decomposition. If you chose C, this shows one seasonal change but does not compare photosynthesis and decomposition effects. If you chose D, this is true but too vague to explain the mechanism.

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to predict a scientific event) + DCI LS2.B (Cycles of Matter) + CCC4 (Describe system components and interactions)

A school district wants to reduce its carbon footprint. They are studying a carbon cycle model that shows: more CO2 leads to more photosynthesis (positive), more photosynthesis reduces CO2 (negative), decomposition releases stored carbon as CO2 (positive), and photosynthesis stores carbon in soil through plant matter (positive). The district wants to take action to reduce CO2 in their local environment.

Based on the model, which action would most effectively reduce local atmospheric CO2?

A. Pave over the schoolyard to prevent decomposition in the soil.
B. Plant trees and maintain green spaces to increase photosynthesis.
C. Remove all fallen leaves so they cannot decompose.
D. Build a dome over the school to block sunlight from heating the air.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: The model shows that photosynthesis has a negative relationship with atmospheric CO2 — more photosynthesis removes more CO2 from the air. Planting trees and maintaining green spaces directly increases photosynthesis, which pulls more CO2 out of the atmosphere and stores carbon in plant matter and soil. If you chose A, paving would eliminate plants, reducing photosynthesis and the ability to absorb CO2. It would also trap heat rather than help. If you chose C, removing leaves removes organic matter that would eventually enrich soil and support more plant growth. The carbon in leaves would still be released as CO2 wherever they end up. If you chose D, blocking sunlight would stop photosynthesis entirely, preventing any CO2 absorption by plants.
