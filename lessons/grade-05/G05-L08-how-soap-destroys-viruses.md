## Platform

### Title
**How Soap Actually Destroys Viruses — The Chemical Reaction That Saves Millions of Lives Every Year**

### Overview
Students model the chemistry of handwashing — discovering that soap doesn't just rinse away viruses but actively destroys them through a chemical interaction with their outer membrane. By building a cause-and-effect model showing how soap concentration and water together determine soap effectiveness, which in turn destroys virus membranes and reduces virus survival, students understand why proper handwashing (with soap AND water, for 20 seconds) is one of the most powerful public health tools ever discovered. This lesson connects real-life personal health practice to chemistry principles.

### NGSS Standard
**5-PS1-4**: Conduct an investigation to determine whether the mixing of two or more substances results in new substances.

### Learning Objectives
- Explain that soap destroys viruses through a chemical interaction, not just physical washing
- Model how soap concentration and water together determine soap effectiveness
- Trace how soap effectiveness leads to virus membrane disruption and lower survival
- Apply the model to explain why proper handwashing technique matters

### Component List (Starting Model: 4 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Soap Concentration | External | How much soap is being applied — determined by how much soap you use |
| Water Present | External | Whether running water is present and activating the soap |
| Soap Effectiveness | Internal | How well the soap molecules are able to attack and disrupt virus membranes |
| Virus Survival Rate | Internal | The percentage of viruses that survive the handwashing process |

```
HOW SOAP ACTUALLY DESTROYS VIRUSES

You've been washing your hands your whole life. But do you know
what's actually happening?

Most people think soap washes away germs. Like, rinses them off.

That's partly true. But soap does something way more powerful
to many viruses: it DESTROYS them.

Here's the chemistry: many viruses — including flu, cold, and
COVID-19 — have an outer coating called a lipid membrane. It's
like their armor. It's also what allows them to infect cells.

Soap molecules are special. One end loves water. The other end
loves oil (and lipids). When you wash with soap and water, soap
molecules push into the virus's lipid armor, wedging between the
molecules that hold it together.

The armor shatters. The virus falls apart. It's dead.

You've been doing molecular demolition every time you wash your hands.
```

### Teacher Guide

```
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
TEACHER GUIDE: How Soap Actually Destroys Viruses
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

LESSON PURPOSE:
This lesson transforms the ordinary act of handwashing into a
fascinating chemical story, increasing students' understanding of
both substance mixing (5-PS1-4) and public health science. The
Semmelweis story provides historical context, a lesson about
scientific courage, and a connection to real lives. Students leave
genuinely understanding WHY handwashing works — making them more
likely to actually do it properly.

NGSS ALIGNMENT:
5-PS1-4: Conduct an investigation to determine whether the mixing
of two or more substances results in new substances.

PACING GUIDE:
• Step 1 (Locate): 8-10 minutes
• Step 2 (Establish): 8-10 minutes
• Step 3 (Visualize): 10-12 minutes
• Step 4 (Extend): 10-15 minutes
• Total: 40-45 minutes (or split across 2 days)

PRE-LESSON PREP:
□ Test ModelIt access on student devices
□ Optional: show a short video of soap molecules attacking a lipid membrane (many exist on YouTube)
□ Optional: have a bottle of soap and water to demonstrate how soap behaves differently in water vs. dry
□ Review vocabulary: lipid, membrane, amphiphilic, concentration, virus

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Soap just washes bacteria/viruses down the drain" → Soap DESTROYS many viruses
  by disrupting their membranes; they don't just get rinsed away intact
• "Water alone is enough" → Water is good for physical removal but can't disrupt
  lipid membranes without soap molecules
• "Hand sanitizer and soap work the same way" → Different mechanisms; soap is
  generally more effective for most viruses (FDA conclusion)
• "Antibacterial soap is better for viruses" → Antibacterial chemicals target
  bacteria, not viruses; regular soap is equally effective against viruses

FACILITATION TIPS:
• Step 1: The "molecular demolition" framing is very engaging for 5th graders.
  Ask them what demolition equipment the soap molecule is like.
• Step 2: Make sure students understand why the Soap Effectiveness → Virus Survival
  relationship is NEGATIVE — it's counterintuitive that "more effective" leads to "less survival."
• Step 3: The three-scenario comparison is the most powerful moment. Run all three
  side by side if possible for class discussion.
• Step 4: The Semmelweis story is excellent for discussing scientific courage,
  institutional resistance to change, and how lives can be lost when good science is ignored.

DISCUSSION PROMPTS:
• "If handwashing saves a million children per year, why don't all people have access to soap?"
• "What would you tell Ignaz Semmelweis if you could go back in time?"
• "Is hand sanitizer 'better' than soap? In what situations?"
• "Why do you think some people don't wash their hands even knowing the science?"

EXTENSION CONNECTIONS:
• Conservation of matter → What happens to virus particles after they're destroyed?
• Public health → How handwashing campaigns are designed and implemented
• Social justice → Access to clean water and soap as a health equity issue

ASSESSMENT:
• Can student explain the mechanism of soap (not just "it cleans")?
• Does student correctly identify the negative relationship as central to the model?
```

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 4 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 4. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format. Each item is three-dimensional, assessing a Science and Engineering Practice (SEP), Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and Crosscutting Concept (CCC) simultaneously, aligned to 5-PS1-4.

---

### Pre-Assessment Questions

### Question 1

Why do we wash our hands with soap instead of just water?

A. Soap makes water wetter
B. Soap smells better than plain water
C. Soap can break apart germs in a way that water alone cannot
D. There is no real difference between soap and water

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: That's right! Soap has a special chemical structure that lets it break apart the protective coatings on viruses and bacteria, something water alone cannot do. Soap isn't just for smell. It has a special molecular structure that can wedge into and break apart the fatty outer layer (membrane) of many viruses, actually destroying them.

---

### Question 2

What do you think happens to a virus when soap touches it?

A. The soap just washes the virus off your hands
B. The soap breaks apart the virus's protective coating, destroying it
C. The soap freezes the virus
D. Nothing happens to the virus

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct! Soap molecules actually break apart the lipid (fatty) membrane that protects many viruses, causing the virus to fall apart and be destroyed. Soap does more than rinse. Its molecules have a special shape that lets them wedge into a virus's fatty outer coating and break it apart. The virus is actually destroyed, not just rinsed away.

---

### Question 3

Why are we told to wash our hands for at least 20 seconds?

A. It takes that long for the water to warm up
B. 20 seconds is just a random number
C. Soap needs enough time to spread across hands and break apart germs
D. It takes 20 seconds for soap to dissolve

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: Good thinking! 20 seconds gives soap molecules enough time to spread to all surfaces of your hands, get into crevices, and break apart virus membranes. The 20-second rule is based on science. Soap molecules need time to spread across all hand surfaces, reach into crevices and under nails, and contact and destroy virus membranes.

---

### Question 4

Which is MORE effective for removing viruses from your hands?

A. Water only
B. Soap only (without water)
C. Soap and water together
D. They are all equally effective

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: Correct! Soap and water work together as a team. Water activates and spreads the soap, while soap does the actual work of destroying virus membranes. Soap and water are a team. Water alone can't break virus membranes. Soap alone can't spread properly without water. Together, they are far more effective than either one alone.

---

### Post-Assessment Questions

### Question 1

In the handwashing model, what type of relationship exists between Soap Effectiveness and Virus Survival Rate?

A. Positive: more effective soap means more viruses survive
B. Negative: more effective soap means fewer viruses survive
C. No relationship: soap doesn't affect viruses
D. Positive: soap helps viruses grow

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct! This is the key negative relationship in the model. More effective soap means more virus membranes are destroyed, so fewer viruses survive. That's the whole goal! Think about the purpose of handwashing. When soap is more effective at breaking apart lipid membranes, more viruses are destroyed, meaning the Virus Survival Rate goes down. This is a negative (-) relationship.

---

### Question 2

A soap molecule is called 'amphiphilic' because it:

A. Can only dissolve in water
B. Is attracted to both water AND fat/oil
C. Is made of two different colors
D. Works only on bacteria, not viruses

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: That's right! Amphiphilic means one end of the soap molecule loves water (hydrophilic) and the other end loves fat (lipophilic). This lets soap bridge between water and the fatty virus membrane. Amphiphilic means 'loving both.' One end of a soap molecule is attracted to water, and the other end is attracted to fat and oil. This dual nature lets soap wedge into fatty virus membranes.

---

### Question 3

In the simulation, what happened when Soap was at 100% but Water was at 0% (dry soap)?

A. Virus Survival Rate dropped to zero
B. Soap Effectiveness was still high because soap alone is enough
C. Soap Effectiveness was low because water is needed to activate and spread soap
D. Viruses were completely destroyed

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: Correct! Without water, soap can't spread across surfaces or activate properly. Both soap AND water are needed for maximum effectiveness against viruses. Remember the 'Dry Soap' scenario. Even with lots of soap, without water to activate and spread it, Soap Effectiveness remained low. Both components work together as a system.

---

### Question 4

Why is regular soap just as effective as antibacterial soap for destroying viruses?

A. Because viruses are easier to kill than bacteria
B. Because antibacterial chemicals target bacteria, not viruses, but regular soap breaks apart all lipid membranes
C. Because antibacterial soap doesn't really work
D. Because viruses and bacteria are the same thing

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Exactly! Antibacterial chemicals specifically target bacteria. But regular soap destroys viruses by physically breaking apart their lipid membranes, which works regardless of the type of pathogen. Antibacterial additives are designed to kill bacteria specifically. Regular soap works differently. It physically breaks apart lipid membranes, which is effective against any membrane-coated pathogen, including viruses.
