## Platform

### Title
**The Water Crisis: Who Gets the Water? — When Rain Runs Out and People Keep Moving In**

### Overview
Students model what happens when a growing community runs out of water. By building a cause-and-effect model of rainfall, population, groundwater, usage, and water availability, students discover that water crises occur when demand exceeds supply. This lesson serves as a hook for deeper inquiry into water distribution, conservation, and human impacts on Earth's resources.

### Cover Image
[A photorealistic, cinematic split image showing a lush green reservoir on one side and a dry, cracked lakebed on the other, dramatic sky with gathering clouds, ultra-crisp detail]

### Grade
5th

### NGSS Standard
**5-ESS2-2, 5-ESS3-1**: Describe and graph the amounts and percentages of water and fresh water in various reservoirs to provide evidence about the distribution of water on Earth. Obtain and combine information about ways individual communities use science ideas to protect the Earth's resources and environment.

### Learning Objectives
- Model how rainfall and population size affect groundwater and water availability
- Explain the relationship between water usage rate and groundwater depletion
- Predict what happens during a drought when population continues to grow
- Evaluate solutions for managing limited water resources

### Component List (Starting Model: 5 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Rainfall Amount | External | How much precipitation falls in the region |
| Population Size | External | The number of people living in the community |
| Groundwater Level | Internal | The amount of water stored underground in aquifers |
| Water Usage Rate | Internal | How fast the community consumes available water |
| Water Availability | Internal | Whether there is enough water to meet everyone's needs |

## Step 2: ESTABLISH — Connect the Relationships

### Text Editor

```
TIME TO DRAW THE INVISIBLE LINES

Those five components don't just sit there — they AFFECT each other.
When one changes, others change too. Let's map those connections.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Task A: OPEN CONNECTION MODE
• Click the "Connect" icon in the TOP LEFT corner of your screen

Task B: DRAW YOUR RELATIONSHIPS
• Click on "Rainfall Amount" and drag an arrow to "Groundwater Level"
• Click on "Population Size" and drag an arrow to "Water Usage Rate"
• Click on "Water Usage Rate" and drag an arrow to "Groundwater Level"
• Click on "Groundwater Level" and drag an arrow to "Water Availability"
• Click on "Population Size" and drag an arrow to "Water Availability"

Task C: SET POSITIVE OR NEGATIVE

  ○ Rainfall Amount → Groundwater Level = POSITIVE (+)
    More rainfall seeps into the ground and refills underground aquifers.

  ○ Population Size → Water Usage Rate = POSITIVE (+)
    More people means more water is used for drinking, bathing, and farming.

  ○ Water Usage Rate → Groundwater Level = NEGATIVE (−)
    Pumping water out of the ground faster depletes aquifer levels.

  ○ Groundwater Level → Water Availability = POSITIVE (+)
    Higher groundwater levels mean more water is available for the community.

  ○ Population Size → Water Availability = NEGATIVE (−)
    More people competing for the same water supply reduces availability per person.

Task D: CHECK YOUR MODEL
• You should have 5 arrows total
• 2 negative relationships, 3 positive relationships
• Notice: Population has BOTH positive and negative effects

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

See the competition? Rainfall fills the tank. Population drains it.
What happens when drainage wins?
```

### Video Script

```
"Your pieces are on the board. Time to draw the connections.

Click the 'Connect' icon. Let's start.

First: Rainfall Amount to Groundwater Level. When it rains more,
does groundwater go up or down? UP. Rain seeps into the ground
and refills aquifers. POSITIVE relationship.

Next: Population Size to Water Usage Rate. More people means
more water used. POSITIVE.

Now: Water Usage Rate to Groundwater Level. When usage goes up,
does groundwater go up or down? DOWN. We're pumping water out
faster than rain can replace it. NEGATIVE.

Connect Groundwater Level to Water Availability. More water
underground means more available. POSITIVE.

Last: Population Size to Water Availability. This one is also
NEGATIVE. More people competing for the same water means less
for each person.

Look at your model. Notice something important? Population has
TWO effects. It increases usage (positive to usage) and decreases
availability (negative to availability). Population is pulling
the system in a dangerous direction.

Meanwhile, rainfall is the only thing pushing back. It's the
only input that ADDS water.

What happens when rainfall drops and population rises at the
same time? That's the water crisis.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

## Step 3: VISUALIZE & EVALUATE — Run Your Water Crisis Model

### Text Editor

```
TIME TO SEE YOUR SYSTEM IN ACTION

You built it. You connected it. Now let's see if it actually WORKS
like the real world.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Task A: RUN THE SIMULATION — NORMAL CONDITIONS
• Set Rainfall and Population to moderate levels
• Click "Play" and watch the graphs
• What do you predict will happen to Groundwater Level?

Task B: DROUGHT + GROWTH
• Lock Rainfall to 20%
• Set Population to 80%
• What do you predict will happen to Water Availability?
• Resume and watch the crisis unfold

Task C: RECOVERY TEST
• Restore rainfall to 60% while keeping population high
• How quickly does the system recover?
• Is recovery faster or slower than the crisis was?

Task D: COMPARE YOUR RESULTS
• Which scenario had the most water?
• Which scenario caused a crisis?
• How long did recovery take compared to depletion?

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

REFLECTION QUESTIONS:
• Why are droughts most dangerous in growing communities?
• Why does the system recover slowly even when rain returns?
• What could a community do to prevent this crisis?
```

### Video Script

```
"You've built the water system. Let's test it.

Click 'Play.' Start with normal conditions — moderate rainfall,
moderate population. Watch the graphs. Groundwater stays stable.
Water Availability stays okay. The system is balanced.

Now let's create a crisis. Lock Rainfall to 20% — a drought.
Set Population to 80% — a growing town.

Watch Groundwater Level. It's dropping. Fast. Rain isn't refilling
the aquifer, but people keep pumping water out. Usage stays high
because the population is large.

And Water Availability? It's crashing. There isn't enough water
for everyone.

This is exactly what happened to Cape Town, South Africa in 2018.
They almost ran completely dry. This is happening in parts of
California right now.

Now let's test recovery. Bring rainfall back to 60%. Keep the
population high. Does the system bounce back?

Watch carefully. Groundwater starts to refill... but slowly. Very
slowly. Why? Because the large population is STILL pulling water
out. Recovery takes much longer than the crisis did.

This is the key insight. Depleting groundwater is fast. Refilling
it takes years. Maybe decades.

Your model just showed why water conservation matters BEFORE
a crisis hits.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

## Step 4: REVISE & EXTEND — Play, Research, Expand

### Text Editor

```
SET THE CONDITIONS — MAKE YOUR MODEL SMARTER

Your model works, but something isn't quite right. When Rainfall Amount
is at 100%, Groundwater Level shoots up — even when it shouldn't!

In the real world, Rainfall Amount only affects Groundwater Level when Water Usage Rate
is active. Without Water Usage Rate, Rainfall Amount alone can't change Groundwater Level.
Let's teach your model this rule.

Task A: FIND THE PROBLEM
   • Run your simulation and watch Groundwater Level
   • Notice how Rainfall Amount pushes Groundwater Level up even when
     Water Usage Rate is OFF — that's not right!

Task B: ADD THE RULE
   • Click on the arrow from Rainfall Amount to Groundwater Level
   • In the toolbar that pops up, click "Conditions"
   • A panel opens. Set: IF Water Usage Rate is ON
   • Click "Save Conditions"
   • This tells the model: "Rainfall Amount only affects Groundwater Level
     when Water Usage Rate is active"

Task C: TEST YOUR SMARTER MODEL
   • Re-run the simulation
   • Try different combinations of Rainfall Amount and Water Usage Rate
   • Does the model match the real world now?

Task D: ADD ANOTHER RULE
   • Click on the arrow from Population Size to Water Availability
   • Click "Conditions"
   • Set: IF Groundwater Level is ON
   • Click "Save Conditions"
   • Population Size only affects Water Availability when
     Groundwater Level is active

Your model is SMARTER now — it knows the rules!
```

```
LEVER FRAMEWORK:
• L: Identify all five components.
• E: Determine that Rainfall refills Groundwater (+), Population
  increases Usage (+), Usage depletes Groundwater (-), Groundwater
  supports Availability (+), and Population strains Availability (-).
• V: Build the water crisis model.
• Ev: Run Drought + Growth and Recovery scenarios.
• R: Add Desalination, Water Recycling, or Agricultural Irrigation.

SLIDE-BY-SLIDE FACILITATION:
• Slide 1 (2 min): Show before/after reservoir photos.

• Slide 5 (8 min): Sort components.
• Slide 6 (8 min): Guide all five relationships.
• Slide 7 (10 min): Run Drought + Growth scenario.
• Slide 8 (5 min): Discuss solutions.
• Slide 9 (10 min): Groups design conservation plans.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "There is plenty of water because Earth is 70% water" — 97% is
  saltwater. Only 3% is fresh, and most is frozen. Less than 1%
  is accessible.
• "Rain always refills groundwater" — The process takes years. If
  pumped faster than replaced, aquifers deplete.
• "Water crises only happen in deserts" — They can happen anywhere
  demand exceeds supply, even in rainy regions.

DIFFERENTIATION:
Support: Labeled diagrams, sentence frames, simple bar graphs
Challenge: Research local water source, add agricultural component
ELL: Visual vocabulary cards, paired work

CROSS-CURRICULAR:
• Math: Calculate school daily water usage and potential savings
• ELA: Write a news article about the Dry Creek crisis
• Social Studies: Research water rights in the western US
```

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format, aligned to 5-ESS2-2 and 5-ESS3-1.

---

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.1 (Determine components of a system) + DCI ESS2.C (Roles of Water) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A scientist is studying a town's water supply. She lists the factors: rainfall amount, population size, groundwater level, water usage rate, and water availability. She sorts them into things she can adjust (external) and things that respond (internal).

Which list correctly sorts the components?

A. External: Water Usage Rate, Population Size — Internal: Rainfall Amount, Groundwater Level, Water Availability
B. External: Rainfall Amount, Groundwater Level — Internal: Population Size, Water Usage Rate, Water Availability
C. External: Rainfall Amount, Population Size — Internal: Groundwater Level, Water Usage Rate, Water Availability
D. External: Rainfall Amount, Water Availability — Internal: Population Size, Groundwater Level, Water Usage Rate

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: Rainfall Amount and Population Size are external because they represent conditions the scientist can set. Groundwater Level, Water Usage Rate, and Water Availability are internal because they respond to the external conditions. If you chose B, Groundwater Level responds to rainfall and usage — it is not an input. If you chose A, Water Usage Rate is determined by population, not set independently. If you chose D, Water Availability is the output of the whole system, not an input.
---

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships among components) + DCI ESS2.C (Roles of Water) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

In a water system model, a student draws an arrow from Water Usage Rate to Groundwater Level and labels it negative. This means that when water usage increases, groundwater decreases. Another student says it should be positive because more usage means more water activity.

Which explanation best supports the negative relationship?

A. When water usage increases, the town builds more wells, which increases groundwater levels.
B. When people use more water, it evaporates faster, creating more rain that refills groundwater.
C. When people use more water, it flows back into the ground immediately.
D. When people pump more water from underground, the aquifer is drained faster than rain can refill it.

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: Groundwater is stored in underground aquifers. When usage increases, water is pumped out faster. If rain cannot keep up, the aquifer level drops. This is a negative relationship: more usage means less groundwater. If you chose B, used water does not simply evaporate and return as rain to the same aquifer. If you chose A, more wells would drain the aquifer faster, not increase levels. If you chose C, used water goes through treatment and sewers; it does not flow directly back into the aquifer.
---

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships) + DCI ESS3.C (Human Impacts) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A student's water model shows: rainfall refills groundwater (positive), population increases usage (positive), usage depletes groundwater (negative), groundwater supports availability (positive), and population reduces availability (negative). The student sets rainfall to 20% and population to 80%.

What would most likely happen?

A. Population would decrease because there is not enough water.
B. Groundwater would increase because the remaining rain would be enough.
C. Water Availability would increase because more people means more demand for water services.
D. Groundwater would deplete rapidly because high usage exceeds the low rainfall input, causing Water Availability to crash.

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: With only 20% rainfall, very little water is being added to the aquifer. But with 80% population, water usage is very high. The aquifer is being drained far faster than it is being refilled. As groundwater drops, water availability drops too. If you chose B, 20% rainfall is not enough to keep up with 80% population demand. If you chose C, more demand does not create more supply. If you chose A, the model does not show population responding to water levels — population is an external input set by the student.
---

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms) + DCI ESS2.C (Roles of Water) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

Scientists tracked a town's water data. In Year 1, rainfall was normal, population was 5,000, and groundwater was stable. In Year 3, population grew to 10,000, rainfall stayed the same, and groundwater started dropping. In Year 5, drought hit (50% less rain), population reached 15,000, and groundwater dropped to critical levels. In Year 7, rainfall returned to normal, population stayed at 15,000, but groundwater only recovered halfway.

Which observation best explains why groundwater recovered slowly in Year 7?

A. In Year 1, groundwater was stable because rainfall was normal.
B. Even though rainfall returned to normal, the large population continued to use water at a high rate, so the aquifer could not fully refill because output still nearly matched input.
C. In Year 5, drought caused the groundwater to drop.
D. Population grew from 5,000 to 15,000 over the period.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Groundwater recovery requires that rainfall input exceeds usage output. Even with normal rain, 15,000 people are still consuming large amounts of water. The aquifer refills slowly because the gap between input and output is small. If you chose A, this describes Year 1 but does not explain Year 7. If you chose C, this describes the crisis but not the slow recovery. If you chose D, this is true but does not explain the mechanism of slow recovery.
---

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to predict) + DCI ESS3.C (Human Impacts) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A growing town faces a potential water crisis. The model shows: rainfall refills groundwater (positive), population increases usage (positive), usage depletes groundwater (negative). The town cannot control rainfall. The mayor wants to prevent a crisis.

Which action would most effectively prevent groundwater depletion?

A. Implement water conservation measures that reduce per-person usage, such as low-flow fixtures and irrigation limits.
B. Build more wells to access deeper groundwater reserves.
C. Move the town to a location with more rainfall.
D. Wait for a wet year to refill the aquifer naturally.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: The model shows that high water usage depletes groundwater. By reducing per-person usage through conservation, total water demand decreases even as population grows. This slows depletion and keeps the system in balance. If you chose B, more wells would pump water faster, accelerating depletion. If you chose D, the model shows that recovery is slow and uncertain — waiting is not a reliable plan. If you chose C, this is impractical and does not address the systemic problem.
---

### Answer Key

Question 1: C (Cognitive Level: Identify — SEP 2.1.1, DCI ESS2.C, CCC2)
Question 2: D (Cognitive Level: Reason — SEP 2.1.2, DCI ESS2.C, CCC2)
Question 3: D (Cognitive Level: Reason — SEP 2.1.2, DCI ESS3.C, CCC2)
Question 4: B (Cognitive Level: Reason + Evidence — SEP 2.1.4, DCI ESS2.C, CCC2)
Question 5: A (Cognitive Level: Predict + Apply — SEP 2.1.4, DCI ESS3.C, CCC2)
