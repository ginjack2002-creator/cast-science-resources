## Platform

### Title
**Earth Systems Collide: Volcanoes, Climate, and Life — How a Volcanic Eruption Can Change the Whole Planet**

### Overview
Students model how a volcanic eruption in one location can change the climate on the other side of the planet. By building a cause-and-effect model of volcanic activity, atmospheric CO2, global temperature, and natural feedback loops, students discover how Earth's four spheres interact and how the planet self-regulates through negative feedback. This lesson serves as a hook for deeper inquiry into Earth systems, climate science, and the greenhouse effect.

### Grade
5th

### NGSS Standard
**5-ESS2-1**: Develop a model using an example to describe ways the geosphere, biosphere, hydrosphere, and/or atmosphere interact.

### Learning Objectives
- Model how volcanic eruptions release CO2 into the atmosphere
- Explain how increased atmospheric CO2 raises global temperature
- Demonstrate how vegetation and ocean absorption create negative feedback loops
- Predict what happens to global temperature when volcanic activity increases

### Component List (Starting Model: 5 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Volcanic Activity | External | The frequency and intensity of volcanic eruptions |
| Atmospheric CO2 | Internal | The amount of carbon dioxide gas in Earth's atmosphere |
| Global Temperature | Internal | Earth's average surface temperature |
| Ocean Absorption | Internal | The amount of CO2 absorbed by the world's oceans |
| Vegetation Growth | Internal | The amount of plant growth responding to temperature and CO2 |

```
A volcano erupts in Asia. Months later, the temperature changes

Task C: SORT YOUR COMPONENTS
• Volcanic Activity is external because it represents a geosphere
```

## Step 2: ESTABLISH — Connect the Relationships

### Text Editor

```
TIME TO DRAW THE INVISIBLE LINES

Earth's spheres don't just sit next to each other — they INTERACT.
A change in one ripples through all the others.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Task A: OPEN CONNECTION MODE
• Click the "Connect" icon in the TOP LEFT corner

Task B: DRAW YOUR RELATIONSHIPS
• Click "Volcanic Activity" → "Atmospheric CO2"
• Click "Atmospheric CO2" → "Global Temperature"
• Click "Global Temperature" → "Vegetation Growth"
• Click "Vegetation Growth" → "Atmospheric CO2"
• Click "Ocean Absorption" → "Atmospheric CO2"

Task C: SET POSITIVE OR NEGATIVE

  ○ Volcanic Activity → Atmospheric CO2 = POSITIVE (+)
    Volcanic eruptions release large amounts of CO2 into the atmosphere.

  ○ Atmospheric CO2 → Global Temperature = POSITIVE (+)
    More CO2 traps more heat, increasing Earth's average temperature.

  ○ Global Temperature → Vegetation Growth = POSITIVE (+)
    Warmer temperatures and more CO2 stimulate plant growth.

  ○ Vegetation Growth → Atmospheric CO2 = NEGATIVE (−)
    More vegetation absorbs more CO2 through photosynthesis.

  ○ Ocean Absorption → Atmospheric CO2 = NEGATIVE (−)
    Oceans absorb CO2 from the atmosphere.

Task D: CHECK YOUR MODEL
• 5 arrows total
• 2 NEGATIVE feedback arrows (vegetation and ocean back to CO2)
• These negative feedbacks are Earth FIGHTING BACK

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

The volcano pushes CO2 UP. Vegetation and oceans push it back
DOWN. Earth self-regulates. But can it handle everything?
```

### Video Script

```
"Time to connect the spheres. Click 'Connect.'

Volcanic Activity to Atmospheric CO2. Eruptions release CO2.
POSITIVE — more eruptions, more CO2.

Atmospheric CO2 to Global Temperature. CO2 is a greenhouse gas.
It traps heat. More CO2, higher temperature. POSITIVE.

Global Temperature to Vegetation Growth. Warmer temperatures
plus more CO2 help plants grow faster. POSITIVE.

Now the feedback loop: Vegetation Growth BACK to Atmospheric CO2.
When plants grow faster, they absorb MORE CO2 through photosynthesis.
That REDUCES atmospheric CO2. This is NEGATIVE.

Earth is pushing back. The volcano dumps CO2. Plants absorb it.
That's negative feedback — the system fighting against the change.

One more: Ocean Absorption to Atmospheric CO2. Oceans absorb about
30% of atmospheric CO2. That's another NEGATIVE feedback.

Look at your model. The volcano pushes CO2 up. But vegetation
and oceans push it back down. Earth has built-in stabilizers.

The question is: can these stabilizers handle a massive eruption?

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

```
You built Earth's feedback system. Now let's test whether

• Click "Play" and watch stability
• What do you predict will happen to Global Temperature?

Task B: MASSIVE ERUPTION
• Set Volcanic Activity to 90%
• What do you predict will happen to Atmospheric CO2?
• And then Global Temperature?
• Watch the cascade unfold

Task C: SELF-REGULATION TEST
• Set Volcanic Activity to 60%
• Will vegetation and ocean absorption bring the system
  back to balance?
• Watch the negative feedback loops in action

Task D: COMPARE RESULTS
• At 90%, could the feedback loops keep up?
• At 60%, did the system stabilize?
• What's the tipping point?

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

REFLECTION QUESTIONS:
• How does Earth regulate its own temperature?
• What surprised you about how vegetation responds?
• Can Earth's feedback systems handle ANY amount of CO2?
```

### Video Script

```
"Let's test Earth. First, a normal day. Low volcanic activity.
Watch the graphs. Everything is stable. CO2 is moderate.
Temperature is steady. The system is balanced.

Now the big test. Set Volcanic Activity to 90%. Massive eruption.

Watch Atmospheric CO2. It spikes. Fast. The volcano is dumping
carbon into the atmosphere.

Global Temperature follows. More CO2 traps more heat. Earth
is warming.

But now watch the feedback loops kick in. Vegetation Growth
is increasing. Why? Warmer temperatures and more CO2 help plants
grow faster. And those growing plants are absorbing CO2.

Ocean Absorption is increasing too. The oceans are soaking up
more CO2 from the air.

The negative feedbacks are fighting back. But at 90% volcanic
activity, can they keep up? Watch carefully. CO2 is still high.
Temperature is still elevated. The feedbacks help, but they
can't fully compensate for an extreme eruption.

Now try 60%. Moderate volcanic activity. Watch again. CO2 rises,
but this time the feedback loops bring it back toward balance.
Vegetation and oceans can handle this level. The system reaches
a new equilibrium.

Earth has self-regulation. But it has limits.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

## Step 4: REVISE & EXTEND — Play, Research, Expand

### Text Editor

```
SET THE CONDITIONS — MAKE YOUR MODEL SMARTER

Your model works, but something isn't quite right. When Volcanic Activity
is at 100%, Atmospheric CO2 shoots up — even when it shouldn't!

In the real world, Volcanic Activity only affects Atmospheric CO2 when Vegetation Growth
is active. Without Vegetation Growth, Volcanic Activity alone can't change Atmospheric CO2.
Let's teach your model this rule.

Task A: FIND THE PROBLEM
   • Run your simulation and watch Atmospheric CO2
   • Notice how Volcanic Activity pushes Atmospheric CO2 up even when
     Vegetation Growth is OFF — that's not right!

Task B: ADD THE RULE
   • Click on the arrow from Volcanic Activity to Atmospheric CO2
   • In the toolbar that pops up, click "Conditions"
   • A panel opens. Set: IF Vegetation Growth is ON
   • Click "Save Conditions"
   • This tells the model: "Volcanic Activity only affects Atmospheric CO2
     when Vegetation Growth is active"

Task C: TEST YOUR SMARTER MODEL
   • Re-run the simulation
   • Try different combinations of Volcanic Activity and Vegetation Growth
   • Does the model match the real world now?

Task D: ADD ANOTHER RULE
   • Click on the arrow from Vegetation Growth to Atmospheric CO2
   • Click "Conditions"
   • Set: IF Ocean Absorption is ON
   • Click "Save Conditions"
   • Vegetation Growth only affects Atmospheric CO2 when
     Ocean Absorption is active

Your model is SMARTER now — it knows the rules!
```

### Video Script

```
"Your Earth systems model shows something powerful: the planet
has built-in stabilizers. But they have limits.

First — tell the story. Brief world leaders on the eruption.
What happened to CO2? Temperature? How did Earth respond?

Second — break the system. Remove vegetation. That simulates
deforestation. Without plants absorbing CO2, what happens?
The feedback loop is broken. CO2 keeps rising with nothing to
pull it back down.

Third — add the human factor.

Human CO2 emissions. We release about 100 times more CO2 than
all volcanoes combined. Every year. Earth's feedback loops
evolved to handle volcanic CO2, which comes in bursts. But
human emissions are CONSTANT and GROWING. Can the feedback
loops keep up?

Volcanic ash. Major eruptions blast particles into the upper
atmosphere that actually BLOCK sunlight and COOL the planet
for a few years. That's the opposite of CO2 warming.

Research ONE new idea. Add it to your model. Test it.

The question your expanded model might answer: If Earth can
handle volcanoes, can it handle us?

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format, aligned to 5-ESS2-1.

---

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.1 (Determine components of a system) + DCI ESS2.A (Earth Materials and Systems) + CCC4 (Systems and System Models)

A scientist models how a volcanic eruption affects Earth's climate. The components are: volcanic activity, atmospheric CO2, global temperature, ocean absorption, and vegetation growth. She sorts them into one she can control (external) and four that respond (internal).

Which component is external?

A. Atmospheric CO2
B. Global Temperature
C. Volcanic Activity
D. Ocean Absorption

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: Volcanic Activity is the only external component because it represents the geosphere input that drives the system. All other components respond to volcanic input and to each other. If you chose A, atmospheric CO2 changes based on volcanic output and absorption — it is not set independently. If you chose B, temperature responds to CO2 levels. If you chose D, ocean absorption responds to how much CO2 is in the atmosphere.
---

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships) + DCI ESS2.A (Earth Materials and Systems) + CCC4 (Systems and System Models)

In an Earth systems model, a student draws an arrow from Vegetation Growth back to Atmospheric CO2 and labels it negative. Another student asks why growing plants would reduce CO2.

Which explanation best supports the negative relationship?

A. Plants release CO2 when they grow, adding to the atmosphere.
B. Plants absorb CO2 during photosynthesis, removing it from the atmosphere, so more plant growth means less atmospheric CO2.
C. Plants block volcanic gases from entering the atmosphere.
D. Growing plants take up space that CO2 would occupy.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: During photosynthesis, plants absorb CO2 from the atmosphere and convert it to glucose. More plant growth means more CO2 is removed from the air. This is a negative feedback loop: the system responds to increased CO2 by growing more plants, which then reduce CO2. If you chose A, plants absorb CO2 during photosynthesis, not release it (they release CO2 during respiration, but the net effect during growth is absorption). If you chose D, plants do not physically displace CO2 in this way. If you chose C, plants do not block volcanic gases.
---

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships) + DCI ESS2.A (Earth Materials and Systems) + CCC4 (Systems and System Models)

A student's Earth systems model shows: volcanic activity increases CO2 (+), CO2 increases temperature (+), temperature increases vegetation (+), vegetation decreases CO2 (-), and ocean absorption decreases CO2 (-). The student sets volcanic activity to 90%.

What would most likely happen?

A. CO2 would immediately drop because the ocean and vegetation absorb it all.
B. Temperature would decrease because volcanoes cool the planet.
C. Vegetation would decrease because volcanic eruptions destroy all plants.
D. CO2 and temperature would spike, then vegetation and oceans would partially bring CO2 back down, but the system would remain warmer than normal because the feedback loops cannot fully compensate for the extreme volcanic input.

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: At 90% volcanic activity, massive CO2 is released. Temperature rises through the greenhouse effect. Vegetation and ocean absorption increase in response (negative feedback), pulling some CO2 back down. But at this extreme level, the feedback loops cannot fully compensate, so CO2 and temperature remain elevated. If you chose A, ocean and vegetation absorption have limits — they cannot instantly absorb massive volcanic CO2. If you chose B, volcanic CO2 causes warming over time (ash can cause temporary cooling, but CO2 causes long-term warming). If you chose C, the model does not show volcanic eruptions destroying vegetation.
---

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms) + DCI ESS2.A (Earth Materials and Systems) + CCC4 (Systems and System Models)

Scientists compared two volcanic scenarios. In Scenario A (moderate eruption), CO2 rose slightly, temperature increased a small amount, vegetation grew faster, and CO2 returned to near-normal levels within a few years. In Scenario B (supervolcano eruption), CO2 spiked dramatically, temperature rose sharply, vegetation and ocean absorption increased but could not keep pace, and CO2 remained elevated for decades.

Which observation best supports the idea that Earth's feedback loops have limits?

A. Both scenarios involved volcanic eruptions.
B. In Scenario A, CO2 returned to near-normal within a few years.
C. In Scenario B, vegetation and ocean absorption increased but could not bring CO2 back to normal because the volcanic input exceeded their capacity, showing that feedback loops work but have a threshold they cannot exceed.
D. In Scenario A, vegetation grew faster after the eruption.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: Comparing the two scenarios reveals the limits of feedback loops. In Scenario A, the feedback was strong enough to restore balance. In Scenario B, the same feedback mechanisms activated but could not keep up with the massive input. This shows that negative feedback has a capacity limit — it works within a range but can be overwhelmed. If you chose D or B, these show feedback working successfully but do not demonstrate limits. If you chose A, this is true but too vague.
---

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to predict) + DCI ESS2.A (Earth Materials and Systems) + CCC4 (Systems and System Models)

A student wonders whether Earth's natural feedback loops can handle human CO2 emissions the same way they handle volcanic CO2. The model shows that vegetation and ocean absorption create negative feedback that counteracts volcanic CO2. However, human CO2 emissions are about 100 times greater than volcanic emissions and are continuous rather than occasional.

Based on the model, what would most likely happen?

A. Human CO2 emissions would overwhelm the feedback loops because the rate and amount of CO2 far exceeds what vegetation and oceans can absorb, causing CO2 and temperature to rise continuously.
B. Earth's feedback loops would easily absorb human CO2 because they evolved to handle volcanoes.
C. Human CO2 would have no effect because it is the same type of CO2 as volcanic CO2.
D. Oceans would absorb all human CO2 with no negative effects.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: Earth's feedback loops evolved to handle occasional volcanic bursts of CO2. Human emissions are far larger and continuous. The model shows that at high input levels, feedback loops cannot fully compensate. Since human CO2 dwarfs volcanic CO2 and never stops, atmospheric CO2 and temperature would continue rising. If you chose B, the feedback loops have capacity limits demonstrated in the massive eruption scenario. If you chose C, the type of CO2 is the same, but the amount and rate matter enormously. If you chose D, oceans absorb about 30% of CO2 but become more acidic in the process, harming marine life.
