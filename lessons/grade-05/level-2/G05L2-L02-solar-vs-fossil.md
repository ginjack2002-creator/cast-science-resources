## Platform

### Title
**Solar vs. Fossil: The Energy Showdown — Which Power Source Wins, and What's the Tradeoff?**

### Overview
Students model and compare how solar panels and fossil fuels produce electricity, and what each does to the environment. By building a cause-and-effect model of sunlight, fossil fuel burning, CO2 emissions, and air quality, students discover the tradeoffs between clean energy and reliable energy. This lesson serves as a hook for deeper inquiry into energy systems, human impacts, and environmental engineering.

[A photorealistic, cinematic split image showing a gleaming solar panel farm on one side and a coal power plant with smokestacks on the other, dramatic sky, ultra-crisp detail, strong contrast between clean and polluted]

### Grade
5th

### NGSS Standard
**5-PS3-1, 5-ESS3-1**: Use models to describe that energy in animals' food was once energy from the sun. Obtain and combine information about ways individual communities use science ideas to protect the Earth's resources and environment.

### Learning Objectives
- Compare how solar panels and fossil fuels produce electricity
- Model how sunlight hours and fossil fuel burn rate affect energy output and emissions
- Explain the relationship between CO2 emissions and air quality
- Evaluate the tradeoffs between solar and fossil fuel energy sources

### Component List (Starting Model: 5 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Sunlight Hours | External | The number of hours of direct sunlight per day |
| Fossil Fuel Burn Rate | External | How much coal, oil, or gas is burned for energy |
| Solar Energy Output | Internal | Electricity generated from solar panels |
| CO2 Emissions | Internal | Greenhouse gases released into the atmosphere |
| Air Quality Index | Internal | How clean and breathable the air is |

## Step 2: ESTABLISH — Connect the Relationships

### Text Editor

```
TIME TO DRAW THE INVISIBLE LINES

Those five components don't just sit there — they AFFECT each other.
When one changes, others change too. Let's map those connections.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Task A: OPEN CONNECTION MODE
• Click the "Connect" icon in the TOP LEFT corner of your screen
• Your cursor is now ready to draw relationship arrows

Task B: DRAW YOUR RELATIONSHIPS
• Click on "Sunlight Hours" and drag an arrow to "Solar Energy Output"
• Click on "Fossil Fuel Burn Rate" and drag an arrow to "CO2 Emissions"
• Click on "CO2 Emissions" and drag an arrow to "Air Quality Index"
• Click on "Solar Energy Output" and drag an arrow to "CO2 Emissions"

Task C: SET POSITIVE OR NEGATIVE
• Look at the +/- toggle in the TOP LEFT corner
• For each connection, ask: "When this goes UP, does the other go UP or DOWN?"

  ○ Sunlight Hours → Solar Energy Output = POSITIVE (+)
    More hours of sunlight means more electricity from solar panels.

  ○ Fossil Fuel Burn Rate → CO2 Emissions = POSITIVE (+)
    Burning more fossil fuel releases more CO2 into the atmosphere.

  ○ CO2 Emissions → Air Quality Index = NEGATIVE (−)
    More CO2 and pollutants in the air means worse air quality.

  ○ Solar Energy Output → CO2 Emissions = NEGATIVE (−)
    When solar provides more energy, less fossil fuel is needed, reducing emissions.

Task D: CHECK YOUR MODEL
• You should have 4 arrows total
• 2 negative relationships, 2 positive relationships
• Notice: solar energy INDIRECTLY improves air quality

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

See the competition? Fossil fuels push CO2 UP. Solar energy
pushes it DOWN. The question is: which one wins?
```

## Step 4: REVISE & EXTEND — Play, Research, Expand

### Text Editor

```
SET THE CONDITIONS — MAKE YOUR MODEL SMARTER

Your model works, but something isn't quite right. When Fossil Fuel Burn Rate
is at 100%, CO2 Emissions shoots up — even when it shouldn't!

In the real world, Fossil Fuel Burn Rate only affects CO2 Emissions when Solar Energy Output
is active. Without Solar Energy Output, Fossil Fuel Burn Rate alone can't change CO2 Emissions.
Let's teach your model this rule.

Task A: FIND THE PROBLEM
   • Run your simulation and watch CO2 Emissions
   • Notice how Fossil Fuel Burn Rate pushes CO2 Emissions up even when
     Solar Energy Output is OFF — that's not right!

Task B: ADD THE RULE
   • Click on the arrow from Fossil Fuel Burn Rate to CO2 Emissions
   • In the toolbar that pops up, click "Conditions"
   • A panel opens. Set: IF Solar Energy Output is ON
   • Click "Save Conditions"
   • This tells the model: "Fossil Fuel Burn Rate only affects CO2 Emissions
     when Solar Energy Output is active"

Task C: TEST YOUR SMARTER MODEL
   • Re-run the simulation
   • Try different combinations of Fossil Fuel Burn Rate and Solar Energy Output
   • Does the model match the real world now?

Your model is SMARTER now — it knows the rules!
```

```
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

LESSON PURPOSE:
This lesson compares two energy sources and their environmental
impacts. Students discover that the energy debate involves
tradeoffs, not simple answers.

NGSS ALIGNMENT:
5-PS3-1: Use models to describe that energy in animals' food was
once energy from the sun.
5-ESS3-1: Obtain and combine information about ways individual
communities use science ideas to protect Earth's resources.

THREE DIMENSIONS:
• Science Practice: Developing and Using Models
• DCI: ESS3.C Human Impacts on Earth Systems
• CCC: Cause and Effect

PACING GUIDE:
• Step 1 (Locate): 8-10 minutes
• Step 2 (Establish): 8-10 minutes
• Step 3 (Visualize): 10-12 minutes
• Step 4 (Extend): 10-15 minutes
• Total: 40-45 minutes

LEVER FRAMEWORK:
• L: Students identify all five system components.
• E: Students determine that Sunlight drives Solar Output (+),
  Fossil Fuels drive CO2 Emissions (+), CO2 reduces Air Quality (-),
  and Solar Output reduces emissions (-).
• V: Students build the energy comparison model.
• Ev: Students run All Solar and Cloudy Day scenarios.
• R: Students add Energy Storage or Wind Energy.

SLIDE-BY-SLIDE FACILITATION:
• Slide 1 (Cover, 2 min): Show split image. Ask students to
  identify what they see on each side.
• Slide 2 (Objectives, 2 min): Students predict which energy
  source is better.
• Slide 3 (Big Question, 2 min): Quick poll: Who thinks solar
  can replace fossil fuels completely?
• Slide 4 (LEVER, 2 min): Preview two inputs, three outputs.
• Slide 5 (Activity 1, 8 min): Sort components, discuss control.
• Slide 6 (Activity 2, 8 min): Guide relationship arrows.
• Slide 7 (Activity 3, 10 min): Run All Solar and Cloudy Day.
• Slide 8 (Discoveries, 5 min): Class discussion: What's best mix?
• Slide 9 (STEM Challenge, 10 min): Groups create energy plans.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Solar panels work at night" — Solar panels require sunlight.
  At night, stored energy from batteries or other sources must be
  used. Ask: What powers your house at 2 AM if you only have solar?
• "Fossil fuels are not related to air quality" — Burning fossil
  fuels releases CO2, sulfur dioxide, and particulate matter directly
  into the air. Show before/after photos of cities that reduced
  fossil fuel use.
• "One energy source is always better" — Both have advantages
  and disadvantages. Challenge students: Name one advantage of
  fossil fuels that solar does not have.

DIFFERENTIATION:
Support: T-chart comparing advantages/disadvantages; sentence frames
Challenge: Research state energy mix; add wind energy component
ELL: Visual vocabulary cards; paired work

CROSS-CURRICULAR:
• Math: Calculate CO2 emissions saved by replacing 50% fossil fuel
• ELA: Write a persuasive letter to the mayor
• Social Studies: Research which countries lead in solar adoption
```

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format. Each item is three-dimensional, assessing a Science and Engineering Practice (SEP), Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and Crosscutting Concept (CCC) simultaneously, aligned to 5-PS3-1 and 5-ESS3-1.

---

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.1 (Determine components of a system) + DCI ESS3.C (Human Impacts on Earth Systems) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

An engineer is studying how a city produces electricity. She lists the factors in the system: sunlight hours, fossil fuel burn rate, solar energy output, CO2 emissions, and air quality index. She needs to sort them into inputs she can change (external) and outcomes that respond (internal).

Which list correctly sorts the components?

A. External: Sunlight Hours, Solar Energy Output — Internal: Fossil Fuel Burn Rate, CO2 Emissions, Air Quality Index
B. External: CO2 Emissions, Fossil Fuel Burn Rate — Internal: Sunlight Hours, Solar Energy Output, Air Quality Index
C. External: Sunlight Hours, Air Quality Index — Internal: Fossil Fuel Burn Rate, Solar Energy Output, CO2 Emissions
D. External: Sunlight Hours, Fossil Fuel Burn Rate — Internal: Solar Energy Output, CO2 Emissions, Air Quality Index

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: Sunlight Hours and Fossil Fuel Burn Rate are external because they are inputs that can be controlled or adjusted. Solar Energy Output, CO2 Emissions, and Air Quality Index are internal because they change in response to the external inputs. If you chose A, Solar Energy Output is not an input — it is a result of how much sunlight is available. If you chose B, CO2 Emissions are an output of burning fossil fuels, not an input the engineer controls. If you chose C, Air Quality Index is a result of emissions, not something that can be set directly.
---

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships among components) + DCI ESS3.C (Human Impacts) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

In an energy model, a student draws an arrow from Solar Energy Output to CO2 Emissions and labels it as a negative relationship. This means that when solar energy output increases, CO2 emissions decrease. Another student says the arrow should be positive because more energy means more emissions.

Which explanation best supports why the relationship is negative?

A. More solar energy output means more factories can run, producing more CO2.
B. When solar energy provides more electricity, less fossil fuel needs to be burned, which means less CO2 is released.
C. Solar panels block sunlight from reaching the ground, which reduces CO2 production from soil.
D. Solar panels create CO2 when they generate electricity, so more solar means more CO2.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Solar panels generate electricity without burning anything, so they produce no CO2 during operation. When solar provides more energy, the community needs less fossil fuel, which means less CO2 is released. This is why the relationship is negative — more solar means less CO2. If you chose D, solar panels do not produce CO2 during electricity generation. If you chose C, solar panels do not affect soil CO2 production. If you chose A, the model tracks the energy source, not how the energy is used.
---

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships among components) + DCI PS3.D (Energy in Chemical Processes) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A student builds an energy model showing that sunlight drives solar output (positive), fossil fuels drive CO2 emissions (positive), CO2 reduces air quality (negative), and solar reduces the need for fossil fuels (negative). The student wants to test what happens on a cloudy day with no fossil fuel backup.

Based on the model, what would most likely happen when sunlight drops to 10% with fossil fuels at 0%?

A. Solar Energy Output would crash because there is very little sunlight to power the panels.
B. Air Quality would decrease because clouds bring pollution.
C. Fossil Fuel Burn Rate would automatically increase to compensate for low solar output.
D. CO2 Emissions would increase because clouds trap heat and produce CO2.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: Solar panels depend on sunlight to generate electricity. With only 10% sunlight and no fossil fuel backup, Solar Energy Output would be extremely low. The city would have almost no electricity. If you chose D, clouds do not produce CO2. If fossil fuels are at 0%, there is no source of CO2 emissions. If you chose B, clouds do not bring pollution — pollution comes from burning fossil fuels. If you chose C, the model does not show automatic adjustment. Fossil Fuel Burn Rate is an external input that stays where the student sets it.
---

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to explain/predict events) + DCI ESS3.C (Human Impacts) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A city tracked its energy data over four months. In January, the city used 90% fossil fuels and 10% solar, and air quality was poor. In March, they switched to 60% fossil fuels and 40% solar, and air quality improved to moderate. In June, they reached 20% fossil fuels and 80% solar, and air quality was good. In August, they went to 5% fossil fuels and 95% solar, and air quality was excellent.

Which observation best supports the claim that reducing fossil fuel use improves air quality?

A. In January, air quality was poor and the city used mostly fossil fuels.
B. In June, the city used 80% solar energy.
C. As the city shifted from 90% fossil fuels to 5% fossil fuels across four months, air quality improved consistently from poor to excellent, showing a direct cause-and-effect relationship.
D. Air quality changed across all four months.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: The data shows a clear trend: as fossil fuel use decreased (from 90% to 5%), air quality improved consistently (from poor to excellent). This pattern across four data points supports the causal claim that reducing fossil fuels reduces emissions, which improves air quality. If you chose A, one data point is not enough to establish a cause-and-effect relationship. If you chose B, this describes solar adoption but does not connect it to air quality improvement. If you chose D, this is true but too vague to explain the mechanism.

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to predict a scientific event) + DCI ESS3.C (Human Impacts) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A town currently uses 100% fossil fuels for electricity. The mayor wants to cut CO2 emissions by at least 50% without causing blackouts. The energy model shows: more sunlight means more solar output (positive), more fossil fuel burning means more CO2 (positive), more CO2 means worse air quality (negative), and more solar output means less need for fossil fuels (negative).

Based on the model, which plan would most likely achieve the mayor's goal?

A. Build more fossil fuel plants but add air filters to the smokestacks.
B. Install solar panels and eliminate all fossil fuels immediately.
C. Install solar panels to provide 60% of energy during the day, keep fossil fuels at 40% as backup, and add battery storage for nighttime.
D. Only use electricity during sunny hours and shut off power at night.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: This plan reduces fossil fuel use by 60%, which would cut CO2 emissions by more than 50%. Solar handles most daytime energy, fossil fuels provide reliable backup, and batteries store solar energy for nighttime. This balances clean energy with reliability. If you chose B, eliminating all fossil fuels immediately would cause blackouts on cloudy days and at night — the model shows solar depends on sunlight. If you chose A, adding filters does not reduce CO2 emissions from burning fuel. If you chose D, shutting off power at night is not a practical solution for a community.

Question 3: A (Cognitive Level: Reason — SEP 2.1.2, DCI PS3.D, CCC2)
