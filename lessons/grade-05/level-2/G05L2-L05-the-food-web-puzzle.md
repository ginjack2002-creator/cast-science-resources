## Platform

### Title
**The Food Web Puzzle — Remove One Piece and Watch Everything Change**

### Overview
Students model the relationships between producers, consumers, and decomposers in a food web and discover what happens when a species disappears. By building a cause-and-effect model of trophic levels and nutrient cycling, students learn that ecosystems are interconnected systems where removing one piece causes a cascade of changes. This lesson serves as a hook for deeper inquiry into ecosystems, biodiversity, and conservation.

### Cover Image
[A photorealistic, cinematic image of a vibrant forest ecosystem showing a hawk perched on a branch above a rabbit in a meadow surrounded by green plants, with mushrooms decomposing a fallen log nearby, ultra-crisp detail, golden hour lighting]

### Grade
5th

### NGSS Standard
**5-LS2-1**: Develop a model to describe the movement of matter among plants, animals, decomposers, and the environment.

### Learning Objectives
- Model the relationships between producers, consumers, and decomposers in a food web
- Explain how energy and matter flow through trophic levels
- Predict the cascading effects of removing a species from the food web
- Demonstrate how decomposers complete the nutrient cycle

### Component List (Starting Model: 5 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Producer Population | External | The number of plants and algae in the ecosystem |
| Primary Consumer Population | Internal | The number of herbivores eating the producers |
| Secondary Consumer Population | Internal | The number of predators eating the herbivores |
| Decomposer Activity | Internal | The rate at which dead organisms are broken down |
| Available Nutrients | Internal | Minerals and nutrients in the soil for producers to use |

  ○ Click "Producer Population" — the base energy input
• INTERNAL components:
  ○ Click "Primary Consumer Population" — herbivores
  ○ Click "Secondary Consumer Population" — predators
  ○ Click "Decomposer Activity" — breakdown of dead matter
  ○ Click "Available Nutrients" — soil nutrients for plants

Task B: ADD TO YOUR MODEL
• Click the PLUS (+) button to add each component
• You should now see 5 components on your canvas

Task C: SORT YOUR COMPONENTS
• Producer Population is external because it represents the base
  energy input that students can adjust. Primary Consumer Population,
  Secondary Consumer Population, Decomposer Activity, and Available
  Nutrients are internal because they respond to producer levels
  and to each other in a cascading system.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Available Nutrients — what's in the soil for plants to use.

Add them all. Sort them. And notice something — this model
has a circle. Nutrients feed producers. Producers feed consumers.
Dead consumers feed decomposers. Decomposers release nutrients.

It's a loop. And we're about to break it.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"

## Step 2: ESTABLISH — Connect the Relationships

### Text Editor

```
TIME TO DRAW THE INVISIBLE LINES

A food web isn't a line — it's a WEB of connections. Every species
affects the others. Let's map those connections.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Task A: OPEN CONNECTION MODE
• Click the "Connect" icon in the TOP LEFT corner

Task B: DRAW YOUR RELATIONSHIPS
• Click "Producer Population" → "Primary Consumer Population"
• Click "Primary Consumer Population" → "Secondary Consumer Population"
• Click "Secondary Consumer Population" → "Primary Consumer Population"
• Click "Decomposer Activity" → "Available Nutrients"
• Click "Available Nutrients" → "Producer Population"

Task C: SET POSITIVE OR NEGATIVE

  ○ Producer Population → Primary Consumer Population = POSITIVE (+)
    More plants provide more food for herbivores.

  ○ Primary Consumer → Secondary Consumer = POSITIVE (+)
    More herbivores provide more food for predators.

  ○ Secondary Consumer → Primary Consumer = NEGATIVE (−)
    More predators eat more herbivores, reducing their population.

  ○ Decomposer Activity → Available Nutrients = POSITIVE (+)
    More decomposition releases more nutrients into the soil.

  ○ Available Nutrients → Producer Population = POSITIVE (+)
    More soil nutrients help producers grow.

Task D: CHECK YOUR MODEL
• 5 arrows total
• 1 negative relationship, 4 positive relationships
• Notice the CIRCLE: nutrients → producers → consumers →
  decomposers → nutrients

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

See the loop? Matter cycles. Energy flows. And predators PROTECT
the system by keeping herbivores in check.
```

### Video Script

```
"Your pieces are on the board. Time to draw the web.

Click 'Connect.' Here we go.

Producers to Primary Consumers. More plants means more food
for herbivores. POSITIVE.

Primary Consumers to Secondary Consumers. More herbivores means
more food for predators. POSITIVE.

Now the important one: Secondary Consumers BACK to Primary
Consumers. When predator numbers go UP, do herbivore numbers
go UP or DOWN?

DOWN. Predators eat herbivores. That's NEGATIVE. This is the
control mechanism. Predators keep herbivores from overpopulating.

Decomposer Activity to Available Nutrients. When decomposers
break down more dead matter, more nutrients are released into
the soil. POSITIVE.

Available Nutrients to Producer Population. More nutrients means
more plant growth. POSITIVE. And this completes the circle.

Look at your model. It's a loop. Nutrients feed plants. Plants
feed herbivores. Herbivores feed predators. Everything that
dies feeds decomposers. Decomposers release nutrients back to
plants.

Matter cycles through the entire food web.

But what happens when you BREAK the loop? That's next.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

## Step 3: VISUALIZE & EVALUATE — Run Your Food Web Model

### Text Editor

```
TIME TO SEE YOUR SYSTEM IN ACTION

You built a food web. Now let's test what happens when we
remove pieces.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Task A: BALANCED ECOSYSTEM
• Set Producer Population to a moderate level
• Click "Play" and watch all five components
• What do you predict will happen?

Task B: REMOVE PREDATORS
• Set Secondary Consumer Population to 0%
• This simulates removing all wolves from Yellowstone
• What do you predict will happen to Primary Consumers?
• And then what happens to Producers?

Task C: REMOVE PRODUCERS
• Lock Producer Population to 0%
• This simulates destroying all plants
• What do you predict will happen to the entire food web?

Task D: COMPARE CASCADES
• Which removal was more devastating?
• Which one surprised you more?
• How long did the cascade take to reach every component?

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

REFLECTION QUESTIONS:
• Why do predators PROTECT ecosystems?
• What happens when the circle is broken?
• How are decomposers the "hidden heroes" of the food web?
```

### Video Script

```
"Time to test the food web. First, a balanced ecosystem. Set
producers to a moderate level and let it run.

Watch all five lines. Primary Consumers rise because they have
food. Secondary Consumers follow. Decomposers process dead
organisms. Nutrients cycle back to producers. Balance.

Now remove the predators. Set Secondary Consumers to zero.
This is the Yellowstone experiment.

Watch Primary Consumers. Without anything eating them, their
population EXPLODES. Way too many herbivores.

Now watch Producers. Herbivores are eating everything. Producer
Population is crashing. The plants are being destroyed.

And here's the cascade: without producers, primary consumers
eventually starve too. The whole system collapses because we
removed ONE species. That's a trophic cascade.

Now try the nuclear option. Lock Producer Population to zero.
Remove all plants.

Watch the dominoes fall. Primary Consumers starve first.
Secondary Consumers follow. Decomposers work for a while on
dead organisms, but eventually they run out too. Available
Nutrients pile up with nobody to use them.

Total ecosystem collapse.

Producers are the foundation. Predators are the regulators.
Decomposers are the recyclers. Remove any one, and the whole
puzzle falls apart.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

push native species out.

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format, aligned to 5-LS2-1.

---

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.1 (Determine components of a system) + DCI LS2.A (Interdependent Relationships) + CCC4 (Systems and System Models)

An ecologist is studying a forest food web. She lists the components: producer population, primary consumer population, secondary consumer population, decomposer activity, and available nutrients. She sorts them into what she can adjust (external) and what responds on its own (internal).

Which list correctly sorts the components?

A. External: Producer Population, Primary Consumer Population — Internal: Secondary Consumer Population, Decomposer Activity, Available Nutrients
B. External: Secondary Consumer Population — Internal: Producer Population, Primary Consumer Population, Decomposer Activity, Available Nutrients
C. External: Producer Population — Internal: Primary Consumer Population, Secondary Consumer Population, Decomposer Activity, Available Nutrients
D. External: Available Nutrients, Producer Population — Internal: Primary Consumer Population, Secondary Consumer Population, Decomposer Activity

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: Producer Population is the only external component because it represents the base energy input that the ecologist can set. All other components respond to producer levels and to each other in a cascading system. If you chose A, Primary Consumer Population responds to producer levels — it is not set independently. If you chose D, Available Nutrients are released by decomposers — they are an output, not an input. If you chose B, Secondary Consumer Population responds to prey availability — it is not set independently.
---

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships) + DCI LS2.A (Interdependent Relationships) + CCC4 (Systems and System Models)

In a food web model, a student draws an arrow from Secondary Consumer Population to Primary Consumer Population and labels it negative. Another student says it should be positive because both are consumers.

Which explanation best supports the negative relationship?

A. When secondary consumers increase, they produce more waste that helps primary consumers grow.
B. Secondary consumers eat primary consumers, so when predators increase, herbivore numbers decrease.
C. Secondary consumers and primary consumers live in different habitats.
D. Secondary consumers compete with primary consumers for the same food.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Secondary consumers are predators that eat primary consumers (herbivores). When predator numbers increase, they eat more herbivores, causing the herbivore population to decrease. This is a predator-prey relationship — a negative relationship. If you chose D, they eat different things — secondary consumers eat herbivores, not plants. If you chose C, habitat differences do not explain the negative relationship. If you chose A, waste products do not significantly benefit prey populations.
---

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships) + DCI LS2.B (Cycles of Matter) + CCC4 (Systems and System Models)

A student's food web model shows: producers feed primary consumers (+), primary consumers feed secondary consumers (+), secondary consumers reduce primary consumers (-), decomposers release nutrients (+), and nutrients support producers (+). The student removes all secondary consumers (predators).

What would most likely happen?

A. Primary consumers would explode in population, then overeat producers, causing producer population to crash.
B. Producers would increase because there are fewer animals eating them.
C. Primary consumers would decrease because they lost their food source.
D. Decomposers would stop working because there are no predators to produce waste.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: Without predators to control their numbers, herbivores multiply rapidly. The exploding herbivore population eats producers faster than they can grow, causing producer population to crash. This is a trophic cascade. If you chose C, primary consumers eat producers, not secondary consumers, so removing predators does not remove their food. If you chose B, fewer predators means MORE herbivores eating MORE producers — producers would decrease, not increase. If you chose D, decomposers break down all dead organisms, not just predator waste.
---

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms) + DCI LS2.A (Interdependent Relationships) + CCC4 (Systems and System Models)

Scientists studied a lake ecosystem over several years. In Year 1, bass (predators) were plentiful, minnow (herbivore) numbers were moderate, and algae (producers) were healthy. In Year 3, pollution killed most bass. By Year 4, minnow populations had tripled. By Year 5, algae were nearly gone because minnows had consumed most of it.

Which observation best supports the idea that predators help maintain ecosystem balance?

A. Pollution caused the bass to decline in Year 3.
B. Algae were nearly gone by Year 5.
C. In Year 1, bass were plentiful and the ecosystem was balanced.
D. After bass declined, minnows tripled and then overate the algae, showing that without predators controlling herbivore numbers, producers are devastated — exactly the pattern predicted by a trophic cascade model.

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: The sequence of events demonstrates a classic trophic cascade. Removing the top predator (bass) allowed prey (minnows) to overpopulate, which destroyed the producer base (algae). This shows predators maintain balance by controlling herbivore numbers. Without them, the whole system destabilizes. If you chose C, one time point does not explain the mechanism. If you chose A, this explains what killed bass but not how their loss affected the ecosystem. If you chose B, this is the end result but does not explain the mechanism.
---

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to predict) + DCI LS2.A (Interdependent Relationships) + CCC4 (Systems and System Models)

A lake lost its top predator (bass) due to pollution. Minnows have tripled and are eating all the algae. A conservation team wants to restore the ecosystem. The food web model shows: producers feed primary consumers (+), primary consumers feed secondary consumers (+), secondary consumers reduce primary consumers (-), and the cycle continues through decomposers and nutrients.

Which action would most effectively restore ecosystem balance?

A. Remove all minnows from the lake so algae can grow back.
B. Add more algae to the lake to feed the growing minnow population.
C. Add fertilizer to the lake to help algae grow faster.
D. Reintroduce bass (the missing predator) to control the minnow population and allow algae to recover.

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: The model shows that secondary consumers (predators) have a negative relationship with primary consumers, controlling their population. Reintroducing bass would reduce the minnow overpopulation, giving algae time to recover. This restores the natural balance. If you chose B, adding more algae would just provide more food for the overpopulated minnows — they would eat it all. If you chose A, removing all minnows would eliminate the herbivore level entirely, which is not natural balance. If you chose C, fertilizer might temporarily boost algae but does not address the root cause — minnow overpopulation.

Question 2: B (Cognitive Level: Reason — SEP 2.1.2, DCI LS2.A, CCC4)
Question 3: A (Cognitive Level: Reason — SEP 2.1.2, DCI LS2.B, CCC4)
Question 4: D (Cognitive Level: Reason + Evidence — SEP 2.1.4, DCI LS2.A, CCC4)
Question 5: D (Cognitive Level: Predict + Apply — SEP 2.1.4, DCI LS2.A, CCC4)
