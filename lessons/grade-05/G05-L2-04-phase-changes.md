## Platform

### Title
**Phase Changes: When Matter Transforms — Same Stuff, Different State, and Nothing Is Lost**

### Overview
Students model how temperature and pressure cause matter to change between solid, liquid, and gas states while total mass remains constant. By building a cause-and-effect model of phase changes, students discover the law of conservation of matter: nothing is created or destroyed during physical changes. This lesson serves as a hook for deeper inquiry into matter, molecular motion, and physical vs. chemical changes.

[A photorealistic, cinematic image showing three states of water side by side: a glistening ice cube, a splash of liquid water, and swirling steam, all against a dark background, ultra-crisp detail, dramatic lighting]

### Grade
5th

### NGSS Standard
**5-PS1-2, 5-PS1-4**: Measure and graph quantities to provide evidence that regardless of the type of change that occurs when heating, cooling, or mixing substances, the total weight of matter is conserved. Conduct an investigation to determine whether the mixing of two or more substances results in new substances.

### Learning Objectives
- Model how temperature and pressure affect the state of matter
- Demonstrate that total mass is conserved during phase changes
- Explain why matter changes states when energy is added or removed
- Predict what happens to solid, liquid, and gas amounts when conditions change

### Component List (Starting Model: 5 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Temperature | External | The amount of heat energy applied to the substance |
| Pressure | External | The atmospheric pressure pushing on the substance |
| Solid Amount | Internal | The mass of the substance in solid form |
| Liquid Amount | Internal | The mass of the substance in liquid form |
| Gas Amount | Internal | The mass of the substance in gas form |

## Step 2: ESTABLISH — Connect the Relationships

### Text Editor

```
TIME TO DRAW THE INVISIBLE LINES

Those five components don't just sit there — they AFFECT each other.
When temperature changes, matter moves between states.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Task A: OPEN CONNECTION MODE
• Click the "Connect" icon in the TOP LEFT corner

Task B: DRAW YOUR RELATIONSHIPS
• Click on "Temperature" and drag an arrow to "Solid Amount"
• Click on "Temperature" and drag an arrow to "Gas Amount"
• Click on "Pressure" and drag an arrow to "Gas Amount"
• Click on "Temperature" and drag an arrow to "Liquid Amount"

Task C: SET POSITIVE OR NEGATIVE

  ○ Temperature → Solid Amount = NEGATIVE (−)
    Increasing temperature causes solids to melt, reducing solid amount.

  ○ Temperature → Gas Amount = POSITIVE (+)
    Increasing temperature causes liquids to evaporate, increasing gas amount.

  ○ Pressure → Gas Amount = NEGATIVE (−)
    Increasing pressure compresses gas molecules back toward liquid form.

  ○ Temperature → Liquid Amount = POSITIVE/NEGATIVE (+/−)
    Moderate heat melts ice into liquid (+), but high heat evaporates
    liquid into gas (−). Liquid amount first rises, then falls.

Task D: CHECK YOUR MODEL
• You should have 4 arrows
• The key pattern: what one state LOSES, another GAINS

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Notice: Temperature drives everything. As heat goes up, matter
flows from solid → liquid → gas. But the TOTAL never changes.
```

### Video Script

```
"Your five pieces are on the board. Now let's connect them.

Click the 'Connect' icon. Here we go.

Temperature to Solid Amount. When you heat ice, does the amount
of solid go UP or DOWN? DOWN. It melts. NEGATIVE relationship.

Temperature to Gas Amount. When you heat water, does the amount
of gas go UP or DOWN? UP. It evaporates. POSITIVE relationship.

Pressure to Gas Amount. When you squeeze gas with high pressure,
does the amount of gas go UP or DOWN? DOWN. Pressure pushes
gas molecules closer together, back toward liquid. NEGATIVE.

Temperature to Liquid Amount. This one's tricky. At first,
heating INCREASES liquid — ice melts into water. But keep
heating, and liquid DECREASES — water evaporates into steam.
It goes up, then down.

Now look at the whole picture. As temperature rises, matter
flows from solid to liquid to gas. Like climbing stairs:
solid is the bottom, gas is the top. Temperature pushes
matter up the staircase.

But here's the rule: the total never changes. If solid goes
down by 10 grams, liquid goes up by 10 grams. If liquid goes
down by 5 grams, gas goes up by 5 grams. Conservation of matter.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

## Step 3: VISUALIZE & EVALUATE — Run Your Phase Change Model

### Text Editor

```
TIME TO SEE YOUR SYSTEM IN ACTION

You built it. You connected it. Now let's watch matter transform
and prove that NOTHING disappears.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Task A: HEATING FROM FROZEN
• Start with Temperature at 0% (everything is frozen solid)
• Slowly increase temperature
• What do you predict will happen to Solid Amount and Liquid Amount?
• Watch the graph carefully

Task B: BOILING POINT
• Set Temperature to 90%
• What do you predict will happen to the amount of gas?
• Watch Liquid Amount — does it go down as Gas Amount goes up?

Task C: PRESSURE SQUEEZE
• Set Pressure to 90%
• What do you predict will happen to Gas Amount?
• Watch gas compress back toward liquid

Task D: THE CONSERVATION CHECK
• At every time step, add Solid + Liquid + Gas
• Does the total EVER change?
• This is the law of conservation of matter in action

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

REFLECTION QUESTIONS:
• Did the total mass ever change during any scenario?
• Where did the water "go" when it boiled?
• How does pressure push gas back to liquid?
```

### Video Script

```
"Time to test this. Let's start frozen.

Set Temperature to 0%. Everything is solid. Solid Amount is at
100%. Liquid and Gas are at zero.

Now slowly increase temperature. Watch what happens.

Solid Amount starts dropping. And look — Liquid Amount is rising.
The ice is melting. Solid is becoming liquid. But the TOTAL hasn't
changed. Solid went down, liquid went up by the same amount.

Keep increasing temperature. At some point, Liquid Amount starts
to drop. And Gas Amount starts to rise. The liquid is evaporating
into steam. But again — Solid plus Liquid plus Gas equals the
same total it always has.

Now let's test pressure. Set Pressure to 90%. Watch Gas Amount.
It decreases. High pressure pushes gas molecules closer together,
squeezing them back toward liquid form. This is how refrigerators
and air conditioners work.

The big discovery: at every single point in this simulation, the
total mass stayed the same. Matter was never created. Matter was
never destroyed. It just moved between states.

The next time someone says water 'disappeared' when it boiled,
you can prove them wrong with science.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

### Video Script

```
"Your phase change model works. Matter is conserved. Always.
But there's more to explore.

First — tell the story. Explain to a younger student why steam
is still water. If you can explain it, you understand it.

Second — break the system. Can you reverse a phase change?
Turn everything to gas, then use pressure to push it back to
liquid. Can you freeze it again by lowering temperature?

Third — ask what's missing.

Sublimation. Some solids skip the liquid phase entirely and go
straight to gas. Dry ice does this. How would you model that?

Particle speed. In solids, molecules barely move. In gas, they
fly everywhere. Temperature controls their speed. That's the
real mechanism behind phase changes.
```

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format, aligned to 5-PS1-2 and 5-PS1-4.

---

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.1 (Determine components of a system) + DCI PS1.A (Structure and Properties of Matter) + CCC3 (Scale, Proportion, and Quantity)

A scientist is studying how water changes states. She lists the factors: temperature, pressure, solid amount, liquid amount, and gas amount. She sorts them into things she can control (external) and things that respond (internal).

Which list correctly sorts the components?

A. External: Temperature, Solid Amount — Internal: Pressure, Liquid Amount, Gas Amount
B. External: Temperature, Pressure — Internal: Solid Amount, Liquid Amount, Gas Amount
C. External: Solid Amount, Liquid Amount — Internal: Temperature, Pressure, Gas Amount
D. External: Temperature, Gas Amount — Internal: Pressure, Solid Amount, Liquid Amount

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Temperature and Pressure are external because they are conditions the scientist controls. Solid Amount, Liquid Amount, and Gas Amount are internal because they change in response to temperature and pressure. If you chose A, Solid Amount is not controllable — it responds to temperature. If you chose C, the amounts of each state are outputs, not inputs. If you chose D, Gas Amount responds to conditions — it is not an input.

---

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships) + DCI PS1.A (Properties of Matter) + CCC3 (Scale, Proportion, and Quantity)

A student builds a phase change model. She increases the temperature and observes that Solid Amount decreases while Liquid Amount increases. She labels the Temperature to Solid Amount relationship as negative.

Which explanation best supports the negative relationship?

A. When temperature increases, solid molecules break apart and are destroyed.
B. When temperature increases, solid molecules gain energy and move apart, transitioning to liquid form, reducing the amount of solid.
C. When temperature increases, the solid gets heavier and sinks below the liquid.
D. When temperature increases, solid molecules slow down and become liquid.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Increasing temperature adds energy to solid molecules, making them vibrate faster until they break free of their fixed positions and slide past each other as a liquid. The solid amount decreases because matter transitions to liquid, not because it is destroyed. If you chose A, molecules are not destroyed during phase changes — they just change arrangement. If you chose C, weight does not change during melting. If you chose D, molecules speed UP with temperature, not slow down.

---

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships) + DCI PS1.A (Properties of Matter) + CCC3 (Scale, Proportion, and Quantity)

A student's model shows: increasing temperature reduces Solid Amount (negative), increasing temperature increases Gas Amount (positive), and increasing pressure reduces Gas Amount (negative). The student heats a sealed container of ice from frozen to very hot.

Based on the model, what would most likely happen?

A. Solid Amount would decrease to zero and Gas Amount would increase, but the total mass inside the container would stay the same.
B. The total mass would decrease because some water evaporated and escaped.
C. Gas Amount would increase but Solid Amount would stay the same because ice cannot melt in a sealed container.
D. All the matter would be destroyed by the high temperature.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: As temperature increases, ice melts to liquid and liquid evaporates to gas. In a sealed container, nothing can escape, so total mass stays the same. Matter is conserved — it just changes form. If you chose B, in a sealed container, nothing escapes. If you chose C, ice melts the same way in sealed and open containers. If you chose D, matter cannot be destroyed by heating — this is the law of conservation of matter.

---

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms) + DCI PS1.A (Properties of Matter) + CCC3 (Scale, Proportion, and Quantity)

A student weighed a sealed flask of water at three different temperatures. At 0 degrees C, the water was frozen solid and the flask weighed 250 grams. At 25 degrees C, the water was liquid and the flask weighed 250 grams. At 100 degrees C, the water was steam and the flask weighed 250 grams.

Which observation best supports the law of conservation of matter?

A. The flask contained water at all three temperatures.
B. The water changed from solid to liquid to gas as temperature increased.
C. At every temperature, regardless of the state of the water, the total mass remained exactly 250 grams, proving that no matter was created or destroyed during phase changes.
D. The temperature increased from 0 to 100 degrees.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: The data shows that mass remained constant (250 grams) through two phase changes (solid to liquid, liquid to gas). This directly supports conservation of matter — the same amount of matter exists regardless of its state. If you chose A, this describes the substance but not conservation. If you chose B, this describes the phase changes but not the mass evidence. If you chose D, this describes the independent variable but not the key finding.

---

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to predict) + DCI PS1.A (Properties of Matter) + CCC3 (Scale, Proportion, and Quantity)

A younger student says "Water disappears when you boil it. The pot gets empty, so the water is gone." A 5th grader wants to design an experiment to prove the younger student wrong.

Which experiment would best demonstrate that boiled water has not disappeared?

A. Boil water in an open pot and weigh the pot before and after to show the water is gone.
B. Boil water in a sealed flask, capture all the steam inside, and weigh the flask before and after to show the mass is the same.
C. Boil water and try to see the steam with a magnifying glass.
D. Boil water and pour cold water on the steam to make rain in the kitchen.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: A sealed flask captures all the steam, preventing anything from escaping. If the mass is the same before and after boiling, it proves the water did not disappear — it just changed to an invisible gas. This is a controlled test of conservation of matter. If you chose A, an open pot lets steam escape, so the mass WILL decrease, but that does not mean matter was destroyed — it just left the pot. If you chose C, steam is often invisible — you cannot see individual water vapor molecules. If you chose D, this might show condensation but does not measure mass to prove conservation.
