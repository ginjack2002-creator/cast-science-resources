## Platform

### Title
**The Disappearing Act — Ice Melts, But the Mass Never Leaves**

### Overview
Students model the apparent "disappearing act" when ice melts — discovering that the mass doesn't vanish, it just changes form. By building a cause-and-effect model showing how temperature shifts matter between solid and liquid states while keeping total mass constant, students internalize the law of conservation of matter. The puzzle hook ("where did it go?") drives curiosity and the model provides the satisfying answer through simulation.

### Cover Image
[Side-by-side images: Left — a clear glass with an ice cube on a scale reading "200g". Right — same glass now filled with water, same scale still reading "200g." A student stares at the scale in delighted surprise.]

### Grade
5th

### NGSS Standard
**5-PS1-2**: Measure and graph quantities to provide evidence that regardless of the type of change that occurs when heating, cooling, or mixing substances, the total weight of matter is conserved.

### Learning Objectives
- Observe and explain why the total mass of a substance doesn't change when it changes state
- Model how temperature shifts matter between solid and liquid form
- Predict what happens to each state of matter as temperature changes
- Apply conservation of matter to explain everyday physical changes

### Component List (Starting Model: 4 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Air Temperature | External | The warmth or cold of the environment — students can't control weather |
| Starting Mass | External | The total amount of matter (stays constant — the key insight!) |
| Ice Amount | Internal | How much of the substance is in solid form |
| Liquid Water Amount | Internal | How much of the substance is in liquid form |

## Step 1: LOCATE — Build Your Matter-Changing System

### Text Editor

```
THE DISAPPEARING ACT

Here's a magic trick. Put an ice cube on a scale. It weighs 50 grams.
Come back an hour later. The ice is GONE. Just a puddle of water.

Now here's the question: how much does the water weigh?

If you guessed "less than 50 grams because some of it disappeared"
— you're not alone. Most people guess that.

The answer? Still exactly 50 grams. Every single time.

Where did the ice go? It didn't go anywhere. It just... changed.

  ○ Click "Starting Mass" — this is the TOTAL matter (it never changes!)
• Find the INTERNAL components (things that change because of other things):
  ○ Click "Ice Amount" — changes based on temperature
  ○ Click "Liquid Water Amount" — changes based on temperature

Task B: ADD TO YOUR MODEL
• Click the PLUS (+) button to add each component to your canvas

Task C: SORT YOUR COMPONENTS

Liquid Water Amount — internal. Also changes with temperature.
More heat = more liquid. More cold = less liquid.

The disappearing act is about to be explained.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"

## Step 2: ESTABLISH — Connect the State-Change System

### Text Editor

```
THE INVISIBLE TRANSFER

When temperature changes, ice and liquid water trade places.
But the total never changes. Let's map those connections.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Task A: OPEN CONNECTION MODE
• Click the "Connect" icon in the TOP LEFT corner

Task B: DRAW YOUR RELATIONSHIPS
• Air Temperature → Ice Amount
• Air Temperature → Liquid Water Amount
• Ice Amount → Liquid Water Amount

Task C: SET POSITIVE OR NEGATIVE
  ○ Air Temperature → Ice Amount = NEGATIVE (−)
    More heat = LESS ice (it melts away)

  ○ Air Temperature → Liquid Water Amount = POSITIVE (+)
    More heat = MORE liquid water (from melting)

  ○ Ice Amount → Liquid Water Amount = NEGATIVE (−)
    More ice = LESS liquid water
    (They're the SAME matter in two different forms — when one goes up,
    the other MUST go down)

Task D: CHECK YOUR MODEL
• 3 arrows: 2 negative, 1 positive
• Look carefully at Ice → Liquid: this is the CONSERVATION relationship!

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

KEY INSIGHT: Ice Amount and Liquid Water Amount are connected by a
NEGATIVE relationship — not because they fight each other, but because
they're the SAME matter in two different forms. They always add up to
the same Starting Mass.
```

### Video Script

```
"Your components are sorted. Now let's map the relationships that
explain the disappearing act.

Click the Connect icon.

First connection: Air Temperature to Ice Amount. When temperature
goes UP — when it gets warmer — does ice amount go UP or DOWN?

Ice melts when it gets warm. More temperature = LESS ice.
That's NEGATIVE. Click the minus sign.

Second: Air Temperature to Liquid Water Amount. When temperature
goes up, what happens to liquid water?

Ice melts INTO liquid water. More warmth = MORE liquid.
That's POSITIVE. Click the plus sign.

Third — and this is the most important one: Ice Amount to Liquid
Water Amount.

Think carefully. When there's MORE ice, is there MORE or LESS
liquid water?

Less! Because ice and liquid water are the SAME substance — just
in different forms. They're sharing the same mass. When ice amount
goes up, liquid water amount goes DOWN, because it's the same matter.

That's NEGATIVE. Click the minus sign.

Here's what your model just captured: Ice and liquid water are
connected by a negative relationship because they ARE the same
stuff. When one form increases, the other decreases. Together,
they always equal the Starting Mass.

That's conservation of matter. That's the law.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

## Step 3: VISUALIZE & EVALUATE — Run the State-Change Model

### Text Editor

```
PROVE THAT MASS IS CONSERVED

Your model connects temperature, ice, and water. Now let's run it
and verify that matter is actually conserved — that nothing disappears.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Task A: RUN THE SIMULATION
• Click "Play" in the TOP LEFT
• Watch all four component lines

Task B: OBSERVE AT NORMAL TEMPERATURE
• Let it run for about 30 time steps
• Notice the relationship between Ice Amount and Liquid Water Amount
• As one goes up, does the other go down?

Task C: SIMULATE A FREEZE
• Lock Air Temperature to LOW (10%) — freezing conditions
• Watch Ice Amount: does it go UP?
• Watch Liquid Water Amount: does it go DOWN?
• Does Starting Mass change? (It should STAY flat!)

Task D: SIMULATE SUMMER HEAT
• Unlock and set Air Temperature to HIGH (90%) — hot conditions
• Watch Ice Amount: does it go DOWN?
• Watch Liquid Water Amount: does it go UP?
• Is Starting Mass still the same flat line?

Task E: WATCH THE MIRROR
• Notice that Ice Amount and Liquid Water Amount are like MIRRORS
• When one goes up, the other goes down by the SAME amount
• Starting Mass stays constant — THAT'S conservation of matter!

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

REFLECTION QUESTIONS:
• Does it matter if matter is a solid or liquid — is the mass the same?
• Why does ice look like it's "disappearing" when it melts?
• Can you think of other examples where matter changes form but not mass?
```

### Video Script

```
"Let's run your model and watch conservation of matter in action.

Click Play. Watch the graph. In normal room-temperature conditions,
you'll see Ice Amount and Liquid Water Amount both present — some
of the substance is frozen, some is liquid.

Now notice something: Starting Mass. It's a flat line. It doesn't
move. No matter what else changes in this model, that line stays put.

Let's test it. Lock Air Temperature to 10%. Freezing cold.

Watch Ice Amount. It's climbing — more and more is freezing.
Watch Liquid Water Amount. It's dropping — the liquid is freezing.

And Starting Mass? Still flat. The matter didn't disappear.
It just moved from liquid form to solid form.

The total weight of your substance never changed.

Now let's do summer. Unlock temperature and lock it to 90%. Hot.

Ice Amount drops rapidly — it's all melting.
Liquid Water Amount climbs — the ice is becoming water.

And Starting Mass? Flat. Still the same. Always the same.

THIS is why your ice cube still weighs 50 grams when it melts.
The matter didn't go anywhere. It changed what it LOOKS LIKE.
Solid to liquid. Same mass. Always.

The magic trick isn't magic. It's conservation of matter.

Look at your model: Ice Amount and Liquid Water Amount are perfect
mirrors of each other. They trade mass between them. But the total?
Never changes.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

### Text Editor

```
SET THE CONDITIONS — MAKE YOUR MODEL SMARTER

Your model works, but something isn't quite right. When Air Temperature
is at 100%, Liquid Water Amount shoots up — even when it shouldn't!

In the real world, Air Temperature only affects Liquid Water Amount when Ice Amount
is active. Without Ice Amount, Air Temperature alone can't change Liquid Water Amount.
Let's teach your model this rule.

Task A: FIND THE PROBLEM
   • Run your simulation and watch Liquid Water Amount
   • Notice how Air Temperature pushes Liquid Water Amount up even when

Task B: ADD THE RULE

   • In the toolbar that pops up, click "Conditions"
   • A panel opens. Set: IF Ice Amount is ON
   • Click "Save Conditions"
   • This tells the model: "Air Temperature only affects Liquid Water Amount
     when Ice Amount is active"

Task C: TEST YOUR SMARTER MODEL
   • Re-run the simulation
   • Try different combinations of Air Temperature and Ice Amount

   • Chemical Changes — when wood burns, the mass becomes CO2 gas + ash (still conserved!)

### Video Script

```
"Your model proved conservation of matter with ice and liquid water.
But here's the bigger question: does this law apply everywhere? To
every kind of change?

Yes. Always. No exceptions.

When wood burns, the ash weighs much less than the original log.
Seems like matter disappeared, right? Wrong. The 'missing' mass
became carbon dioxide gas and water vapor — it floated into the
atmosphere. If you could capture all the gas from burning, you'd
get the exact same mass as the original log.

When you digest food, the food seems to 'disappear.' But your body
uses those molecules for energy, releases some as CO2 when you
breathe, and uses the rest to rebuild your cells. Nothing disappears.
It all goes somewhere.

This is why conservation of matter is called a LAW. Not a suggestion.
Not a guideline. A law. Matter doesn't appear or disappear. It changes form.

Here's your extension challenge: what happens to water BEYOND melting?

Water vapor. When liquid water gets hot enough, it evaporates —
becomes an invisible gas. Is the mass still there?

Yes! Water vapor is still H2O molecules. They're just moving too
fast to see. The mass is in the air.

Add 'Water Vapor Amount' to your model. When temperature is very
high, liquid water should decrease AND ice should be zero — because
it's all becoming vapor.

Connect the relationships. Run the simulation. Does everything
still add up to Starting Mass?

You've now modeled all three states of matter — solid, liquid, gas —
all following the same law. The law of conservation of matter is
one of the most powerful ideas in all of science.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"

### Teacher Guide

```
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
TEACHER GUIDE: The Disappearing Act
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

LESSON PURPOSE:
This lesson uses the mystery of a melting ice cube to make conservation
of matter personal and testable. By building a model where Starting Mass
never changes while Ice Amount and Liquid Water Amount trade off, students
see the law of conservation demonstrated visually in a simulation. The
"magic trick" framing creates genuine curiosity before the explanation.

NGSS ALIGNMENT:
5-PS1-2: Measure and graph quantities to provide evidence that regardless
of the type of change that occurs when heating, cooling, or mixing
substances, the total weight of matter is conserved.

PACING GUIDE:
• Step 1 (Locate): 8-10 minutes
• Step 2 (Establish): 8-10 minutes
• Step 3 (Visualize): 10-12 minutes
• Step 4 (Extend): 10-15 minutes
• Total: 40-45 minutes (or split across 2 days)

PRE-LESSON PREP:
□ Test ModelIt access on student devices
□ Optional: bring a scale, ice cube, and container — do the demo live!
□ Review vocabulary: conservation, states of matter, physical change

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Ice weighs more than water because it's solid" → Same mass, different density
• "Matter disappears when it evaporates" → Water vapor is still matter;
  it just becomes invisible gas molecules
• "Burning destroys matter" → Combustion converts solid mass into gases
  (CO2, H2O vapor) — mass is still conserved

FACILITATION TIPS:
• Step 1: If possible, actually put an ice cube on a scale at the start
  and let students predict what the mass will be after melting. The live
  demo is powerful.
• Step 2: Emphasize that the negative Ice → Liquid relationship exists
  because they're sharing the same mass, not because they're opponents.
• Step 3: Point to the flat Starting Mass line as the "proof" —
  ask students what it would mean if that line moved.
• Step 4: The burning log example is a common confusion point —
  address it directly. Where does the "missing" mass go?

DISCUSSION PROMPTS:
• "If you weigh yourself before and after breathing out, are you lighter?"
• "When food digests, where does the mass of the food go?"
• "Does the mass of a candle change as it burns? Where does it go?"
• "If you dissolve sugar in water and drink it, is the sugar 'gone'?"

EXTENSION CONNECTIONS:
• Chemical reactions unit → Conservation applies to chemical changes too
• Water cycle → Evaporation conserves mass but moves it to atmosphere
• Human body → Digestion and metabolism as matter transformation

ASSESSMENT:
• Does student correctly identify the negative relationship between
  Ice Amount and Liquid Water Amount?
• Can student explain why Starting Mass stays flat in the simulation?
• Did student apply conservation of matter to a second example beyond ice?

DIFFERENTIATION:
Support: Pre-labeled "before and after" diagram showing mass staying constant

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format. Each item is three-dimensional, assessing a Science and Engineering Practice (SEP), Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and Crosscutting Concept (CCC) simultaneously, aligned to 5-PS1-2.

---

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.1 (Determine components of a system) + DCI PS1.A.2 (Matter is conserved during physical changes) + CCC4 (Describe a system in terms of its components)

A student is building a model to show what happens when ice melts. She identifies four components: air temperature, starting mass, ice amount, and liquid water amount. She needs to sort them into external components (inputs from outside the system) and internal components (things that change within the system).

Which list correctly sorts the components of the state-change system?

A. External: Ice Amount, Liquid Water Amount — Internal: Air Temperature, Starting Mass
B. External: Starting Mass, Liquid Water Amount — Internal: Air Temperature, Ice Amount
C. External: Air Temperature, Ice Amount — Internal: Starting Mass, Liquid Water Amount
D. External: Air Temperature, Starting Mass — Internal: Ice Amount, Liquid Water Amount

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: Air temperature is determined by the environment, which the student cannot control, making it external. Starting mass is a fixed quantity set at the beginning that does not change, making it an external constant. Ice amount and liquid water amount change based on conditions within the system, making them internal. If you chose C, ice amount is not an outside input. It changes as temperature rises and the ice melts. If you chose A, air temperature comes from outside the system, not from within it. If you chose B, starting mass is a constant that does not respond to internal changes. It is set from outside the system.
---

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships among components) + DCI PS1.A.2 (Total weight of matter is conserved) + CCC4 (Describe components and interactions)

In a state-change model, a student draws an arrow from Ice Amount to Liquid Water Amount and labels it as a negative relationship. This means that when ice amount increases, liquid water amount decreases. A classmate says the arrow should be positive because melting ice creates water.

Which explanation best supports why the relationship between Ice Amount and Liquid Water Amount is negative?

A. When ice amount increases, liquid water amount decreases because they share the same total mass, and matter cannot be in two states at once.
B. When ice increases, the total mass increases too, so there is more liquid water.
C. When ice increases, liquid water increases because melting always adds new water.
D. When ice decreases, liquid water also decreases because both are being used up.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: A negative relationship means when one component goes up, the other goes down. Since the starting mass is constant (conservation of matter), ice and liquid water must always add up to the same total. If more matter is in solid form (ice), less is in liquid form. If you chose B, the total mass never increases. Conservation of matter means the total stays the same. More ice means less liquid, not more total. If you chose D, this describes both going down together, which would be a positive relationship. In the model, when one goes up, the other must go down. If you chose C, melting does not add new water. It converts existing ice into liquid. The total amount of matter stays the same.
---

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships among components) + DCI PS1.A.2 (Matter is conserved regardless of change type) + CCC4 (Describe interactions within a system)

A student builds a model of state changes. The model shows that when air temperature increases above freezing, ice amount decreases (a negative relationship). It also shows that when ice amount decreases, liquid water amount increases (a negative relationship between ice and liquid means they move in opposite directions). The student wants to test conservation of matter, so she raises the temperature until all the ice melts.

Based on the model, what would the graph show after all ice has melted?

A. Starting mass would decrease because the ice is gone.
B. Liquid water amount would be greater than starting mass because heat added extra mass.
C. Liquid water amount would equal the starting mass, and ice amount would be zero, while starting mass stays flat.
D. Both ice amount and liquid water amount would be zero because the matter disappeared.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: The model demonstrates conservation of matter. When all ice melts, every gram of ice becomes a gram of liquid water. Ice amount drops to zero, liquid water amount rises to equal the starting mass, and the starting mass line remains perfectly flat throughout. The total never changes. If you chose A, starting mass never decreases. It is constant because matter cannot be created or destroyed. The ice did not vanish; it changed form. If you chose D, matter does not disappear when it changes state. The ice molecules are now liquid water molecules. If you chose B, heat is energy, not matter. Adding heat changes the state of the matter but does not add mass to the system.
---

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to explain/predict events) + DCI PS1.A.2 (Weight is conserved during heating, cooling, or mixing) + CCC4 (Describe components and interactions)

A student placed 150 grams of ice in an open cup on a warm day. After two hours, only 120 grams of liquid water remained in the cup. Another student said that 30 grams of matter must have been destroyed. A third student disagreed and said conservation of matter still applies.

Which observation best supports the idea that conservation of matter still applies even though mass in the cup decreased?

A. 30 grams of water evaporated into the air as water vapor. The cup is an open system, so matter left but was not destroyed.
B. The ice melted, which always reduces mass because liquid weighs less than ice.
C. The cup absorbed 30 grams of water, which is now inside the cup material.
D. 30 grams of matter turned into heat energy when the ice melted.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: Conservation of matter means matter is never created or destroyed. In an open cup, water can escape. The 30 grams evaporated into the air as invisible water vapor. If you could capture and weigh the vapor, it would account for exactly 30 grams. The total matter in the universe did not change. If you chose B, liquid water does not weigh less than ice. The same amount of matter in solid or liquid form has the same mass. If you chose C, cups do not absorb significant amounts of water. The missing mass left through evaporation, not absorption. If you chose D, matter cannot turn into heat energy. Heat is energy, not matter. The 30 grams changed form (liquid to gas) but still exists as water vapor in the air.
---

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to predict a scientific event) + DCI PS1.A.2 (Total weight of matter is conserved) + CCC4 (Describe system components and interactions)

A teacher asks students to design an experiment that proves conservation of matter during a state change. The model shows that starting mass stays constant as ice melts into liquid water. The teacher says that in a previous class, students heated ice in an open beaker and the mass decreased, which confused them.

Based on the model, which experimental setup would best prove that total mass stays the same during melting?

A. Melt ice and then pour the water into a different container to see if the mass changes.
B. Place ice in a sealed, airtight container and weigh it before and after the ice fully melts.
C. Heat ice in an open beaker and weigh it every five minutes.
D. Heat ice very quickly so the water does not have time to evaporate.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: A sealed container prevents any matter from escaping. When ice melts inside a closed system, all the water molecules stay inside. Weighing before and after will show the exact same mass, proving conservation of matter. If you chose C, an open beaker allows water vapor to escape, which makes it look like mass decreased. This would not prove conservation because the system is open. If you chose A, pouring water between containers does not test conservation of matter during a state change. It only moves water around and could spill. If you chose D, heating quickly does not prevent evaporation. Some water will still escape as vapor, reducing the measured mass and confusing the results.

Question 3: C (Cognitive Level: Reason — SEP 2.1.2, DCI PS1.A.2, CCC4)
