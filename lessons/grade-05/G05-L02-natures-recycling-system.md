## Platform

### Title
**Nature's Recycling System — Why Earth Doesn't Fill Up with Dead Stuff**

### Overview
Students model Earth's decomposition cycle — the invisible system that breaks down dead leaves, fallen trees, and animal remains and returns their nutrients to living things. By building a cause-and-effect model of decomposer activity, students discover that nature runs the world's most efficient recycling operation, and what happens when the system slows down. This lesson anchors the matter cycling standard in a funny, surprising question that Gen Alpha students immediately want to answer.

### Cover Image
[Split image: Enormous pile of dead leaves on left with an arrow pointing right to rich, dark soil teeming with earthworms — before/after of decomposition]

### Grade
5th

### NGSS Standard
**5-LS2-1**: Develop a model to describe the movement of matter among plants, animals, decomposers, and the environment.

### Learning Objectives
- Identify decomposers as a key component of matter cycling in ecosystems
- Model how soil moisture and organic matter input affect decomposer activity
- Trace how dead matter is converted into nutrients available for living plants
- Predict what happens to an ecosystem when decomposer activity slows down

### Component List (Starting Model: 4 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Dead Organic Matter | External | Leaves, fallen trees, dead animals — inputs students can't control |
| Soil Moisture | External | Rainfall and water content that enables decomposer survival |
| Decomposer Activity | Internal | Bacteria, fungi, and worms actively breaking down dead material |
| Nutrient Release | Internal | Nitrogen, phosphorus, and carbon returning to soil for plants to use |

### Text Editor

```
WIRING THE RECYCLING MACHINE

Dead stuff goes IN. Nutrients come OUT. But what happens in between?

That's where the decomposers come in — and your model is about to

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Task A: OPEN CONNECTION MODE
• Click the "Connect" icon in the TOP LEFT corner
• Your cursor is now ready to draw relationship arrows

Task B: DRAW YOUR RELATIONSHIPS
• Dead Organic Matter → Decomposer Activity
• Soil Moisture → Decomposer Activity
• Decomposer Activity → Nutrient Release

Task C: SET POSITIVE OR NEGATIVE
• For each arrow, ask: "When this goes UP, does the other go UP or DOWN?"

  ○ Dead Organic Matter → Decomposer Activity = POSITIVE (+)
    More dead stuff = MORE food for decomposers = more decomposers

  ○ Soil Moisture → Decomposer Activity = POSITIVE (+)
    Wetter soil = MORE decomposer activity (they need water to survive)

  ○ Decomposer Activity → Nutrient Release = POSITIVE (+)
    More decomposer work = MORE nutrients released to the soil

Task D: CHECK YOUR MODEL
• You should have 3 arrows total
• All 3 are POSITIVE relationships
• This is your recycling chain!

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Notice: there are no negative relationships here. When conditions
are right, EVERYTHING goes up together. That's a healthy ecosystem.
But what happens when one component crashes?
```

## Step 3: VISUALIZE & EVALUATE — Run the Recycling System

### Text Editor

```
LET'S SEE NATURE'S RECYCLER IN ACTION

You built it. You connected it. Now let's test whether your model
works like Earth's actual decomposition system.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Task A: RUN THE SIMULATION
• Click the "Play" button in the TOP LEFT
• Watch the graph panel — you'll see lines for each component

Task B: OBSERVE THE HEALTHY ECOSYSTEM
• Let it run for about 30 time steps
• When Dead Organic Matter is HIGH, what happens to Decomposer Activity?
• When Decomposer Activity is HIGH, what happens to Nutrient Release?

Task C: SIMULATE A DROUGHT
• Find the LOCK icon next to "Soil Moisture"
• Set Soil Moisture to 0% (drought conditions)
• What happens to Decomposer Activity?
• What happens to Nutrient Release?
• What happens to Dead Organic Matter over time? (hint: it starts piling up)

Task D: BRING BACK THE RAIN
• Unlock Soil Moisture
• Watch how fast the system recovers
• Can you see the decomposers "catching up" on all that piled-up matter?

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

REFLECTION QUESTIONS:
• What happens to a forest ecosystem when decomposers slow down?
• If nutrients stop being released, what happens to living plants?
• Why do forests on moist tropical islands have almost no dead material on the ground?
```

### Video Script

```
"Your recycling machine is built. Let's flip it on.

Click the Play button. Watch the graph. When Dead Organic Matter is
high and Soil Moisture is present, watch what happens to Decomposer

The recycling chain is running smoothly.

This is what a healthy forest floor looks like. Dead leaves don't pile up

And look at what's happening to Dead Organic Matter. It's piling
up. Because the recyclers aren't working.

And Nutrient Release? Crashing. Plants can't get nutrients if
decomposers aren't releasing them.

This is what happens in desert ecosystems. Dead material accumulates
because there's not enough moisture to keep decomposers active.

Now unlock Soil Moisture. Bring the rain back. Watch the system
recover — the decomposers come back to life and start chewing
through that backed-up pile of dead matter.

This is Earth's recycling system. And it runs on something as
simple as moisture.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

```
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

LESSON PURPOSE:
This lesson makes decomposition viscerally real by starting with a
surprising, slightly funny question: why doesn't Earth fill up with
dead stuff? Students model the conditions that drive decomposer
activity and discover how matter cycles through ecosystems. The
lesson builds scientific modeling skills while anchoring the
5-LS2-1 standard in an accessible, engaging phenomenon.

NGSS ALIGNMENT:
5-LS2-1: Develop a model to describe the movement of matter among
plants, animals, decomposers, and the environment.

PACING GUIDE:
• Step 1 (Locate): 8-10 minutes
• Step 2 (Establish): 8-10 minutes
• Step 3 (Visualize): 10-12 minutes
• Step 4 (Extend): 10-15 minutes
• Total: 40-45 minutes (or split across 2 days)

PRE-LESSON PREP:
□ Test ModelIt access on student devices
□ Optional: bring in a handful of compost or dark soil to show
□ Review vocabulary: decomposer, nutrient, organic matter, moisture

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Decomposers only work on dead animals" → Decomposers break down
  ALL organic material: leaves, wood, food waste, dead plants
• "Dirt is just dirt" → Healthy soil is a living ecosystem with
  billions of organisms per teaspoon
• "Decomposition only happens underground" → Decomposers are also in
  water, on surfaces, even inside other organisms

FACILITATION TIPS:
• Step 1: Ask students "When was the last time you saw a pile of
  dead leaves still there years later?" This prompts them to recognize
  that dead matter disappears — creating the authentic question.
• Step 2: Use the analogy of an assembly line — dead matter is
  the raw material, moisture is the power, decomposers are the workers,
  nutrients are the finished product.
• Step 3: The drought scenario is the key insight moment. Let
  students observe the accumulation of dead matter silently before
  asking what they notice.
• Step 4: If students ask about plastic not decomposing, validate
  it — that's a real and important extension connecting to the pollution unit.

DISCUSSION PROMPTS:
• "Why do tropical rainforests have almost no dead leaves on the ground?"
• "What would happen to plant life if all decomposers disappeared overnight?"
• "How is your backyard compost bin a controlled version of this model?"
• "If climate change warms the soil, how might that affect the recycling system?"

EXTENSION CONNECTIONS:
• Human impact unit → Decomposers can't break down plastic; landfills accumulate
• Water cycle → Moisture drives decomposition rates; drought slows recycling
• Soil health and food production → Nutrients from decomposition feed crops

ASSESSMENT:
• Can student explain the cause-effect chain from dead matter to nutrient release?
• Does student correctly identify all positive relationships in the model?
• Did student identify a meaningful gap in the model and add a researched component?

DIFFERENTIATION:
Support: Provide sentence frames — "When __ goes up, __ goes __ because..."
Challenge: Add temperature and earthworms; calculate decomposition rate changes
ELL: Provide bilingual vocabulary cards; use the visual graph for explanation
```

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format. Each item is three-dimensional, assessing a Science and Engineering Practice (SEP), Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and Crosscutting Concept (CCC) simultaneously, aligned to 5-LS2-1.

---

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.1 (Determine components of a system) + DCI LS2.1 + CCC4 (Systems and System Models)

A student is using the ModelIt platform to study the system in this lesson. The model includes these components: Dead Organic Matter, Soil Moisture, Decomposer Activity, Nutrient Release. Some components are external (Dead Organic Matter, Soil Moisture) and some are internal (Decomposer Activity, Nutrient Release). The student needs to understand what each component represents and how they are organized.

In the decomposition model, what happens when Soil Moisture drops to 0%?

A. Decomposer Activity increases because matter dries out faster
B. Nutrient Release increases because dry soil has more nutrients
C. Decomposer Activity drops and dead organic matter piles up
D. Nothing changes because decomposers don't need water

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: Correct! Without moisture, decomposers can't function. Dead organic matter accumulates because it isn't being broken down. If you chose A, this answer suggests something is being added to the system. Look carefully at the model — the total amount stays the same even when components change. The system is conserving matter or energy. If you chose B, this answer suggests something is being added to the system. Look carefully at the model — the total amount stays the same even when components change. The system is conserving matter or energy. If you chose D, this reflects a common misconception. Matter cannot be created or destroyed — it can only change form. The total amount of matter in the system stays the same.

### Question 2

A. Negative: more dead matter means less decomposer activity
B. Positive: more dead matter gives decomposers more food to process

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: That's right! More dead organic matter provides more food for decomposers, increasing their activity. This is a positive relationship.
