## Platform

### Title
**Why Does Ice Cream Melt Faster on a Hot Day? — How Heat Energy Transfers from the Environment to Frozen Treats**

### Overview
Ice cream melts faster on a hot day because the greater difference between the air temperature and the ice cream temperature causes heat energy to transfer more quickly. The heat flows from the warm air into the cold ice cream, raising its temperature until it changes from solid to liquid.... Students build a cause-and-effect model exploring how air temperature and container insulation interact with heat transfer rate, melting speed, and ice cream state in this system.

[A photorealistic, cinematic image of a scoop of ice cream melting on a cone in bright summer sunlight, with visible heat waves and dripping liquid, vibrant colors, ultra-crisp detail]

### NGSS Standard
**5-PS1-1, 5-PS1-3**: Develop a model to describe that matter is made of particles too small to be seen. Make observations and measurements to identify materials based on their properties, including thermal conductivity and insulation.

### Learning Objectives
- Model how air temperature and insulation affect the rate of heat transfer
- Explain how heat energy moves from warmer surroundings to colder objects
- Predict how changing container insulation affects melting speed
- Connect particle motion to temperature and state changes

### Component List (Starting Model: 5 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Air Temperature | External | The temperature of the surrounding environment |
| Container Insulation | External | How well the container blocks heat from reaching the ice cream |
| Heat Transfer Rate | Internal | How quickly thermal energy moves from the air to the ice cream |
| Melting Speed | Internal | How fast the ice cream changes from solid to liquid |
| Ice Cream State | Internal | The current condition of the ice cream — fully frozen, slushy, or melted |

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format. Each item is three-dimensional, assessing a Science and Engineering Practice (SEP), Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and Crosscutting Concept (CCC) simultaneously, aligned to 5-PS1-1, 5-PS1-3.

---

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.1 (Determine components of a system) + DCI + CCC4 (Describe a system in terms of its components)

A scientist is studying why does ice cream melt faster on a hot day?. She identifies these components: Air Temperature, Container Insulation, Heat Transfer Rate, Melting Speed, Ice Cream State. She sorts them into external (things we set or cannot control) and internal (things that change based on other factors).

Which list correctly sorts the components?

A. External: Air Temperature, Heat Transfer Rate — Internal: Container Insulation, Melting Speed, Ice Cream State
B. External: Air Temperature, Container Insulation — Internal: Heat Transfer Rate, Melting Speed, Ice Cream State
C. External: Heat Transfer Rate, Melting Speed — Internal: Air Temperature, Container Insulation, Ice Cream State
D. External: Ice Cream State, Air Temperature — Internal: Container Insulation, Heat Transfer Rate, Melting Speed

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Air Temperature and Container Insulation are external because they represent conditions that drive the system. Heat Transfer Rate, Melting Speed, and Ice Cream State are internal because they change based on other factors in the system.

---

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships among components) + DCI + CCC4 (Describe components and interactions)

Two identical ice cream scoops are placed outside on a 95°F day. One is in a foam container and one is on a plate. Which melts first and why?

A. Air Temperature has no effect on Heat Transfer Rate
B. As Air Temperature increases, Heat Transfer Rate increases
C. As Air Temperature decreases, Heat Transfer Rate increases
D. Heat Transfer Rate controls Air Temperature, not the other way around

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Higher air temperature creates a larger temperature difference, driving faster heat transfer.

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to explain/predict events) + DCI + CCC4 (Describe components and interactions)

Scientists studying why does ice cream melt faster on a hot day? collected data across multiple scenarios. In the first scenario, Set Air Temperature to 90% and Container Insulation to 10%. In the second scenario, Set Air Temperature to 90% and Container Insulation to 90%.

Which observation best supports the model's predictions?

A. The components in both scenarios showed identical results.
B. The first scenario showed higher Heat Transfer Rate values than the second scenario.
C. External components changed in response to internal components.
D. No pattern was observed between the scenarios.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: The model predicts that different external conditions produce different internal responses. Heat always flows from warmer surroundings to cooler objects — never the other way

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.5 (Use a model to make predictions) + DCI + CCC4 (Use a model to predict)

A student wants to use the why does ice cream melt faster on a hot day? model to make predictions about a new situation. Based on the model, if Air Temperature is set to an extreme high value, what would the model predict?

A. Heat Transfer Rate would increase significantly, and this would cascade to affect Ice Cream State
B. Only Air Temperature would change; internal components would not respond
C. Ice Cream State would change first, before Heat Transfer Rate
D. The model cannot make predictions about extreme values

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: The model shows clear cause-and-effect chains. Extreme values in external components produce amplified responses in internal components. Melting speed depends on BOTH air temperature and insulation working together
