### Title
**Building a Star on Earth: The Fusion Energy Challenge — Modeling the Engineering Extremes of Controlled Nuclear Fusion**

### Overview
The Sun converts 600 million tons of hydrogen into helium every second, releasing energy equivalent to 10 billion nuclear bombs. This process — nuclear fusion — has powered every star in the universe for billions of years. Recreating it on Earth would provide virtually unlimited clean energy. But building a star is, unsurprisingly, extraordinarily difficult. Students investigate the driving question: Why have scientists spent 70 years and billions of dollars trying to recreate what the Sun does — and why haven't they succeeded yet? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

### NGSS Standard
**HS-ESS1-1, HS-PS3-5**: Develop a model based on evidence to illustrate the life span of the sun and the role of nuclear fusion in the sun's core to release energy. Plan and conduct an investigation to provide evidence that an electric current can produce a magnetic field and that a changing magnetic field can produce an electric current.

### Learning Objectives
- Model how nine interdependent variables must be simultaneously controlled to achieve sustained nuclear fusion
- Analyze the engineering trade-offs between plasma temperature, magnetic containment, and material survival in a fusion reactor
- Evaluate why achieving net energy gain from fusion remains one of the hardest engineering challenges in history
- Predict the conditions under which a fusion reactor transitions from energy consumer to energy producer

### Component List (Starting Model: 9 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Plasma Temperature | External | The core temperature of the fusion fuel, which must reach 150+ million degrees Celsius for deuterium-tritium fusion — ten times hotter than the center of the Sun |
| Magnetic Containment | External | The strength and stability of the magnetic field confining the plasma, measured in Tesla — stronger fields keep plasma denser and more stable but require enormous superconducting magnets |
| Fuel Injection Rate | External | The rate at which deuterium and tritium fuel is introduced into the plasma, which must balance burn rate to maintain steady-state fusion conditions |
| Energy Output | Internal | The total thermal energy produced by fusion reactions per second, determined by plasma density, temperature, and confinement time |
| Neutron Damage | Internal | The degradation of reactor wall materials caused by high-energy neutrons escaping the plasma — each fusion reaction produces a 14.1 MeV neutron that damages structural materials |
| Cooling System | Internal | The heat removal system that captures thermal energy from the reactor blanket and transfers it to power generation while preventing structural overheating |
| Power Conversion | Internal | The efficiency of converting captured thermal energy into electricity through steam turbines or other conversion systems |
| Materials Degradation | Internal | The cumulative structural damage to reactor components from neutron bombardment, thermal cycling, and plasma disruption events over operational lifetime |
| Net Energy Gain | Internal | The ratio of energy output to energy input (Q factor) — the ultimate measure of whether the reactor produces usable power or is an expensive science experiment |

```
Task B: DRAW YOUR RELATIONSHIPS
• Click on "Plasma Temperature" and drag an arrow to "Energy Output"
• Click on "Energy Output" and drag an arrow to "Neutron Damage"
• Click on "Neutron Damage" and drag an arrow to "Materials Degradation"

Task C: SET POSITIVE OR NEGATIVE
• Look at the +/− toggle in the TOP LEFT corner
• For each connection, ask: "When this goes UP, does the other go UP or DOWN?"

  ○ Plasma Temperature → Energy Output = POSITIVE (+)
    Higher plasma temperature increases the rate of fusion reactions exponentially (following the fusion cross-section curve), directly increasing energy output. The reaction rate scales strongly with temperature above the ignition threshold.

  ○ Energy Output → Neutron Damage = POSITIVE (+)
    Higher energy output means more fusion reactions per second, and each reaction produces a 14.1 MeV neutron. More fusion events mean more neutrons bombarding reactor walls, accelerating structural damage to the first wall and blanket materials.

  ○ Neutron Damage → Materials Degradation = POSITIVE (+)
    Accumulated neutron damage displaces atoms from their crystal lattice positions, creating voids, helium bubbles, and transmutation products. Over time, this makes structural materials brittle, swollen, and prone to cracking, ultimately limiting reactor operational lifetime.

Task D: CHECK YOUR MODEL
• You should have 3 arrows total
• 0 negative relationship(s), 3 positive relationship(s)
• This is your system model!

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
```

```
WHAT DID YOU DISCOVER?
• Fusion requires simultaneously achieving extreme temperature, density, and confinement time — the Lawson criterion — and falling short on any single parameter means zero net energy
• Every fusion reaction that produces energy also produces a high-energy neutron that damages the reactor, creating a fundamental tension between output and longevity
• Magnetic containment is inherently unstable — plasma turbulence constantly tries to escape confinement, requiring real-time feedback control systems adjusting thousands of times per second
• Net energy gain requires not just producing fusion energy but producing ENOUGH to overcome the enormous energy cost of heating plasma, powering magnets, and running cooling systems

THE ANSWER: Fusion has taken 70 years because it requires simultaneously solving problems at the extremes of physics and engineering. The plasma must reach 150 million degrees — but no material can survive contact with it, so it must be suspended by magnetic fields. The magnets must be incredibly powerful — but they require supercooling to near absolute zero, just meters from the hottest material on Earth. Every fusion reaction produces energy — but also a neutron that damages the reactor walls. And ALL the energy consumed by heating, containment, cooling, and control must be exceeded by the energy produced, or the reactor is just an extremely expensive heater. The Q factor captures this: we need Q significantly greater than 1, and we only recently achieved Q greater than 1 for brief moments.
```

```
BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• How Fusion Works:
  Nuclear fusion occurs when light atomic nuclei combine to form heavier ones, releasing energy from the strong nuclear force. The most achievable reaction combines deuterium (hydrogen with one neutron) and tritium (hydrogen with two neutrons) to produce helium-4, a high-energy neutron, and 17.6 MeV of energy. But nuclei are positively charged and repel each other — overcoming this Coulomb barrier requires temperatures exceeding 150 million degrees Celsius, ten times hotter than the Sun's core. At these temperatures, matter exists as plasma.

• The Confinement Problem:
  No physical container can hold 150-million-degree plasma — it would vaporize instantly. Instead, fusion reactors use magnetic confinement: powerful superconducting magnets create a magnetic bottle that suspends the plasma in a vacuum. The most common design is the tokamak, a donut-shaped chamber where magnetic field lines spiral around the plasma. But plasma is inherently unstable — turbulence, instabilities, and edge effects constantly try to break confinement, requiring real-time feedback control systems.

• The Materials Crisis:
  Every deuterium-tritium fusion reaction produces a 14.1 MeV neutron that escapes the magnetic field (neutrons are uncharged). These neutrons slam into reactor wall materials, displacing atoms from their crystal lattice in a process called radiation damage. Over years of operation, structural materials become brittle, swollen, and radioactive. No existing material can withstand the neutron flux of a commercial fusion reactor for its intended 30-year lifetime. This materials challenge is arguably the biggest unsolved problem in fusion engineering.

• The Race to Net Energy:
  In December 2022, the National Ignition Facility achieved fusion ignition for the first time — producing more energy from fusion than the laser energy used to trigger it (Q greater than 1). But this was using inertial confinement (lasers), not magnetic confinement, and the total facility energy consumption was far higher than the fusion output. ITER, the international tokamak being built in France, aims to achieve Q=10 by the early 2030s. Private companies like Commonwealth Fusion Systems and Helion Energy are pursuing faster timelines with innovative designs. The race to commercial fusion is accelerating.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Fusion reactors can explode like nuclear bombs"
  Reality: A fusion reactor cannot explode like a thermonuclear weapon. Weapons require precisely shaped implosion to compress fuel to extreme density in nanoseconds — conditions impossible to achieve accidentally. If a fusion reactor loses containment, the plasma cools in milliseconds and fusion stops. The total fuel in a reactor at any moment is about one gram — there is no chain reaction to sustain. A fusion failure is a very expensive equipment malfunction, not a nuclear explosion.
  Strategy: Thought experiment: A nuclear bomb requires compressing fuel to stellar core density in nanoseconds using precisely shaped explosives. What would happen if you just let one gram of fusion fuel out of a magnetic bottle? It would cool to room temperature in milliseconds — no explosion, just expensive damage to the reactor wall.

• "We just need hotter plasma to make fusion work"
  Reality: Temperature is necessary but not sufficient. The Lawson criterion requires high temperature AND high density AND long confinement time — simultaneously. Many experiments achieve extreme temperatures but cannot maintain confinement long enough or at sufficient density. It is like saying 'we just need more flour to make bread' — you also need water, yeast, heat, and time, all in the right proportions.
  Strategy: Use the model: Push Plasma Temperature to maximum while leaving other variables at current levels. Does Net Energy Gain reach commercial levels? Why not?

• "Fusion produces no radioactive waste"
  Reality: Fusion produces far less radioactive waste than fission and no long-lived actinides, but it is not waste-free. The 14.1 MeV neutrons from fusion reactions activate structural materials through neutron capture, creating radioactive isotopes. These materials must be stored for 50-100 years before they decay to safe levels — much shorter than fission waste's thousands of years, but not zero. Tritium is also radioactive with a 12.3-year half-life and must be carefully contained.
  Strategy: Compare: Fission waste remains dangerous for 100,000+ years. Fusion-activated materials decay to safe levels in 50-100 years. Neither is zero, but the difference is enormous. Graph the decay curves side by side.

ANSWER KEY:
Big Question: Why have scientists spent 70 years and billions of dollars trying to recreate what the Sun does — and why haven't they succeeded yet?
Answer: Fusion has taken 70 years because it requires simultaneously solving problems at the extremes of physics and engineering. The plasma must reach 150 million degrees — but no material can survive contact with it, so it must be suspended by magnetic fields. The magnets must be incredibly powerful — but they require supercooling to near absolute zero, just meters from the hottest material on Earth. Every fusion reaction produces energy — but also a neutron that damages the reactor walls. And ALL the energy consumed by heating, containment, cooling, and control must be exceeded by the energy produced, or the reactor is just an extremely expensive heater. The Q factor captures this: we need Q significantly greater than 1, and we only recently achieved Q greater than 1 for brief moments.

Simulation Answers:
• ITER Baseline Scenario: At 150 million degrees Celsius with 5.3 Tesla magnetic containment and moderate fuel injection, the model predicts significant Energy Output with Q approaching 10. Neutron Damage accumulates at a manageable rate, Materials Degradation remains within design limits for the planned operational period, and the Cooling System effectively captures thermal energy for Power Conversion. This scenario demonstrates why ITER is designed at these specific parameters — they represent the sweet spot between energy production and system longevity.
• Ignition Attempt Scenario: Pushing Plasma Temperature to maximum and increasing Fuel Injection Rate dramatically increases Energy Output and pushes Net Energy Gain higher. However, the exponential increase in fusion reactions also exponentially increases Neutron Damage. Materials Degradation accelerates to rates that would destroy the reactor wall within months rather than decades. The Cooling System approaches maximum capacity. The model reveals the fundamental trade-off: maximum energy extraction and maximum reactor lifetime are competing objectives that must be balanced.

Reflection Exemplars:
• Q: Why is the output-damage coupling the central challenge?
  A: Every fusion reaction produces both useful energy (17.6 MeV total) and a destructive neutron (14.1 MeV). You cannot have one without the other — they are products of the same nuclear reaction. This means maximizing energy output inherently maximizes reactor damage. Engineers must find materials that can withstand decades of neutron bombardment, or design reactors that can be economically rebuilt when materials fail. The model shows this coupling clearly: Energy Output and Neutron Damage curves track each other almost perfectly.
• Q: Why has fusion been '30 years away' for 60 years?
  A: The model reveals that fusion is not a single problem but a system of coupled problems. Solving plasma physics (achieving high temperature and confinement) is necessary but not sufficient — you also need materials that survive neutron flux, magnets that maintain field stability, tritium breeding that sustains fuel supply, and total energy balance that produces net gain. Progress on one front often reveals new challenges on another. The 30-year prediction kept resetting because each breakthrough uncovered the next bottleneck. Today, with advances in high-temperature superconductors and computational plasma control, most experts believe the timeline has finally become real.

Key Concepts:
• Tokamak vs. Stellarator vs. Inertial: Multiple approaches to fusion confinement exist: tokamaks use a combination of external magnets and internal plasma current, stellarators use twisted external magnets alone (more stable but harder to build), and inertial confinement uses lasers to compress fuel pellets. Each has different strengths and weaknesses in the nine-variable model.
• High-Temperature Superconductors: Recent breakthroughs in high-temperature superconducting (HTS) magnets allow much stronger magnetic fields in more compact reactors. Commonwealth Fusion Systems' SPARC reactor uses HTS magnets to achieve ITER-level performance in a reactor one-sixty-fifth the volume, potentially accelerating the path to commercial fusion.
• Advanced Materials: Reduced-activation ferritic-martensitic steels, silicon carbide composites, and tungsten alloys are leading candidates for fusion reactor structural materials. Research continues on self-healing materials and liquid metal walls that could mitigate neutron damage by continuously refreshing the reactor surface.
```
