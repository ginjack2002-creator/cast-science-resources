### Title
**Can the Ocean Power Entire Cities? — Modeling Ocean Thermal Energy Conversion and Marine Renewable Power**

### Overview
The ocean is the largest solar collector on Earth. Tropical surface waters absorb enough solar energy to power human civilization hundreds of times over. Just below the surface, at depths of 800-1,000 meters, water temperatures hover near 4 degrees Celsius — essentially unchanged for centuries. This temperature difference between surface and deep water represents an enormous energy reservoir that Ocean Thermal Energy Conversion technology can tap — but thermodynamics imposes strict limits on how much energy can actually be extracted. Students investigate the driving question: The ocean absorbs more solar energy in one day than humanity uses in a year — can we tap this enormous reservoir to power civilization? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

### Learning Objectives
- Model how nine thermodynamic and engineering variables determine the feasibility of ocean thermal energy conversion
- Analyze the fundamental physics of heat engines applied to the small temperature difference between surface and deep ocean water
- Evaluate the engineering trade-offs between energy extraction, environmental impact, and infrastructure cost
- Predict the conditions under which ocean thermal energy becomes economically competitive with other renewable sources

### Component List (Starting Model: 9 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Surface Water Temperature | External | The temperature of seawater at the ocean surface, typically 25-30 degrees Celsius in tropical regions — the heat source for the OTEC system that evaporates the working fluid |
| Deep Water Temperature | External | The temperature of seawater pumped from 800-1,000 meters depth, typically 4-5 degrees Celsius — the cold sink that condenses the working fluid after it passes through the turbine |
| Temperature Differential | Internal | The difference between surface and deep water temperatures — at least 20 degrees Celsius is needed for viable OTEC operation, and efficiency increases with larger differentials |
| Fluid Flow Rate | Internal | The volume of seawater pumped through the heat exchangers per second — both warm surface water and cold deep water must flow at enormous rates to extract sufficient thermal energy |
| Turbine Efficiency | Internal | The percentage of thermal energy in the working fluid vapor that is converted to mechanical rotation of the turbine — limited by practical engineering to 80-90% of the theoretical Carnot maximum |
| Energy Conversion | Internal | The net electrical energy output after subtracting the energy consumed by massive seawater pumps, working fluid pumps, and auxiliary systems — typically only 2-3% of the total thermal energy processed |
| Environmental Impact | Internal | The ecological effects of pumping billions of gallons of deep cold water to the surface, including nutrient upwelling, temperature changes in surrounding waters, and disruption of marine ecosystems |
| Infrastructure Cost | Internal | The capital and operating cost of the massive offshore platform, deep-water intake pipes (1,000+ meters long), heat exchangers, turbines, and submarine power cables — currently $25,000-$50,000 per installed kilowatt |
| Power Output | Internal | The total electricity delivered to shore in megawatts — current designs target 10-100 MW per platform, compared to 1,000+ MW for conventional power plants |

    Higher flow rates mean more cold, nutrient-rich deep water is brought to the surface and more warm surface water passes through the system. WHAT DID YOU DISCOVER?
• The Carnot efficiency limit for OTEC is brutally low — about 6.7% for a 20 degree Celsius differential — meaning the system must process enormous volumes of water to extract useful energy, driving up infrastructure cost and environmental impact
• Net energy conversion (after pumping costs) is only 2-3% of thermal energy processed, requiring flow rates of millions of gallons per minute per 100 MW — making the pipes and pumps the dominant engineering challenge
• Environmental impact is a double-edged variable: nutrient-rich deep water brought to the surface can enhance marine productivity (positive) but also disrupts existing ecosystems and temperature patterns (negative)
• OTEC provides uniquely valuable baseload power — unlike solar and wind, the temperature differential exists 24 hours a day, 365 days a year, making it one of the most consistent renewable sources possible

THE ANSWER: The ocean stores an almost inconceivable amount of solar energy — enough to power civilization many times over. OTEC technology can tap this energy by running a heat engine between warm surface water and cold deep water. The physics works: warm water evaporates a working fluid, the vapor drives a turbine, cold water condenses it back to liquid. But the tiny temperature differential (about 20 degrees Celsius) limits Carnot efficiency to approximately 7%, and net efficiency after pumping costs to only 2-3%. This means generating 100 MW requires pumping millions of gallons of seawater per minute through massive offshore infrastructure. The technology is proven but the economics remain challenging at current energy prices. Its unique advantage is 24/7 baseload renewable power.

LESSON PURPOSE:
The ocean is the largest solar collector on Earth. Tropical surface waters absorb enough solar energy to power human civilization hundreds of times over. Just below the surface, at depths of 800-1,000 meters, water temperatures hover near 4 degrees Celsius — essentially unchanged for centuries. This temperature difference between surface and deep water represents an enormous energy reservoir that Ocean Thermal Energy Conversion technology can tap — but thermodynamics imposes strict limits on how much energy can actually be extracted.

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• The OTEC Thermodynamic Cycle:
  OTEC uses a closed-cycle Rankine heat engine. Warm surface water (25-30 degrees Celsius) heats a working fluid (usually ammonia, which boils at -33 degrees Celsius) in an evaporator. The ammonia vapor expands through a turbine, generating mechanical energy that drives a generator. Cold deep water (4-5 degrees Celsius) then condenses the ammonia vapor back to liquid in a condenser. The liquid ammonia is pumped back to the evaporator, completing the cycle. The critical constraint is the Carnot efficiency: with only a 20 degree temperature difference, the theoretical maximum efficiency is about 6.7%.

• The Pumping Challenge:
  Because efficiency is so low, OTEC must process enormous volumes of water. A 100 MW net output plant requires pumping approximately 400 cubic meters per second of warm water and 200 cubic meters per second of cold water — comparable to a medium-sized river. The cold water pipe alone extends 1,000+ meters to the deep ocean and may be 8-10 meters in diameter. Pumping this much water consumes 30-40% of the gross power generated, which is why net efficiency drops from the theoretical 6.7% to practical 2-3%.

• Environmental Considerations:
  Deep ocean water is cold, nutrient-rich (high in nitrogen, phosphorus, and silica), and low in pathogens. Bringing it to the surface has multiple effects: nutrient upwelling can enhance marine productivity near the discharge, potentially supporting fisheries. But it also introduces cold water into warm surface environments, potentially disrupting coral reefs and tropical marine ecosystems. Large-scale OTEC deployment could even affect local ocean circulation patterns. Environmental impact assessment is essential for any OTEC project.

• Economic Reality and Island Nations:
  OTEC electricity costs $0.15-0.25 per kWh — far more expensive than onshore solar ($0.03-0.05) or wind ($0.03-0.06) in most markets. But for island nations dependent on imported diesel at $0.30-0.50 per kWh, OTEC is already economically competitive. It also provides 24/7 baseload power (unlike solar and wind), produces desalinated water as a byproduct, and the cold water discharge can support air conditioning and cold-water agriculture. For tropical island nations, OTEC may be the most comprehensive clean energy solution available.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "The ocean has unlimited energy so OTEC can produce unlimited power"
  Reality: While the ocean's thermal energy is enormous, the Carnot efficiency limit means only a tiny fraction can be extracted as useful work. A 20 degree Celsius temperature difference limits efficiency to about 7% theoretical and 2-3% practical. This means a 100 MW OTEC plant must process the same thermal energy as a 3,000+ MW fossil fuel plant — requiring massive infrastructure. Additionally, large-scale extraction would locally cool surface waters and warm deep waters, reducing the temperature differential and setting a natural upper limit on OTEC energy extraction in any given area.
  Strategy: Calculate: If you extracted enough heat to lower a cubic kilometer of surface water by 1 degree Celsius, how much electrical energy would that produce at 2.5% net efficiency? How does that compare to one day of electricity use by a city of 1 million people?

• "OTEC is just like any other heat engine — make it bigger and it works better"
  Reality: OTEC is fundamentally different from conventional heat engines because its temperature differential is extremely small (20 degrees Celsius versus 500+ degrees Celsius for fossil fuel plants). This tiny differential means the system operates near the bottom of the Carnot curve where efficiency is extremely sensitive to temperature changes — a 5 degree Celsius reduction in differential can cut net power output by 50% or more. Scaling up helps reduce per-unit costs but cannot overcome the thermodynamic constraint. Size helps economics but not physics.
  Strategy: Graph exercise: Plot Carnot efficiency versus temperature differential from 5 to 500 degrees Celsius (with cold sink at 4 degrees Celsius). Note how steep the curve is at low differentials — a small change in OTEC range causes a much larger percentage efficiency change than the same change in the high-temperature range of a fossil fuel plant.

• "Pumping cold water from the deep ocean has no environmental impact"
  Reality: Deep ocean water is fundamentally different from surface water: it is cold (4-5 degrees Celsius), dense, nutrient-rich (high nitrogen, phosphorus, silica), and supports different biological communities. Bringing millions of gallons per minute to the surface introduces cold, nutrient-rich water that can disrupt tropical surface ecosystems adapted to warm, nutrient-poor conditions. The nutrients can trigger algae blooms. The cold discharge can stress coral reefs. However, these impacts can also be beneficial — the nutrients support enhanced fisheries and the cold water enables aquaculture. The environmental impact is complex and site-specific, not negligible.
  Strategy: Case study: The Natural Energy Laboratory of Hawaii Authority (NELHA) has operated deep seawater pipelines for decades. Research shows both beneficial effects (successful cold-water agriculture, aquaculture) and disruptions (changes in local plankton communities). Understanding these trade-offs is essential for responsible OTEC development.

ANSWER KEY:
Big Question: The ocean absorbs more solar energy in one day than humanity uses in a year — can we tap this enormous reservoir to power civilization?
Answer: The ocean stores an almost inconceivable amount of solar energy — enough to power civilization many times over. OTEC technology can tap this energy by running a heat engine between warm surface water and cold deep water. The physics works: warm water evaporates a working fluid, the vapor drives a turbine, cold water condenses it back to liquid. But the tiny temperature differential (about 20 degrees Celsius) limits Carnot efficiency to approximately 7%, and net efficiency after pumping costs to only 2-3%. This means generating 100 MW requires pumping millions of gallons of seawater per minute through massive offshore infrastructure. The technology is proven but the economics remain challenging at current energy prices. Its unique advantage is 24/7 baseload renewable power.

Simulation Answers:
• Tropical Optimal Scenario: With 28 degrees Celsius surface water and 4 degrees Celsius deep water, the 24 degree Celsius Temperature Differential yields a Carnot efficiency of approximately 8%. After accounting for real-world losses (heat exchanger approach temperatures, turbine inefficiency, working fluid properties), gross efficiency is about 4-5%. After subtracting 30-40% of gross power for seawater pumping, net Energy Conversion is approximately 2.5-3%. A plant processing 500 MW of thermal energy produces approximately 12-15 MW of net electrical Power Output. Infrastructure Cost is high but the 24/7 baseload output and co-product potential (desalinated water, cold water agriculture) improve overall economics.
• Reduced Differential Scenario: When Temperature Differential drops from 24 to 15 degrees Celsius, Carnot efficiency decreases from 8% to approximately 5%. But the impact on net power is far worse than the efficiency drop suggests: gross power decreases proportionally, but pumping costs remain nearly constant (the same volume of water must flow through the same pipes). Net Energy Conversion drops to approximately 1-1.5%, and Power Output may decrease by 50-60%. The model demonstrates that OTEC viability is extremely sensitive to temperature differential — the system has a sharp threshold below about 18-20 degrees Celsius where net power approaches zero.

Reflection Exemplars:
• Q: Why does the Carnot limit make OTEC so different from other energy sources?
  A: Fossil fuel power plants operate with temperature differentials of 500+ degrees Celsius, giving Carnot efficiencies of 60-65% and practical efficiencies of 35-45%. Solar panels convert photon energy directly to electricity at 20-25% efficiency. Wind turbines extract kinetic energy at 35-45% of the Betz limit. OTEC's 20 degree Celsius differential gives a Carnot limit of only about 7%, and practical efficiency of 2-3%. This means OTEC must process 10-20 times more working material per kilowatt than other sources, driving up every physical dimension of the system — pipe diameter, pump capacity, platform size, heat exchanger area. The energy is abundant but the thermodynamic quality is low.
• Q: Why is OTEC's 24/7 baseload capability uniquely valuable?
  A: Solar power peaks at noon and produces zero at night. Wind is intermittent and unpredictable. Both require expensive battery storage to provide reliable 24/7 power. OTEC operates on the ocean's thermal mass, which changes slowly — surface water temperature varies by only 1-2 degrees Celsius between day and night and 3-5 degrees Celsius between seasons. This makes OTEC one of the most consistent renewable energy sources available, producing steady power around the clock without storage. For island grids that cannot afford massive battery systems, this baseload characteristic may be more valuable than the lower per-kWh cost of intermittent solar or wind.

Key Concepts:
• Closed-Cycle vs. Open-Cycle OTEC: Closed-cycle systems use ammonia or another low-boiling-point fluid in a sealed loop. Open-cycle systems flash-evaporate seawater itself under vacuum, producing desalinated water as a direct byproduct. Hybrid systems combine both approaches to maximize electricity and freshwater production.
• Co-Product Economics: OTEC economics improve dramatically when co-products are valued: cold deep water for air conditioning (replacing 80% of electricity used for cooling in tropical buildings), desalinated water production (5-10 million liters per day per 10 MW plant), cold-water agriculture (growing temperate crops like strawberries in tropical climates), and aquaculture (nutrient-rich deep water supports marine farming).
• Scaling and Learning Curves: The first commercial OTEC plants will be expensive, but costs are projected to decrease 30-50% with scaling and manufacturing learning. A 10 MW demonstration plant might cost $0.25 per kWh, but a 100 MW production plant could achieve $0.10-0.15 per kWh — competitive with diesel in most island markets.

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format. Each item is three-dimensional, assessing a Science and Engineering Practice (SEP), Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and Crosscutting Concept (CCC) simultaneously, aligned to HS-PS3-2, HS-ESS3-1.

### Pre-Assessment Questions

### Question 1

Ocean surface water in the tropics is approximately 25-30 degrees Celsius while deep water at 1,000 meters is approximately 4-5 degrees Celsius. What physical principle allows this temperature difference to be used for electricity generation?

A. Cold water naturally generates electricity when brought to the surface
B. A heat engine can extract work from any temperature difference — warm water evaporates a working fluid, the vapor drives a turbine, and cold water condenses it back to liquid, completing the thermodynamic cycle
C. The temperature difference creates pressure that directly drives a turbine
D. The mixing of warm and cold water creates a chemical reaction that produces electricity

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. OTEC uses the same thermodynamic principle as any heat engine (like a steam power plant): heat flows from a hot source to a cold sink, and work is extracted during the transfer. The unique aspect is that both the heat source and sink are seawater at relatively low temperatures. Think about how a conventional power plant works: it uses a temperature difference to evaporate a fluid, drive a turbine, and condense the fluid. OTEC applies the same principle but uses ocean water as both the heat source and cold sink.

---

### Question 2

The Carnot efficiency formula gives the maximum possible efficiency for a heat engine: efficiency = 1 - (T_cold / T_hot), where temperatures are in Kelvin. For OTEC with surface water at 28 degrees C (301 K) and deep water at 4 degrees C (277 K), what is the maximum theoretical efficiency?

A. Approximately 86% (very high efficiency like a modern gas turbine)
B. Approximately 50% (moderate efficiency)
C. Approximately 8% (very low efficiency due to the small temperature difference)
D. Approximately 0.1% (essentially zero usable energy)

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: Correct. Carnot efficiency = 1 - (277/301) = 1 - 0.920 = 0.080 or about 8%. This is the theoretical MAXIMUM — actual net efficiency is 2-3% after pumping costs. The small temperature differential (24 degrees vs. hundreds of degrees in fossil plants) fundamentally limits OTEC efficiency. Apply the Carnot formula: efficiency = 1 - (T_cold/T_hot) = 1 - (277/301). Note that both temperatures must be in Kelvin (add 273 to Celsius). The result shows the maximum fraction of thermal energy that can be converted to work.

---

### Question 3

A conventional natural gas power plant operates with a temperature difference of approximately 1,000 degrees Celsius. OTEC operates with approximately 20 degrees Celsius. What engineering consequence does this 50-fold difference in temperature gradient have?

A. No engineering consequence — both systems produce similar amounts of electricity
B. OTEC must process approximately 50 times more working fluid per unit of electricity generated, requiring massive heat exchangers and seawater pumps
C. OTEC compensates by running 50 times faster than gas turbines
D. The temperature difference only affects efficiency, not equipment size

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. Because each unit of seawater contains very little extractable energy (due to the small temperature difference and low Carnot efficiency), enormous volumes must be processed. A 100 MW OTEC plant must pump millions of gallons of seawater per minute, requiring massive infrastructure that dominates the engineering challenge and cost. If each gallon of water provides very little energy (due to low efficiency), how much water must be processed to generate significant electricity? Compare this to a gas plant that extracts much more energy per unit of fuel.

---

### Question 4

OTEC deep-water intake pipes must extend to 800-1,000 meters depth. What is the primary engineering challenge of building and maintaining these pipes in the open ocean?

A. The pipes must be heated to prevent the cold water from freezing
B. The pipes face enormous hydrostatic pressure, ocean current forces, corrosive saltwater, biofouling by marine organisms, and potential damage from storms — all while maintaining structural integrity over decades
C. Deep water cannot be pumped to the surface because of pressure differences
D. The pipes are too expensive to manufacture but are otherwise straightforward to maintain

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. The deep-water pipe faces multiple simultaneous challenges: hydrostatic pressure increases with depth, ocean currents exert lateral forces, saltwater corrodes metals, marine organisms (barnacles, algae) colonize surfaces and reduce thermal efficiency, and tropical storms can damage surface connections. These combined stresses must be managed for 20-30 year operational lifespans. Consider all the environmental forces acting on a pipe that extends 1 km into the ocean: pressure, currents, corrosion, biological growth, and storm damage. What makes maintaining such infrastructure challenging?

---

### Question 5

Unlike solar and wind energy, OTEC can generate electricity 24 hours a day, 365 days a year. What physical characteristic of the ocean makes this baseload consistency possible?

A. The ocean is heated by underwater volcanoes that operate continuously
B. Ocean surface temperature and deep water temperature remain relatively stable regardless of time of day or season in tropical regions — the thermal gradient is always present because of the ocean's enormous heat capacity
C. OTEC plants store energy during the day for nighttime use
D. Wind drives the temperature differential, and wind blows at night too

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. The ocean's enormous thermal mass (high heat capacity) means surface temperatures in the tropics vary by only 1-2 degrees Celsius between day and night, and deep water temperature is essentially constant. Unlike solar (no sun at night) or wind (intermittent), the temperature gradient is continuously available. Consider what drives solar and wind intermittency versus what drives OTEC. Solar depends on sunlight (absent at night). Wind varies unpredictably. What about the ocean temperature gradient — does it change significantly between day and night?

### Post-Assessment Questions

### Question 1

The model shows that reducing the Temperature Differential from 24 degrees to 15 degrees Celsius causes Power Output to drop by more than 50%, even though the temperature change is only about 35%. What physical relationship explains this nonlinear response?

A. The model has a calculation error for small temperature differentials
B. Carnot efficiency is proportional to the temperature differential relative to the absolute temperature, so a 35% reduction in differential reduces maximum efficiency by a similar fraction — but the net power output drops more because the fixed energy cost of pumping becomes a larger fraction of the reduced output
C. Temperature differential has a linear relationship with power output
D. The deep water pump automatically shuts down at lower differentials

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. The model reveals a compounding effect: reduced differential lowers both the Carnot efficiency ceiling and the thermal energy available per unit of water. Meanwhile, pumping costs (which are roughly fixed) consume a larger percentage of the reduced output. Net power drops faster than the differential reduction because of this double squeeze on efficiency. Consider two effects: (1) lower temperature differential means lower theoretical efficiency, and (2) the energy cost of pumping water remains roughly the same. If the gross output decreases but the pumping cost stays constant, what happens to net output?

### Question 2

The model includes Environmental Impact as an internal variable that increases with Fluid Flow Rate. A student argues this trade-off makes OTEC environmentally harmful. What evidence from the model provides a more nuanced evaluation?

A. The model shows zero environmental impact at all flow rates
B. Environmental Impact has both negative effects (ecosystem disruption from temperature changes) and potential positive effects (nutrient-rich deep water upwelling enhances marine productivity) — the net effect depends on flow rate, location, and management practices
C. OTEC has no environmental impact because it uses natural ocean water
D. The model does not address environmental impact

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. The model captures a genuine duality: cold, nutrient-rich deep water brought to the surface can fertilize marine ecosystems and enhance fisheries (positive), but it also changes local temperature patterns and disrupts existing ecosystem structure (negative). Responsible OTEC design must manage this trade-off through careful siting and discharge management. Consider what happens when you pump nutrient-rich deep water to the nutrient-depleted surface. Is the ecological effect entirely negative, or could there be beneficial consequences alongside the disruptive ones?

### Question 3

The model classifies Surface Water Temperature and Deep Water Temperature as external variables. Why is Temperature Differential classified as internal even though it is simply the mathematical difference between them?

A. All mathematical relationships between variables must be internal
B. Temperature Differential is an emergent system property — it depends on both external temperatures and on local conditions like mixing, seasonal variation, and the OTEC system's own cold water discharge, making it more than a simple subtraction of two inputs
C. Temperature Differential cannot be calculated, only measured empirically
D. The classification is incorrect and should be changed

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. While Temperature Differential appears to be a simple calculation, in practice it is affected by the system's own operation. Cold water discharged near the surface can reduce the local surface temperature, decreasing the effective differential. This feedback makes it an emergent property rather than a pure input. Consider whether the OTEC system itself can change the local temperature conditions. If the system discharges cold deep water near the surface, what happens to the local surface temperature? Does the differential remain constant?

### Question 4

The maximum scale scenario shows that increasing Fluid Flow Rate 10x increases Power Output approximately 10x but increases Infrastructure Cost by 15x and Environmental Impact by 12x. What does this disproportionate scaling reveal?

A. OTEC scales perfectly and should be built as large as possible
B. Diminishing returns at scale — each additional unit of power becomes progressively more expensive and environmentally impactful because larger pipes, pumps, and platforms have disproportionately higher costs and ecological footprints
C. The model incorrectly overestimates the cost of larger systems
D. Infrastructure Cost and Environmental Impact are unrelated to Fluid Flow Rate

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. The model reveals that power output scales approximately linearly with flow rate, but costs and impacts scale superlinearly (faster than linear). This means there is an economically optimal facility size beyond which each additional megawatt costs more and causes more harm per unit of energy — a critical insight for OTEC deployment planning. Compare the scaling factors: power goes up 10x but cost goes up 15x. Does the cost per unit of energy decrease, stay the same, or increase as the facility scales up?

### Question 5

An island nation currently pays $0.40/kWh for diesel electricity. The model predicts OTEC could deliver power at $0.15-0.25/kWh. A student argues OTEC is clearly the better option. What important variable from the model should temper this conclusion?

A. The model proves diesel is always cheaper than OTEC
B. Infrastructure Cost — the massive upfront capital investment for the offshore platform, deep-water pipes, and submarine power cables requires billions of dollars and decades of operation to recover, creating financial risk even when per-kWh costs are lower
C. OTEC produces electricity of lower quality than diesel generators
D. The diesel price will decrease to below OTEC cost in the future

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. While operating cost per kWh favors OTEC, the model shows that Infrastructure Cost is enormous — $25,000-$50,000 per installed kilowatt, orders of magnitude higher than diesel generators. The island must finance billions in upfront construction costs and operate for decades to achieve the per-kWh savings. This capital barrier, not operating cost, is typically the limiting factor. Consider the difference between operating cost (per kWh) and capital cost (upfront investment). Even if OTEC is cheaper to run, what about the cost to build the facility in the first place?
