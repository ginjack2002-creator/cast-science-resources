### Title
**De-Extinction: Should We Bring Back the Woolly Mammoth? — Modeling the Genetics, Ecology, and Ethics of Resurrecting Extinct Species**

### Overview
In 2021, Colossal Biosciences received $15 million in funding to bring back the woolly mammoth — or at least a cold-adapted elephant with mammoth traits. The last mammoths died approximately 4,000 years ago on Wrangel Island, and well-preserved specimens in Siberian permafrost have yielded significant amounts of ancient DNA. But the distance between recovering DNA fragments and restoring a self-sustaining wild population spans ten interconnected challenges that together define the frontier of both biotechnology and conservation biology. Students investigate the driving question: If we have the technology to bring back extinct species, does that mean we should — and could they even survive in a world that has moved on without them? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

### Learning Objectives
- Model how ten interdependent genetic, ecological, and ethical variables determine whether a de-extinction project can create a viable, integrated population
- Analyze the cascade of biological challenges from DNA recovery through ecosystem reintegration
- Evaluate the trade-off between the scientific possibility and the ecological wisdom of species resurrection
- Predict the minimum conditions required for a de-extinct population to be self-sustaining rather than permanently dependent on human intervention

### Component List (Starting Model: 10 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| DNA Recovery Quality | External | The completeness and accuracy of the ancient DNA extracted from preserved specimens — degradation produces gaps, damage artifacts, and contamination that must be resolved by comparison with related living species |
| Gene Editing Accuracy | External | The precision of CRISPR and other gene editing tools in modifying a living relative's genome to incorporate recovered extinct species genes — off-target edits can cause developmental defects or inviable embryos |
| Surrogate Compatibility | Internal | The physiological compatibility between the de-extinct embryo and the surrogate mother species used to gestate it — the surrogate's uterine environment, gestation period, and immune system must support the modified embryo |
| Population Viability | Internal | The probability that a founding population can grow to self-sustaining numbers without inbreeding depression — determined by initial genetic diversity, reproductive rate, and population management |
| Ecosystem Readiness | Internal | The degree to which the target ecosystem can support the de-extinct species — including food availability, habitat extent, climate suitability, and absence of novel threats the species never evolved to handle |
| Habitat Availability | Internal | The total area of suitable habitat available for the de-extinct population, accounting for human land use changes since the species' extinction — mammoths evolved for steppe-tundra that has largely converted to boreal forest and tundra |
| Genetic Diversity | Internal | The total genetic variation within the de-extinct founding population — limited by the number of distinct ancient DNA sources available and the accuracy of genome reconstruction |
| Disease Resistance | Internal | The de-extinct organism's ability to resist pathogens that have evolved during the species' absence — a mammoth would encounter bacteria, viruses, and parasites its immune system never adapted to |
| Ecological Integration | Internal | The degree to which the de-extinct species establishes functional relationships with existing species — predator-prey dynamics, competition, symbiosis, and ecosystem engineering effects |
| Ethical Threshold | External | The societal consensus on whether de-extinction is justified given the opportunity cost, animal welfare concerns, and philosophical questions about human intervention in evolution — affects funding, regulation, and public support |

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

WHAT DID YOU DISCOVER?
• De-extinction is not a single technological challenge but a cascade of ten interdependent barriers — solving the genetics is necessary but insufficient without solving ecology, health, and ethics simultaneously
• Ancient DNA is always incomplete and damaged — the 'mammoth' genome is actually a patchwork of recovered mammoth sequences and gap-filling from Asian elephant DNA, creating a hybrid rather than a true resurrection
• The ecological niche a species occupied may no longer exist — the mammoth steppe ecosystem has largely disappeared, and reintroducing mammoths would require either habitat restoration or accepting that the animals will live in a fundamentally different environment
• The minimum viable population problem means de-extinction is not about creating one animal but about creating hundreds to thousands with sufficient genetic diversity to avoid inbreeding collapse

THE ANSWER: De-extinction faces a cascade of ten interdependent challenges. First, ancient DNA from permafrost specimens is fragmented and damaged — no complete mammoth genome has been recovered. Scientists must reconstruct the genome by filling gaps with Asian elephant DNA, creating a mammoth-elephant hybrid. Gene editing can modify elephant cells with mammoth traits, but off-target edits create risks. A surrogate elephant mother must carry the modified embryo. Even if born successfully, a single animal is not a species — hundreds are needed for genetic diversity. These animals must survive diseases they never evolved to fight, find food in ecosystems that have reorganized without them, and live in habitats dramatically altered by 4,000 years of climate change and human land use. And all of this must be justified ethically when the same resources could protect existing endangered species. De-extinction is scientifically approaching feasibility but ecologically and ethically far more complex than the technology alone suggests.

LESSON PURPOSE:
In 2021, Colossal Biosciences received $15 million in funding to bring back the woolly mammoth — or at least a cold-adapted elephant with mammoth traits. The last mammoths died approximately 4,000 years ago on Wrangel Island, and well-preserved specimens in Siberian permafrost have yielded significant amounts of ancient DNA. But the distance between recovering DNA fragments and restoring a self-sustaining wild population spans ten interconnected challenges that together define the frontier of both biotechnology and conservation biology.

NGSS ALIGNMENT:
HS-LS4-4, HS-LS2-7: Construct an explanation based on evidence for how natural selection leads to adaptation of populations. Design, evaluate, and refine a solution for reducing the impacts of human activities on the environment and biodiversity.

THREE-DIMENSIONAL LEARNING:
• Science Practice: Constructing Explanations and Designing Solutions
  Students construct evidence-based explanations of why natural selection shaped mammoth adaptations and design solutions for the genetic, ecological, and ethical challenges of de-extinction.
• Disciplinary Core Idea: LS4.C Adaptation / LS2.C Ecosystem Dynamics, Functioning, and Resilience
  Students analyze how natural selection created mammoth adaptations to Pleistocene conditions and evaluate whether modern ecosystems can support species adapted to environments that no longer exist.
• Crosscutting Concept: Stability and Change
  Students model the tension between ecosystem stability (which has reorganized without mammoths) and deliberate change (reintroducing a species to restore ecosystem functions), evaluating whether change would create new stability or new disruption.

LEVER FRAMEWORK:
• Locate: Students identify ten key components of the de-extinction system: DNA recovery quality, gene editing accuracy, surrogate compatibility, population viability, ecosystem readiness, habitat availability, genetic diversity, disease resistance, ecological integration, and ethical threshold.
• Establish: Students map relationships showing that DNA recovery quality and gene editing accuracy determine the starting biological material, which must survive surrogate gestation, then face ecological barriers (ecosystem readiness, habitat availability, disease resistance) that determine whether a viable population can establish, all within the constraints of ethical societal acceptance.
• Visualize: Students build a computational model showing the ten-component de-extinction system with cascading barriers from genome to ecosystem.
• Evaluate: Students run best case technology, ecological mismatch, and ethics versus science scenarios to identify which barriers are most limiting and whether any configuration produces a viable outcome.
• Revise: Students add behavioral repertoire, climate trajectory, or keystone effects to model more realistic long-term de-extinction outcomes.

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• The Ancient DNA Challenge:
  Mammoth DNA recovered from permafrost specimens is always fragmented — broken into millions of short pieces averaging 50-200 base pairs long (the full genome is 4 billion base pairs). Fragments must be painstakingly assembled, like completing a puzzle where most pieces are missing and many are damaged. Chemical modifications from centuries of degradation (cytosine deamination, strand breaks, cross-linking) create systematic errors. No complete mammoth genome has been assembled; current references are about 80-90% complete, with gaps filled using the Asian elephant genome as a template.

• From Genome to Organism:
  Even with a complete genome, creating a living mammoth requires multiple additional steps. CRISPR gene editing can modify Asian elephant cells with mammoth-specific genes (cold adaptation, hemoglobin variants, fat distribution, hair growth). But off-target edits — unintended changes elsewhere in the genome — can cause developmental abnormalities. The modified cells must be used to create embryos through somatic cell nuclear transfer (cloning). The embryo must develop in a surrogate elephant mother for 22 months — the longest gestation of any land mammal. Success rates for cloning in mammals are typically 1-5%.

• The Ecological Gauntlet:
  A healthy mammoth born today would face an environment radically different from the Pleistocene. The mammoth steppe — a vast grassland spanning Eurasia and North America — has largely transitioned to boreal forest and tundra. Food plants have changed. New diseases, parasites, and competitors have evolved. Predators the mammoth never encountered (including modern humans with firearms) now exist. The animal would have no cultural knowledge of migration routes, seasonal food sources, or social behaviors that were transmitted between generations in the original population. It would be genetically a mammoth but ecologically a stranger.

• The Ethics of Resurrection:
  De-extinction raises profound ethical questions. Should resources be spent resurrecting extinct species when thousands of existing species face imminent extinction? What is the welfare status of a mammoth born into a world it was not designed for — is it ethical to create an animal that may suffer? If the de-extinct population requires permanent human management, is it a wild species or an elaborate zoo exhibit? And philosophically, is a mammoth-elephant hybrid with 99% elephant genes actually a mammoth, or something entirely new? These questions do not have scientific answers — they require ethical frameworks that society has not yet developed.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "De-extinction means bringing back the exact same species"
  Reality: Current de-extinction technology creates a hybrid — not a resurrection. The mammoth genome is assembled from incomplete ancient DNA with gaps filled by Asian elephant sequences. CRISPR edits modify elephant cells with mammoth-specific genes, but the resulting organism is an elephant with mammoth traits, not a true mammoth. Its mitochondrial DNA, epigenetic programming, microbiome, and developmental environment are all elephant. Whether this constitutes a mammoth is a philosophical question, not a scientific one. The model treats this as a variable: what percentage of the genome must be mammoth for the organism to count?
  Strategy: Analogy: If you replaced 50% of the parts in a Ford with Toyota parts, is it still a Ford? What about 90%? What about 99%? There is no clear boundary — which is the same challenge with defining when a modified elephant becomes a mammoth.

• "If we can clone a mammoth, the species is saved"
  Reality: Creating one or even ten individuals is not saving a species. A species requires a self-sustaining population with enough genetic diversity to avoid inbreeding depression over generations. The minimum viable population for most large mammals is 500-5,000 individuals. Creating this many mammoths would require thousands of cloning attempts, each with a 1-5% success rate, using surrogate elephants with 22-month gestations. The timeline to reach viable population size is decades to centuries, and every individual in that population must survive in the wild. A few animals in a zoo is not species restoration.
  Strategy: Calculate: With 2% cloning success rate and 22-month elephant gestation, how many surrogate pregnancies are needed to produce 500 viable mammoth calves? How many surrogate elephants would be needed? How does this compare to the total number of Asian elephants alive today?

• "De-extinct animals can just be released into the wild"
  Reality: Release into the wild requires suitable habitat (which may have changed dramatically), appropriate food sources (which may have shifted), absence of novel threats (new diseases, predators, human activity), and learned behaviors (migration routes, social structures, foraging strategies) that cannot be genetically inherited. Most de-extinct animals would initially require managed reserves — essentially large-scale captive habitats — with gradual introduction to more natural conditions. Even wild-born animals of existing species raised in captivity often fail to survive release. A de-extinct animal facing a world it never evolved in would face even greater challenges.
  Strategy: Case study: The California condor captive breeding program has spent decades and hundreds of millions of dollars reintroducing a species that went extinct in the wild only 35 years ago into its original habitat with its original food sources. Even with all these advantages, condor numbers remain fragile. Now imagine the same challenge for a species absent for 4,000 years in a completely changed ecosystem.

ANSWER KEY:
Big Question: If we have the technology to bring back extinct species, does that mean we should — and could they even survive in a world that has moved on without them?
Answer: De-extinction faces a cascade of ten interdependent challenges. First, ancient DNA from permafrost specimens is fragmented and damaged — no complete mammoth genome has been recovered. Scientists must reconstruct the genome by filling gaps with Asian elephant DNA, creating a mammoth-elephant hybrid. Gene editing can modify elephant cells with mammoth traits, but off-target edits create risks. A surrogate elephant mother must carry the modified embryo. Even if born successfully, a single animal is not a species — hundreds are needed for genetic diversity. These animals must survive diseases they never evolved to fight, find food in ecosystems that have reorganized without them, and live in habitats dramatically altered by 4,000 years of climate change and human land use. And all of this must be justified ethically when the same resources could protect existing endangered species. De-extinction is scientifically approaching feasibility but ecologically and ethically far more complex than the technology alone suggests.

Simulation Answers:
• Best Case Technology Scenario: With maximum DNA Recovery Quality and Gene Editing Accuracy, the model shows successful creation of a mammoth-elephant hybrid with key mammoth phenotypic traits — cold adaptation, hemoglobin variants, fat distribution, and hair growth. Surrogate Compatibility remains a significant barrier due to the 22-month elephant gestation and low cloning success rates (1-5%), but is achievable with sufficient attempts. However, even with perfect technology, Population Viability requires hundreds of genetically distinct individuals, Ecosystem Readiness is low due to habitat changes since the Pleistocene, and Disease Resistance is unknown. Technology solves the first three barriers but leaves the ecological barriers unaddressed.
• Ecological Mismatch Scenario: When technology succeeds but the animal faces real modern conditions, the model reveals devastating challenges. Habitat Availability is limited — the mammoth steppe that supported millions of mammoths has largely converted to boreal forest and tundra. Ecosystem Readiness is low because food plant communities, predator-prey dynamics, and seasonal patterns have all changed. Disease Resistance is the wild card — a 4,000-year gap means the animal's immune system has no experience with modern pathogens. Ecological Integration requires decades of observation to evaluate. The model shows that creating a viable wild population requires simultaneous ecosystem restoration at massive scale — not just bringing back the animal but rebuilding its world.

Reflection Exemplars:
• Q: Why is de-extinction not just about genetics?
  A: Creating a mammoth genome and birthing a mammoth-like animal are necessary first steps but represent only three of the ten model variables. The animal still faces seven ecological and ethical barriers: finding a compatible surrogate, surviving novel diseases, integrating into a changed ecosystem, finding enough suitable habitat, building a genetically diverse population, establishing functional ecological relationships, and gaining societal acceptance. The model reveals that genetic success is perhaps the easiest part — the ecological and population challenges are far more daunting. A genetically perfect mammoth that cannot find food, dies of a modern pathogen, or lives solely in captivity is not a de-extinction success — it is an expensive science experiment.
• Q: How does the opportunity cost argument affect the ethical threshold?
  A: The $150 million proposed for mammoth de-extinction could protect approximately 150,000 hectares of critical habitat for existing endangered species, fund anti-poaching programs for dozens of species, or support captive breeding for 50+ endangered species simultaneously. The opportunity cost is enormous — every dollar spent on de-extinction is a dollar not spent on preventing the next extinction. The model includes Ethical Threshold as a variable because this trade-off is not abstract: conservation budgets are limited and priorities must be set. Proponents argue that de-extinction drives innovation that benefits all conservation. Opponents argue that it diverts resources from proven strategies for species that can still be saved. The model does not resolve this debate — but it forces students to confront it with evidence.

Key Concepts:
• Surrogate Gestation and Artificial Wombs: Asian elephants — the mammoth's closest living relative — have a 22-month gestation and produce one calf at a time. With cloning success rates of 1-5%, producing 500+ mammoths would require thousands of surrogate pregnancies over decades. Artificial womb technology could bypass this bottleneck but does not yet exist for large mammals and raises additional animal welfare questions.
• Rewilding and Ecosystem Restoration: Some proponents argue that mammoth de-extinction is really about ecosystem restoration. Mammoths were ecosystem engineers — their grazing maintained grasslands, and their snow-compacting behavior may have kept permafrost frozen. Restoring mammoth-like grazers to the Arctic could slow permafrost thaw, which contains enough carbon to significantly accelerate climate change if released. This ecosystem services argument is the strongest scientific case for de-extinction.
• The Conservation Triage Debate: Conservation biology faces a painful reality: there are more species in need of protection than resources to protect them. Conservation triage asks which species should receive priority — those most endangered, those most ecologically important, those most culturally valued, or those most likely to be saved? De-extinction adds a new dimension: should resources go to species already lost or those about to be lost?

Evaluation Rubric:
• Expert (4): Assessment evaluates all ten model variables with evidence, identifies the most critical barriers, proposes specific modifications to address barriers, includes opportunity cost analysis, and delivers a clear recommendation with comprehensive justification
• Proficient (3): Assessment addresses most model variables, identifies key barriers, and provides a recommendation with model-based evidence
• Developing (2): Assessment discusses some variables but lacks systematic evaluation or provides a recommendation without sufficient model evidence
• Beginning (1): Assessment is incomplete, opinion-based without model evidence, or does not address the cascade of challenges beyond genetics

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format. Each item is three-dimensional, assessing a Science and Engineering Practice (SEP), Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and Crosscutting Concept (CCC) simultaneously, aligned to HS-LS4-4, HS-LS2-7.

---

### Pre-Assessment Questions

### Question 1

Scientists have recovered fragmented DNA from woolly mammoth specimens preserved in permafrost. Why is ancient DNA always incomplete, and what does this mean for de-extinction?

A. Ancient DNA is complete but encoded differently than modern DNA
B. DNA degrades over time through hydrolysis, oxidation, and cross-linking, breaking into increasingly short fragments — meaning the reconstructed genome is a patchwork of recovered mammoth sequences and gap-filling from the closest living relative (Asian elephant)
C. Permafrost perfectly preserves DNA in its original complete form
D. DNA degradation only occurs in tropical environments, not permafrost

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. Even under the best preservation conditions (permafrost at -50 degrees Celsius), chemical processes slowly break DNA strands and modify individual bases. After 4,000+ years, the mammoth genome is fragmentary. Scientists must assemble these fragments and fill gaps with Asian elephant DNA, creating a hybrid genome rather than a true mammoth genome. Consider what happens to complex organic molecules over thousands of years. Even in frozen conditions, chemical reactions slowly break and modify DNA. What implications does this have for reconstructing a complete genome?

---

### Question 2

CRISPR gene editing can modify an Asian elephant cell to express mammoth traits like cold-adapted hemoglobin, thick fat layers, and long hair. Why does this not constitute true de-extinction?

A. CRISPR cannot make precise enough edits for de-extinction
B. The result is an Asian elephant with selected mammoth traits — a hybrid organism with primarily elephant genetics — not a complete resurrection of the mammoth species with its full genome, behavioral repertoire, and ecological relationships
C. The edited cells cannot develop into a living organism
D. CRISPR only works on bacteria, not mammalian cells

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. CRISPR can insert specific mammoth genes into an elephant genome, but a species is more than a collection of traits. The resulting organism has an elephant developmental program, elephant immune system, and no inherited mammoth behaviors (migration routes, social structures, foraging strategies). It is a cold-adapted elephant, not a mammoth. Consider what makes a species more than its visible physical traits. A mammoth's genome contained tens of thousands of genes controlling development, immunity, behavior, and physiology. Can editing a few dozen genes recreate the full organism?

---

### Question 3

The minimum viable population (MVP) for large mammals is estimated at 500-5,000 individuals. Why does de-extinction require creating hundreds of genetically diverse individuals rather than just one?

A. One individual is sufficient to establish a population through cloning
B. A population below MVP lacks sufficient genetic diversity, leading to inbreeding depression — reduced fertility, increased disease susceptibility, and loss of adaptive potential that causes population decline toward extinction a second time
C. Multiple individuals are needed only for companionship
D. The MVP concept applies only to currently living species, not de-extinct ones

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. A self-sustaining population requires genetic diversity to maintain fertility, disease resistance, and adaptive potential across generations. A population founded from one or a few individuals becomes increasingly inbred, accumulating harmful recessive alleles and losing the genetic variation needed to adapt to environmental changes. Consider what happens to a population with very few individuals. Over generations, they must mate with relatives. What happens to the health and reproductive success of inbred populations?

---

### Question 4

The mammoth steppe — the vast grassland ecosystem mammoths inhabited — has largely converted to boreal forest and tundra over the past 10,000 years. What does this habitat transformation mean for de-extinction plans?

A. The habitat change is irrelevant because mammoths can adapt to any environment
B. Mammoths evolved for an ecosystem that no longer exists, meaning reintroduced mammoths would face unfamiliar vegetation, different predators, novel diseases, and a fundamentally altered ecological context
C. The mammoth steppe still exists exactly as it was 10,000 years ago in Siberia
D. Habitat changes occurred too slowly to affect large mammals

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. De-extinction faces the 'ecological niche' problem: the ecological role mammoths played (maintaining grasslands through grazing, compacting snow, dispersing seeds) may no longer be supported by the current ecosystem. The food sources, competitors, predators, and climate conditions have all changed. Reintroduced mammoths would inhabit a world that has moved on without them. Consider what happens when you place an organism in an ecosystem it did not evolve to inhabit. Are the right food plants available? Are there new diseases? Has the climate changed?

---

### Question 5

Some scientists argue that de-extinction funds would be better spent protecting currently endangered species. What is the strongest scientific basis for this opportunity cost argument?

A. Endangered species are less interesting to the public than mammoths
B. Currently endangered species have intact genomes, known ecological roles, existing habitats (though threatened), and established conservation methods — the same investment could protect entire ecosystems with existing species rather than attempting to recreate one species for an uncertain ecological outcome
C. De-extinction is guaranteed to fail so the money would be completely wasted
D. Endangered species do not need additional funding because they are already protected by law

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. Conservation triage asks where limited resources produce the greatest biodiversity benefit. Protecting existing species preserves functional ecosystems, intact genomes, and evolved ecological relationships. De-extinction requires solving DNA recovery, genome reconstruction, surrogate pregnancy, population founding, habitat restoration, and disease resistance — with uncertain outcomes. The comparison is between proven conservation and experimental resurrection. Compare what you know and can protect (existing species with known needs) versus what you must reconstruct from scratch (an extinct species with incomplete DNA, no habitat, and no immune defense against modern diseases). Where does each dollar produce more conservation value?

### Post-Assessment Questions

### Question 1

The model shows that even with maximum DNA Recovery Quality and Gene Editing Accuracy, Population Viability remains low until hundreds of genetically distinct individuals are created. What bottleneck does this reveal?

A. DNA technology is not advanced enough for de-extinction
B. The genetic diversity bottleneck — each ancient specimen provides only one genome's worth of variation, and creating a viable population requires many distinct genomes to prevent inbreeding depression, regardless of how accurately individual genomes are reconstructed
C. Population Viability is unrelated to genetic diversity in the model
D. Creating one individual is sufficient because it can reproduce asexually

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. The model reveals that technological perfection in reconstructing one genome does not solve the population-level challenge. Each ancient specimen provides unique genetic variation, and few well-preserved specimens exist. Without enough distinct genomes, the founding population lacks the diversity needed for long-term viability, regardless of the quality of individual reconstructions. Consider the difference between individual and population genetics. Even a perfectly reconstructed genome from one specimen provides only that individual's genetic variation. How many different individuals' DNA do you need for a diverse founding population?

### Question 2

The ecological mismatch scenario shows viable de-extinct individuals but low Ecosystem Readiness and Habitat Availability. The model predicts Ecological Integration remains low despite healthy individuals. What does this demonstrate?

A. Healthy individuals always integrate successfully into any ecosystem
B. De-extinction is not just a genetics problem but an integrated ecological challenge — a genetically viable organism that lacks an ecological niche, appropriate habitat, and established species relationships cannot sustain itself in the wild
C. The model incorrectly separates genetics from ecology
D. Ecological factors are irrelevant to de-extinction success

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. The model demonstrates the cascade structure of de-extinction: solving technology (DNA + gene editing) is necessary but insufficient. Without solving ecology (habitat + niche + species interactions), the animals are confined to managed care — expensive zoo exhibits rather than self-sustaining wild populations. All ten variables must reach viable levels simultaneously. Consider what happens when you place a healthy animal in an ecosystem where its food sources, climate conditions, and ecological relationships have changed. Can the animal survive even if it is genetically healthy?

### Question 3

The model classifies Ethical Threshold as an external variable alongside DNA Recovery Quality and Gene Editing Accuracy. Why is an ethical variable included in a scientific model?

A. Ethics is not relevant to science and should be removed from the model
B. Ethical and societal considerations directly affect funding, regulatory approval, and public support — which determine whether a technically viable project actually proceeds and receives the resources needed for success
C. The model includes Ethical Threshold only as a placeholder with no functional connections
D. All external variables in scientific models must include ethical considerations

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. The model recognizes that scientific projects exist within societal contexts. High ethical concerns can reduce funding, increase regulatory barriers, and erode public support — all of which constrain the project's ability to achieve the scale needed for population viability. Ethics is not separate from feasibility; it is a determining factor. Consider what happens to a research project when the public objects to it, regulators impose restrictions, or funding agencies redirect money to less controversial alternatives. Do ethical concerns affect whether a scientifically possible project actually happens?

### Question 4

The model includes Disease Resistance as a variable showing that de-extinct mammoths would face modern pathogens their immune systems never evolved to fight. Which analogy from history best illustrates this vulnerability?

A. The domestication of cats, which adapted easily to human environments
B. The introduction of European diseases to Indigenous populations in the Americas — populations without evolutionary exposure to specific pathogens suffered catastrophic mortality because their immune systems lacked evolved defenses
C. The extinction of dinosaurs from asteroid impact, which is unrelated to disease
D. Modern humans traveling to Mars, who would face only known Earth pathogens

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. This is a direct parallel: populations without evolutionary exposure to specific pathogens are highly vulnerable. Mammoths have been absent for 4,000 years, during which bacteria, viruses, and parasites have continued evolving. The de-extinct mammoth's immune system would have no evolved defense against these modern pathogens, creating a serious survival challenge. Think about what happens when an organism encounters pathogens it has no evolutionary history with. Has this happened before in human history? What were the consequences?

### Question 5

After running all three scenarios, a student argues that de-extinction should proceed because the technology is nearly viable, even though ecological barriers remain unsolved. Using model evidence, what is the strongest counterargument?

A. The technology is not actually viable, so the argument fails on its first premise
B. The model shows that Population Viability, Ecological Integration, and Habitat Availability must ALL reach viable levels for a self-sustaining wild population — technology alone produces managed zoo specimens, not species restoration, and the ecological barriers require habitat-scale interventions that may take decades or centuries
C. There are no counterarguments to proceeding with de-extinction
D. The ecological barriers will resolve themselves once the technology is perfected

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. The model demonstrates that de-extinction success requires ALL ten variables at viable levels simultaneously. Technology addresses 2-3 variables (DNA, gene editing, surrogate pregnancy). The remaining 7 ecological, population, and ethical variables require separate, potentially more difficult solutions. Proceeding on technology alone creates expensive captive animals, not restored species. Count how many of the ten model variables are addressed by technology alone versus how many require ecological and societal solutions. If only 2-3 out of 10 are solved, is the project likely to achieve its goal of species restoration?
