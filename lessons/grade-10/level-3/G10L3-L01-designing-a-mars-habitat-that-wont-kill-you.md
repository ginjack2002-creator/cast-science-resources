## Platform

### Title
**Designing a Mars Habitat That Won't Kill You — Modeling the Interdependent Systems of Extraterrestrial Human Survival**

### Overview
Designing a habitat for Mars is the most complex systems engineering challenge humanity has ever attempted. Unlike the International Space Station, which orbits just 400 km above Earth and can be resupplied in hours, a Mars habitat must function autonomously for years with no possibility of quick rescue. Every system depends on every other system, and the margin for error is measured in hours, not days. Students investigate the driving question: What combination of engineering systems would actually keep humans alive on Mars — and what happens when one fails? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

### Learning Objectives
- Model how nine interdependent habitat systems must function simultaneously to sustain human life on Mars
- Analyze the cascade effects when a single critical system degrades or fails in an isolated extraterrestrial environment
- Evaluate trade-offs between mass, energy, and redundancy in Mars habitat engineering design
- Predict the minimum viable configuration of systems needed for long-term Mars habitation

### Component List (Starting Model: 9 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Radiation Shielding | Internal | The effectiveness of barriers protecting inhabitants from galactic cosmic rays and solar particle events, measured in equivalent dose reduction percentage |
| Atmosphere Control | Internal | The system maintaining breathable air composition — 21% oxygen, less than 1% CO2 — and pressure at ~101 kPa inside the habitat |
| Water Recycling | Internal | The efficiency of systems that purify and recirculate water from humidity, wastewater, and urine, targeting 98%+ recovery rates |
| Food Production | Internal | The caloric and nutritional output of hydroponic and aeroponic growing systems per unit area per crew member |
| Energy Generation | External | The total power output from solar arrays and/or nuclear reactors available to run all habitat systems, measured in kilowatts |
| Structural Integrity | Internal | The habitat's ability to maintain pressure containment, resist micrometeorite impacts, and withstand Martian dust storms |
| Temperature Regulation | Internal | The heating and cooling systems maintaining interior temperatures between 18-24 degrees Celsius despite exterior swings from -125 to 20 degrees Celsius |
| Waste Management | Internal | The processing and conversion of solid biological and industrial waste into usable compost, fuel, or inert storage materials |
| Psychological Well-Being | External | The mental health status of crew members affected by isolation, confinement, light deprivation, communication delays, and monotony |

Task B: DRAW YOUR RELATIONSHIPS
• Click on "Energy Generation" and drag an arrow to "Atmosphere Control"
• Click on "Water Recycling" and drag an arrow to "Food Production"
• Click on "Food Production" and drag an arrow to "Psychological Well-Being"

Task C: SET POSITIVE OR NEGATIVE
• Look at the +/− toggle in the TOP LEFT corner
• For each connection, ask: "When this goes UP, does the other go UP or DOWN?"

  ○ Energy Generation → Atmosphere Control = POSITIVE (+)
    Higher energy generation powers the electrolysis units that split CO2 into oxygen and the fans that circulate air through scrubbers. When energy drops, atmosphere processing slows and CO2 levels rise dangerously.

  ○ Water Recycling → Food Production = POSITIVE (+)
    Higher water recycling efficiency means more purified water is available for hydroponic crop irrigation. When water recycling degrades, food production drops because plants cannot grow without consistent water supply.

  ○ Food Production → Psychological Well-Being = POSITIVE (+)
    Adequate food production provides nutrition and the psychological comfort of fresh food variety. When food production drops and the crew shifts to emergency rations, morale and mental health decline measurably.

• You should have 3 arrows total
• 0 negative relationship(s), 3 positive relationship(s)
• This is your system model!

## Step 3: VISUALIZE & EVALUATE — Run Your Model

### Text Editor

```

• Click the "Play" button in the TOP LEFT corner
• Watch the graph panel — you'll see percentage lines for each component

Task B: OBSERVE THE BASELINE
• Let it run for about 30 time steps
• Notice how the lines relate to each other
• When Energy Generation is HIGH, what happens to the internal components?

Task C: SCENARIO — NOMINAL OPERATIONS
• All nine systems at optimal levels
• PREDICT FIRST: What do you predict crew survivability and well-being look like when every system is functioning as designed?
• Resume the simulation and observe what happens
• Was your prediction correct?

Task D: SCENARIO — DUST STORM CRISIS
• Energy Generation drops by 50% for 30 days
• PREDICT FIRST: Which systems do you predict will fail first when available power is cut in half, and how will you prioritize?
• Resume the simulation and observe what happens
• Was your prediction correct?

Task E: SCENARIO — WATER SYSTEM FAILURE
• Water Recycling drops from 98% to 60% efficiency
• PREDICT FIRST: What do you predict happens to Food Production and crew health when 40% of recycled water is lost?
• Resume the simulation and observe what happens
• Was your prediction correct?

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

WHAT DID YOU DISCOVER?
• Mars habitat systems are so deeply interconnected that degradation in any single system cascades through multiple others within days to weeks
• Energy generation is the master variable — nearly every other system depends on continuous power, making energy redundancy the highest engineering priority
• Psychological well-being is not a luxury but a survival variable — crew mental health directly affects decision-making, maintenance quality, and conflict resolution during crises
• The habitat must be designed for graceful degradation rather than binary success or failure — the ability to operate at reduced capacity keeps the crew alive during emergencies

THE ANSWER: Keeping humans alive on Mars requires nine interdependent systems operating simultaneously with no possibility of quick rescue. Energy generation is the keystone — it powers atmosphere control, water recycling, temperature regulation, food production lighting, and waste processing. When energy drops during a dust storm, engineers must triage: atmosphere and temperature first, then water, then food. Psychological well-being degrades as conditions worsen, creating a feedback loop where stressed crews make worse decisions. The solution is redundancy, graceful degradation, and designing every system to survive at reduced capacity.
```

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

LESSON PURPOSE:
Designing a habitat for Mars is the most complex systems engineering challenge humanity has ever attempted. Unlike the International Space Station, which orbits just 400 km above Earth and can be resupplied in hours, a Mars habitat must function autonomously for years with no possibility of quick rescue. Every system depends on every other system, and the margin for error is measured in hours, not days.

NGSS ALIGNMENT:
HS-ESS1-4, HS-ETS1-1: Use mathematical or computational representations to predict the motion of orbiting objects in the solar system. Analyze a major global challenge to specify qualitative and quantitative criteria and constraints for solutions that account for societal needs and wants.

THREE-DIMENSIONAL LEARNING:
• Science Practice: Using Mathematics and Computational Thinking
  Students use computational models to predict how energy constraints cascade through interconnected habitat systems and calculate minimum viable operating parameters.
• Disciplinary Core Idea: ESS1.B Earth and the Solar System / ETS1.A Defining and Delimiting Engineering Problems
  Students apply knowledge of Mars' orbital mechanics, atmospheric conditions, and radiation environment to define quantitative criteria for habitat engineering solutions.
• Crosscutting Concept: Systems and System Models
  Students model the Mars habitat as a complex system where feedback loops, energy flows, and interdependencies determine whether the system sustains life or fails catastrophically.

LEVER FRAMEWORK:
• Locate: Students identify nine key components of a Mars habitat system: radiation shielding, atmosphere control, water recycling, food production, energy generation, structural integrity, temperature regulation, waste management, and psychological well-being.
• Establish: Students map the complex web of relationships between components, identifying energy generation as the keystone variable and tracing how its reduction cascades through atmosphere, water, food, temperature, and ultimately psychological well-being.
• Visualize: Students build a computational model showing the nine-component habitat system with feedback loops, energy dependencies, and cascade failure pathways.
• Evaluate: Students run nominal operations, dust storm crisis, and water system failure scenarios to test their model and identify critical thresholds for each system.
• Revise: Students add communication relay, medical capability, or spare parts inventory to model even more realistic habitat dynamics and failure modes.

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• The Mars Environment Challenge:
  Mars presents a hostile combination of threats: atmospheric pressure at 0.6% of Earth's (instant ebullism without a pressure suit), 95% CO2 atmosphere with no breathable oxygen, average temperatures of -60 degrees Celsius with swings of 100+ degrees daily, radiation doses 200+ times Earth's surface level due to the absent magnetosphere, and global dust storms lasting months that can reduce solar energy by 97%. Every one of these threats is independently lethal.

• Systems Interdependence:
  A Mars habitat is a web of interdependencies. Atmosphere control requires energy for electrolysis and CO2 scrubbing. Water recycling requires energy and produces oxygen as a byproduct. Food production requires water, light energy, CO2, and stable temperatures. Waste management feeds composted material back to food production. Temperature regulation demands constant energy. When one system degrades, others follow in a predictable cascade that engineers must anticipate and design for.

• The Energy Bottleneck:
  Energy is the master variable in Mars habitat design. Solar panels on Mars receive only 43% of the sunlight Earth gets, and global dust storms can reduce that to 3% for weeks. Nuclear fission reactors like NASA's Kilopower provide reliable baseline power but are heavy and difficult to maintain. The habitat must balance solar's mass efficiency against nuclear's reliability, with enough total capacity to run all systems plus a safety margin for emergencies.

• Human Factors in Extreme Isolation:
  Psychological research from Antarctic stations, submarine deployments, and ISS missions shows that crew mental health degrades predictably in isolation: performance drops after 3-4 months, interpersonal conflicts intensify, and decision-making quality decreases. On Mars, with 4-24 minute communication delays to Earth and no possibility of evacuation, psychological well-being is not a luxury — it directly affects whether the crew can maintain the systems keeping them alive.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Mars habitats just need to be airtight and warm"
  Reality: Maintaining breathable air and comfortable temperature are necessary but nowhere near sufficient. A sealed, warm box without radiation shielding exposes the crew to 0.67 millisieverts per day — enough to significantly increase cancer risk within months. Without water recycling, the crew runs out of water in weeks. Without food production, stored supplies last 1-2 years at most. Mars habitat design is a nine-dimensional optimization problem, not a two-variable one.
  Strategy: Challenge: Give students a sealed, warm box and ask them to list everything else they would need to survive for one year. Watch the list grow to dozens of items — that is the real scope of the problem.

• "Solar panels work the same on Mars as on Earth"
  Reality: Mars receives only 43% of the solar energy Earth does because it is 1.5 times farther from the Sun. During global dust storms, which occur every few Mars years and last months, solar energy can drop to as low as 3% of Earth-normal levels. This means a Mars habitat cannot rely solely on solar power the way a house on Earth can — it needs either massive battery storage, nuclear backup, or both.
  Strategy: Calculate: If a solar panel produces 300 watts on Earth, how much does it produce on Mars on a clear day? During a dust storm? How many panels would you need to power a 40 kW habitat during a storm?

• "Psychological well-being is a comfort issue, not an engineering issue"
  Reality: Research from Antarctic stations, submarines, and the ISS consistently shows that crew psychological deterioration directly causes system failures. The Mars-500 isolation experiment (520 days in a simulated Mars habitat) documented sleep disruption, cognitive decline, interpersonal conflicts, and motivation loss that would have compromised system maintenance on a real mission. On Mars, a crew member who is too depressed to perform maintenance checks could miss a failing component that kills everyone.
  Strategy: Case study: In the Biosphere 2 experiment, crew conflicts became so severe that the team split into two factions that refused to speak to each other — while responsible for maintaining the life support systems keeping them all alive. What would happen on Mars?

ANSWER KEY:
Big Question: What combination of engineering systems would actually keep humans alive on Mars — and what happens when one fails?
Answer: Keeping humans alive on Mars requires nine interdependent systems operating simultaneously with no possibility of quick rescue. Energy generation is the keystone — it powers atmosphere control, water recycling, temperature regulation, food production lighting, and waste processing. When energy drops during a dust storm, engineers must triage: atmosphere and temperature first, then water, then food. Psychological well-being degrades as conditions worsen, creating a feedback loop where stressed crews make worse decisions. The solution is redundancy, graceful degradation, and designing every system to survive at reduced capacity.

Simulation Answers:
• Nominal Operations Scenario: When all nine systems operate at designed capacity, the habitat maintains stable conditions for crew survival. Atmosphere stays at 21% oxygen, water recycling exceeds 98% efficiency, food production meets caloric needs, temperature remains stable, and crew psychological well-being scores remain high. The model shows significant margin in most systems, demonstrating that a well-designed habitat operates well within safety limits during normal conditions.
• Dust Storm Crisis Scenario: When Energy Generation drops to 50%, the cascade begins within hours. Atmosphere Control and Temperature Regulation compete for limited power. Water Recycling efficiency drops as pumps slow. Food Production lighting is rationed, slowing crop growth. Waste Management processing slows. Within two weeks, crew must shift to emergency rations. Psychological Well-Being declines as conditions worsen, creating a feedback loop where stressed crew members make more maintenance errors. The model reveals that survival depends on pre-planned triage protocols that prioritize atmosphere and temperature over food and waste processing.

Reflection Exemplars:
• Q: Why is energy the keystone variable?
  A: Energy Generation connects to seven of the eight other systems in the model. Atmosphere control needs power for electrolysis and CO2 scrubbing. Water recycling needs power for pumps and filtration. Food production needs power for grow lights. Temperature regulation needs power for heating in the Martian cold. Even waste management needs power for processing. When energy drops, every downstream system degrades simultaneously, creating multiple cascading crises that compound each other. This is why NASA prioritizes energy redundancy above almost everything else in habitat design.
• Q: How does Psychological Well-Being create feedback loops?
  A: Psychological Well-Being interacts bidirectionally with multiple systems. When food production drops and crew shifts to emergency rations, morale decreases. When temperature regulation struggles and the habitat gets cold, stress increases. But the feedback runs both ways — psychologically stressed crew members make more errors in system maintenance, respond more slowly to alarms, and conflict with each other, all of which degrade system performance further. Antarctic research shows that after 3-4 months in isolation, crew performance drops 20-30%, which on Mars could be the difference between catching a system failure early and catastrophic cascading failure.

Key Concepts:
• Graceful Degradation: Well-designed systems do not fail catastrophically when a component degrades — they reduce performance incrementally while maintaining core functions. A Mars habitat must be designed so that reduced energy triggers automatic load-shedding that preserves life-critical systems.
• Triage Engineering: When resources are insufficient to maintain all systems, engineers must prioritize based on failure timeline — systems where degradation kills in hours (atmosphere, pressure) are prioritized over those where degradation kills in weeks (food, psychological health).
• Human Factors Integration: Engineering solutions that ignore human psychology fail in practice. Crew scheduling, communication protocols, exercise requirements, and social structures are as essential to Mars survival as air recycling and water purification.

Evaluation Rubric:
• Expert (4): Protocol includes evidence-based priority ranking of all nine systems, quantitative energy allocation percentages, crew rotation schedules, psychological intervention triggers, and contingency plans for secondary failures during the crisis
• Proficient (3): Protocol prioritizes systems logically, allocates energy with justification from model data, and addresses crew well-being
• Developing (2): Protocol identifies critical systems but lacks quantitative energy allocation or does not address psychological factors
• Beginning (1): Protocol is incomplete, does not reference model data, or fails to address cascading failures

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format. Each item is three-dimensional, assessing a Science and Engineering Practice (SEP), Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and Crosscutting Concept (CCC) simultaneously, aligned to HS-ESS1-4, HS-ETS1-1.

---

### Pre-Assessment Questions

### Question 1

A Mars habitat relies on solar panels for energy and a closed-loop system for water recycling. If a dust storm reduces solar output by 50%, which outcome is most likely based on systems engineering principles?

A. Only the lighting system is affected because other systems have independent power sources
B. Water recycling, temperature regulation, and food production all degrade because they depend on the shared energy supply
C. The habitat automatically switches to backup wind power from Martian winds
D. Crew psychological well-being improves because dust storms reduce outside radiation exposure

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. In an interdependent system, energy generation is the keystone variable. When it degrades, all downstream systems that require power — water recycling, temperature regulation, food production lighting — are affected in a cascading failure. Consider that most habitat systems require continuous electrical power. When the primary energy source degrades, any system dependent on that power will also degrade. This is the principle of cascading failure in interdependent systems.

---

### Question 2

Why is systems redundancy considered essential in Mars habitat engineering but less critical for buildings on Earth?

A. Mars habitats use more advanced technology that breaks down more frequently
B. Earth buildings never experience system failures
C. On Mars, there is no possibility of rapid resupply or external rescue, so a single-point failure could be fatal
D. Redundancy is equally important on Earth but is never implemented due to cost

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: Correct. The nearest help is 225 million km away with a 6-9 month transit time. Unlike Earth, where emergency services, replacement parts, and evacuation are available, Mars demands built-in redundancy because there is no external backup. Think about what makes Mars fundamentally different from Earth as an engineering environment. The key factor is isolation — the inability to receive help, parts, or rescue in a timely manner.

### Question 3

In-situ resource utilization (ISRU) on Mars involves extracting oxygen from atmospheric CO2. Which scientific principle best explains why this is preferable to transporting oxygen from Earth?

A. Martian oxygen is chemically purer than Earth-produced oxygen
B. The energy cost of launching mass from Earth's gravity well makes local resource harvesting far more economical
C. Oxygen cannot survive the radiation environment during the transit from Earth to Mars
D. Earth does not have sufficient oxygen reserves to supply a Mars colony

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. Launching mass from Earth costs approximately $10,000-$50,000 per kilogram. Extracting resources locally eliminates launch mass costs, making ISRU essential for long-term sustainability despite the energy required for extraction. Consider the economics and physics of space travel. Moving mass from Earth's surface to Mars requires overcoming Earth's gravitational pull with enormous amounts of rocket fuel, making every kilogram extremely expensive.

### Question 4

A habitat engineer argues that psychological well-being should be deprioritized during a crisis because it is not a physical survival system. What is the strongest scientific counterargument?

A. Psychological well-being has no measurable effect on physical systems
B. Degraded mental health directly impairs crew decision-making, maintenance quality, and conflict resolution — creating feedback loops that worsen the crisis
C. Crew members can simply be ordered to maintain good morale during emergencies
D. Psychological monitoring equipment uses too much energy during power shortages

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. Psychological well-being is a survival variable, not a luxury. Research from ISS missions and Antarctic isolation studies shows that degraded mental health leads to poor decisions, maintenance errors, and interpersonal conflict — all of which can compound a physical crisis. Consider how human performance under stress affects every other system. If crew members are making poor decisions due to psychological deterioration, every system they maintain or manage is at greater risk.

### Question 5

Which engineering design philosophy is most appropriate for a Mars habitat that must survive unpredictable equipment failures?

A. Design for maximum efficiency so every component operates at peak capacity with no wasted resources
B. Design for graceful degradation so the habitat can operate at reduced capacity rather than failing completely
C. Design for minimum mass to reduce launch costs, even if it means eliminating backup systems
D. Design for rapid replacement so failed components can be swapped out within hours

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. Graceful degradation means the habitat continues functioning at reduced capacity during failures rather than experiencing catastrophic collapse. This approach keeps the crew alive while repairs are made, unlike binary success/failure designs. Think about what happens when a system fails on Mars. The best engineering approach is one where failure of a component reduces performance but does not cause total system collapse.

### Question 1

A student's computational model shows that reducing Energy Generation by 40% causes Atmosphere Control to drop to critical levels within 2 days, but Food Production does not reach critical levels for 30 days. What does this difference reveal about crisis management priorities?

A. Food Production is not important during a crisis
B. Atmosphere Control has a shorter time-to-critical because breathable air cannot be stored in large quantities, while food reserves provide a buffer
C. The model must contain an error because all systems should fail at the same rate
D. Energy Generation does not actually affect Atmosphere Control

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. Different systems have different buffer capacities. Atmosphere composition changes within hours to days without active management, while stored food can sustain crew for weeks. This time-to-critical difference directly informs triage priorities during energy crises. Consider that different systems have different reserves or buffers. A system with large stored reserves (food) can tolerate power loss longer than a system requiring continuous operation (air composition management).

### Question 2

In the Mars habitat model, Energy Generation is classified as an external (input) variable while Temperature Regulation is classified as internal. What is the best justification for this distinction?

A. External variables are always more important than internal variables
B. Energy Generation represents a resource that engineers directly control, while Temperature Regulation is a system response that depends on energy availability and environmental conditions
C. Internal variables cannot be measured, only estimated
D. The classification is arbitrary and could be reversed without affecting the model

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. In computational modeling, external (input) variables represent quantities that operators set or control directly. Internal variables emerge from the interactions between inputs and system dynamics. Energy Generation is set by the power system design; Temperature Regulation responds to available energy and Mars surface conditions. Think about the difference between variables you directly set (inputs you control) and variables that result from the system's behavior (outputs that emerge from interactions).

### Question 3

The model predicts that during a 45-day dust storm, crew psychological well-being degrades, which causes maintenance quality to decline, which causes additional system failures. This pattern is best described as:

A. A linear cause-and-effect chain with no feedback
B. A positive feedback loop where declining well-being and system failures reinforce each other
C. A negative feedback loop that stabilizes the system at a lower performance level
D. An artifact of the model that would not occur in a real habitat

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. This is a positive (amplifying) feedback loop: psychological stress reduces maintenance quality, which causes more system failures, which increases stress further. Without intervention, the loop spirals toward increasingly dangerous conditions. Consider the direction of the cycle: does each step make the next step better or worse? When worsening conditions cause further worsening in a circular pattern, that is a specific type of feedback loop.

### Question 4

A team redesigns the habitat to use nuclear power instead of solar as the primary energy source. According to the model's systems logic, which prediction is best supported?

A. All other variables remain unchanged because only the energy source is different
B. The dust storm crisis scenario becomes far less severe because nuclear power is unaffected by atmospheric dust, eliminating the primary trigger for cascading failures
C. Nuclear power eliminates the need for systems redundancy entirely
D. Psychological well-being automatically improves because nuclear energy is more powerful

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. The dust storm crisis in the model is triggered by reduced solar energy generation. Nuclear power operates independently of atmospheric conditions, so the primary cascade trigger is eliminated. However, nuclear introduces different risks (radiation, mechanical failure) that would need to be modeled separately. Consider what specifically triggers the cascading failure in the dust storm scenario. If the energy source is not affected by dust, does the same cascade occur?

### Question 5

A model limitation is that it uses nine components to represent a habitat that would actually contain hundreds of subsystems. How does this simplification most likely affect the model's predictions?

A. The model cannot provide any useful predictions because it is too simplified
B. The model accurately captures overall system behavior and cascade dynamics but may miss specific failure modes or unexpected interactions between subsystems not represented
C. Simplification makes the model more accurate because complexity introduces errors
D. The model overestimates system resilience because it includes too many redundant components

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. Model simplification is a standard scientific practice that captures essential dynamics while sacrificing detail. The nine-component model reveals fundamental interdependencies and cascade patterns but cannot predict every specific failure pathway that hundreds of subsystems might produce. Think about the purpose of modeling. All models are simplifications of reality. The question is whether the simplification preserves the important dynamics (cascade effects, interdependencies) even if it misses specific details.
