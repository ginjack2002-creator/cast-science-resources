### Title
**Carbon Capture at Scale: Can We Vacuum the Atmosphere? — Modeling the Engineering and Economics of Removing CO2 from the Sky**

### Overview
Humanity has added over 1.5 trillion tons of CO2 to the atmosphere since the Industrial Revolution, raising concentrations from 280 to 420+ parts per million. Even if all emissions stopped today, the CO2 already in the atmosphere will continue warming the planet for centuries. Carbon capture technology offers the possibility of actively removing this legacy CO2 — but the gap between what is technically possible and what is needed to impact climate is staggering. Students investigate the driving question: Can technology remove enough CO2 from the atmosphere to actually reverse climate change — or is it just an expensive distraction? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

### Learning Objectives
- Model how ten interdependent variables determine whether carbon capture technology can operate at a climate-relevant scale
- Analyze the engineering trade-offs between capture efficiency, energy consumption, and economic viability
- Evaluate whether direct air capture can remove CO2 fast enough to counteract ongoing emissions
- Predict the conditions under which carbon capture becomes economically self-sustaining through carbon credit markets

### Component List (Starting Model: 10 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| CO2 Concentration | External | The atmospheric concentration of carbon dioxide at the capture site, currently approximately 420 parts per million and rising — higher concentrations make capture easier but indicate worsening climate conditions |
| Capture Rate | Internal | The mass of CO2 removed from the atmosphere per unit time, measured in tons per day — a single large facility currently captures about 4,000 tons per year, while global emissions exceed 36 billion tons per year |
| Sorbent Efficiency | Internal | The percentage of CO2 molecules that the sorbent material successfully captures from the air passing through the contactor — affected by temperature, humidity, sorbent degradation, and contact time |
| Energy Input | External | The total energy required to operate fans, regenerate sorbents, compress CO2, and run support systems — currently 6-10 gigajoules per ton of CO2 captured |
| Storage Capacity | Internal | The total volume of CO2 that can be permanently sequestered in available geological formations — globally estimated at 2,000+ gigatons, far exceeding removal needs |
| Reaction Temperature | Internal | The temperature required to release captured CO2 from the sorbent and regenerate it for reuse — typically 80-120 degrees Celsius for solid sorbents, requiring significant thermal energy |
| Regeneration Cost | Internal | The economic cost of the energy and materials needed to regenerate sorbents and compress CO2 for transport and storage — the single largest operating expense in direct air capture |
| Transport Distance | External | The distance between the capture facility and the geological storage site — longer distances increase cost and energy consumption for pipeline or tanker transport of compressed CO2 |
| Geological Sequestration | Internal | The effectiveness and permanence of underground CO2 storage, including injection rate, formation integrity, monitoring requirements, and risk of leakage over centuries |
| Carbon Credit Value | Internal | The market price paid per ton of CO2 removed, which must exceed the total cost of capture, transport, and storage for the operation to be economically viable — currently ranging from $100-$1,000 per ton |

• Click on "Carbon Credit Value" and drag an arrow to "Capture Rate"

• Look at the +/− toggle in the TOP LEFT corner
• For each connection, ask: "When this goes UP, does the other go UP or DOWN?"

  ○ Energy Input → Capture Rate = POSITIVE (+)
    More energy powers more fans and sorbent regeneration cycles, increasing the volume of air processed and the mass of CO2 captured per day. Energy is the primary driver of capture throughput.

  ○ Sorbent Efficiency → Regeneration Cost = NEGATIVE (−)
    Higher sorbent efficiency means more CO2 is captured per regeneration cycle, reducing the number of cycles needed and the thermal energy consumed. More efficient sorbents lower the per-ton cost of regeneration.

  ○ Carbon Credit Value → Capture Rate = POSITIVE (+)
    Higher carbon credit prices create economic incentive to invest in larger facilities and more efficient technology, increasing capture capacity. When credits are too cheap, facilities cannot cover operating costs and scale stagnates.

STEP 4: CHECK YOUR MODEL
• You should have 3 arrows total
• 1 negative relationship(s), 2 positive relationship(s)
• This is your system model!

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

WHAT DID YOU DISCOVER?
• The energy source powering a carbon capture facility determines whether it is a climate solution or a climate problem — fossil-powered capture can emit more CO2 than it removes
• At current costs of $400-1,000 per ton, direct air capture is far more expensive than most emission reduction strategies — planting trees captures CO2 at $5-50 per ton, though with less permanence
• Scaling to climate-relevant levels (gigatons per year) requires building an industry comparable in size to the current oil and gas sector — in reverse
• Carbon credits create a necessary economic driver, but the market price must reach $200+ per ton sustainably for direct air capture to be economically viable without subsidies

THE ANSWER: Carbon capture technology works — the chemistry and engineering are proven. The challenge is scale and economics. Humanity emits 36+ billion tons of CO2 per year. The largest direct air capture facility removes about 4,000 tons per year. Reaching climate-relevant scale requires building millions of facilities, powered entirely by clean energy, at costs that the carbon credit market can sustain. Current costs of $400-1,000 per ton must drop below $200 for economic viability. It is not a distraction — it is a necessary part of the climate solution — but it cannot substitute for reducing emissions at the source.

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• How Direct Air Capture Works:
  Direct air capture (DAC) uses large fans to draw ambient air across chemical sorbents that selectively bind CO2 molecules. Solid sorbent systems (like Climeworks' technology) use amine-functionalized filters that capture CO2 at ambient temperature and release it when heated to 80-120 degrees Celsius. Liquid solvent systems (like Carbon Engineering's approach) bubble air through potassium hydroxide solution, then use a calcination process at 900 degrees Celsius to release pure CO2. Both approaches require significant energy for sorbent regeneration and CO2 compression.

• The Scale Challenge:
  Global CO2 emissions exceed 36 billion tons per year. The world's largest DAC facility (Climeworks' Orca in Iceland) captures 4,000 tons per year. To capture just 1% of annual emissions would require 90,000 Orca-sized facilities. To make a meaningful climate impact by removing 10 billion tons per year by 2050 would require building an industry roughly the size of the current global oil and gas sector — but in reverse. This scale challenge is the most sobering aspect of carbon capture.

• Energy and Economics:
  Current DAC costs range from $400-1,000 per ton of CO2 captured, depending on technology and energy source. The energy required is 6-10 GJ per ton — meaning capturing 1 billion tons would consume roughly 1% of global energy production. If that energy comes from fossil fuels, the capture process emits substantial CO2, potentially negating 30-50% of what it captures. Only when powered by clean energy does DAC achieve its full removal potential. Cost projections suggest $100-200 per ton may be achievable by 2050 with technology improvements and scale.

• Geological Storage and Permanence:
  Captured CO2 must be permanently stored to have climate benefit. Geological sequestration injects compressed CO2 into deep formations: saline aquifers (porous rock saturated with saltwater), depleted oil and gas reservoirs, or basalt formations where CO2 mineralizes into carbonate rock within years. Global geological storage capacity is estimated at 2,000+ gigatons — far more than needed. The key challenges are ensuring long-term integrity (no leakage over centuries), monitoring stored CO2, and building injection infrastructure near capture sites.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Carbon capture means we do not need to reduce emissions"
  Reality: Carbon capture and emission reduction are complementary, not substitutional. The scale mismatch is enormous: we emit 36+ billion tons per year and capture thousands. Even optimistic projections suggest capture reaching 10 billion tons per year by 2050 — still less than a third of current emissions. Carbon capture is essential for removing legacy CO2 and addressing sectors where emissions are hardest to eliminate (aviation, cement, steel), but it cannot compensate for continued large-scale fossil fuel burning. Framing it as an alternative to emission reduction is both scientifically and economically unrealistic.
  Strategy: Scale exercise: If carbon capture costs $200/ton and we need to remove 36 billion tons per year, that is $7.2 trillion annually — roughly 8% of global GDP. Clearly capture alone is not the answer.

• "Planting trees is always better than technology-based capture"
  Reality: Trees are excellent carbon sinks at $5-50 per ton, but they have limitations: they require large land areas (competing with agriculture), take decades to reach full sequestration capacity, are vulnerable to wildfires and disease that release stored carbon, and saturate as forests mature. Direct air capture is more expensive but offers permanent geological storage, requires less land, and captures CO2 regardless of season or weather. The optimal strategy uses both: trees for affordable, near-term removal and DAC for permanent, scalable, verifiable removal.
  Strategy: Compare: A hectare of forest sequesters about 10 tons of CO2 per year but takes 20 years to mature and can burn down. A DAC facility occupies less land, starts at full capacity immediately, and stores CO2 underground permanently. Neither is the complete answer alone.

• "Captured CO2 will leak back out of underground storage"
  Reality: Geological sequestration is remarkably permanent when done correctly. CO2 injected into deep saline aquifers dissolves in brine, reacts with minerals, and eventually converts to carbonate rock — a process that takes centuries but is essentially permanent. Natural CO2 reservoirs have held gas underground for millions of years. Monitoring technology can detect leaks of less than 0.01% per year. The real risk is not geological — it is regulatory: ensuring that storage sites are properly selected, operated, and monitored for the long term.
  Strategy: Evidence: Natural CO2 deposits like those in the Pisgah Crater formation in California have held CO2 underground for over 100,000 years. If nature can store CO2 for millennia, engineered storage with monitoring can certainly do the same.

ANSWER KEY:
Big Question: Can technology remove enough CO2 from the atmosphere to actually reverse climate change — or is it just an expensive distraction?
Answer: Carbon capture technology works — the chemistry and engineering are proven. The challenge is scale and economics. Humanity emits 36+ billion tons of CO2 per year. The largest direct air capture facility removes about 4,000 tons per year. Reaching climate-relevant scale requires building millions of facilities, powered entirely by clean energy, at costs that the carbon credit market can sustain. Current costs of $400-1,000 per ton must drop below $200 for economic viability. It is not a distraction — it is a necessary part of the climate solution — but it cannot substitute for reducing emissions at the source.

Simulation Answers:
• Current Technology Scenario: At 2024 parameters with a mixed energy grid, the facility captures CO2 but the net removal is reduced by 30-50% due to emissions from fossil-powered energy inputs. The cost per ton exceeds $600, well above current carbon credit prices for most markets. The model shows that current technology is a successful proof of concept but is not yet economically self-sustaining — it requires subsidies or premium voluntary carbon credits from corporations with net-zero commitments.
• Clean Energy Powered Scenario: When powered entirely by renewable energy, the net CO2 removal matches the gross capture rate — no emissions from energy generation offset the removal. This dramatically improves the climate impact per ton captured. However, the cost remains high because renewable energy infrastructure must be dedicated to the capture facility. The model reveals that the energy source is the single most important variable determining whether carbon capture is genuinely beneficial for the climate.

Reflection Exemplars:
• Q: Why is the energy source so critical?
  A: The energy-emissions paradox is the central insight of the model. Capturing one ton of CO2 requires 6-10 GJ of energy. If that energy comes from natural gas, it generates roughly 0.3-0.5 tons of CO2 in the process, immediately reducing net removal by 30-50%. If powered by coal, the capture process could emit MORE CO2 than it captures, making it actively harmful to the climate. Only clean energy — solar, wind, nuclear, geothermal — delivers the full removal benefit. This is not just an engineering detail; it is the fundamental determinant of whether carbon capture is a solution or a distraction.
• Q: Can carbon capture substitute for reducing emissions?
  A: The model makes the scale mismatch devastatingly clear. Global emissions are 36+ billion tons per year. The largest capture facility removes 4,000 tons per year. Even if costs dropped to $100 per ton and we built millions of facilities, removing 10 billion tons per year would cost $1 trillion annually and consume enormous amounts of clean energy that could otherwise replace fossil fuels. Carbon capture is necessary for removing legacy CO2 and offsetting hard-to-abate emissions (aviation, cement, steel), but it cannot compensate for continued large-scale fossil fuel burning. Both emission reduction AND carbon removal are needed.

Key Concepts:
• Techno-Economic Analysis: Evaluating a technology requires analyzing both technical performance (capture rate, efficiency, energy use) and economic feasibility (capital cost, operating cost, revenue streams). A technically superior system that costs too much to operate will never scale.
• Learning Curves: Technology costs typically decrease by 15-25% with each doubling of cumulative production — solar panels dropped 99% in cost over 40 years following this pattern. Carbon capture is early on its learning curve, with significant cost reduction potential.
• Carbon Market Mechanisms: Carbon credits can be compliance-based (required by regulation) or voluntary (purchased by companies for net-zero goals). Compliance markets (like the EU Emissions Trading System) provide more stable demand but lower prices, while voluntary markets offer higher prices but less predictable demand.

Evaluation Rubric:
• Expert (4): Business plan includes detailed technology selection, clean energy sourcing, geological storage assessment, 10-year cost projection with learning curve assumptions, and carbon credit revenue analysis demonstrating path to profitability — all justified with model evidence
• Proficient (3): Business plan specifies technology and energy source, identifies storage location, and projects costs with reasonable economic assumptions
• Developing (2): Business plan describes a capture approach but lacks quantitative cost projections or does not address the energy-emissions paradox
• Beginning (1): Business plan is incomplete or does not demonstrate understanding of the relationship between cost, energy, and climate impact
