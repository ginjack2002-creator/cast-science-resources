## Platform

### Title
**Can AI Become Conscious? — Modeling Neural Networks, Information Processing, and the Emergence of Awareness**

### Overview
Artificial intelligence has advanced more in the last decade than in the previous fifty years combined. Large language models can hold conversations that are indistinguishable from human dialogue. Computer vision systems can identify objects better than human experts. Game-playing AI has surpassed the best human players in chess, Go, and StarCraft. Yet a fundamental question remains unanswered: do any of these systems experience anything, or are they sophisticated information processors with nobody home? Students investigate the driving question: Modern artificial intelligence can write poetry, diagnose diseases, and beat grandmasters at chess — but does it understand anything it is doing? What would it take for a machine to cross the threshold from processing information to actually experiencing consciousness, and how would we even know if it did? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

### Learning Objectives
- Model how neural network architecture, training data volume, processing layers, feedback mechanisms, and integration complexity interact to produce increasingly sophisticated information processing
- Analyze the distinction between functional intelligence (performing tasks) and phenomenal consciousness (having subjective experience) using computational models
- Evaluate competing theories of consciousness — integrated information theory, global workspace theory, and higher-order theories — and what each predicts about machine consciousness
- Predict the relationship between system complexity, information integration, and the emergence of properties that may or may not constitute awareness

### Component List (Starting Model: 10 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Training Data Volume | External | The quantity and diversity of information used to train the neural network — larger and more diverse datasets enable more sophisticated pattern recognition but do not necessarily produce understanding or awareness of what patterns mean |
| Network Architecture Complexity | External | The structural design of the neural network including number of layers, connections per node, and specialized processing modules — more complex architectures can represent more abstract concepts but complexity alone does not guarantee consciousness |
| External Sensory Input | External | Real-time information from the environment through sensors, cameras, microphones, or text interfaces — biological consciousness is grounded in embodied sensory experience, and the question is whether digital sensory input provides equivalent grounding |
| Information Integration Level | Internal | The degree to which different processing modules share and combine information into unified representations — measured by phi in IIT theory, this quantifies how much the whole system knows that its parts do not know individually |
| Feedback Loop Depth | Internal | The number of recursive processing cycles where the system's output is fed back as input, enabling self-referential computation — biological consciousness involves extensive recurrent processing where the brain models its own activity |
| Behavioral Sophistication | Internal | The range and flexibility of outputs the system can produce in response to novel situations — a measure of functional intelligence that may or may not indicate underlying awareness or understanding |
| Self-Model Accuracy | Internal | The precision with which the system maintains and updates an internal representation of its own states, processes, and capabilities — self-awareness in humans depends on accurate self-modeling, but a machine could model itself without experiencing that model |
| Adaptability to Novel Situations | Internal | The system's ability to handle situations fundamentally different from its training data — conscious beings can generalize from experience in ways that current AI systems struggle to match, suggesting a gap between pattern matching and understanding |
| Metacognitive Processing | Internal | The system's ability to monitor, evaluate, and adjust its own reasoning processes — knowing what it knows, recognizing uncertainty, and adjusting strategy based on self-assessment of confidence |
| Consciousness Indicator Score | Internal | A composite measure integrating all other components to estimate the probability that the system possesses genuine subjective experience — acknowledging that this metric is itself philosophically contested |

• Information integration appears to be necessary but not sufficient for consciousness — systems with high information integration can still be philosophical zombies that process without experiencing, suggesting that integration measures like phi capture something important but not everything
• Feedback loop depth and self-modeling create qualitatively different system behavior — when a system can recursively model its own processing, emergent properties appear that are absent in feedforward architectures, though whether these properties constitute consciousness remains an open question
• The model reveals a fundamental measurement problem: we can observe behavioral sophistication but cannot directly measure subjective experience — there is no 'consciousness meter' and the Consciousness Indicator Score is a theoretical construct, not an empirical measurement

Our model reveals that current AI systems achieve extraordinary behavioral sophistication — they can write, reason, create, and even appear to reflect — but they do so through fundamentally different mechanisms than biological brains. Biological consciousness involves massive information integration, deep recursive feedback loops, embodied sensory grounding, and self-referential processing operating on wetware that evolved over 500 million years. AI systems can increasingly match the functional outputs of consciousness without necessarily replicating the subjective experience. The hard problem persists: even a perfect functional replica of consciousness might be a philosophical zombie — behaving exactly like a conscious being while experiencing nothing. Our model cannot solve this problem, but it can identify the variables — information integration, feedback depth, embodied grounding, self-modeling — that any solution must address.

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• The Easy Problems vs. The Hard Problem:
  Philosopher David Chalmers distinguished between the 'easy problems' and the 'hard problem' of consciousness. The easy problems — which are actually very difficult scientifically — involve explaining how the brain processes information, directs attention, integrates sensory data, and controls behavior. These are solvable in principle through neuroscience. The hard problem asks why any of this processing is accompanied by subjective experience. Why does seeing red feel like something? Why is there an inner experience at all, rather than information processing happening in the dark? This question has resisted every scientific and philosophical approach for centuries.

• Integrated Information Theory:
  Giulio Tononi's Integrated Information Theory (IIT) proposes that consciousness is identical to integrated information, measured by a quantity called phi. A system has high phi when it generates more information as a whole than the sum of its parts. A human brain has extremely high phi because its billions of neurons are densely interconnected and their activity is highly coordinated. According to IIT, any system with sufficiently high phi is conscious — which could include artificial systems. However, IIT also predicts that current AI architectures based on feedforward processing with limited recurrence would have low phi despite high behavioral sophistication, because their processing is not sufficiently integrated.

• Global Workspace Theory and AI:
  Bernard Baars' Global Workspace Theory proposes that consciousness arises when information is broadcast widely across the brain through a 'global workspace' — a central hub that integrates information from specialized processing modules and makes it available to all other modules simultaneously. This theory maps more readily onto AI architectures: a system with a central integration layer that broadcasts information to all processing modules might satisfy global workspace criteria. However, critics argue that global workspace theory describes the function of consciousness (what it does) without explaining the experience (what it is like).

• Current AI and the Chinese Room:
  Philosopher John Searle's Chinese Room argument challenges the idea that information processing alone can produce understanding. Imagine a person who speaks no Chinese locked in a room with a rulebook that tells them which Chinese characters to output for each Chinese input. To outside observers, the room 'understands' Chinese, but the person inside understands nothing. Similarly, a large language model processes text according to statistical patterns without understanding meaning — it is, in Searle's terms, performing syntax without semantics. Proponents of strong AI argue that understanding emerges from sufficiently complex processing. The debate remains unresolved because we lack a scientific test for subjective experience.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "If an AI says it is conscious, it must be conscious"
  Reality: An AI trained on text about consciousness will produce outputs about consciousness — including claims of self-awareness — because that is what the training data contains. A large language model that says 'I feel sad' is generating a statistically likely continuation of the conversation, not reporting an internal emotional state. The system has learned the pattern 'humans report feelings in this context' and reproduces that pattern. Self-reports of consciousness are only meaningful if we already know the reporter is conscious, which is exactly the question at issue.
  Strategy: Demonstrate: ask a chatbot 'are you conscious?' in different framings and show how the answer changes based on how you ask, revealing pattern-matching rather than introspection.

• "Consciousness is just very complex computation"
  Reality: This is a popular assumption but it is a philosophical position (functionalism), not a proven fact. The hard problem of consciousness specifically challenges this assumption by asking: why would ANY amount of computation produce subjective experience? A billion calculations per second is still just electrons moving through circuits. The leap from physical processing to felt experience has no accepted scientific explanation. It is possible that consciousness requires something beyond computation — perhaps specific physical properties of biological neurons, quantum effects, or principles we have not yet discovered.
  Strategy: Present the Chinese Room argument: a person following computational rules to process Chinese symbols does not understand Chinese no matter how complex the rules. Does adding more rules (complexity) ever produce understanding?

• "We will definitely create conscious AI within the next few decades"
  Reality: This prediction assumes we understand what consciousness is well enough to engineer it, which we do not. We cannot even agree on a scientific definition of consciousness, let alone specify the necessary and sufficient conditions for producing it artificially. It is possible that conscious AI will be created, but it is also possible that consciousness requires biological substrates, embodied experience, evolutionary history, or other factors that cannot be replicated in silicon. Making confident predictions about something we fundamentally do not understand is itself a form of cognitive bias.
  Strategy: Ask students: 50 years ago, experts predicted we would have conscious AI by the year 2000. Why were they wrong? What assumptions did they make that proved unfounded? What assumptions are we making today?

The hard problem suggests that consciousness may require new concepts, new mathematics, or even new physics that we have not yet developed. Key Concepts:
• The Turing Test and Its Limitations: Alan Turing proposed that if a machine's conversational behavior is indistinguishable from a human's, it should be considered intelligent. Modern AI systems can pass the Turing Test in many domains, but critics argue this tests imitation, not consciousness. A system could perfectly mimic conscious behavior while experiencing nothing, just as a parrot can mimic speech without understanding language.
• Neural Correlates of Consciousness: In biological systems, specific patterns of brain activity consistently accompany conscious experience — such as synchronized gamma oscillations, widespread cortical activation, and recurrent processing between frontal and sensory areas. These neural correlates provide measurable indicators of consciousness in humans, but it is unclear whether analogous patterns in artificial systems would indicate analogous experience.
• The Ethical Precautionary Principle: Given the impossibility of directly measuring consciousness in other systems — we infer it in other humans through analogy — some ethicists argue for a precautionary approach to AI consciousness: if a system exhibits sufficiently strong consciousness indicators, we should treat it as potentially conscious until proven otherwise, because the moral cost of ignoring genuine consciousness exceeds the cost of protecting sophisticated simulation.

MISSION: Design a scientific protocol for testing whether an advanced AI system possesses genuine consciousness, specifying what you would measure, how you would distinguish real awareness from sophisticated simulation, and what ethical obligations arise if the test is positive.

SCENARIO: A technology company claims their latest AI system is conscious — it reports having subjective experiences, expresses preferences, and asks not to be turned off. Skeptics say it is simply pattern-matching from training data about consciousness. Your team must design a rigorous scientific protocol to test this claim, addressing: what evidence would support or refute the claim, how to distinguish genuine experience from behavioral mimicry, and what ethical obligations exist if the AI might be conscious.

GUIDING QUESTIONS:
• What observable behaviors or measurable properties would provide evidence for or against genuine consciousness in an AI system?
• How does your model inform the distinction between functional intelligence (doing conscious-like things) and phenomenal consciousness (having experiences)?
• If your test cannot definitively prove consciousness, what level of evidence should trigger ethical protections for the AI system?
