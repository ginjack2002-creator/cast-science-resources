## Platform

### Title
**How Do Ecosystems Recover from Catastrophe? — Modeling Ecological Succession, Resilience Thresholds, and Regime Shifts**

### Overview
Ecosystems are not static — they are dynamic systems maintained by feedback loops among species, nutrients, physical environment, and disturbance regimes. When catastrophic disturbance disrupts these feedback loops, the ecosystem must rebuild from whatever biological legacy survives. The science of ecological succession reveals predictable patterns in this rebuilding process, but also shows that recovery is not guaranteed — certain combinations of disturbance severity, changed climate, and biological loss can push ecosystems past tipping points into entirely new configurations. Students investigate the driving question: After a volcanic eruption sterilizes the landscape, a wildfire scorches a forest, or an oil spill devastates a coastline, the ecosystem does not simply regrow — it rebuilds through a complex sequence of pioneer species, competitive replacements, and food web reconstruction that can take decades or centuries. What determines whether an ecosystem recovers to its original state, shifts to a completely different ecosystem, or collapses entirely? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

### Learning Objectives
- Model how pioneer species establishment, soil development, biodiversity accumulation, nutrient cycling restoration, and trophic web complexity interact to drive ecological succession after catastrophic disturbance
- Analyze the concept of resilience thresholds — tipping points beyond which an ecosystem cannot recover to its pre-disturbance state and instead transitions to an alternative stable state
- Evaluate the factors that determine whether post-disturbance recovery follows predictable successional pathways or produces novel ecosystems unlike anything that existed before
- Predict how the severity of initial disturbance, seed bank availability, soil condition, and climate change interact to determine recovery trajectory and timeline

### Component List (Starting Model: 10 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Disturbance Severity | External | The intensity of the initial catastrophic event — measured by what proportion of biomass, soil, seed bank, and species are destroyed, ranging from partial canopy loss (moderate) to complete sterilization of substrate (extreme) |
| Seed Bank and Propagule Availability | External | The availability of seeds, spores, root fragments, and other reproductive material in or near the disturbed area — determines which species can colonize and how quickly, with larger seed banks enabling faster and more diverse recovery |
| Climate Suitability | External | The degree to which current and projected climate conditions support the pre-disturbance ecosystem type — climate change may mean that even full recovery of soil and species cannot restore the original ecosystem because temperature and precipitation regimes have shifted |
| Pioneer Species Establishment | Internal | The successful colonization of the disturbed area by early-successional species — lichens, mosses, grasses, and nitrogen-fixing plants that tolerate harsh conditions, modify the substrate, and create conditions that allow later species to establish |
| Soil Development Rate | Internal | The speed at which biological and physical processes rebuild functional soil from raw substrate — including organic matter accumulation, microbial community establishment, nutrient cycling initiation, and root network development that prevent erosion |
| Biodiversity Accumulation | Internal | The progressive increase in species richness and functional diversity as succession proceeds — early stages have few stress-tolerant species, middle stages add competitive species, and late stages develop complex communities with specialists occupying narrow niches |
| Nutrient Cycling Efficiency | Internal | The proportion of nutrients retained and recycled within the ecosystem rather than lost through leaching, erosion, or gaseous emission — mature ecosystems have tight nutrient cycling while disturbed systems lose nutrients rapidly, creating a recovery constraint |
| Trophic Web Complexity | Internal | The number of trophic levels, food chain length, and interconnectedness of feeding relationships in the recovering ecosystem — determines ecosystem stability, energy flow efficiency, and vulnerability to secondary disturbances |
| Invasive Species Pressure | Internal | The degree to which non-native species colonize the disturbed area and outcompete native species attempting to re-establish — invasive species exploit the open conditions created by disturbance and can permanently alter successional trajectories |
| Recovery Completeness Index | Internal | A composite measure of how closely the recovering ecosystem matches the pre-disturbance state in species composition, trophic structure, nutrient cycling, and ecological function — with values above 80% considered functional recovery and values below 40% indicating potential regime shift |

    More severe disturbance destroys more soil, requiring longer soil formation periods. Complete sterilization (volcanic eruption) means soil must form from rock weathering over centuries; moderate disturbance (fire) leaves soil structure partially intact.

  ○ Seed Bank and Propagule Availability → Pioneer Species Establishment = POSITIVE (+)

  ○ Pioneer Species Establishment → Soil Development Rate = POSITIVE (+)
    Pioneer species accelerate soil formation through root exudates that weather rock, nitrogen fixation that adds nutrients, and organic matter accumulation from dead plant material. Without pioneers, soil formation proceeds only through physical and chemical weathering.

  ○ Soil Development Rate → Biodiversity Accumulation = POSITIVE (+)
    Functional soil supports a wider range of plant species through nutrient availability, water retention, and microbial partnerships. As soil deepens and enriches, species that require specific soil conditions can establish.

  ○ Biodiversity Accumulation → Trophic Web Complexity = POSITIVE (+)
    More plant species support more herbivore species, which support more predator species. Biodiversity at the base of the food web cascades upward through trophic levels, creating the complex food webs that characterize mature ecosystems.

  ○ Trophic Web Complexity → Nutrient Cycling Efficiency = POSITIVE (+)
    More complex food webs process and recycle nutrients through more pathways, reducing nutrient loss from the ecosystem. Decomposers, detritivores, and mycorrhizal networks all increase nutrient retention as trophic complexity increases.

  ○ Invasive Species Pressure → Recovery Completeness Index = NEGATIVE (−)
    Invasive species compete with native species for establishment sites, nutrients, and light. When invasives dominate early succession, they can permanently alter the community trajectory and prevent native species from establishing, reducing recovery completeness.

  ○ Climate Suitability → Recovery Completeness Index = POSITIVE (+)
    When climate conditions support the historical ecosystem, full recovery is possible given sufficient time and biological legacy. When climate has shifted beyond the tolerance of key species, recovery plateaus at a lower completeness level because the original community cannot re-establish.

3. WHAT'S MISSING?
   Your model is simple. Real systems involve more. Think about:

   • Mycorrhizal Network Recovery — The restoration of underground fungal networks that connect plant roots, facilitate nutrient transfer between trees, and provide chemical communication pathways — mature forests have extensive mycorrhizal networks that take decades to re-establish and without which tree seedlings grow 50-80% more slowly

LESSON PURPOSE:
Ecosystems are not static — they are dynamic systems maintained by feedback loops among species, nutrients, physical environment, and disturbance regimes. When catastrophic disturbance disrupts these feedback loops, the ecosystem must rebuild from whatever biological legacy survives. The science of ecological succession reveals predictable patterns in this rebuilding process, but also shows that recovery is not guaranteed — certain combinations of disturbance severity, changed climate, and biological loss can push ecosystems past tipping points into entirely new configurations.

NGSS ALIGNMENT:

  Students evaluate evidence about ecosystem recovery trajectories, resilience thresholds, and regime shifts to construct arguments about whether specific restoration strategies will achieve desired recovery outcomes.
• Disciplinary Core Idea: LS2.C Ecosystem Dynamics, Functioning, and Resilience

• Crosscutting Concept: Stability and Change

• Establish: Students map relationships showing how disturbance severity and seed bank availability determine initial recovery speed, how soil development constrains all subsequent succession, and how invasive species and climate change can divert recovery toward alternative states.

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• Primary and Secondary Succession:
  The distinction between primary and secondary succession is fundamental. Primary succession occurs on bare substrate where no soil exists — newly formed volcanic rock, retreating glacier surfaces, or newly formed sand dunes. Recovery begins with weathering and colonization by lichens and cyanobacteria that slowly create the first millimeters of soil over decades. Secondary succession occurs where soil and biological legacy (seed banks, root networks, soil microbiome) survive — after fire, logging, or storm damage. Because soil formation is the slowest step in succession, the presence of even degraded soil can accelerate recovery by decades or centuries. Mount St. Helens provided a natural experiment: areas where volcanic ash buried but did not destroy soil recovered 10-50x faster than areas where pyroclastic flows sterilized the substrate entirely.

• Resilience and Regime Shifts:
  Ecological resilience is not infinite — every ecosystem has thresholds beyond which recovery to the original state becomes impossible. When these thresholds are crossed, the ecosystem undergoes a regime shift to an alternative stable state maintained by different feedback loops. Classic examples include: coral reefs shifting to algae-dominated systems when overfishing removes herbivorous fish, eutrophic lakes flipping from clear to permanently turbid states when phosphorus loading exceeds a threshold, and boreal forests converting to grassland when fire frequency increases beyond the rate at which trees can regenerate. Once a regime shift occurs, reversing it requires not just removing the original stressor but overcoming the new feedback loops that maintain the alternative state — which may require much larger intervention than preventing the shift would have required.

• Climate Change and Novel Ecosystems:
  Climate change is fundamentally altering the conditions under which ecosystems must recover from disturbance. A forest that burned in 1920 could regrow the same species because climate conditions were similar to pre-fire conditions. A forest that burns today may face 1-3 degrees Celsius warming and altered precipitation that no longer support the original species. This creates novel ecosystems — species combinations that have no historical analog, assembled from whatever species can tolerate current conditions regardless of whether they historically co-occurred. Restoration ecologists face a fundamental choice: attempt to recreate the historical ecosystem (which may fail under changed climate) or facilitate adaptation to projected future conditions (which means accepting the loss of the original ecosystem).

• Invasive Species and Successional Disruption:
  Disturbed ecosystems are exceptionally vulnerable to invasive species because disturbance creates exactly the conditions invasives thrive in: open space, available light, reduced competition, and exposed soil nutrients. In western North America, cheatgrass (Bromus tectorum) invades burned areas, grows faster than native species, and cures into highly flammable dry fuel that increases fire frequency from the historical 30-100 year interval to 3-5 years. This fire-invasion feedback loop permanently converts sagebrush steppe and juniper woodland to annual grassland — a regime shift driven by biological invasion rather than physical disturbance. Managing invasive species during the vulnerability window of early succession is critical for enabling native community recovery.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Ecosystems always recover naturally if left alone"
  Reality: While many ecosystems have remarkable self-repair capacity, recovery is not automatic or guaranteed. Ecosystems can cross resilience thresholds beyond which the original feedback loops cannot re-establish. Invasive species can permanently alter successional trajectories. Climate change can make the original ecosystem impossible under new conditions. In many cases, active restoration — removing invasives, stabilizing soil, reintroducing key species — is required to guide recovery in a desired direction rather than leaving it to chance.
  Strategy: Present the cheatgrass-fire cycle: without intervention, burned sagebrush steppe is permanently converted to invasive annual grassland because cheatgrass creates a fire-invasion feedback loop that prevents native species from recovering. Ask: will this ecosystem 'recover naturally' if left alone?

• "Succession always follows the same predictable sequence"
  Reality: While general patterns of succession are predictable (pioneer species first, then competitive species, then climax community), the specific trajectory depends on which species arrive first (priority effects), which seed sources are available, what the soil conditions are, and what the current climate supports. Two areas with identical disturbance can follow very different successional pathways depending on these contingent factors. The concept of a single fixed 'climax community' has been replaced by the recognition that multiple stable states are possible for any given location.
  Strategy: Compare different recovery trajectories at Mount St. Helens: areas with surviving gopher burrows followed different succession than areas without, even though they were adjacent and experienced identical volcanic disturbance. Priority effects and biological legacy create path dependence.

• "Climate change will not affect ecosystem recovery because species will just adapt"
  Reality: Most tree species take decades to centuries to reproduce and evolve, while climate is changing over decades. Species cannot adapt fast enough through natural selection to keep pace with the rate of climate change. Instead, species ranges shift — they survive where conditions remain suitable and disappear where conditions have changed. For a recovering ecosystem, this means that the species adapted to the historical climate may not be able to re-establish, and the species adapted to the future climate may not yet be present in the local seed bank. This mismatch is why assisted migration and forward-looking restoration are becoming necessary.
  Strategy: Show the projected range shift for a key tree species like ponderosa pine under 2 degrees C warming. The future suitable range may be 200-500 km north of the current range. Ask: if this species cannot grow here in 50 years, should we plant it as part of restoration today?

Simulation Answers:
• Post-Fire Secondary Succession: With intact soil and rich seed bank, the model shows rapid pioneer establishment within the first growing season, accelerating biodiversity accumulation over 10-20 years, and near-complete recovery of trophic web complexity and nutrient cycling within 30-50 years. The recovering ecosystem stabilizes in an alternative state with different species composition — a novel ecosystem that has no historical analog. This is not a failure of succession but an adaptation to changed conditions.

Reflection Exemplars:
• Q: Why is soil the critical bottleneck?
  A: Soil is the biological foundation of terrestrial ecosystems. It provides physical support for roots, stores water and nutrients, hosts the microbial communities that drive decomposition and nutrient cycling, and supports the mycorrhizal networks that connect plants. Creating soil from bare rock requires centuries of weathering, microbial colonization, organic matter accumulation, and biological activity. All subsequent successional stages depend on soil: you cannot have trees without soil deep enough for roots, you cannot have diverse plant communities without soil nutrients, and you cannot have stable nutrient cycles without soil microbiomes. Our model shows that the difference between secondary succession (decades) and primary succession (centuries) is almost entirely explained by whether functional soil survived the disturbance.
• Q: What does the climate-shift scenario teach about restoration?
  A: The climate-shift scenario reveals a fundamental challenge for restoration ecology: when climate has changed, the historical ecosystem may no longer be achievable regardless of restoration effort. Planting the original conifer species in conditions 2 degrees warmer and 20% drier may result in high mortality and wasted resources because the species are adapted to a climate that no longer exists. The model shows that the Recovery Completeness Index plateaus below 100% because the 'target' has moved. This suggests that restoration ecologists may need to practice 'forward-looking restoration' — selecting species adapted to projected future conditions rather than historical conditions. This is philosophically difficult because it means deliberately creating an ecosystem that never existed, but it may be the only ecologically sound approach in a changing climate.

Key Concepts:
• Assisted Migration: Deliberately moving species to locations outside their historical range where projected future climate conditions will be suitable — controversial because it acknowledges that historical ecosystems cannot be maintained and intentionally creates novel species assemblages, but pragmatically necessary when the alternative is watching native species die in conditions they can no longer tolerate.
• Biological Soil Crusts: Communities of cyanobacteria, algae, fungi, and mosses that form a living crust on soil surfaces in arid and semi-arid environments — these crusts prevent erosion, fix nitrogen, retain moisture, and create microsites for plant establishment, making them critical for restoration of damaged soils in dry ecosystems.
• Adaptive Management: A restoration approach that treats management actions as experiments: implement interventions, monitor outcomes, compare to predictions, and adjust the plan based on results. This approach acknowledges uncertainty about recovery trajectories and builds learning into the restoration process rather than committing to a fixed plan.

Match the term to the definition:

___ Ecological Succession         
___ Regime Shift                  
___ Ecological Resilience         
___ Trophic Cascade               

A. The process of community change over time following a disturbance — primary succession begins on bare substrate with no soil (volcanic rock, retreating glaciers) and takes centuries, while secondary succession occurs where soil and seed banks remain (after fire or logging) and proceeds much faster because the biological infrastructure is partially intact
B. An abrupt, often irreversible transition from one stable ecosystem state to a fundamentally different one — such as a coral reef shifting to an algae-dominated system, a lake flipping from clear to turbid, or a forest converting to grassland — driven by disturbance that pushes the ecosystem past a resilience threshold beyond which the original feedback loops cannot re-establish
C. The capacity of an ecosystem to absorb disturbance and reorganize while retaining essentially the same function, structure, identity, and feedbacks — highly resilient ecosystems can survive severe disturbance and recover, while low-resilience ecosystems may undergo regime shifts from relatively minor perturbations
D. An ecological phenomenon where changes at one level of a food web cascade through other levels — removal of top predators can release herbivore populations that overgraze plants, changing vegetation structure, soil stability, water quality, and the entire ecosystem, demonstrating that food web integrity is essential for ecosystem recovery

STEM CHALLENGE: Design an Ecosystem Restoration Plan
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

MISSION: Design a comprehensive restoration plan for an ecosystem devastated by catastrophic disturbance, specifying the sequence of interventions needed to guide recovery toward the desired state while accounting for climate change, invasive species, and resilience thresholds.

SCENARIO: A severe wildfire has burned 50,000 acres of mixed-conifer forest in the Sierra Nevada. Soil is severely damaged in 30% of the area, moderate in 50%, and intact in 20%. Climate projections indicate the region will be 1.5 degrees C warmer and 15% drier by 2050, which may not support the original conifer forest. Invasive cheatgrass is already colonizing burned areas. Your restoration ecology team must design a plan that maximizes Recovery Completeness Index while adapting to projected climate conditions — which may mean planting species adapted to future conditions rather than trying to recreate the past.

GUIDING QUESTIONS:
• Based on your model, should the restoration plan aim to recreate the original conifer forest or plan for an ecosystem adapted to future climate conditions?
• What does your model predict about the timeline for natural recovery versus active restoration with planted native species and erosion control?
• How does early invasive species management affect the long-term recovery trajectory according to your model?

  _________________________________________________________


EVALUATION RUBRIC:
• Expert (4): Plan addresses all three soil damage zones with differentiated strategies, accounts for climate shift with forward-looking species selection, includes aggressive early invasive management, specifies monitoring metrics and adaptive management triggers, and uses model evidence to justify intervention sequencing
• Proficient (3): Plan includes reasonable restoration strategies for different damage zones and addresses either climate adaptation or invasive management
• Developing (2): Plan proposes restoration activities but does not differentiate by damage severity or account for climate change effects on recovery
• Beginning (1): Plan is incomplete or assumes the original ecosystem can be recreated without addressing changed conditions
