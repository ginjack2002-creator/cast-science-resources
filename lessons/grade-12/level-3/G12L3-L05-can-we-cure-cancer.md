## Platform

### Title
**Can We Cure Cancer? — Modeling Cell Cycle Regulation, Oncogene Activation, and Immunotherapy Strategies**

### Overview
Cancer is not one disease but hundreds — each characterized by a unique combination of genetic mutations, immune evasion strategies, and microenvironment interactions. Yet all cancers share a common origin: normal cells that accumulate mutations allowing them to escape the body's multi-layered growth control systems. Understanding these control systems and how they fail is the foundation of modern cancer biology and the key to developing more effective treatments. Students investigate the driving question: Every day, your body produces billions of new cells through mitosis. Dozens of those cells acquire mutations that could become cancerous, yet your immune system destroys almost all of them before they can grow. What happens when this defense system fails, and can we engineer the immune system to become an unstoppable cancer-killing machine? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

### Learning Objectives
- Model how tumor suppressor genes, oncogenes, cell cycle checkpoints, immune surveillance, and the tumor microenvironment interact to determine whether mutated cells are destroyed or become cancerous
- Analyze the multi-hit hypothesis of cancer — why multiple mutations are required for malignancy and how this explains both cancer's rarity in young people and its prevalence in older adults
- Evaluate the mechanism and effectiveness of immunotherapy approaches including checkpoint inhibitors, CAR-T cell therapy, and cancer vaccines using model-derived evidence
- Predict how changes in mutation rate, immune function, and tumor microenvironment affect cancer progression and treatment outcomes

### Component List (Starting Model: 10 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Mutation Rate | External | The frequency of DNA replication errors and environmentally induced DNA damage per cell division — increased by carcinogens (tobacco, UV radiation, certain chemicals), chronic inflammation, and inherited DNA repair deficiencies |
| Carcinogen Exposure | External | The level of environmental agents that damage DNA or promote cell proliferation — includes chemical carcinogens (benzene, asbestos), radiation (UV, ionizing), biological agents (HPV, H. pylori), and lifestyle factors (tobacco, alcohol, processed meat) |
| Inherited Genetic Risk | External | Germline mutations in cancer-predisposition genes like BRCA1/2, TP53, or APC that are present in every cell from birth — these provide a head start on the multi-hit pathway by eliminating one copy of a critical tumor suppressor before any somatic mutations occur |
| Oncogene Activation Level | Internal | The cumulative activating mutations in growth-promoting genes like RAS, MYC, and HER2 — each activated oncogene increases the growth signal, and multiple oncogene activations work synergistically to drive uncontrolled proliferation |
| Tumor Suppressor Function | Internal | The remaining functional capacity of genes like TP53, RB1, and APC that normally prevent cancer — as somatic mutations inactivate these genes, the cell progressively loses its ability to detect and respond to DNA damage, stop abnormal division, and trigger self-destruction |
| Cell Cycle Checkpoint Integrity | Internal | The functionality of the molecular checkpoints (G1/S, G2/M, spindle assembly) that verify DNA integrity and cell readiness before allowing division to proceed — when checkpoint genes are mutated, cells with damaged DNA divide rather than being repaired or destroyed |
| Immune Surveillance Effectiveness | Internal | The ability of natural killer cells, cytotoxic T cells, and other immune cells to detect and destroy abnormal cells before they can form tumors — effectiveness depends on tumor antigen presentation, checkpoint receptor status, and the immunosuppressive properties of the tumor microenvironment |
| Tumor Microenvironment Immunosuppression | Internal | The degree to which the growing tumor has remodeled its surrounding tissue to suppress immune attack — through secretion of immunosuppressive cytokines, recruitment of regulatory T cells, and expression of immune checkpoint ligands that deactivate attacking immune cells |
| Angiogenesis Rate | Internal | The speed at which new blood vessels grow into the tumor to supply oxygen and nutrients — tumors larger than 1-2mm cannot grow without angiogenesis, making it both a requirement for tumor progression and a target for anti-cancer therapies like bevacizumab |
| Treatment Response Score | Internal | A composite measure of how effectively the current treatment strategy (surgery, chemotherapy, radiation, immunotherapy) is controlling tumor growth — integrating tumor shrinkage rate, immune reactivation, and probability of recurrence |

• Cancer requires multiple mutations in specific combinations — the model reveals that a single oncogene activation or single tumor suppressor loss is usually insufficient for malignancy because the remaining defense layers (checkpoints, immune surveillance) compensate, explaining why cancer is rare before age 50

The future of cancer treatment is personalized medicine — using genomic analysis to identify each tumor's specific vulnerabilities and designing combination therapies that attack all of them simultaneously.

3. WHAT'S MISSING?
   Your model is simple. Real systems involve more. Think about:

   • Metastasis Potential — The likelihood that cancer cells will detach from the primary tumor, enter the bloodstream or lymphatic system, and establish secondary tumors in distant organs — metastasis is responsible for approximately 90% of cancer deaths and requires cells to overcome multiple physical and immunological barriers
   • Epigenetic Dysregulation — Changes in gene expression caused by modifications to DNA packaging (histones) and chemical marks (methylation) rather than changes to the DNA sequence itself — epigenetic changes can silence tumor suppressors or activate oncogenes without mutation, and are increasingly recognized as a parallel pathway to cancer development
   • Drug Resistance Evolution — The process by which cancer cell populations evolve resistance to treatment through natural selection — resistant subclones that pre-exist within the tumor or emerge through new mutations survive treatment and repopulate the tumor, analogous to antibiotic resistance in bacteria

LESSON PURPOSE:
Cancer is not one disease but hundreds — each characterized by a unique combination of genetic mutations, immune evasion strategies, and microenvironment interactions. Yet all cancers share a common origin: normal cells that accumulate mutations allowing them to escape the body's multi-layered growth control systems. Understanding these control systems and how they fail is the foundation of modern cancer biology and the key to developing more effective treatments.

NGSS ALIGNMENT:

• Crosscutting Concept: Cause and Effect
  Students trace the causal chain from individual mutations through multiple cellular defense failures to tumor formation and immune escape, identifying how each cause-effect link creates a potential intervention target for cancer therapy.

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• The Multi-Hit Model of Cancer:
  In 1971, Alfred Knudson proposed that cancer requires multiple genetic hits — mutations that disable successive layers of cellular growth control. We now know that most solid tumors carry 4-7 driver mutations in specific gene categories: oncogenes (growth accelerators), tumor suppressors (growth brakes), DNA repair genes (error correctors), and apoptosis regulators (self-destruct signals). The reason cancer incidence increases exponentially with age is that each additional year provides more cell divisions for random mutations to accumulate. A 70-year-old has undergone roughly 10 trillion more cell divisions than a 20-year-old, providing vastly more opportunities for the critical combination of mutations to occur in a single cell lineage.

• Immune Surveillance and Escape:
  The immune system continuously patrols the body for abnormal cells. Cytotoxic T cells can recognize and kill cells displaying mutant proteins (neoantigens) on their surface. Natural killer cells detect cells that have lost normal surface markers. This immune surveillance destroys the vast majority of potentially cancerous cells before they can form tumors. However, successful cancers evolve immune evasion strategies: downregulating surface antigens to become invisible, expressing checkpoint proteins like PD-L1 to deactivate T cells, secreting immunosuppressive molecules, and recruiting regulatory T cells that suppress the immune response locally. The tumor microenvironment becomes an immunological dead zone.

• The Immunotherapy Revolution:
  Traditional cancer treatments — surgery, chemotherapy, radiation — attack cancer cells directly but also damage normal tissue. Immunotherapy takes a fundamentally different approach: it empowers the patient's own immune system to attack the cancer. Checkpoint inhibitors (anti-PD-1, anti-PD-L1, anti-CTLA-4) block the molecular brakes that tumors use to suppress immune attack. CAR-T cell therapy genetically engineers a patient's own T cells to recognize and kill cancer cells. Cancer vaccines train the immune system to recognize tumor-specific antigens. Since 2011, immunotherapy has achieved lasting remissions in melanoma, lung cancer, kidney cancer, and blood cancers that were previously untreatable, earning James Allison and Tasuku Honjo the 2018 Nobel Prize in Medicine.

• The Challenge of Solid Tumors:
  While immunotherapy has revolutionized treatment of some cancers, solid tumors remain the frontier challenge. Unlike blood cancers, solid tumors build physical barriers (dense fibrous stroma), create immunosuppressive microenvironments, restrict T cell infiltration, and develop heterogeneous subclone populations where different cells have different mutations and vulnerabilities. Combination therapies that simultaneously attack multiple vulnerabilities — checkpoint inhibitors plus targeted therapy plus anti-angiogenic agents — show the most promise. The future of oncology is personalized medicine: sequencing each patient's tumor to identify the specific combination of mutations, immune markers, and microenvironment characteristics, then designing a tailored multi-agent treatment protocol.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Cancer is caused by a single mutation"
  Reality: Cancer requires the accumulation of 4-7 driver mutations in specific gene categories — oncogenes, tumor suppressors, DNA repair genes, and apoptosis regulators — in a single cell lineage. A single mutation is almost always insufficient because the body's redundant defense systems compensate. This is why inherited single-gene mutations like BRCA1 increase cancer risk but do not guarantee cancer — they provide a head start by eliminating one defense layer, but additional somatic mutations are still required.
  Strategy: Draw the multi-hit model on the board: start with a normal cell, add one mutation at a time, and ask 'is it cancer yet?' at each step. Students see that multiple simultaneous failures are required.

• "The immune system has nothing to do with cancer"
  Reality: The immune system is constantly detecting and destroying potentially cancerous cells — a process called immune surveillance. People with weakened immune systems (organ transplant recipients on immunosuppressive drugs, HIV/AIDS patients) have dramatically higher cancer rates, proving that immune surveillance is an active cancer defense. Immunotherapy drugs that enhance immune function have achieved lasting remissions in previously incurable cancers. The tumor's ability to evade immune detection is now recognized as one of the hallmarks of cancer.
  Strategy: Show the data: organ transplant recipients on immunosuppressive drugs have 3-5x higher rates of many cancers. Ask: what does this tell us about the role of the immune system in preventing cancer?

• "Cancer is always a death sentence"
  Reality: While some cancers remain very difficult to treat, five-year survival rates have improved dramatically. The overall five-year survival rate for all cancers combined has increased from 50% in the 1970s to over 68% today. Some cancers that were uniformly fatal — like certain melanomas and blood cancers — now have 50-90% response rates with immunotherapy. Early detection, targeted therapy, and combination approaches continue to improve outcomes. The model shows that treatment effectiveness depends on the specific combination of tumor characteristics, which is why personalized medicine is the future of oncology.
  Strategy: Show survival rate trends over decades for specific cancers. Highlight dramatic improvements in melanoma (from 15% to 50%+ five-year survival with immunotherapy) and childhood leukemia (from 10% to 90%+ five-year survival).

This multi-layered defense explains why cancer is rare relative to the billions of cell divisions happening in your body daily. Simulation Answers:
• Multi-Hit Cancer Development: The model shows that a single oncogene activation has minimal effect on tumor growth because functional tumor suppressors and checkpoints compensate. Adding tumor suppressor loss creates a more aggressive growth pattern but the immune system still controls it. Only when checkpoint integrity is also compromised AND immune surveillance is evaded does the model show uncontrolled malignant growth. This demonstrates why cancer requires 4-7 driver mutations and why most single mutations never lead to cancer.
• Immune Escape: Even with robust Immune Surveillance Effectiveness, the model shows that a tumor progressively increasing PD-L1 expression and microenvironment immunosuppression eventually overcomes immune control. The tipping point occurs when immunosuppression in the local microenvironment exceeds the immune system's activation capacity — T cells arrive at the tumor but are deactivated before they can kill. This explains why some patients with strong immune systems still develop cancer.
• Checkpoint Inhibitor Therapy: When anti-PD-1 checkpoint inhibitor is applied to a tumor with high PD-L1 expression, the model shows a dramatic reversal: Immune Surveillance Effectiveness rapidly increases as T cells that were being suppressed are released to attack the tumor. Treatment Response Score rises sharply. However, the response depends on the pre-existing immune infiltration — if there are no T cells in the tumor microenvironment, removing the brakes has nothing to release, explaining why checkpoint inhibitors do not work for all patients.

Reflection Exemplars:
• Q: Why does cancer require multiple mutations?
  A: The body has redundant defense systems specifically because any single system can fail. Oncogene activation is countered by tumor suppressors. Tumor suppressor loss is caught by cell cycle checkpoints. Checkpoint failure is detected by immune surveillance. Only when multiple systems fail in the same cell lineage can cancer develop. Our model demonstrates this layered defense: removing one defense layer barely changes tumor probability, but removing three or four layers creates a dramatic increase. This is why cancer is rare in young people (insufficient time for multiple mutations) and common in older adults (decades of cumulative mutations). It also explains why inherited mutations like BRCA1 dramatically increase cancer risk — they eliminate one defense layer at birth, so fewer additional mutations are needed.
• Q: How does immunotherapy represent a paradigm shift?
  A: Traditional cancer treatments — surgery, chemotherapy, radiation — directly attack cancer cells but also damage normal tissue. Immunotherapy fundamentally changes the paradigm by empowering the patient's own adaptive immune system, which can recognize virtually any tumor antigen, multiply in response to threats, form memory cells for long-term protection, and adapt to tumor mutations. Our model shows that checkpoint inhibitor therapy works by removing the molecular brakes (PD-L1/PD-1 interaction) that tumors use to suppress T cell attack. When these brakes are released, the patient's pre-existing anti-tumor T cells reactivate and can achieve complete, durable responses that chemotherapy rarely produces. The limitation revealed by the model is that immunotherapy requires pre-existing immune recognition of the tumor — patients whose tumors have low mutational burden or have completely excluded immune cells from the microenvironment may not respond.

Key Concepts:
• Checkpoint Inhibitor Therapy: Drugs like pembrolizumab (anti-PD-1) and atezolizumab (anti-PD-L1) block the molecular handshake between tumor PD-L1 and T cell PD-1, preventing the tumor from deactivating immune cells. Most effective in tumors with high PD-L1 expression and high tumor mutational burden, which creates more neoantigens for T cells to recognize. Response rates range from 20-50% depending on cancer type and biomarkers.
• Targeted Therapy: Drugs designed to block specific molecular drivers of cancer — such as EGFR inhibitors, ALK inhibitors, and BRAF inhibitors. These precision medicines target the exact oncogene mutation driving the patient's tumor, causing dramatic responses when the target is present but no benefit when it is absent. Resistance typically develops within 9-12 months as the tumor evolves alternative growth pathways.
• Combination Therapy Rationale: Using multiple drugs that attack cancer through different mechanisms simultaneously — for example, checkpoint inhibitor plus chemotherapy plus anti-angiogenic agent. Combination therapy reduces the probability of resistance because a cancer cell would need to evolve resistance to all agents simultaneously, which is exponentially less likely than evolving resistance to a single agent.

Match the term to the definition:

___ Oncogene                      
___ Tumor Suppressor Gene         
___ Immune Checkpoint             
___ Tumor Microenvironment        

A. A mutated version of a normal gene (proto-oncogene) that promotes uncontrolled cell division — oncogenes act like a stuck accelerator pedal, sending constant growth signals even when the cell should stop dividing, and a single activating mutation can contribute to cancer development
B. A gene whose protein product inhibits cell division, promotes DNA repair, or triggers apoptosis in damaged cells — tumor suppressors act like the brakes on cell growth, and both copies must be inactivated (Knudson's two-hit hypothesis) before their protective function is lost
C. A molecular brake on immune cell activation that normally prevents autoimmune attacks on healthy tissue — cancer cells exploit these checkpoints by expressing proteins like PD-L1 that bind to PD-1 on T cells, essentially putting the brakes on the immune response and hiding from immune surveillance
D. The complex ecosystem surrounding a tumor that includes blood vessels, immune cells, fibroblasts, signaling molecules, and extracellular matrix — tumors actively remodel their microenvironment to suppress immune responses, promote blood vessel growth for nutrient supply, and create conditions favorable for continued growth and metastasis

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

MISSION: Design a personalized treatment protocol for a patient with a specific cancer profile, integrating surgery, chemotherapy, immunotherapy, and targeted therapy based on the tumor's genomic and immunological characteristics, using model evidence to justify each treatment decision.

SCENARIO: A 45-year-old patient has been diagnosed with stage III non-small cell lung cancer. Genomic sequencing reveals: KRAS oncogene mutation, TP53 tumor suppressor loss, high PD-L1 expression (70%), moderate tumor mutational burden, and an immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment with high regulatory T cell infiltration. Your oncology team must design a comprehensive treatment plan specifying the sequence and combination of therapies, predicted response based on the tumor profile, and monitoring strategy for treatment resistance.

GUIDING QUESTIONS:
• Based on the tumor's high PD-L1 expression, what does your model predict about the likely effectiveness of checkpoint inhibitor immunotherapy?
• How does the KRAS oncogene mutation and TP53 loss inform which targeted therapies might complement immunotherapy?
• What does your model suggest about the risk of treatment resistance and how would you design a combination strategy to minimize it?

EVALUATION RUBRIC:
• Expert (4): Protocol matches specific therapies to the patient's genomic profile with molecular rationale, sequences treatments strategically, includes biomarker monitoring plan, addresses resistance with combination strategy, and cites model evidence for each decision
• Proficient (3): Protocol selects appropriate therapies based on tumor characteristics and includes some monitoring and resistance planning
• Developing (2): Protocol names treatments but does not connect them to the specific tumor profile or model evidence
• Beginning (1): Protocol is incomplete or applies generic treatments without personalizing to the patient's tumor characteristics
