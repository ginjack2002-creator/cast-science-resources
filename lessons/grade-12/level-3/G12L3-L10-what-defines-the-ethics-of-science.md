## Platform

### Title
**What Defines the Ethics of Science? — Modeling Scientific Responsibility, Dual-Use Research, and Evidence-Based Policy**

### Overview
Science is not a neutral tool — it is a human activity shaped by values, funding, politics, and power. The same nuclear physics that generates clean electricity also created weapons capable of ending civilization. The same genetic engineering that produces life-saving medicines also enables biological weapons. The same artificial intelligence that diagnoses cancer also powers autonomous weapons and mass surveillance. As scientific power increases exponentially, the ethical frameworks governing its use have struggled to keep pace. Students investigate the driving question: Science has given humanity vaccines, electricity, computers, and the ability to feed 8 billion people. It has also given us nuclear weapons, biological warfare agents, surveillance technology, and the tools for ecological destruction. Who decides which research should be pursued, who benefits from scientific progress, and what happens when the same knowledge can both save and destroy lives? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

### Learning Objectives
- Model how research funding priorities, institutional review processes, publication norms, dual-use potential, and public engagement interact to shape the trajectory and impact of scientific research
- Analyze the tension between scientific freedom (the right to pursue any question) and scientific responsibility (the obligation to consider consequences) using model-derived evidence about dual-use research
- Evaluate case studies in scientific ethics — gain-of-function virology, AI weapons systems, genetic enhancement, geoengineering — using an ethical framework that balances innovation with precaution
- Predict how changes in funding models, oversight mechanisms, and public trust affect the direction, pace, and equity of scientific progress

### Component List (Starting Model: 10 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Research Funding Allocation | External | The distribution of financial resources across scientific disciplines and research questions — determines which questions get asked and which are ignored, with funding agencies (NIH, NSF, DOD, private industry) each imposing different priorities, timelines, and accountability structures |
| Dual-Use Potential | External | The degree to which the research outputs could be repurposed for harmful applications — ranges from low (most basic research) to extreme (gain-of-function pathogen engineering, autonomous weapons AI, heritable human gene editing) and determines the level of oversight and restriction appropriate for the research |
| Public Trust in Science | External | The degree to which the general public believes scientific institutions and researchers act honestly, competently, and in the public interest — eroded by perceptions of conflicts of interest, inaccessible jargon, and failure to address community concerns, and essential for public support of research funding and science-based policy |
| Institutional Oversight Rigor | Internal | The strength and independence of ethics review processes — including IRBs for human research, IACUCs for animal research, biosafety committees for pathogen work, and AI ethics boards for autonomous systems — determining how thoroughly ethical risks are identified and mitigated before research proceeds |
| Publication Transparency | Internal | The degree to which research methods, data, and results are openly shared with the scientific community and public — including pre-registration of studies, open data policies, and honest reporting of negative results, which collectively determine the self-correcting capacity of science |
| Researcher Ethical Awareness | Internal | The degree to which individual scientists actively consider the ethical implications of their work — influenced by training, institutional culture, personal values, and the incentive structures that reward or punish ethical behavior in academic career advancement |
| Benefit Distribution Equity | Internal | How evenly the benefits of scientific research are distributed across communities, nations, and socioeconomic groups — including access to medicines, technology, clean energy, and educational resources that science produces, with historical patterns showing that benefits disproportionately flow to wealthy nations and communities |
| Risk Distribution Equity | Internal | How evenly the risks and harms of scientific research are distributed — including environmental contamination from research facilities, health risks from clinical trials, and the deployment of surveillance or weapons technology, with historical patterns showing that risks disproportionately burden marginalized communities |
| Innovation Rate | Internal | The pace at which scientific research produces new knowledge, technologies, and applications — influenced by funding levels, regulatory environment, international collaboration, and the balance between enabling rapid discovery and ensuring responsible conduct |
| Societal Impact Score | Internal | A composite measure integrating the net benefit to society from scientific research — balancing innovation gains, equitable distribution of benefits and risks, long-term environmental and social consequences, and the preservation of public trust in scientific institutions |

LESSON PURPOSE:
Science is not a neutral tool — it is a human activity shaped by values, funding, politics, and power. The same nuclear physics that generates clean electricity also created weapons capable of ending civilization. The same genetic engineering that produces life-saving medicines also enables biological weapons. The same artificial intelligence that diagnoses cancer also powers autonomous weapons and mass surveillance. As scientific power increases exponentially, the ethical frameworks governing its use have struggled to keep pace.

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• The Social Contract of Science:
  Since World War II, science has operated under an implicit social contract: society funds basic research through taxes, and in return, science delivers innovations that improve health, prosperity, and security. This contract depends on public trust — the belief that scientific institutions act honestly and in the public interest. When trust erodes (through perceived conflicts of interest, failure to address community concerns, or high-profile fraud), public support for science funding declines, vaccine hesitancy increases, and evidence-based policy becomes politically impossible. Rebuilding trust requires transparency, accountability, community engagement, and honest acknowledgment of uncertainty and limitations.

• The Dual-Use Dilemma:
  Dual-use research of concern (DURC) creates knowledge that can both benefit and threaten humanity. The most dramatic example is gain-of-function virology: researchers deliberately make pathogens more transmissible or virulent to understand pandemic mechanisms and develop countermeasures. This research helped prepare for COVID-19 but also creates blueprints for potential bioweapons. In 2011, researchers created a highly transmissible H5N1 influenza strain and sought to publish the methods. The resulting controversy led to a US government moratorium on gain-of-function research from 2014-2017 and the development of new oversight frameworks. The core dilemma: restricting publication prevents misuse but also prevents the scientific scrutiny that catches errors, and blocking research in one country simply moves it to less regulated jurisdictions.

• Equity in Science:
  Scientific benefits and risks are not distributed equally. Clinical trials historically over-enrolled white male participants, producing treatments optimized for one demographic. Environmental research facilities and hazardous waste sites are disproportionately located in low-income communities and communities of color. Genomic databases predominantly represent European ancestry, limiting the precision medicine benefits available to other populations. The COVID-19 pandemic revealed that vaccine distribution tracked national wealth, with high-income countries achieving 70% coverage while low-income countries remained below 20%. Equitable science requires deliberate design: representative clinical trials, community-engaged research, open-access publication, technology transfer to developing nations, and governance structures that give affected communities voice in research decisions.

• Emerging Ethical Frontiers:
  The 21st century presents ethical challenges that existing frameworks were not designed to address. AI systems that make life-altering decisions (criminal sentencing, hiring, medical diagnosis) encode biases from training data and operate with limited transparency. Heritable human gene editing (germline CRISPR) could eliminate genetic diseases but also enable genetic enhancement, potentially creating genetic inequity between those who can and cannot afford modification. Solar geoengineering (stratospheric aerosol injection) could cool the planet but would affect weather patterns globally, raising questions about who has the right to modify Earth's climate. Synthetic biology enables engineering entirely new organisms, with applications from biofuels to biological weapons. Each frontier requires new ethical frameworks developed through inclusive dialogue between scientists, ethicists, policymakers, and the communities affected by the research.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Ethics slows down science and reduces innovation"
  Reality: While ethics review adds process time, the overall impact on innovation is positive. Ethical research is more reliable (because it is conducted carefully and transparently), more trusted (because the public supports it), better funded (because trust enables political support), and less likely to produce catastrophic setbacks (because risks are managed proactively). The history of science shows that ethical failures — Tuskegee, thalidomide, the He Jiankui gene-editing scandal — cause moratoriums, funding cuts, and loss of public trust that set entire fields back by years or decades. Ethics review is an investment in sustainable innovation, not a cost.
  Strategy: Ask students: after He Jiankui created gene-edited babies without proper oversight, China imposed strict new regulations that slowed ALL gene therapy research in the country for years. Was the 'fast' unethical approach actually faster in the end?

• "Scientists are objective and do not need external ethical oversight"
  Reality: Scientists are human beings subject to the same biases, incentive structures, and conflicts of interest as everyone else. The academic incentive system rewards publication quantity, novelty, and positive results — creating pressure to cut corners, overstate findings, or ignore ethical concerns that might delay publication. External oversight provides independent checks that complement individual integrity. Every profession with significant power over others — medicine, law, engineering, finance — has external regulatory oversight. Science should not be exempt simply because scientists believe they are objective.
  Strategy: Present the replication crisis: 50-70% of psychology studies and 60-90% of preclinical cancer studies fail to replicate. Ask: if scientists were perfectly objective and self-regulating, would this be happening? What oversight mechanisms could improve replicability?

• "The benefits of science automatically reach everyone equally"
  Reality: Scientific benefits follow existing power and wealth structures unless deliberately redirected. Pharmaceutical companies develop drugs for profitable markets (wealthy nations) rather than unprofitable ones (malaria, tuberculosis). Precision medicine advances fastest for populations overrepresented in genomic databases (European ancestry). Clean energy technology is deployed first in nations that can afford it. COVID-19 vaccines reached wealthy countries months before poor countries despite the virus not respecting borders. Equitable distribution requires intentional design: tiered pricing, technology transfer agreements, representative clinical trials, and community-engaged research priorities.
  Strategy: Show COVID-19 vaccination rate maps by country income level at 6 months and 12 months after vaccine availability. Ask: does scientific innovation automatically benefit everyone? What would it take to change this pattern?

Who decides which research should be pursued, who benefits from scientific progress, and what happens when the same knowledge can both save and destroy lives?
Answer: The ethics of science is defined by the recognition that knowledge is power — and power can be used for good or harm. Our model reveals that scientific ethics is not an external constraint imposed on science but an integral system property that determines whether research produces net benefit for society. Maximum innovation without ethical governance leads to inequitable distribution of benefits, concentrated risks on vulnerable communities, dual-use threats, and erosion of public trust that ultimately undermines science itself. The most productive scientific systems are those that balance freedom with responsibility: robust institutional oversight catches ethical problems before they cause harm, transparent publication enables self-correction, equitable benefit distribution maintains public support, and honest engagement with dual-use risks prevents knowledge from being weaponized. Science's greatest ethical challenge in the 21st century is ensuring that the extraordinary power of modern technology — gene editing, artificial intelligence, climate engineering, synthetic biology — serves all of humanity rather than exacerbating existing disparities.

Simulation Answers:
• Standard Research Environment: With low dual-use potential, moderate funding, standard oversight, and high public trust, the model shows a stable system with moderate Innovation Rate and high Societal Impact Score. The positive feedback loop between transparency, trust, and funding operates smoothly because the ethical risks are manageable. This scenario represents the baseline state that most scientific research operates within — the system works well when ethical challenges are modest.
• Dual-Use Crisis: When dual-use potential is extreme, the model shows a sharp tension: maximum oversight restricts publication and slows innovation, but inadequate oversight risks catastrophic misuse. Reflection Exemplars:
• Q: Why does maximum innovation not maximize societal impact?
  A: The model reveals a counterintuitive but critical insight: unrestricted innovation produces negative externalities that reduce net societal benefit. Without oversight, some research causes direct harm (unsafe experiments, dual-use misuse). Without equity considerations, benefits concentrate among wealthy populations while risks burden marginalized communities, creating net harm for the majority. Without transparency, errors and fraud go undetected, producing unreliable knowledge that leads to failed applications. Without public trust, funding and political support for science collapse. Each of these factors reduces the actual societal value of innovation below its theoretical potential. Moderate innovation with strong governance, transparency, and equity produces higher Societal Impact Score because it avoids the harm, inequity, distrust, and unreliability that plague unrestricted innovation.
• Q: How does public trust function as a system foundation?
  A: The model shows that public trust is not an outcome of good science but a prerequisite for it. Trust drives funding: democratic societies fund research through taxes, and taxpayers must believe science serves the public interest. Trust enables evidence-based policy: climate action, pandemic response, and environmental regulation all require public acceptance of scientific evidence. Trust supports oversight: ethics review processes are accepted as legitimate only when the public trusts that science governs itself responsibly. When trust collapses, every other component degrades: funding decreases, oversight is resisted, policy ignores evidence, and innovation stalls. The trust-building feedback loop (transparency -> trust -> funding -> innovation -> benefit -> trust) takes years to establish but can collapse rapidly through a single high-profile scandal, conflict of interest revelation, or perceived dishonesty. This asymmetry is why maintaining trust is so critical and why scientific integrity must be a non-negotiable institutional priority.

A. Scientific research that produces knowledge or technology with both beneficial applications and the potential for misuse that could threaten public health or national security — the defining ethical challenge of modern science, where the same gain-of-function virology that helps develop pandemic vaccines also creates blueprints for biological weapons
B. An ethics committee that reviews proposed research involving human subjects to ensure it meets ethical standards for informed consent, risk minimization, equitable participant selection, and privacy protection — the primary mechanism by which research institutions enforce ethical guidelines before experiments begin
C. The adherence to ethical principles in conducting and reporting research — including honest data collection, accurate reporting of methods and results, proper attribution of others' work, disclosure of conflicts of interest, and willingness to publish negative results that contradict the researcher's hypothesis
D. The ethical principle that when a scientific activity raises potential threats to human health or the environment, precautionary measures should be taken even if causal relationships are not fully established scientifically — shifting the burden of proof from 'prove it is harmful' to 'prove it is safe' before proceeding

STEM CHALLENGE: Design a Science Ethics Governance Framework
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

MISSION: Design a comprehensive governance framework for a national research institution that maximizes beneficial innovation while preventing dual-use misuse, ensuring equitable distribution of benefits and risks, and maintaining public trust in science.

SCENARIO: A new national research institute is being created to pursue frontier science in artificial intelligence, synthetic biology, and climate engineering — three fields with enormous beneficial potential and serious dual-use concerns. The institute will receive $5 billion annually in public funding. Your ethics advisory board must design the governance framework: ethical review processes, publication policies, dual-use research oversight, public engagement mechanisms, equity mandates, and accountability structures. The framework must enable rapid innovation while preventing research from causing disproportionate harm to any community.

GUIDING QUESTIONS:
• Based on your model, what level of Institutional Oversight Rigor maximizes Societal Impact Score without unnecessarily reducing Innovation Rate?
• How does your model inform the design of publication policies for dual-use research — when should results be fully published, partially redacted, or classified?
• What mechanisms does your model suggest are most effective at ensuring equitable distribution of both benefits and risks from scientific research?

DESIGN THINKING:
• What is the structure and composition of your ethics review board, and how does it maintain independence from researchers and funders?

• What specific criteria does your framework use to classify research as dual-use and what restrictions apply at each classification level?

  _________________________________________________________

• How does your framework ensure that communities affected by research — both positively and negatively — have a voice in research governance?

  _________________________________________________________

• What accountability mechanisms does your framework include when research causes unintended harm despite ethical review?

  _________________________________________________________


EVALUATION RUBRIC:
• Expert (4): Framework includes multi-tiered ethics review scaled to risk level, dual-use classification system with specific criteria, publication policy that balances transparency and security, equity mandate with enforcement mechanisms, public engagement structure, and accountability process for unintended harms — all connected to model evidence about how each mechanism affects Societal Impact Score
• Proficient (3): Framework addresses major governance needs including ethics review, dual-use oversight, and some equity and public engagement provisions
• Developing (2): Framework describes governance structures but does not scale them to risk level or connect them to model evidence about societal impact
• Beginning (1): Framework is incomplete or does not address the specific challenges of dual-use research, equity, or public trust
