## Platform

### Title
**What Defines the Ethics of Science? — Modeling Scientific Responsibility, Dual-Use Research, and Evidence-Based Policy**

### Overview
Science is not a neutral tool — it is a human activity shaped by values, funding, politics, and power. The same nuclear physics that generates clean electricity also created weapons capable of ending civilization. The same genetic engineering that produces life-saving medicines also enables biological weapons. The same artificial intelligence that diagnoses cancer also powers autonomous weapons and mass surveillance. As scientific power increases exponentially, the ethical frameworks governing its use have struggled to keep pace. Students investigate the driving question: Science has given humanity vaccines, electricity, computers, and the ability to feed 8 billion people. It has also given us nuclear weapons, biological warfare agents, surveillance technology, and the tools for ecological destruction. Who decides which research should be pursued, who benefits from scientific progress, and what happens when the same knowledge can both save and destroy lives? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

### Learning Objectives
- Model how research funding priorities, institutional review processes, publication norms, dual-use potential, and public engagement interact to shape the trajectory and impact of scientific research
- Analyze the tension between scientific freedom (the right to pursue any question) and scientific responsibility (the obligation to consider consequences) using model-derived evidence about dual-use research
- Evaluate case studies in scientific ethics — gain-of-function virology, AI weapons systems, genetic enhancement, geoengineering — using an ethical framework that balances innovation with precaution
- Predict how changes in funding models, oversight mechanisms, and public trust affect the direction, pace, and equity of scientific progress

### Component List (Starting Model: 10 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Research Funding Allocation | External | The distribution of financial resources across scientific disciplines and research questions — determines which questions get asked and which are ignored, with funding agencies (NIH, NSF, DOD, private industry) each imposing different priorities, timelines, and accountability structures |
| Dual-Use Potential | External | The degree to which the research outputs could be repurposed for harmful applications — ranges from low (most basic research) to extreme (gain-of-function pathogen engineering, autonomous weapons AI, heritable human gene editing) and determines the level of oversight and restriction appropriate for the research |
| Public Trust in Science | External | The degree to which the general public believes scientific institutions and researchers act honestly, competently, and in the public interest — eroded by perceptions of conflicts of interest, inaccessible jargon, and failure to address community concerns, and essential for public support of research funding and science-based policy |
| Institutional Oversight Rigor | Internal | The strength and independence of ethics review processes — including IRBs for human research, IACUCs for animal research, biosafety committees for pathogen work, and AI ethics boards for autonomous systems — determining how thoroughly ethical risks are identified and mitigated before research proceeds |
| Publication Transparency | Internal | The degree to which research methods, data, and results are openly shared with the scientific community and public — including pre-registration of studies, open data policies, and honest reporting of negative results, which collectively determine the self-correcting capacity of science |
| Researcher Ethical Awareness | Internal | The degree to which individual scientists actively consider the ethical implications of their work — influenced by training, institutional culture, personal values, and the incentive structures that reward or punish ethical behavior in academic career advancement |
| Benefit Distribution Equity | Internal | How evenly the benefits of scientific research are distributed across communities, nations, and socioeconomic groups — including access to medicines, technology, clean energy, and educational resources that science produces, with historical patterns showing that benefits disproportionately flow to wealthy nations and communities |
| Risk Distribution Equity | Internal | How evenly the risks and harms of scientific research are distributed — including environmental contamination from research facilities, health risks from clinical trials, and the deployment of surveillance or weapons technology, with historical patterns showing that risks disproportionately burden marginalized communities |
| Innovation Rate | Internal | The pace at which scientific research produces new knowledge, technologies, and applications — influenced by funding levels, regulatory environment, international collaboration, and the balance between enabling rapid discovery and ensuring responsible conduct |
| Societal Impact Score | Internal | A composite measure integrating the net benefit to society from scientific research — balancing innovation gains, equitable distribution of benefits and risks, long-term environmental and social consequences, and the preservation of public trust in scientific institutions |

LESSON PURPOSE:
Science is not a neutral tool — it is a human activity shaped by values, funding, politics, and power. The same nuclear physics that generates clean electricity also created weapons capable of ending civilization. The same genetic engineering that produces life-saving medicines also enables biological weapons. The same artificial intelligence that diagnoses cancer also powers autonomous weapons and mass surveillance. BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• The Social Contract of Science:
  Since World War II, science has operated under an implicit social contract: society funds basic research through taxes, and in return, science delivers innovations that improve health, prosperity, and security. This contract depends on public trust — the belief that scientific institutions act honestly and in the public interest. When trust erodes (through perceived conflicts of interest, failure to address community concerns, or high-profile fraud), public support for science funding declines, vaccine hesitancy increases, and evidence-based policy becomes politically impossible. Rebuilding trust requires transparency, accountability, community engagement, and honest acknowledgment of uncertainty and limitations.

• The Dual-Use Dilemma:
  Dual-use research of concern (DURC) creates knowledge that can both benefit and threaten humanity. The most dramatic example is gain-of-function virology: researchers deliberately make pathogens more transmissible or virulent to understand pandemic mechanisms and develop countermeasures. This research helped prepare for COVID-19 but also creates blueprints for potential bioweapons. In 2011, researchers created a highly transmissible H5N1 influenza strain and sought to publish the methods. The resulting controversy led to a US government moratorium on gain-of-function research from 2014-2017 and the development of new oversight frameworks. The core dilemma: restricting publication prevents misuse but also prevents the scientific scrutiny that catches errors, and blocking research in one country simply moves it to less regulated jurisdictions.

• Equity in Science:
  Scientific benefits and risks are not distributed equally. Clinical trials historically over-enrolled white male participants, producing treatments optimized for one demographic. Environmental research facilities and hazardous waste sites are disproportionately located in low-income communities and communities of color. Genomic databases predominantly represent European ancestry, limiting the precision medicine benefits available to other populations. The COVID-19 pandemic revealed that vaccine distribution tracked national wealth, with high-income countries achieving 70% coverage while low-income countries remained below 20%. Equitable science requires deliberate design: representative clinical trials, community-engaged research, open-access publication, technology transfer to developing nations, and governance structures that give affected communities voice in research decisions.

• Emerging Ethical Frontiers:
  The 21st century presents ethical challenges that existing frameworks were not designed to address. AI systems that make life-altering decisions (criminal sentencing, hiring, medical diagnosis) encode biases from training data and operate with limited transparency. Heritable human gene editing (germline CRISPR) could eliminate genetic diseases but also enable genetic enhancement, potentially creating genetic inequity between those who can and cannot afford modification. Solar geoengineering (stratospheric aerosol injection) could cool the planet but would affect weather patterns globally, raising questions about who has the right to modify Earth's climate. Synthetic biology enables engineering entirely new organisms, with applications from biofuels to biological weapons. Each frontier requires new ethical frameworks developed through inclusive dialogue between scientists, ethicists, policymakers, and the communities affected by the research.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Ethics slows down science and reduces innovation"
  Reality: While ethics review adds process time, the overall impact on innovation is positive. Ethical research is more reliable (because it is conducted carefully and transparently), more trusted (because the public supports it), better funded (because trust enables political support), and less likely to produce catastrophic setbacks (because risks are managed proactively). The history of science shows that ethical failures — Tuskegee, thalidomide, the He Jiankui gene-editing scandal — cause moratoriums, funding cuts, and loss of public trust that set entire fields back by years or decades. Ethics review is an investment in sustainable innovation, not a cost.
  Strategy: Ask students: after He Jiankui created gene-edited babies without proper oversight, China imposed strict new regulations that slowed ALL gene therapy research in the country for years. Was the 'fast' unethical approach actually faster in the end?

• "Scientists are objective and do not need external ethical oversight"
  Reality: Scientists are human beings subject to the same biases, incentive structures, and conflicts of interest as everyone else. The academic incentive system rewards publication quantity, novelty, and positive results — creating pressure to cut corners, overstate findings, or ignore ethical concerns that might delay publication. External oversight provides independent checks that complement individual integrity. Every profession with significant power over others — medicine, law, engineering, finance — has external regulatory oversight. Science should not be exempt simply because scientists believe they are objective.
  Strategy: Present the replication crisis: 50-70% of psychology studies and 60-90% of preclinical cancer studies fail to replicate. Ask: if scientists were perfectly objective and self-regulating, would this be happening? What oversight mechanisms could improve replicability?

• "The benefits of science automatically reach everyone equally"
  Reality: Scientific benefits follow existing power and wealth structures unless deliberately redirected. Pharmaceutical companies develop drugs for profitable markets (wealthy nations) rather than unprofitable ones (malaria, tuberculosis). Precision medicine advances fastest for populations overrepresented in genomic databases (European ancestry). Clean energy technology is deployed first in nations that can afford it. COVID-19 vaccines reached wealthy countries months before poor countries despite the virus not respecting borders. Equitable distribution requires intentional design: tiered pricing, technology transfer agreements, representative clinical trials, and community-engaged research priorities.
  Strategy: Show COVID-19 vaccination rate maps by country income level at 6 months and 12 months after vaccine availability. Ask: does scientific innovation automatically benefit everyone? What would it take to change this pattern?

Answer: The ethics of science is defined by the recognition that knowledge is power — and power can be used for good or harm. Our model reveals that scientific ethics is not an external constraint imposed on science but an integral system property that determines whether research produces net benefit for society. Maximum innovation without ethical governance leads to inequitable distribution of benefits, concentrated risks on vulnerable communities, dual-use threats, and erosion of public trust that ultimately undermines science itself. The most productive scientific systems are those that balance freedom with responsibility: robust institutional oversight catches ethical problems before they cause harm, transparent publication enables self-correction, equitable benefit distribution maintains public support, and honest engagement with dual-use risks prevents knowledge from being weaponized. Science's greatest ethical challenge in the 21st century is ensuring that the extraordinary power of modern technology — gene editing, artificial intelligence, climate engineering, synthetic biology — serves all of humanity rather than exacerbating existing disparities.

Simulation Answers:
• Standard Research Environment: With low dual-use potential, moderate funding, standard oversight, and high public trust, the model shows a stable system with moderate Innovation Rate and high Societal Impact Score. • Dual-Use Crisis: When dual-use potential is extreme, the model shows a sharp tension: maximum oversight restricts publication and slows innovation, but inadequate oversight risks catastrophic misuse. Reflection Exemplars:
• Q: Why does maximum innovation not maximize societal impact?
  A: The model reveals a counterintuitive but critical insight: unrestricted innovation produces negative externalities that reduce net societal benefit. Without oversight, some research causes direct harm (unsafe experiments, dual-use misuse). Without equity considerations, benefits concentrate among wealthy populations while risks burden marginalized communities, creating net harm for the majority. Without transparency, errors and fraud go undetected, producing unreliable knowledge that leads to failed applications. Without public trust, funding and political support for science collapse. Each of these factors reduces the actual societal value of innovation below its theoretical potential. Moderate innovation with strong governance, transparency, and equity produces higher Societal Impact Score because it avoids the harm, inequity, distrust, and unreliability that plague unrestricted innovation.
• Q: How does public trust function as a system foundation?
  A: The model shows that public trust is not an outcome of good science but a prerequisite for it. Trust drives funding: democratic societies fund research through taxes, and taxpayers must believe science serves the public interest. Trust enables evidence-based policy: climate action, pandemic response, and environmental regulation all require public acceptance of scientific evidence. Trust supports oversight: ethics review processes are accepted as legitimate only when the public trusts that science governs itself responsibly. When trust collapses, every other component degrades: funding decreases, oversight is resisted, policy ignores evidence, and innovation stalls. The trust-building feedback loop (transparency -> trust -> funding -> innovation -> benefit -> trust) takes years to establish but can collapse rapidly through a single high-profile scandal, conflict of interest revelation, or perceived dishonesty. This asymmetry is why maintaining trust is so critical and why scientific integrity must be a non-negotiable institutional priority.

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format. Each item is three-dimensional, assessing a Science and Engineering Practice (SEP), Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and Crosscutting Concept (CCC) simultaneously, aligned to HS-ETS1-3.

### Pre-Assessment Questions

### Question 1

Dual-use research of concern (DURC) produces knowledge with both beneficial applications and potential for misuse. Which of the following is the most accurate example of a dual-use dilemma?

A. Research on improving crop yields, which has no potential for misuse
B. Gain-of-function virology research that enhances pathogen transmissibility, which can inform pandemic preparedness and vaccine development but also provides a blueprint for creating biological weapons
C. Teaching basic chemistry in high school, which enables students to make dangerous chemicals
D. Publishing weather forecasts, which could be used by enemy nations to plan military operations

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. Gain-of-function research is the paradigmatic dual-use dilemma. Making a pathogen more transmissible reveals how natural variants might evolve (essential for pandemic preparedness), but the same knowledge could be used to engineer a biological weapon. The dual-use potential is not speculative; the knowledge and techniques are directly transferable between beneficial and harmful applications. A true dual-use dilemma involves research where the same knowledge is directly applicable to both beneficial and harmful purposes. Gain-of-function virology is the clearest example: understanding enhanced pathogen transmissibility helps prepare for pandemics but also provides the technical knowledge to create biological weapons.

### Question 2

An Institutional Review Board (IRB) reviews research proposals involving human subjects. What is the primary purpose of IRB review?

A. To ensure the research will produce profitable results for the institution
B. To verify that the research meets ethical standards for informed consent, risk minimization, equitable participant selection, and privacy protection before experiments begin
C. To determine whether the research will receive government funding
D. To evaluate whether the research hypothesis is scientifically correct

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. IRBs exist to protect human research participants by ensuring that ethical standards are met before research begins. They evaluate whether informed consent is genuine, whether risks are minimized and justified by potential benefits, whether participant selection is equitable (not exploiting vulnerable populations), and whether privacy is protected. IRBs are ethics committees, not scientific or financial review boards. Their role is to protect human research participants by verifying informed consent, evaluating risk-benefit ratios, ensuring equitable selection (not exploiting vulnerable populations), and protecting privacy.

### Question 3

The precautionary principle states that when a scientific activity raises potential threats, precautionary measures should be taken even before causal relationships are fully established. How does this differ from the standard of 'prove it is harmful before regulating it'?

A. There is no difference; both approaches are identical
B. The precautionary principle shifts the burden of proof: instead of requiring proof of harm before acting, it requires proof of safety before proceeding with potentially dangerous research or technology
C. The precautionary principle prohibits all scientific research as potentially dangerous
D. The standard approach is more cautious than the precautionary principle

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. The precautionary principle reverses the burden of proof. Instead of 'it is allowed unless proven harmful,' it adopts 'it should not proceed until proven safe.' This is particularly relevant for irreversible technologies (gene drives, geoengineering) where waiting for proof of harm may mean the harm is already done and cannot be undone. The precautionary principle shifts the burden of proof from 'prove harm before regulating' to 'prove safety before proceeding.' This distinction matters most for irreversible technologies where waiting for evidence of harm may mean the damage is already done.

### Question 4

Scientific integrity includes honest data collection, accurate reporting, and willingness to publish negative results. Why is publishing negative results (experiments that did not support the hypothesis) important for scientific integrity?

A. Negative results are not important because only successful experiments advance knowledge
B. Publishing negative results prevents other researchers from wasting resources repeating failed experiments, reduces publication bias that distorts the scientific record, and demonstrates that the researcher is committed to truth rather than personal advancement
C. Negative results are only published in low-quality journals
D. Scientists are legally required to publish all results, including negative ones

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. When only positive results are published, the scientific literature becomes a biased sample that overstates the effectiveness of treatments, the strength of relationships, and the reproducibility of findings. Publishing negative results corrects this bias, saves resources, and maintains the self-correcting nature of science. The replication crisis in psychology and medicine is partly attributable to publication bias against negative results. Not publishing negative results creates publication bias: the literature disproportionately represents 'successful' experiments, overstating effect sizes and reliability. This contributed to the replication crisis. Publishing negative results saves resources (others do not repeat failed approaches) and maintains science's self-correcting capacity.

### Question 5

Who should decide which scientific research gets funded and which gets restricted? This question is central to science ethics. Which consideration most complicates simple answers?

A. Scientists should decide everything because they understand the research best
B. Politicians should decide everything because they represent the public
C. The question is complicated because scientists may have conflicts of interest (wanting to pursue their own research), politicians may lack technical understanding, and the public may not have access to sufficient information, suggesting that governance requires balanced input from multiple stakeholders
D. No one should make these decisions; all research should be unregulated

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: Correct. No single group can optimally govern science alone. Scientists have expertise but may have conflicts of interest. Politicians represent public interests but may lack technical understanding. The public deserves a voice but may not have specialized knowledge. Effective science governance requires balanced input from all stakeholders, with structures that mitigate each group's limitations. Each potential decision-maker has both strengths and limitations. Scientists have expertise but conflicts of interest. Politicians have democratic mandate but may lack technical knowledge. The public deserves input but may lack information. Effective governance requires balanced structures drawing on all perspectives.

### Question 1

The model shows that maximizing Innovation Rate does not maximize Societal Impact Score. Instead, the highest Societal Impact Score occurs at a moderate Innovation Rate with robust oversight. What does this finding reveal about the relationship between scientific freedom and societal benefit?

A. All scientific research should be stopped to maximize societal benefit
B. Scientific freedom without ethical governance produces innovations that are inequitably distributed, generate dual-use risks, and erode public trust, creating net harm that partially offsets the gains from rapid innovation
C. Innovation always produces net positive societal impact regardless of governance
D. Societal Impact Score is a meaningless metric that should be removed from the model

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. The model reveals that unrestricted innovation generates benefits but also produces dual-use risks (technologies that can harm), inequitable distribution (benefits concentrated among the wealthy), and trust erosion (public loses faith in science due to real or perceived irresponsibility). These negative effects partially offset innovation gains, meaning a moderate pace with ethical governance produces greater net societal benefit than unchecked speed. The model demonstrates that innovation speed is not the only determinant of societal benefit. Unrestricted innovation generates dual-use risks, inequitable distribution, and trust erosion that reduce net benefit. Moderate innovation with robust ethical governance maximizes the gap between benefits generated and harms produced.

### Question 2

The model reveals a positive feedback loop between Publication Transparency, Public Trust in Science, and funding support. When this loop operates in reverse (eroded trust), what cascade does the model predict?

A. Eroded trust has no effect on funding or research because they are independent
B. Reduced trust leads to reduced public support for research funding, which leads to less research, which leads to fewer scientific solutions to public problems, which further erodes trust in science's value, creating a self-reinforcing downward spiral
C. Eroded trust increases funding because politicians want to prove science is still valuable
D. Trust can never be eroded because science is inherently trustworthy

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. The model reveals a self-reinforcing trust spiral. When transparency decreases or misconduct occurs, public trust drops. Lower trust reduces political support for research funding. Less funding means fewer scientific breakthroughs and solutions. Fewer solutions mean the public perceives less value from science. This further erodes trust, continuing the cycle. Rebuilding trust once lost requires sustained transparency far beyond what was needed to maintain it. The model shows a reinforcing feedback loop: trust loss reduces funding, which reduces scientific output, which reduces public perception of science's value, which further erodes trust. This downward spiral is much easier to trigger than to reverse, which is why maintaining transparency is critical.

### Question 3

The model shows that Benefit Distribution Equity and Risk Distribution Equity are independent variables. A student discovers that a scenario with high total benefit but low equity scores produces a lower Societal Impact Score than a scenario with moderate total benefit but high equity. What does this reveal?

A. Total benefit does not matter; only equity matters
B. The distribution of benefits and risks matters as much as their total magnitude, because scientific advances that benefit only wealthy communities while imposing risks on marginalized communities create social harm that reduces net societal value even when total benefit is high
C. Equity should not be measured because it is subjective
D. The model incorrectly penalizes high-benefit research

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. The model captures the insight that a scientific advance is not socially optimal just because total benefit is high. If benefits flow to those who need them least while risks burden those who are already disadvantaged, the advance perpetuates and amplifies inequity. Historical examples (Tuskegee experiments, environmental pollution in minority communities) demonstrate that inequitable research creates lasting social harm that reduces net societal value. Both total benefit and equity distribution matter. High total benefit with low equity means benefits concentrate among the privileged while risks burden the disadvantaged. History shows this pattern (Tuskegee, environmental racism) creates lasting social harm that reduces net societal value even when total scientific output is impressive.

### Question 4

In the 'Dual-Use Crisis' scenario, the model shows that restricting publication of extreme dual-use research reduces Innovation Rate by 15% but improves Societal Impact Score by 25% due to reduced misuse risk. A student argues this proves all research should be restricted. Why is this generalization incorrect?

A. The student is correct; all research should be restricted
B. The model shows that the optimal balance between restriction and openness depends on the specific dual-use potential of each research area. Low dual-use research benefits greatly from open publication, while extreme dual-use research warrants careful restriction. A blanket policy in either direction is suboptimal
C. Restricting any research always reduces Societal Impact Score
D. Dual-use potential does not vary between research areas

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. The model reveals that the restriction-openness balance is case-dependent. For low dual-use research (most basic science), open publication maximizes societal benefit through knowledge sharing and reproducibility. For extreme dual-use research (gain-of-function pathogens, autonomous weapons AI), measured restriction protects against catastrophic misuse. Blanket restriction would stifle beneficial research, while blanket openness would enable dangerous misuse. The model shows that the optimal governance approach depends on the specific dual-use potential. Most research benefits from open publication (transparency, reproducibility, collaboration). Only research with extreme misuse potential warrants restriction. Case-by-case ethical evaluation produces better outcomes than blanket policies in either direction.

### Question 5

Adding 'International Governance Coordination' to the model reveals that unilateral research restrictions by one country are largely ineffective. What mechanism does the model identify as the cause of this ineffectiveness?

A. International laws automatically prevent any country from conducting dangerous research
B. When one country restricts research, scientists and research programs migrate to countries with fewer restrictions, meaning the dangerous research still occurs but without the oversight and transparency that governance was designed to provide, producing worse outcomes than coordinated international regulation
C. Unilateral restrictions are effective because each country can control its own researchers completely
D. International coordination is unnecessary because all countries have identical ethical standards

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. The model demonstrates regulatory arbitrage: when restrictions are unilateral, research moves to less-regulated jurisdictions. The dangerous research still occurs, but now without the oversight, safety protocols, and transparency that the restricting country would have provided. This produces worse outcomes than either universal regulation or universal openness, because it combines the innovation cost of restriction (in the restricting country) with the safety cost of unrestricted research (in the receiving country). Unilateral restrictions drive research to less-regulated jurisdictions rather than preventing it. The model shows this regulatory arbitrage produces the worst of both worlds: the restricting country loses innovation while the receiving country gains dangerous research without adequate oversight. Only coordinated international regulation can effectively govern dual-use research.
