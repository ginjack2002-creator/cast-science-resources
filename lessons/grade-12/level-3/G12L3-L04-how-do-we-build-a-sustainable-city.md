### Title
**How Do We Build a Sustainable City? — Modeling Urban Systems, Energy Networks, and Resource Flows for Resilient Communities**

### Overview
Cities are the defining habitat of the 21st century. More than 4.4 billion people — 56% of humanity — live in urban areas, and that number is projected to reach 6.7 billion by 2050. Cities occupy only 3% of Earth's land surface but consume 75% of natural resources, produce 70% of global carbon emissions, and generate 50% of global waste. How we design, power, and manage cities will largely determine whether humanity can address climate change and build a sustainable future. Students investigate the driving question: By 2050, over 68% of humanity will live in cities. These urban centers consume 75% of global energy, produce 70% of carbon emissions, and generate billions of tons of waste annually. Can we redesign cities as self-sustaining ecosystems rather than resource-consuming machines, and what would that transformation require? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

### Learning Objectives
- Model how energy generation, water management, waste processing, transportation networks, food systems, and green infrastructure interact to determine the sustainability of an urban system
- Analyze the feedback loops between population density, resource consumption, emissions output, and ecosystem health that drive urban sustainability or decline
- Evaluate the trade-offs between economic growth, environmental sustainability, and social equity in urban planning decisions using model-derived evidence
- Predict how changes in energy mix, transportation mode share, and waste management strategy affect a city's carbon footprint and long-term viability

### Component List (Starting Model: 10 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Population Density | External | The number of people per square kilometer in the urban area — higher density enables efficient public transit and shared infrastructure but increases demand on all resource systems and can create crowding-related health and quality-of-life issues |
| Solar Radiation Availability | External | The amount of solar energy available for rooftop and utility-scale solar generation — determined by latitude, climate, cloud cover, and building orientation, representing the renewable energy ceiling for the city |
| Regional Water Supply | External | The volume of freshwater available from rivers, aquifers, and rainfall — a hard constraint on urban growth that climate change is altering through shifting precipitation patterns and increasing drought frequency |
| Energy Mix Composition | Internal | The proportion of energy from renewable sources (solar, wind, geothermal) versus fossil fuels (natural gas, coal, oil) — directly determines the city's carbon emissions per unit of energy consumed and its vulnerability to fuel price volatility |
| Transportation Mode Share | Internal | The percentage of trips made by walking, cycling, public transit, and private vehicles — determines transportation-related emissions, infrastructure costs, land use for roads and parking, and accessibility equity across income levels |
| Water Recycling Rate | Internal | The percentage of wastewater that is treated and reused for irrigation, industrial processes, or potable use — higher recycling reduces demand on Regional Water Supply and decreases pollution discharge into waterways |
| Waste Diversion Rate | Internal | The percentage of solid waste diverted from landfills through recycling, composting, and waste-to-energy conversion — a key metric for circular economy progress and a major determinant of methane emissions from decomposing organic waste |
| Green Infrastructure Coverage | Internal | The proportion of urban area covered by parks, urban forests, green roofs, bioswales, and permeable surfaces — provides ecosystem services including stormwater management, air purification, temperature regulation, and mental health benefits |
| Carbon Emissions Rate | Internal | The total greenhouse gas emissions per capita per year — the integrated output of energy, transportation, waste, and building systems that determines the city's contribution to climate change |
| Urban Sustainability Index | Internal | A composite measure integrating environmental, social, and economic indicators to assess the city's long-term viability — incorporating resource efficiency, emissions trajectory, livability, equity, and resilience to climate disruption |

Task E: SCENARIO — INTEGRATED REDESIGN
• Energy: 80% renewable | Transport: 60% transit/walk/bike | Water Recycling: 80% | Waste Diversion: 75% | Green Space: 30%
• PREDICT FIRST: When all urban systems are simultaneously improved, do you predict the sustainability gains are additive or multiplicative? Why?
• Resume the simulation and observe what happens
• Was your prediction correct?

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

WHAT DID YOU DISCOVER?
• No single system change can make a city sustainable — the model reveals that renewable energy alone, transit improvements alone, or waste reduction alone each have limited impact because emissions and resource consumption are distributed across multiple interconnected systems
• Transportation mode share has a surprisingly large impact on overall sustainability because it affects not just emissions but land use (parking lots versus parks), public health (air quality, physical activity), equity (car dependency excludes low-income residents), and energy demand simultaneously
• Green infrastructure provides non-linear benefits — beyond a threshold of approximately 20-25% coverage, ecosystem services like stormwater management, temperature reduction, and air purification compound to significantly reduce the cost and energy demand of engineered infrastructure solutions
• The urban heat island effect creates a positive feedback loop: higher temperatures increase cooling energy demand, which increases emissions from fossil fuel power plants, which contributes to warming — green infrastructure breaks this loop by providing passive cooling through evapotranspiration

THE ANSWER: Building a sustainable city requires simultaneously transforming multiple interconnected systems — energy, transportation, water, waste, food, and land use — because no single change is sufficient. Our model reveals that a city powered by 80% renewable energy but dependent on cars, landfills, and sprawl remains unsustainable. True sustainability emerges when the whole urban system is redesigned: transit-oriented development reduces emissions and reclaims land from parking; circular waste systems eliminate landfills and recover resources; green infrastructure provides ecosystem services that replace energy-intensive engineered solutions; and water recycling creates resilience against climate-driven drought. The sustainable city is not a utopian dream — cities like Copenhagen, Singapore, and Curitiba have already implemented many of these changes. The challenge is not technology but political will, equitable implementation, and systems thinking.

LESSON PURPOSE:
Cities are the defining habitat of the 21st century. More than 4.4 billion people — 56% of humanity — live in urban areas, and that number is projected to reach 6.7 billion by 2050. Cities occupy only 3% of Earth's land surface but consume 75% of natural resources, produce 70% of global carbon emissions, and generate 50% of global waste. How we design, power, and manage cities will largely determine whether humanity can address climate change and build a sustainable future.

NGSS ALIGNMENT:
HS-ESS3-3: Create a computational simulation to illustrate the relationships among management of natural resources, the sustainability of human populations, and biodiversity.

THREE-DIMENSIONAL LEARNING:
• Science Practice: Using Mathematics and Computational Thinking
  Students use computational simulations to model the relationships among urban resource management, sustainability metrics, and population dynamics, quantifying the impact of different intervention strategies.
• Disciplinary Core Idea: ESS3.C Human Impacts on Earth Systems
  Students model how urban design decisions affect energy consumption, carbon emissions, water cycles, waste accumulation, and ecosystem health, analyzing the cumulative impact of cities on Earth systems.
• Crosscutting Concept: Systems and System Models
  Students analyze how the sustainability of a city emerges from the interactions among energy, transportation, water, waste, and land use systems, demonstrating that optimizing individual systems does not optimize the whole.

PACING GUIDE:
• Step 1 (Locate): 8-10 minutes
• Step 2 (Establish): 8-10 minutes

• Step 4 (Revise & Extend): 10-15 minutes

LEVER FRAMEWORK:
• Locate: Students identify ten key components of the urban sustainability system: population density, solar availability, water supply, energy mix, transportation mode share, water recycling, waste diversion, green infrastructure, carbon emissions, and urban sustainability index.
• Establish: Students map relationships showing how energy mix and transportation directly drive emissions, how green infrastructure provides non-linear ecosystem service benefits, and how all systems interact to produce the composite sustainability index.
• Visualize: Students build a ten-component computational model showing the interconnected urban metabolism with feedback loops between emissions, heat island effects, energy demand, and green infrastructure benefits.
• Evaluate: Students run business-as-usual, energy-only, and integrated redesign scenarios to quantify why systemic change produces dramatically better outcomes than single-system optimization.
• Revise: Students add food system localization, social equity index, or climate resilience score to model the full complexity of sustainable urban development including justice and adaptation dimensions.

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• Urban Metabolism: The City as Ecosystem:
  The concept of urban metabolism treats cities as ecosystems with inputs (energy, water, food, materials), internal processes (manufacturing, transportation, building operations), and outputs (emissions, wastewater, solid waste, heat). A linear urban metabolism takes resources, uses them once, and discards the waste. A circular urban metabolism closes loops: waste becomes feedstock, wastewater becomes irrigation, waste heat warms buildings, and organic waste becomes compost for urban farms. The most sustainable cities are those that maximize the circularity of their metabolism — reducing inputs, extending the useful life of materials, and recovering value from outputs.

• Transportation and Land Use:
  Transportation is the single largest source of emissions in most American cities, and it is inseparable from land use. Sprawling cities with separated residential, commercial, and industrial zones force long car commutes and make public transit economically unviable. Dense, mixed-use cities with transit-oriented development enable walking, cycling, and public transit as primary modes. The car-dependent model consumes enormous amounts of land for roads and parking — in many US cities, 30-50% of urban land is dedicated to vehicles. Reclaiming this land for housing, parks, and productive use is one of the largest untapped sustainability opportunities.

• Green Infrastructure and Ecosystem Services:
  Traditional engineering approaches to urban challenges — concrete stormwater drains, air conditioning, water treatment plants — are energy-intensive and expensive. Green infrastructure provides many of the same services using natural processes at lower cost: urban forests absorb CO2, filter air pollution, and reduce temperatures by 2-5 degrees through evapotranspiration; bioswales and permeable surfaces manage stormwater naturally, reducing flooding and sewer overflows; green roofs insulate buildings, reducing heating and cooling energy by 25-40%. These ecosystem services compound as coverage increases, creating a tipping point where green infrastructure significantly reduces the need for engineered solutions.

• Equity in Sustainability:
  Environmental sustainability and social equity are inseparable. Low-income neighborhoods and communities of color consistently bear disproportionate environmental burdens: more air pollution from highways and industrial facilities, less green space, higher urban heat island temperatures, more flooding from inadequate infrastructure, and less access to public transit. Sustainability interventions that ignore equity can worsen disparities — for example, green gentrification occurs when parks and bike lanes increase property values, displacing long-term residents who most need the improvements. Equitable sustainability requires that the communities most affected by environmental harm receive the benefits of sustainability investments first.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Technology alone can make cities sustainable"
  Reality: While technology is necessary — renewable energy, electric vehicles, smart grids, water recycling — technology without systemic redesign merely makes an unsustainable system slightly less harmful. An electric car still requires roads, parking, tire production, and battery materials. A smart building in a car-dependent suburb still generates sprawl. Sustainability requires changing the systems within which technology operates: land use patterns, transportation networks, resource flows, and governance structures.
  Strategy: Ask students: if every car in America were replaced with an electric car tomorrow, would our cities be sustainable? Why or why not? What else would need to change?

• "Dense cities are bad for the environment because they are crowded and polluted"
  Reality: Dense cities actually have lower per-capita emissions than suburbs and rural areas because they enable public transit, shared walls (less heating/cooling energy per person), shorter distances to services, and more efficient infrastructure. New York City has one-third the per-capita carbon footprint of the average American, primarily because most residents walk, bike, or take transit. The pollution associated with dense cities comes from cars and fossil fuel power plants, not from density itself.
  Strategy: Compare per-capita emissions: Manhattan (5.6 tons CO2/year) vs. US average (16 tons CO2/year). Ask: what explains this 3x difference? Density enables efficiency.

• "Sustainability means sacrificing quality of life"
  Reality: The most sustainable cities in the world — Copenhagen, Amsterdam, Vienna, Tokyo — consistently rank among the highest in quality of life. Walkable neighborhoods with good transit, clean air, green spaces, and local services are more pleasant to live in than car-dependent sprawl with long commutes, traffic congestion, and air pollution. Sustainability improvements often increase quality of life while reducing costs: transit is cheaper than car ownership, bike commuting improves health, and green spaces reduce stress.
  Strategy: Survey the class: would you rather live in a neighborhood where everything is a 15-minute walk or a neighborhood where everything requires a 30-minute drive? Most students prefer walkability — which is also the sustainable option.

• Step 1: Let students explore the interface. Don't over-explain.

  guide positive/negative relationship decisions. Let students debate.

  things on/off and observe. This is where real insight happens.

ANSWER KEY:
Big Question: By 2050, over 68% of humanity will live in cities. These urban centers consume 75% of global energy, produce 70% of carbon emissions, and generate billions of tons of waste annually. Can we redesign cities as self-sustaining ecosystems rather than resource-consuming machines, and what would that transformation require?
Answer: Building a sustainable city requires simultaneously transforming multiple interconnected systems — energy, transportation, water, waste, food, and land use — because no single change is sufficient. Our model reveals that a city powered by 80% renewable energy but dependent on cars, landfills, and sprawl remains unsustainable. True sustainability emerges when the whole urban system is redesigned: transit-oriented development reduces emissions and reclaims land from parking; circular waste systems eliminate landfills and recover resources; green infrastructure provides ecosystem services that replace energy-intensive engineered solutions; and water recycling creates resilience against climate-driven drought. The sustainable city is not a utopian dream — cities like Copenhagen, Singapore, and Curitiba have already implemented many of these changes. The challenge is not technology but political will, equitable implementation, and systems thinking.

Simulation Answers:
• Business as Usual: The model shows that a typical American city with 30% renewable energy, 85% car mode share, and 35% waste diversion produces high Carbon Emissions Rate and a low Urban Sustainability Index. The most striking finding is how transportation dominates emissions — even moderate renewable energy adoption cannot compensate for car-dependent sprawl because transportation, building energy, waste, and water systems each contribute substantially to the total.
• Energy-Only Solution: Shifting to 80% renewable energy reduces Carbon Emissions Rate by approximately 30-40% from the energy sector, but the Urban Sustainability Index improves only modestly because transportation (still 85% car), waste (still 65% to landfill), and water (still low recycling) continue to drive emissions and resource consumption through non-energy pathways. This scenario definitively shows that sustainability cannot be achieved through energy transition alone.
• Integrated Redesign: When all systems improve simultaneously, the Urban Sustainability Index increases by 3-4x more than the sum of individual improvements would predict. This multiplicative effect occurs because system improvements reinforce each other: reduced car usage means less road infrastructure, which means more land for green space, which reduces heat islands, which reduces cooling energy demand, which makes the renewable energy transition more achievable. Systemic change creates virtuous cycles that amplify each intervention's impact.

Reflection Exemplars:
• Q: Why can't energy alone make a city sustainable?
  A: Energy generation accounts for only about 25-30% of a city's total environmental footprint. Transportation contributes another 25-30%, waste management 10-15%, water systems 5-10%, and building operations the remainder. Even with 100% renewable energy, a city dependent on single-occupancy vehicles still produces massive emissions from tire and brake wear, road construction, and the embedded energy in manufacturing vehicles. Landfill waste still produces methane. Inefficient water systems still consume energy. The model shows that sustainability is a systems property — it emerges from the interaction of all urban systems, not from optimizing one system in isolation.
• Q: How does equity relate to sustainability?
  A: The model reveals that sustainability interventions have different impacts on different communities. Car-dependent infrastructure disproportionately harms low-income residents who cannot afford vehicles and are exposed to highway pollution. Green infrastructure improvements can increase property values and displace long-term residents through gentrification. Transit investments in wealthy suburbs may not reach the communities that need them most. Adding a Social Equity Index to the model would reveal that the most environmentally optimal intervention sequence is not necessarily the most equitable one, and that true sustainability requires explicitly prioritizing communities that currently bear the greatest environmental burden.

Key Concepts:
• 15-Minute City Concept: A urban planning model where all essential services — grocery stores, schools, healthcare, parks, workplaces — are accessible within a 15-minute walk or bike ride from every residence. This concept reduces transportation emissions, improves quality of life, supports local businesses, and creates more equitable access to resources regardless of car ownership.
• District Energy Systems: Centralized heating and cooling networks that serve entire neighborhoods through underground pipes, achieving 2-4x higher efficiency than individual building systems. District energy can utilize waste heat from industrial processes, data centers, and sewage systems, converting thermal waste into community heating.
• Green Gentrification Prevention: Strategies to ensure that sustainability improvements benefit existing residents rather than displacing them — including community land trusts, affordable housing mandates near transit stations, anti-displacement policies, and community ownership of green infrastructure projects.
