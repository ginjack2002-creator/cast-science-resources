## Platform

### Title
**Can We Colonize Mars? — Modeling Life Support Systems and Planetary Engineering Challenges**

### Overview
Mars is the most Earth-like planet in our solar system, yet it remains profoundly hostile to human life. With an atmosphere less than 1% as dense as Earth's, composed of 95% carbon dioxide, surface temperatures averaging negative 60 degrees Celsius, and no global magnetic field to deflect lethal cosmic radiation, every breath a human takes on Mars must be engineered. The question of Mars colonization is ultimately a question of systems engineering at a scale and complexity never before attempted. Students investigate the driving question: Mars has no breathable atmosphere, no liquid water on its surface, lethal radiation levels, and temperatures that average negative sixty degrees Celsius. Given these extreme conditions, is it scientifically feasible to engineer a self-sustaining human colony on Mars, or are we chasing science fiction? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

### Learning Objectives
- Model how atmospheric composition, radiation shielding, water recycling, food production, energy generation, and waste management interact to sustain human life in a closed system on Mars
- Analyze the feedback loops between life support subsystems and predict cascade failures when individual components degrade
- Evaluate the engineering trade-offs between in-situ resource utilization and Earth-supplied materials for long-term colony viability
- Predict how changes in solar energy input, atmospheric pressure, and regolith composition affect the sustainability of a Martian habitat

### Component List (Starting Model: 10 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Solar Energy Input | External | The amount of solar radiation reaching the Martian surface — approximately 43% of Earth's solar constant due to greater distance from the Sun, further reduced by dust storms that can last months and block up to 99% of sunlight |
| Atmospheric CO2 Availability | External | The concentration of carbon dioxide in Mars's thin atmosphere (95% CO2 but only 0.6% of Earth's atmospheric pressure) — the primary feedstock for oxygen production and potential greenhouse gas for warming |
| Subsurface Ice Reserves | External | Water ice deposits beneath the Martian regolith and at the polar caps — the critical resource for drinking water, oxygen generation, rocket fuel production, and agricultural irrigation |
| Radiation Exposure Level | External | The cumulative ionizing radiation dose from galactic cosmic rays and solar particle events reaching the habitat — without shielding, surface radiation is approximately 0.67 millisieverts per day, roughly 200 times Earth's background level |
| Oxygen Production Rate | Internal | The volume of breathable oxygen generated per hour through electrolysis of water or CO2 splitting using MOXIE-type technology — must maintain partial pressure between 19.5 and 23.5 kPa to support human respiration |
| Water Recycling Efficiency | Internal | The percentage of wastewater successfully purified and returned to potable quality — current ISS systems achieve approximately 93% efficiency, but a Mars colony would need 98%+ to minimize dependence on ice extraction |
| Food Production Capacity | Internal | The caloric and nutritional output of hydroponic or aeroponic growing systems per unit area — must provide approximately 2,500 calories per person per day with complete nutritional profiles including vitamins, minerals, and essential amino acids |
| Habitat Structural Integrity | Internal | The physical condition of pressurized habitat modules that maintain Earth-like atmospheric pressure against the near-vacuum of Mars — micrometeorite impacts, thermal cycling, and material fatigue all degrade structural integrity over time |
| Waste Processing Rate | Internal | The speed at which biological and industrial waste is converted into reusable resources through composting, chemical processing, and pyrolysis — unprocessed waste accumulates toxins and consumes limited habitat volume |
| Colony Viability Index | Internal | An integrated measure of the colony's ability to sustain human life indefinitely — a composite of all life support metrics that indicates whether the system is self-sustaining, dependent on resupply, or in decline toward failure |

• Look at the +/− toggle in the TOP LEFT corner
• For each connection, ask: "When this goes UP, does the other go UP or DOWN?"

  ○ Solar Energy Input → Oxygen Production Rate = POSITIVE (+)
    Solar energy powers the electrolysis systems that split CO2 or water to produce oxygen. When solar input drops during dust storms, oxygen production decreases proportionally unless backup power is available.

  ○ Solar Energy Input → Food Production Capacity = POSITIVE (+)
    Hydroponic and aeroponic growing systems require artificial lighting powered by solar energy. Reduced solar input means less light for photosynthesis and less power for climate control in growing modules.

  ○ Atmospheric CO2 Availability → Oxygen Production Rate = POSITIVE (+)
    Carbon dioxide is the feedstock for MOXIE-type electrolysis systems. Higher atmospheric CO2 availability provides more raw material for oxygen production, though the thin atmosphere limits collection rates.

  ○ Subsurface Ice Reserves → Water Recycling Efficiency = POSITIVE (+)
    Access to ice reserves provides a buffer that reduces the pressure on recycling systems. When ice is available, the system can tolerate lower recycling efficiency; when reserves are depleted, recycling must approach 100%.

  ○ Radiation Exposure Level → Habitat Structural Integrity = NEGATIVE (−)
    Prolonged radiation exposure degrades certain habitat materials and forces colonists to spend more time indoors, increasing wear on internal systems. Extreme radiation events may require emergency shelter protocols that stress structural systems.

  ○ Water Recycling Efficiency → Food Production Capacity = POSITIVE (+)
    Food production requires irrigation water. Higher recycling efficiency means more water available for hydroponics. A 5% drop in recycling efficiency can reduce food production by 15-20% as water is rationed to drinking and oxygen generation first.

  ○ Oxygen Production Rate → Colony Viability Index = POSITIVE (+)
    Breathable atmosphere is a non-negotiable requirement. If oxygen production falls below the minimum threshold for human respiration, colony viability drops to zero regardless of all other system performance.

  ○ Waste Processing Rate → Water Recycling Efficiency = POSITIVE (+)
    Waste processing recovers water from biological waste, graywater, and humidity. Faster waste processing feeds more water back into the recycling system, improving overall water availability for the colony.

• 1 negative relationship(s), 7 positive relationship(s)

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

WHAT DID YOU DISCOVER?
• Energy is the master variable — when solar input drops during dust storms, every other life support system degrades in a predictable cascade: oxygen production falls first, then food production, then water recycling loses power for purification pumps
• Water recycling efficiency is the most critical internal variable because water is used in oxygen generation, food production, cooling systems, and hygiene — even a 5% drop in recycling efficiency creates compounding resource deficits
• Radiation shielding is a binary threshold — below adequate shielding, cumulative radiation damage reduces crew capacity and eventually becomes lethal, creating a hard constraint that cannot be compensated by other systems
• True self-sufficiency requires redundancy in every system — the model reveals that a colony dependent on a single oxygen source, single food system, or single energy source will eventually fail when that system degrades

THE ANSWER: Colonizing Mars is scientifically feasible but extraordinarily difficult. The fundamental challenge is building a closed-loop life support system that can sustain human life indefinitely with minimal Earth resupply. Our model reveals that energy is the master variable: solar power drives oxygen production, food growth, water recycling, and waste processing, and Mars receives less than half of Earth's solar energy. Dust storms that last months can crash the entire system. Radiation shielding creates a hard survival threshold. Water recycling must exceed 98% efficiency. No single technology is the bottleneck — it is the integration of ten interdependent systems, each of which must work continuously for decades without the repair infrastructure we take for granted on Earth. Mars colonization is not science fiction, but it requires solving engineering challenges of a complexity humanity has never attempted.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

LESSON PURPOSE:
Mars is the most Earth-like planet in our solar system, yet it remains profoundly hostile to human life. With an atmosphere less than 1% as dense as Earth's, composed of 95% carbon dioxide, surface temperatures averaging negative 60 degrees Celsius, and no global magnetic field to deflect lethal cosmic radiation, every breath a human takes on Mars must be engineered. The question of Mars colonization is ultimately a question of systems engineering at a scale and complexity never before attempted.

NGSS ALIGNMENT:
HS-ESS2-4: Use a model to describe how variations in the flow of energy into and out of Earth's systems result in changes in climate.

THREE-DIMENSIONAL LEARNING:
• Science Practice: Developing and Using Models
  Students develop a multi-component computational model to simulate the interdependent life support systems required for a Mars colony and test system resilience under crisis scenarios.
• Disciplinary Core Idea: ESS2.D Weather and Climate
  Students analyze how variations in solar energy input, atmospheric composition, and surface conditions on Mars create climate challenges fundamentally different from Earth that constrain all engineering solutions.
• Crosscutting Concept: Systems and System Models
  Students identify how the behavior of the Mars colony emerges from the interactions among ten components rather than from any individual component, revealing that system-level properties like viability cannot be predicted from parts alone.

PACING GUIDE:
• Step 1 (Locate): 8-10 minutes
• Step 2 (Establish): 8-10 minutes
• Step 3 (Visualize & Evaluate): 10-12 minutes
• Step 4 (Revise & Extend): 10-15 minutes
• Total: 50-70 minutes (or split across 2 class periods)

□ Prepare Mars has no breathable atmosphere, no liquid water on its surface, lethal radiation levels, and temperatures that average negative sixty degrees Celsius. LEVER FRAMEWORK:
• Locate: Students identify ten key components of the Mars colony life support system: solar energy input, atmospheric CO2, subsurface ice, radiation exposure, oxygen production, water recycling, food production, habitat integrity, waste processing, and colony viability.
• Establish: Students map the causal relationships showing how solar energy drives oxygen, food, and water systems, how radiation creates hard survival thresholds, and how water recycling efficiency compounds across all subsystems.
• Visualize: Students build a ten-component computational model showing the interdependent life support architecture with cascading dependencies, feedback loops, and threshold effects.
• Evaluate: Students run optimal conditions, dust storm crisis, and aging colony scenarios to identify which variables most strongly determine colony viability and where critical failure thresholds exist.
• Revise: Students add psychological stress, regolith toxicity, or communication delay to model how human factors and environmental hazards compound the engineering challenges of Mars colonization.

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• The Martian Environment:
  Mars presents a suite of challenges that interact in complex ways. Surface atmospheric pressure is about 600 pascals — less than 1% of Earth's sea level pressure and below the Armstrong limit where water boils at body temperature. The atmosphere is 95.3% CO2, 2.7% nitrogen, and 1.6% argon with only trace oxygen. Surface gravity is 38% of Earth's, which may cause bone density loss, muscle atrophy, and cardiovascular deconditioning over time. The average surface temperature is negative 60 degrees Celsius with extremes from negative 140 to positive 20. Global dust storms can envelope the entire planet for months, reducing solar energy to near-zero levels. Without a global magnetic field, the surface receives approximately 0.67 millisieverts of radiation per day from galactic cosmic rays and solar particle events.

• Life Support Engineering:
  A Mars colony life support system must replicate the essential functions that Earth provides for free: breathable atmosphere, drinkable water, food, temperature regulation, radiation protection, and waste processing. The International Space Station demonstrates that humans can survive in space for over a year, but ISS depends on regular resupply from Earth — approximately 6,000 kilograms per crew rotation. A Mars colony cannot rely on resupply because launch windows occur only every 26 months and transit takes 6-9 months. The system must therefore be closed-loop: recycling water at 98%+ efficiency, regenerating oxygen from CO2, growing food hydroponically, processing all waste into reusable materials, and maintaining atmospheric pressure and composition within narrow human tolerance ranges.

• In-Situ Resource Utilization:
  The economics and physics of launching materials from Earth to Mars make ISRU essential. Every kilogram launched to Mars costs approximately $100,000-$500,000 depending on the mission architecture. NASA's MOXIE experiment on the Perseverance rover successfully demonstrated CO2-to-oxygen conversion on the Martian surface in 2021, producing about 12 grams of oxygen per hour. Scaling this to colony needs requires approximately 1,000 times MOXIE's capacity. Water extraction from subsurface ice deposits at the mid-latitudes or polar regions could provide the hydrogen needed for rocket fuel (via the Sabatier reaction combining H2 and CO2 to produce methane and water) and the feedstock for electrolytic oxygen production. Regolith processing for construction materials, radiation shielding mass, and potentially even metals is being actively researched.

• The Redundancy Imperative:
  Perhaps the most critical lesson from aerospace engineering is that any system without redundancy will eventually fail. On Earth, system failures are inconveniences; on Mars, they are potentially fatal. Every critical life support function must have at least two independent pathways. Oxygen can be produced by CO2 electrolysis and by water electrolysis. Water can be sourced from recycling and from ice extraction. Energy can come from solar panels and nuclear reactors. Food can be grown in hydroponics and supplemented with stored reserves. The engineering challenge is not making any single system work — it is making ten interdependent systems work simultaneously, continuously, for decades, with sufficient redundancy to survive the failure of any individual component.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "We can just terraform Mars to make it Earth-like"
  Reality: Terraforming Mars would require centuries to millennia of sustained effort. Mars has lost most of its atmosphere to solar wind stripping because it lacks a global magnetic field. Even if we could release all CO2 locked in polar caps and regolith, atmospheric pressure would reach only about 7% of Earth's — far below breathable levels. Creating a breathable oxygen atmosphere would require converting megatons of CO2 over thousands of years.
  Strategy: Show the math: Earth's atmosphere weighs 5.15 x 10^18 kg. Mars has lost most of its atmosphere over 4 billion years. Ask students to calculate how long it would take to rebuild at any conceivable rate of gas production.

• "The main challenge is getting to Mars"
  Reality: Landing humans on Mars is a solved engineering problem in principle — the challenge is keeping them alive after they arrive. The 6-9 month transit is difficult but finite. Living on Mars indefinitely requires solving closed-loop life support, radiation protection, food production, medical care without hospitals, and psychological endurance — all simultaneously and without resupply for years.
  Strategy: Compare the ISS budget (approximately $3 billion per year for 6 people with constant resupply) to what a self-sufficient Mars colony would require. Ask: what happens when something breaks and the nearest spare part is 6-9 months away?

• "Mars is basically like a cold desert on Earth"
  Reality: Mars is far more hostile than any desert on Earth. The atmosphere is unbreathable. The pressure is so low that exposed liquid water boils instantly. The soil contains toxic perchlorates. Radiation levels are 200 times higher than Earth's surface. There is no emergency rescue possible. The psychological analogy to a desert minimizes the engineering challenges by many orders of magnitude.
  Strategy: Have students list all the things they rely on that Earth provides for free — breathable air, drinkable water, food, radiation shielding, comfortable temperature, atmospheric pressure — and calculate what it would cost to engineer each one artificially.

• Step 1: Let students explore the interface. Don't over-explain.

ANSWER KEY:
Big Question: Mars has no breathable atmosphere, no liquid water on its surface, lethal radiation levels, and temperatures that average negative sixty degrees Celsius. Given these extreme conditions, is it scientifically feasible to engineer a self-sustaining human colony on Mars, or are we chasing science fiction?
Answer: Colonizing Mars is scientifically feasible but extraordinarily difficult. The fundamental challenge is building a closed-loop life support system that can sustain human life indefinitely with minimal Earth resupply. Our model reveals that energy is the master variable: solar power drives oxygen production, food growth, water recycling, and waste processing, and Mars receives less than half of Earth's solar energy. Dust storms that last months can crash the entire system. Radiation shielding creates a hard survival threshold. Water recycling must exceed 98% efficiency. No single technology is the bottleneck — it is the integration of ten interdependent systems, each of which must work continuously for decades without the repair infrastructure we take for granted on Earth. Mars colonization is not science fiction, but it requires solving engineering challenges of a complexity humanity has never attempted.

Simulation Answers:
• Optimal Conditions: With maximum solar energy, full ice reserves, and effective radiation shielding, the model shows all life support systems operating above minimum thresholds and the Colony Viability Index in the sustainable range. However, even under optimal conditions, the model reveals that Water Recycling Efficiency is the tightest margin — it must remain above 97% to prevent gradual water loss from exceeding ice extraction capacity.
• Dust Storm Crisis: When solar energy drops by 90% for three months, the cascade is devastating and predictable: Oxygen Production Rate falls first because electrolysis is energy-intensive, Food Production Capacity crashes within weeks as grow lights fail, and Water Recycling Efficiency degrades as purification pumps lose power. The Colony Viability Index drops below the survival threshold within 6-8 weeks unless the colony has sufficient energy storage or nuclear backup power to maintain critical systems.
• Aging Colony: After ten years with degraded solar panels, reduced water recycling, and compromised habitat integrity, the model shows a slow but accelerating decline in Colony Viability Index. The critical insight is that degradation compounds: 70% solar efficiency reduces oxygen and food production, which increases stress on water systems, which further reduces food output. Without proactive maintenance and component replacement, the colony enters a death spiral.

Reflection Exemplars:
• Q: Why is energy the master variable?
  A: Energy is the master variable because every other life support system depends on it. Oxygen production requires electrical power for electrolysis. Food production requires power for grow lights, pumps, and climate control. Water recycling requires power for filtration, UV sterilization, and distillation. Waste processing requires power for heating and chemical reactions. Even radiation shielding requires power for active magnetic systems. When solar energy drops during a dust storm, every system degrades simultaneously because they all compete for the same shrinking power supply. No other single variable has this cascading effect across all components.
• Q: What does the dust storm scenario teach about redundancy?
  A: The dust storm scenario demonstrates that redundancy is not a luxury but a survival requirement. A colony with only solar power faces catastrophic cascade failure during a three-month dust storm because every life support system loses its energy source simultaneously. A colony with both solar and nuclear power can maintain critical systems indefinitely regardless of dust conditions. The model shows that the cost of redundancy — building two independent systems for each critical function — is far less than the cost of failure, which is the death of every colonist. This principle applies to every subsystem: two oxygen sources, two water sources, two food strategies.

Key Concepts:
• Nuclear Power as Backup: Small modular nuclear reactors like NASA's Kilopower system can provide reliable baseline power regardless of dust conditions. A 40-kilowatt Kilopower reactor could maintain critical life support for months, but nuclear systems add mass, complexity, and radiation management challenges to the colony design.
• Bioregenerative Life Support: Systems that use living organisms — algae, plants, bacteria — to process waste, generate oxygen, purify water, and produce food simultaneously. These systems are more resilient than purely mechanical systems because biological processes self-regulate, but they are slower to respond to sudden changes and can crash if conditions deviate too far from optimal.
• Closed-Loop Water Systems: The ISS Water Recovery System achieves 93% recycling efficiency by processing urine, humidity condensate, and hygiene wastewater. Mars colonies would need multi-stage systems combining vapor compression distillation, catalytic oxidation, and membrane filtration to achieve 98%+ efficiency. Every percentage point of improvement significantly extends ice reserve lifetimes.

• Developing (2): Design covers basic life support needs but lacks redundancy strategy or quantitative connection to model evidence

STEM CHALLENGE: Design a Mars Colony Life Support Architecture
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

MISSION: Design an integrated life support system for a 100-person Mars colony that can survive a 6-month global dust storm without resupply from Earth, specifying redundancy strategies for each critical subsystem.

SCENARIO: SpaceX and NASA have selected your engineering team to design the life support architecture for the first permanent Mars settlement. The colony must support 100 people indefinitely. Your design must address energy generation and storage, atmospheric management, water recycling, food production, radiation protection, and waste processing. The critical test: your system must survive a six-month global dust storm that reduces solar input by 95% without any resupply from Earth.

GUIDING QUESTIONS:
• What energy storage capacity is needed to maintain critical life support during a six-month dust storm with 95% solar reduction?
• How does your model data inform the minimum water recycling efficiency required for colony survival?
• What redundancy strategy prevents a single-point failure in oxygen production from becoming a colony-wide catastrophe?

DESIGN THINKING:
• What is your primary and backup energy generation strategy and how much storage capacity does your design include?

  _________________________________________________________

• How does your water recycling system achieve the efficiency your model shows is necessary for survival?

  _________________________________________________________

• What specific radiation shielding approach does your habitat use and what is the predicted crew exposure level?

  _________________________________________________________

• How would you test your life support architecture on Earth before committing 100 people to Mars?

  _________________________________________________________


EVALUATION RUBRIC:
• Expert (4): Design specifies performance requirements derived from model data for all six subsystems, includes independent redundancy for each critical function, addresses dust storm survival with quantitative energy storage calculations, and identifies the weakest link in the architecture
• Proficient (3): Design addresses most subsystems with reasonable redundancy and references model data to justify key design choices
• Developing (2): Design covers basic life support needs but lacks redundancy strategy or quantitative connection to model evidence
• Beginning (1): Design is incomplete or treats life support systems as independent rather than interconnected
