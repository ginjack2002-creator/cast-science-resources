## Platform

### Title
**Plate Tectonics: Predicting Where Earth Will Crack Next — Using Models to Forecast Earthquakes and Volcanic Activity**

### Overview
Students model the dynamic stress system at tectonic plate boundaries, discovering why we can predict WHERE earthquakes happen but not WHEN. By building a model with mantle convection, boundary types, tectonic stress accumulation, and the earthquake-stress release feedback cycle, students explore how different plate boundaries produce different geological hazards.

[A dramatic photorealistic satellite view of Earth showing tectonic plate boundaries glowing with seismic activity markers, convergent boundaries in red and divergent in blue, cinematic Earth science visualization]

### Grade
6th (Level 2)

### NGSS Standard
**MS-ESS2-2**: Construct an explanation based on evidence for how geoscience processes have changed Earth's surface at varying time and spatial scales.
**MS-ESS2-3**: Analyze and interpret data on the distribution of fossils and rocks, continental shapes, and seafloor structures to provide evidence of past plate motions.

### Learning Objectives
- Explain how mantle convection drives tectonic plate movement at measurable rates
- Model how different plate boundary types produce different geological hazards
- Analyze the relationship between tectonic stress buildup and earthquake frequency
- Predict volcanic activity based on plate boundary type and mantle convection speed
- Describe how negative feedback between earthquakes and stress creates cyclical patterns

### Component List (Starting Model: 5 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Mantle Convection Speed | External | How fast hot rock circulates in the mantle, driving plate movement above |
| Plate Boundary Type | External | Whether plates are colliding (convergent), separating (divergent), or sliding (transform) |
| Tectonic Stress | Internal | The pressure accumulating at the boundary as plates interact, storing potential energy |
| Earthquake Frequency | Internal | How often seismic events occur at the boundary, related to stress release patterns |
| Volcanic Activity | Internal | The likelihood and intensity of volcanic eruptions, dependent on boundary type and magma availability |

### Video Script

```
"Time to connect the pieces of Earth's stress engine.

First: 'Mantle Convection Speed' to 'Tectonic Stress.' Faster
convection pushes plates harder, building more stress at boundaries.
POSITIVE.

Second: 'Tectonic Stress' to 'Earthquake Frequency.' More accumulated
stress means more stored energy that must release. More stress, more
quakes. POSITIVE.

Third: 'Plate Boundary Type' to 'Volcanic Activity.' This one is
interesting. At convergent boundaries, one plate dives under another
and melts, creating magma. Volcanoes! But at transform boundaries,
plates slide past each other horizontally. No diving, no melting,
no magma, no volcanoes. The boundary type determines the volcanic
hazard profile.

Now the critical connection: 'Earthquake Frequency' back to
'Tectonic Stress.' This is NEGATIVE. Earthquakes RELEASE accumulated
stress. After a big quake, stress drops. Fewer quakes for a while.
Then stress builds again. And the cycle repeats.

This negative feedback loop is why we cannot predict exact timing.
We know stress is building. We know it will release. But the
exact moment when accumulated stress overcomes rock friction
depends on microscopic variables we cannot measure. It is like
knowing a stretched rubber band will snap but not exactly when.

That is the fundamental challenge of earthquake prediction.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

slowly. But Volcanic Activity? Nearly zero. Transform boundaries
have plates sliding PAST each other horizontally. Neither plate
dives into the mantle. No melting. No magma. No volcanoes.

Japan: frequent earthquakes AND volcanoes. The slow transform is
like the San Andreas: less frequent earthquakes, no volcanoes.

Now look at the stress cycle graph closely. See how stress builds
gradually but drops suddenly? That is the real problem with
prediction. We know the cycle exists. We know stress is building.
But the EXACT moment of release is chaotic. Microscopic fractures,
water content in rock, temperature variations. Too many tiny
variables to predict precisely.

We can forecast probabilities, not exact dates. And that is what
your model shows.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"

tiny fractures, water content, mineral composition, temperature
variations. Deterministic in principle, chaotic in practice.

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions follow the CAST stimulus-response format, aligned to MS-ESS2-2 and MS-ESS2-3.

---

### Question 1

A student sorts components of a tectonic plate boundary model. Mantle Convection Speed, Plate Boundary Type, Tectonic Stress, Earthquake Frequency, and Volcanic Activity.

Which correctly sorts external from internal?

A. External: Mantle Convection Speed — Internal: Plate Boundary Type, Tectonic Stress, Earthquake Frequency, Volcanic Activity
B. External: Mantle Convection Speed, Plate Boundary Type — Internal: Tectonic Stress, Earthquake Frequency, Volcanic Activity
C. External: Tectonic Stress, Earthquake Frequency — Internal: Mantle Convection Speed, Plate Boundary Type, Volcanic Activity
D. External: Mantle Convection Speed, Tectonic Stress — Internal: Plate Boundary Type, Earthquake Frequency, Volcanic Activity

Correct Answer: B

---

### Question 2

In the model, earthquakes RELEASE accumulated tectonic stress. This is a negative feedback relationship.

Why does this negative feedback make earthquake timing unpredictable?

A. Because negative feedback stops earthquakes from happening entirely.
B. Because the stress-release cycle creates repeating patterns, but microscopic rock conditions make the exact moment of failure chaotic.
C. Because negative feedback means stress never builds at plate boundaries.
D. Because earthquakes happen randomly and have nothing to do with stress.

Correct Answer: B

---

### Question 3

A student compares two scenarios: a fast convergent boundary and a slow transform boundary. The convergent boundary shows both high earthquake frequency and high volcanic activity. The transform boundary shows moderate earthquakes and nearly zero volcanic activity.

Which explanation best accounts for the difference in volcanic activity?

A. Transform boundaries are too cold for volcanoes.
B. Convergent boundaries force one plate under another (subduction), where it melts and creates magma. Transform boundaries slide horizontally without subduction.
C. Convergent boundaries are closer to the Sun, which heats the rock.
D. Transform boundaries have stronger rock that prevents eruptions.

Correct Answer: B

---

### Question 4

Scientists observe that a transform fault has been quiet for 150 years while a similar fault nearby has frequent small earthquakes.

Based on tectonic stress models, which fault poses a greater risk?

A. The quiet fault, because 150 years of stress accumulation means a larger eventual release.
B. The active fault, because frequent earthquakes prove it is more dangerous.
C. Neither, because earthquake risk cannot be estimated from stress data.
D. Both pose equal risk because they are the same type of fault.

Correct Answer: A

---

### Question 5

Japan sits near a convergent boundary where the Pacific Plate subducts under the Eurasian Plate. California sits on the San Andreas Fault, a transform boundary.

Based on plate tectonic models, which hazard preparedness plan is most appropriate for each location?

A. Japan: earthquake preparedness only. California: volcano preparedness only.
B. Japan: earthquake, tsunami, AND volcano preparedness. California: earthquake preparedness with minimal volcanic concern.
C. Both need identical preparedness plans because all plate boundaries are the same.
D. California needs more preparedness because transform boundaries are more dangerous.

Correct Answer: B

---

Question 1: B | Question 2: B | Question 3: B | Question 4: A | Question 5: B
