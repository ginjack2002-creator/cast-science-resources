## Platform

### Title
**Why Your Hot Cocoa Betrays You — The Science of Thermal Energy Transfer**

### Overview
Thermal energy transfer is one of the most fundamental and observable physics concepts. Every student has experienced a hot drink cooling down — this lesson connects that everyday experience to the science of energy flow. Students investigate the driving question: No matter what you do, your hot cocoa always gets cold — where does the heat actually go? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

6th

### NGSS Standard
**MS-PS3-3, MS-PS3-4**: Apply scientific principles to design, construct, and test a device that either minimizes or maximizes thermal energy transfer. Plan an investigation to determine the relationships among the energy transferred, the type of matter, the mass, and the change in the average kinetic energy of the particles as measured by the temperature of the sample.

### Learning Objectives
- Explain how thermal energy transfers from hot objects to cold surroundings through conduction, convection, and radiation
- Model how temperature difference, insulation, surface area, and air movement affect heat loss
- Design a system that minimizes thermal energy transfer to keep a drink hot longer
- Connect thermal energy transfer to real-world engineering problems

LESSON PURPOSE:
Thermal energy transfer is one of the most fundamental and observable physics concepts. Every student has experienced a hot drink cooling down — this lesson connects that everyday experience to the science of energy flow.

NGSS ALIGNMENT:
MS-PS3-3, MS-PS3-4: Apply scientific principles to design, construct, and test a device that either minimizes or maximizes thermal energy transfer. THREE-DIMENSIONAL LEARNING:
• Science Practice: Developing and Using Models

• Disciplinary Core Idea: PS3.A Definitions of Energy / PS3.B Conservation of Energy and Energy Transfer

• Crosscutting Concept: Energy and Matter: Flows, Cycles, and Conservation
  Students trace the flow of thermal energy from the hot drink through the container to the surrounding environment, understanding that energy is conserved but transferred.

LEVER FRAMEWORK:

• Visualize: Students build a model showing how these four factors interact to determine how quickly a hot drink cools.
• Evaluate: Students run open mug vs. travel mug vs. windy day scenarios to quantify the impact of each variable.
• Revise: Students add radiation rate or container material to make the model more comprehensive.

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• Three Methods of Heat Transfer:
  Conduction: energy transfer through direct molecular contact (metal spoon in hot soup). Convection: energy transfer through fluid movement (warm air rising from a heater). Radiation: energy transfer through electromagnetic waves (feeling warmth from the sun). All three happen simultaneously with your hot cocoa.

• Temperature and Particle Motion:
  Temperature measures the average kinetic energy of particles. In a hot drink, water molecules are moving fast. They transfer their kinetic energy to slower-moving air molecules through collisions. This is conduction at the molecular level. The drink cools because its particles slow down.

• Insulation Science:
  Good insulators work by trapping pockets of air. Air is a poor conductor of heat, so trapped air layers create barriers to conduction. Foam cups, down jackets, and double-pane windows all use this principle. A vacuum (no air at all) is the best insulator — that's how a thermos works.

• Real-World Applications:
  Thermal engineering is critical in building design (insulation, HVAC systems), spacecraft (extreme temperature management), electronics (heat sinks), and food safety (keeping hot foods hot and cold foods cold). Understanding heat transfer saves energy, money, and lives.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Cold flows into warm objects"
  Reality: Cold is not a substance that flows. Only thermal energy (heat) flows — always from hot to cold. When your cocoa cools, heat is leaving the drink, not cold entering it. 'Feeling cold' means your body is losing heat to the environment.
  Strategy: Ask: When you open a freezer, does cold come out or does heat go in? Feel the air — it's your body heat leaving!

• "Insulation creates heat"
  Reality: Insulation doesn't generate any energy. It simply slows down the rate of heat transfer. A jacket keeps you warm by trapping your body's heat, not by creating warmth. If you put a coat on a snowman, it would actually melt SLOWER.
  Strategy: Demo: Put a thermometer inside a jacket with no heat source — the temperature doesn't rise.

• "Metal objects are colder than wooden objects"
  Reality: At room temperature, metal and wood are the SAME temperature. Metal just FEELS colder because it conducts heat away from your hand much faster than wood does. Your brain interprets rapid heat loss as 'cold.'
  Strategy: Measure both with a thermometer — they're identical! But your hand says otherwise because of conduction speed.

ANSWER KEY:
Big Question: No matter what you do, your hot cocoa always gets cold — where does the heat actually go?
Answer: Your hot cocoa loses heat to its cooler surroundings through three mechanisms: conduction (through the mug), convection (warm air rising off the surface), and radiation (infrared energy). Heat always flows from hot to cold until both reach the same temperature — that's the Second Law of Thermodynamics in your kitchen!

Simulation Answers:
• Open Mug vs. Travel Mug: The open mug loses heat rapidly — temperature drops quickly in the first 15-20 minutes then slows as it approaches room temperature. The travel mug (80% insulation) loses heat much more slowly, maintaining a drinkable temperature 3-4 times longer. The model shows insulation's dramatic effect.
• Windy Day Scenario: With maximum air movement, the open mug cools even faster than in still air. Wind accelerates convection, the most significant heat loss mechanism for a hot liquid. The model shows that convection can double or triple the cooling rate compared to still air.

Reflection Exemplars:
• Q: Why does blowing on soup cool it down?
  A: Blowing on soup accelerates convection. Your breath pushes away the layer of warm air sitting above the soup and replaces it with cooler air. This increases the temperature difference at the surface and speeds up heat transfer. You're essentially creating artificial wind that strips away the warm air boundary layer.
• Q: How does a coat keep you warm if it doesn't create heat?
  A: Your body generates heat through metabolism. A coat traps air between its fibers, creating insulation layers. These trapped air pockets slow the conduction of your body heat to the cold outside air. The coat doesn't warm you — it prevents your own heat from escaping. That's why a coat in a closet isn't warm but a coat on your body is.

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.1 (Determine components of a system) + DCI PS3.A.1 (Describe thermal energy transfer) + CCC4 (Describe a system in terms of its components)

A student is building a model of how a hot cup of cocoa loses heat to its surroundings. She identifies four factors that affect how quickly the cocoa cools: Temperature Difference between the cocoa and the room, Insulation Level of the container, Container Surface Area, and Air Movement around the container. She needs to classify each as external or internal to the container system.

Which list correctly sorts the components of the thermal energy system?

A. External: Temperature Difference, Air Movement — Internal: Insulation Level, Container Surface Area
B. External: Temperature Difference, Insulation Level — Internal: Air Movement, Container Surface Area
C. External: Air Movement, Container Surface Area — Internal: Temperature Difference, Insulation Level
D. External: Insulation Level, Container Surface Area — Internal: Temperature Difference, Air Movement

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: Temperature Difference depends on the room environment and the cocoa's starting temperature, both set by conditions outside the container system. Air Movement is a wind or draft from the external environment. Insulation Level and Container Surface Area are physical properties of the container itself, making them internal. If you chose D, this reverses the classification — insulation and surface area are design features of the container, not external forces. If you chose B, Insulation Level is a built-in property of the container, not an external input. If you chose C, Container Surface Area is fixed by the container's shape and cannot change from outside forces.

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships among components) + DCI PS3.B.1 (Describe energy transfer mechanisms) + CCC4 (Describe components and interactions)

In a thermal energy model, a student draws an arrow from Insulation Level to Heat Loss Rate and labels it as a negative relationship. This means that when insulation increases, heat loss rate decreases. A classmate says the relationship should be positive because thicker insulation means the container is 'doing more work.'

Which explanation best supports why the relationship between Insulation Level and Heat Loss Rate is negative?

A. Insulation only works in cold weather, so the relationship depends on the season.
B. Insulation slows the transfer of thermal energy through the container walls, so more insulation means heat escapes more slowly.
C. Insulation absorbs all the heat permanently, so the heat is destroyed rather than transferred.
D. Insulation generates cold air that counteracts the heat, so more insulation means more cooling.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: A negative relationship means when one goes up, the other goes down. Insulation creates a barrier that slows conduction through container walls. More insulation means thermal energy transfers more slowly to the surroundings, reducing the heat loss rate. If you chose D, insulation does not generate cold — it simply slows down energy transfer in both directions. If you chose C, insulation does not destroy heat energy — it only slows the rate of transfer. The heat will eventually escape; it just takes longer. If you chose A, insulation works in any temperature condition where there is a temperature difference between two sides, regardless of season.

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships among components) + DCI PS3.A.2 (Explain thermal equilibrium) + CCC5 (Describe energy flow within a system)

A student builds a thermal model showing that Temperature Difference has a positive relationship with Heat Loss Rate (bigger difference means faster loss), Insulation Level has a negative relationship with Heat Loss Rate (more insulation means slower loss), and Container Surface Area has a positive relationship with Heat Loss Rate (more area means faster loss). The student pours hot cocoa into a tall, thin ceramic mug with no lid and sets it on a windy porch.

Based on the model, what would most likely happen to the cocoa's temperature compared to the same cocoa in an insulated thermos indoors?

A. The mug on the porch would cool much faster because it has no insulation, more surface area exposure, and wind increases convective heat loss.
B. Both would cool at the same rate because the cocoa starts at the same temperature.
C. The mug on the porch would stay warm because outdoor air is thicker than indoor air.
D. The thermos cocoa would cool faster because the thermos traps heat and overheats the cocoa.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: The model identifies three factors that all work against the porch mug: no insulation (high heat loss rate), tall thin shape means more surface area exposed (more area for heat to escape), and wind increases air movement (faster convective cooling). The thermos indoors has insulation, less effective surface area, and no wind. If you chose B, identical starting temperatures do not guarantee identical cooling rates — the environment and container matter greatly. If you chose D, a thermos does not overheat cocoa — it slows heat loss, keeping the cocoa warm longer. If you chose C, air thickness (density) does not prevent cooling — wind actively carries heat away through convection.

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to explain/predict events) + DCI PS3.B.1 (Analyze energy transfer data) + CCC5 (Track energy flow through a system)

A student tested three containers holding the same amount of cocoa at 85 degrees C. Container A was a ceramic mug (no insulation). Container B was the same mug wrapped in one layer of foam. Container C was the same mug wrapped in three layers of foam. After 20 minutes: Container A was at 42 degrees C, Container B was at 58 degrees C, and Container C was at 71 degrees C. Room temperature was 22 degrees C throughout the experiment.

Which observation best supports the claim that insulation has a negative relationship with heat loss rate?

A. Container A cooled the most because ceramic is the worst insulator.
B. All containers lost some heat, proving that insulation does not work.
C. As insulation layers increased from zero to one to three, the temperature after 20 minutes increased from 42 to 58 to 71 degrees C, showing that more insulation systematically reduced heat loss.
D. Container C stayed at 71 degrees C because three layers of foam stopped all heat transfer.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: The data shows a clear pattern: 0 layers lost 43 degrees, 1 layer lost 27 degrees, and 3 layers lost only 14 degrees. Each increase in insulation resulted in less heat loss, directly demonstrating the negative relationship between insulation and heat loss rate. If you chose A, while true that A cooled the most, this only describes one data point and does not show the systematic pattern across all three containers. If you chose B, the fact that all containers lost some heat actually confirms the model — insulation slows but does not eliminate heat transfer. The key is the rate difference. If you chose D, Container C did lose heat (from 85 to 71 degrees), so insulation did not stop all transfer — it just slowed it significantly.

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to predict a scientific event) + DCI PS3.A.1 (Apply thermal energy principles) + CCC5 (Describe energy transfer in a designed system)

A company wants to design a travel mug that keeps coffee hot for 4 hours during a commute. Their thermal model shows that Temperature Difference has a positive relationship with heat loss (bigger gap means faster loss), Insulation Level has a negative relationship with heat loss (more insulation means slower loss), Container Surface Area has a positive relationship with heat loss (more area means faster loss), and Air Movement has a positive relationship with heat loss (more wind means faster loss). The designers cannot control the outside temperature or wind on a commute.

Based on the model, which design combination would most effectively keep coffee hot for 4 hours?

A. A tall, thin mug with a wide opening and no insulation, because more surface area means the coffee can 'breathe.'
B. A metal mug with no lid, because metal retains heat better than plastic.
C. A glass mug with thin walls, because glass allows heat to stay inside.
D. A short, wide mug with thick insulation, a sealed lid, and a compact shape that minimizes surface area.

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: The model identifies two factors the designers can control: insulation (negative relationship with heat loss — more is better) and surface area (positive relationship — less is better). A compact shape minimizes surface area, thick insulation slows heat transfer, and a sealed lid reduces convective loss from the top. If you chose A, tall and thin means more surface area (more heat loss), and no insulation means nothing to slow heat transfer — this is the worst design. If you chose B, metal is actually a better conductor of heat than plastic, meaning it transfers heat away faster, not slower. A metal mug without a lid would cool very quickly. If you chose C, glass is a poor insulator and thin walls provide almost no barrier to heat transfer.
