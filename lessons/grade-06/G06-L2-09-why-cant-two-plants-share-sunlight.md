## Platform

### Title
**Why Can't Two Plants Share the Same Sunlight? — Modeling Competition for Limited Resources in Ecosystems**

### Overview
Students model why two plants growing side by side do not share resources equally. By building a cause-and-effect model of sunlight, soil nutrients, growth rates, and resource depletion, students discover how small initial advantages compound through feedback loops to create clear winners and losers. This lesson serves as a hook for deeper inquiry into ecosystem dynamics, biodiversity, and the forces that shape communities of organisms.

### Cover Image
[Two plant species growing close together in a forest, one tall receiving full sunlight while the other is shaded below, dappled forest light with green tones]

### Grade
6th (Level 2)

### NGSS Standard
**MS-LS2-1**: Analyze and interpret data to provide evidence for the effects of resource availability on organisms and populations of organisms in an ecosystem.
**MS-LS2-2**: Construct an explanation that predicts patterns of interactions among organisms across multiple ecosystems.

### Learning Objectives
- Model how two plants compete for limited sunlight and soil nutrients
- Explain how resource availability determines which organisms thrive and which decline
- Predict the outcome of competition when one plant species has an advantage in resource capture
- Analyze how resource depletion creates feedback loops that intensify competition over time
- Connect interspecific competition to patterns of biodiversity and species distribution

### Component List (Starting Model: 5 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Sunlight Available | External | The total amount of light energy reaching the ecosystem, which both plants need for photosynthesis |
| Soil Nutrients | External | The minerals and nitrogen in the soil that both plants require for growth but exist in limited quantities |
| Plant A Growth | Internal | The rate at which Plant A increases in biomass by capturing sunlight and absorbing soil nutrients |
| Plant B Growth | Internal | The rate at which Plant B increases in biomass by capturing sunlight and absorbing soil nutrients |
| Resource Depletion | Internal | The rate at which available sunlight and soil nutrients are consumed as both plants grow and compete |

### Video Script

```
"Your pieces are on the board, but they are not talking to each
other yet. Time to draw the connections. And this system has a
feedback loop that makes competition incredibly powerful.

Click the 'Connect' icon in the top left corner.

First: both plants need sunlight. Connect 'Sunlight Available'
to 'Plant A Growth' and to 'Plant B Growth.' Both are POSITIVE.
More sunlight means more growth for both plants.

Same thing with soil nutrients. Connect 'Soil Nutrients' to both
plant growth rates. Both POSITIVE. More nutrients, more growth.

So far it looks fair, right? Both plants getting the same inputs.
Here is where it gets interesting.

Connect 'Plant A Growth' to 'Resource Depletion.' POSITIVE. As
Plant A grows, it USES UP resources. It shades the ground below.
Its roots absorb nutrients.

Connect 'Plant B Growth' to 'Resource Depletion.' Also POSITIVE.
Plant B is doing the same thing.

Now here is the critical connection — the feedback loop.

Connect 'Resource Depletion' BACK to 'Plant A Growth.' This one
is NEGATIVE. As resources get depleted, Plant A has less to work
with, so its growth slows.

And connect 'Resource Depletion' to 'Plant B Growth.' Also
NEGATIVE.

Look at the loop: growth depletes resources, depleted resources
limit growth. Both plants are caught in this cycle.

But here is the brutal part. If Plant A is even a tiny bit taller,
it captures slightly more sunlight. That extra growth depletes
resources slightly faster. And who gets hurt more by depleted
resources? The SMALLER plant. Plant B. Because Plant A is already
above it, shading it.

This feedback loop means even a tiny advantage snowballs into
total domination. That is competition in ecology.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"

### Video Script

```
"You have built the competition machine. Now let us see it run.

First scenario: equal start. Both plants start the same size,
same access to sunlight and nutrients. Click Play and watch.

At first, they grow at nearly the same rate. Seems fair, right?
But keep watching. Any tiny random variation will tip the balance.
Once one plant gets even slightly ahead, watch what happens.

The taller plant captures a fraction more sunlight. That extra
growth depletes resources a fraction faster. The shorter plant
gets a fraction less. Its growth slows by a fraction. The taller
plant pulls further ahead.

Each cycle through the feedback loop amplifies the difference.
This is not a slow process. Within a few dozen time steps, one
plant is thriving and the other is struggling.

Now let us make it obvious. Reset the simulation. Give Plant A
a slight height advantage. Not much. Just a small head start.

Run it. Watch Plant A's growth line climb. Watch Plant B's line
flatten and then start falling. Plant A is shading Plant B,
absorbing nutrients Plant B needs, growing faster and faster
while Plant B starves.

That tiny head start became total domination. This is what
ecologists call competitive exclusion.

One more test. Lock Soil Nutrients to low. Start both plants
equal. Now resources are scarce from the beginning. What happens?

The competition is even more fierce. With fewer nutrients to go
around, the feedback loop kicks in faster. The difference between
winner and loser appears sooner and grows more dramatically.

Your model just demonstrated one of the most important principles
in all of ecology: in competition for the same resources, small
advantages compound into large outcomes.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
TEACHER GUIDE: Why Can't Two Plants Share the Same Sunlight?
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

LESSON PURPOSE:
This lesson uses the observable phenomenon of forest layers to
teach competition and resource dynamics in ecosystems. Students
build a model showing how two plants compete for shared resources,
discovering the feedback loop that amplifies small advantages into
competitive exclusion. It serves as a hook for deeper inquiry into
biodiversity, niche partitioning, and ecosystem dynamics.

NGSS ALIGNMENT:
MS-LS2-1: Analyze and interpret data to provide evidence for the
effects of resource availability on organisms and populations of
organisms in an ecosystem.
MS-LS2-2: Construct an explanation that predicts patterns of
interactions among organisms across multiple ecosystems.

PACING GUIDE:
• Step 1 (Locate): 8-10 minutes
• Step 2 (Establish): 10-12 minutes (more connections)
• Step 3 (Visualize): 10-12 minutes
• Step 4 (Extend): 10-15 minutes
• Total: 40-50 minutes (or split across 2 days)

PRE-LESSON PREP:
□ Test ModelIt access on student devices
□ Have photos of forest canopy layers ready
□ Two potted plants of different heights under a lamp (optional demo)
□ Review vocabulary: competition, competitive exclusion, resource partitioning

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Plants cooperate and share resources" → While mycorrhizal networks
   exist, plants in close proximity generally compete intensely.
   They do not share resources voluntarily.
• "Competition only matters when resources are scarce" → Competition
   shapes growth even with abundant resources. A taller plant shades
   a shorter one even on the brightest day.
• "The bigger plant always wins" → Size is a major advantage, but
   species with different root depths or shade tolerance can coexist.

FACILITATION TIPS:
• Step 1: Show a forest photo. Ask why the floor is dark.
• Step 2: The feedback loop is the key concept. Draw it on the board.
   Growth → depletion → less growth → but who gets hurt more?
• Step 3: The equal start scenario is unstable. Let students discover
   that any tiny random variation breaks the symmetry.
• Step 4: Push students toward the biodiversity question. If
   competition always produces one winner, why are there so many species?

DISCUSSION PROMPTS:
• "Why does weeding a garden help your plants grow better?"
• "What happens when an invasive species arrives in a new ecosystem?"
• "If competitive exclusion always eliminates the loser, why do forests
   have hundreds of different plant species?"
• "How is a small garden with limited space different from a huge field?"

EXTENSION CONNECTIONS:
• Ecosystem dynamics → Food webs and energy flow
• Evolution → How competition drives natural selection
• Human impact → Invasive species and habitat destruction
• Agriculture → Why farmers use spacing and companion planting

ASSESSMENT:
• Can student explain the feedback loop between growth and resource depletion?
• Does student's model show correct positive/negative relationships?
• Can student explain why small advantages compound?
• Did student add at least one researched component?

DIFFERENTIATION:
Support: Pre-label relationships; use sentence starters; demonstrate
with two plants and a lamp

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format. Each item is three-dimensional, assessing a Science and Engineering Practice (SEP), Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and Crosscutting Concept (CCC) simultaneously, aligned to MS-LS2-1 and MS-LS2-2.

---

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.1 (Determine components of a system) + DCI LS2.A (Interdependent Relationships in Ecosystems) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A student is building a model of competition between two plants. She identifies five things that affect the outcome: sunlight available, soil nutrients, Plant A growth, Plant B growth, and resource depletion. She needs to sort them into two groups: things provided by the environment (external) and things that change through competition (internal).

Which list correctly sorts the components of the competition system?

A. External: Sunlight Available, Plant A Growth — Internal: Soil Nutrients, Plant B Growth, Resource Depletion
B. External: Sunlight Available, Soil Nutrients — Internal: Plant A Growth, Plant B Growth, Resource Depletion
C. External: Resource Depletion, Sunlight Available — Internal: Soil Nutrients, Plant A Growth, Plant B Growth
D. External: Sunlight Available, Soil Nutrients, Resource Depletion — Internal: Plant A Growth, Plant B Growth

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Sunlight Available and Soil Nutrients are provided by the environment and exist before the plants begin competing, making them external inputs. Plant A Growth, Plant B Growth, and Resource Depletion are all driven by the competition dynamics within the system, making them internal. If you chose A, Plant A Growth is not external; it responds to available resources. If you chose C, Resource Depletion is a consequence of the plants consuming shared resources, not an environmental input. If you chose D, Resource Depletion is generated by the competition, not provided by the environment.

---

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships among components) + DCI LS2.A (Interdependent Relationships) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

In a competition model, a student draws an arrow from Resource Depletion to Plant B Growth and labels it as a negative relationship. Another student disagrees and says it should be positive because when resources are depleted, Plant B has to grow harder to compete.

Which explanation best supports why the relationship between Resource Depletion and Plant B Growth is negative?

A. When resources are depleted, Plant B works harder and grows faster to compete for what remains.
B. When resources are depleted, there is less sunlight and fewer nutrients available for Plant B, so its growth rate decreases.
C. When resources are depleted, Plant A also declines, so Plant B benefits from less competition.
D. When resources are depleted, Plant B moves to a new area where resources are more available.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: A negative relationship means when one goes up, the other goes down. When Resource Depletion increases, the available pool of sunlight and nutrients shrinks. With fewer resources to absorb, Plant B cannot maintain its growth rate. If you chose A, wanting to grow harder does not change the physical reality of fewer available resources. Without sufficient sunlight and nutrients, growth must slow. If you chose C, this describes potential indirect effects, but the direct relationship is that depletion reduces the resources available to Plant B. If you chose D, plants cannot relocate. They are rooted in place and must compete with whatever resources are locally available.

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships among components) + DCI LS2.A (Interdependent Relationships) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A student builds a competition model. Sunlight and soil nutrients positively drive both Plant A and Plant B growth. Both growth rates positively increase resource depletion. Resource depletion negatively affects both growth rates. Plant A starts 2 cm taller than Plant B.

Based on the model, what would most likely happen over 50 time steps?

A. Both plants would grow equally because they have the same resources available.
B. Plant A would grow faster, deplete more resources, and Plant B would decline because the feedback loop amplifies Plant A's initial advantage.
C. Plant B would catch up because it is shorter and closer to the soil nutrients.
D. Both plants would stop growing because resource depletion would eliminate all available resources.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Plant A starts taller, so it captures slightly more sunlight from the beginning. This extra growth means Plant A contributes more to resource depletion, leaving less for Plant B. With fewer resources, Plant B grows slower, while Plant A continues to dominate. Each cycle through the feedback loop amplifies Plant A's advantage. If you chose A, they do not have equal access. Plant A is taller and intercepts more light. If you chose C, being shorter is a disadvantage for light capture, not an advantage. Root depth could help, but the model does not include root depth difference. If you chose D, Plant A would slow but not stop, because as Plant B declines, Plant A captures a larger share of the remaining resources.

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to explain/predict events) + DCI LS2.A (Interdependent Relationships) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

Scientists studied two plant species growing in the same plot over 12 weeks. In Week 1, both species were the same height. By Week 4, Species A was 5 cm taller. By Week 8, Species A was 20 cm taller and Species B had stopped growing. By Week 12, Species A dominated the plot and Species B was wilting.

Which observation best supports the idea that competition creates a feedback loop that amplifies small advantages?

A. In Week 1, both species started at the same height.
B. The height difference grew from 5 cm in Week 4, to 20 cm in Week 8, to total domination by Week 12, showing the gap accelerated over time.
C. By Week 12, Species A dominated the plot.
D. Species B stopped growing by Week 8.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: A feedback loop amplifies differences over time, meaning the gap should grow faster and faster, not at a constant rate. The data shows exactly this: 5 cm gap at Week 4, 20 cm gap at Week 8 (the gap quadrupled in the same time), and total domination by Week 12. The acceleration of the difference is the signature of a positive feedback loop. If you chose A, the starting point does not show feedback. If you chose C, this is the end result but does not show the amplification process. If you chose D, this describes Plant B's decline but does not illustrate the accelerating nature of the feedback loop.

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to predict a scientific event) + DCI LS2.A (Interdependent Relationships) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A community garden wants to grow two plant species in the same raised bed. The garden manager knows that competition for sunlight and nutrients will eventually cause one species to dominate. She wants both species to coexist.

Based on the competition model, which strategy would most likely allow both species to coexist?

A. Water both plants equally every day to ensure fair resource distribution.
B. Choose two species with different root depths and different sunlight needs so they access different resources.
C. Plant twice as many of the weaker species to give it a numerical advantage.
D. Add extra fertilizer so there is more than enough nutrients for both species.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: The competition model shows that two species competing for the exact same resources cannot coexist because the feedback loop will amplify any advantage. The solution is resource partitioning: choosing species that use different resources or the same resource at different levels. If one plant has deep roots and the other has shallow roots, they access different nutrient zones. If one needs full sun and the other is shade-tolerant, they can share vertical space. If you chose A, equal watering does not solve the fundamental problem of light competition. The taller plant still shades the shorter one. If you chose C, more individuals of the weaker species does not change the resource capture dynamics. The taller species will still shade and outcompete them over time. If you chose D, extra nutrients help, but the light competition remains. The taller plant will still shade the shorter one, eventually winning.
