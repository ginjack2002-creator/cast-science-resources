## Platform

### Title
**Why Does It Feel Colder When It's Windy? — Modeling Wind Chill and Convective Heat Loss**

### Overview
Students model why a windy day feels much colder than a calm day at the same temperature. By building a cause-and-effect model of wind speed, air temperature, convective heat loss, and skin temperature, students discover that wind strips away the thin insulating layer of warm air the body creates around itself. This lesson serves as a hook for deeper inquiry into thermal energy transfer, weather phenomena, and how the human body interacts with its environment.

### Learning Objectives
- Explain how wind increases the rate of convective heat loss from the skin
- Model the relationship between wind speed, air temperature, and perceived cold
- Distinguish between actual air temperature and the wind chill effect on the human body
- Predict how changes in wind speed affect skin temperature at different air temperatures
- Analyze why wind chill is a measure of heat loss rate rather than actual temperature

### Component List (Starting Model: 5 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Wind Speed | External | The velocity of moving air in the environment, which strips away the insulating boundary layer near the skin |
| Air Temperature | External | The actual measured temperature of the surrounding air, independent of wind conditions |
| Convective Heat Loss | Internal | The rate at which thermal energy is carried away from the skin by moving air currents |
| Skin Temperature | Internal | The surface temperature of the skin, which drops as heat is lost to the environment faster than the body replaces it |
| Perceived Cold | Internal | How cold the body feels based on the combined effect of air temperature and wind-driven heat loss |

### Text Editor

```

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Task A: OPEN CONNECTION MODE
• Click the "Connect" icon in the TOP LEFT corner of your screen
• Your cursor is now ready to draw relationship arrows

Task B: DRAW YOUR RELATIONSHIPS
• Click on "Wind Speed" and drag an arrow to "Convective Heat Loss"
• Click on "Air Temperature" and drag an arrow to "Convective Heat Loss"
• Click on "Convective Heat Loss" and drag an arrow to "Skin Temperature"
• Click on "Skin Temperature" and drag an arrow to "Perceived Cold"

Task C: SET POSITIVE OR NEGATIVE
• Look at the +/- toggle in the TOP LEFT corner
• For each connection, ask: "When this goes UP, does the other go UP or DOWN?"

  ○ Wind Speed → Convective Heat Loss = POSITIVE (+)
    More wind = MORE heat loss (wind strips your warm air layer faster!)

  ○ Air Temperature → Convective Heat Loss = NEGATIVE (−)
    Higher air temp = LESS heat loss (warm air takes less heat away)

  ○ Convective Heat Loss → Skin Temperature = NEGATIVE (−)
    More heat loss = LOWER skin temperature (heat is being stolen!)

  ○ Skin Temperature → Perceived Cold = NEGATIVE (−)
    Lower skin temp = MORE perceived cold (cold receptors fire!)

Task D: CHECK YOUR MODEL
• You should have 4 arrows total
• 1 positive relationship, 3 negative relationships
• This is your wind chill system!

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Notice the chain: Wind strips heat → skin cools → you feel cold.
Wind does not change the air. It changes YOU.
```

### Video Script

```
"Your pieces are on the board, but they are not talking to each
other yet. Time to draw the invisible lines — the relationships
that make this a SYSTEM, not just a pile of parts.

Click the 'Connect' icon in the top left corner. Now you are in
connection mode.

First connection: Click on 'Wind Speed' and drag an arrow to
'Convective Heat Loss.' When wind speed goes UP, does heat loss
go UP or DOWN?

Think about it. More wind means the warm air layer around your
skin gets stripped away faster. More wind, more heat loss. That is
a POSITIVE relationship. Both go up together. Click the plus sign.

Second connection: Click on 'Air Temperature' and drag to
'Convective Heat Loss.' When air temperature goes UP, does heat
loss go UP or DOWN?

If the air is warmer, there is a smaller temperature difference
between your skin and the air. Less difference means less heat
transfer. So warmer air means LESS convective heat loss. That is
a NEGATIVE relationship. Click the minus sign.

Third: Connect 'Convective Heat Loss' to 'Skin Temperature.'
When heat loss goes UP, skin temperature goes DOWN. Your body
is losing heat faster than it can replace it. Another NEGATIVE.

Finally: Connect 'Skin Temperature' to 'Perceived Cold.' When
skin temperature goes DOWN, perceived cold goes UP. Your cold
receptors are firing harder. Another NEGATIVE.

Look at your model now. You have got one positive relationship
and three negatives. And look at the chain: wind drives heat loss,
heat loss drops skin temperature, dropping skin temperature
increases the feeling of cold.

Here is the key insight: wind does not change the air temperature.
Not even a little. It changes how fast YOUR BODY loses heat TO
that air. The thermometer stays the same. You do not.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

```
"You have built the machine. You have wired the connections. Now let
us flip the switch and see if wind chill works in your model like it
works on your face.

First scenario: the calm cold day. Set Wind Speed to zero and Air
Temperature to low — about 30 degrees. Click Play and watch the
graph panel.

With no wind, convective heat loss is low. The thermal boundary
layer stays intact. Your body keeps a thin jacket of warm air
right against your skin. Skin temperature drops slowly, and
perceived cold is manageable.

Now comes the real test. Keep Air Temperature exactly the same,
but crank Wind Speed to 30 mph. This is a normal windy winter day.

Watch Convective Heat Loss spike. Wind is stripping that warm air
layer away faster than your body can rebuild it. And as heat loss
rises, watch Skin Temperature drop. It drops faster and lower
than the calm day, even though the air is the same temperature.

And Perceived Cold? It jumps. Same thermometer reading, but your
body feels dramatically colder.

Now here is something interesting. Lock Air Temperature at 40
degrees and slowly increase Wind Speed from 0 to 60 mph. Watch
Perceived Cold climb.

Notice the pattern. The jump from 0 to 10 mph is huge. The jump
from 10 to 20 mph is big. But the jump from 40 to 50 mph? Much
smaller. The effect levels off.

Why? Because at moderate wind speeds, the thermal boundary layer
is already mostly gone. More wind cannot strip away a layer that
is already destroyed. This is called diminishing returns.

Your model just explained a real weather phenomenon that most
adults do not understand. Wind does not make the air colder. It
makes YOU colder by stealing your body's invisible insulation.
```

### Teacher Guide

```
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
TEACHER GUIDE: Why Does It Feel Colder When It's Windy?
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

LESSON PURPOSE:
This lesson uses the universally relatable experience of wind chill
to teach thermal energy transfer through convection. Students build
a model connecting wind speed to heat loss rate, discovering that
wind does not change air temperature but changes how fast the body
loses heat. It serves as a hook for deeper inquiry into energy
transfer, weather phenomena, and material science (insulation).

NGSS ALIGNMENT:
MS-PS1-4: Develop a model that predicts and describes changes in
particle motion, temperature, and state of a pure substance when
thermal energy is added or removed.
MS-PS3-4: Plan an investigation to determine the relationships
among the energy transferred, the type of matter, the mass, and
the change in the average kinetic energy of the particles as
measured by the temperature of the sample.

PACING GUIDE:
• Step 1 (Locate): 8-10 minutes
• Step 2 (Establish): 8-10 minutes
• Step 3 (Visualize): 10-12 minutes
• Step 4 (Extend): 10-15 minutes
• Total: 40-45 minutes (or split across 2 days)

PRE-LESSON PREP:
□ Test ModelIt access on student devices
□ Have a fan and thermometer for demonstration (optional)
□ Review vocabulary: convection, thermal boundary layer, heat transfer
□ Pull up NWS wind chill chart for reference

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Wind makes the air colder" → Wind does not change air temperature.
   A thermometer reads the same with or without wind. Wind increases
   the rate of heat loss from warm objects.
• "Wind chill temperature is a real temperature" → Wind chill is an
   index describing heat loss rate, not actual temperature. A rock
   outside cannot cool below air temperature regardless of wind.
• "More wind always means proportionally more cold" → The relationship
   is nonlinear with diminishing returns. The first 10 mph of wind
   has a much greater effect than going from 40 to 50 mph.

FACILITATION TIPS:
• Step 1: Ask students to share personal wind chill experiences.
• Step 2: The negative relationship between Air Temperature and
   Convective Heat Loss can be tricky. Emphasize: warmer air means
   less temperature difference, so less heat flows.
• Step 3: The diminishing returns at high wind speeds is a key
   discovery. Let students notice it before explaining.
• Step 4: Physical demonstration with a fan and warm water bottle
   makes the concept tangible.

DISCUSSION PROMPTS:
• "Why does blowing on hot soup cool it down?"
• "If wind chill is -10°F, will your water bottle freeze?"
• "Why do mittens work better than gloves in wind?"
• "How is a winter coat like an artificial thermal boundary layer?"

EXTENSION CONNECTIONS:
• Thermal energy transfer → Conduction, convection, radiation comparison
• Material science → How insulation materials slow heat transfer
```

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format. Each item is three-dimensional, assessing a Science and Engineering Practice (SEP), Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and Crosscutting Concept (CCC) simultaneously, aligned to MS-PS1-4 and MS-PS3-4.

---

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.1 (Determine components of a system) + DCI PS3.B (Energy Transfer) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A student is building a model to explain why windy days feel colder. She identifies five things that matter: wind speed, air temperature, convective heat loss, skin temperature, and perceived cold. She needs to sort them into two groups: things that come from outside the system (external) and things that respond inside the system (internal).

Which list correctly sorts the components of the wind chill system?

A. External: Wind Speed, Convective Heat Loss — Internal: Air Temperature, Skin Temperature, Perceived Cold
B. External: Wind Speed, Air Temperature — Internal: Convective Heat Loss, Skin Temperature, Perceived Cold
C. External: Perceived Cold, Wind Speed — Internal: Air Temperature, Convective Heat Loss, Skin Temperature
D. External: Wind Speed, Skin Temperature — Internal: Air Temperature, Convective Heat Loss, Perceived Cold

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Wind Speed and Air Temperature are environmental conditions determined by weather, making them external inputs to the system. Convective Heat Loss, Skin Temperature, and Perceived Cold all respond to those external inputs through cause-and-effect relationships, making them internal. If you chose A, convective heat loss is not an external force; it is a process that happens because of wind and temperature. If you chose C, perceived cold is the final output of the system, not an external input. If you chose D, skin temperature changes in response to heat loss and is internal, not external.

---

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships among components) + DCI PS3.A (Definitions of Energy) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

In a wind chill model, a student draws an arrow from Wind Speed to Convective Heat Loss and labels it as a positive relationship. Another student says it should be negative because wind makes you colder, not warmer.

Which explanation best supports why the relationship between Wind Speed and Convective Heat Loss is positive?

A. When wind speed increases, the air temperature drops, causing more heat loss.
B. When wind speed increases, it strips the warm boundary layer from the skin faster, so the rate of heat loss increases.
C. When wind speed increases, the body produces more heat to stay warm, so heat loss increases.
D. When wind speed increases, skin temperature drops, which means heat loss must have increased first.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: A positive relationship means when one goes up, the other goes up too. Wind strips the thermal boundary layer (the thin warm air layer against skin) more rapidly at higher speeds, directly increasing the rate at which heat is carried away. If you chose A, wind does not change the air temperature. A thermometer reads the same with or without wind. If you chose C, while the body does increase heat production in cold, this is not the mechanism by which wind increases heat loss. The question asks about the direct relationship. If you chose D, this describes the result of increased heat loss, not the cause. The question asks why wind increases heat loss, not what happens after.

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships among components) + DCI PS3.B (Conservation of Energy and Energy Transfer) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A student builds a wind chill model with these relationships: Wind Speed positively increases Convective Heat Loss. Air Temperature negatively affects Convective Heat Loss (warmer air means less heat loss). Convective Heat Loss negatively affects Skin Temperature (more heat loss means lower skin temperature). The student wants to test what happens on a windy day at 30°F.

Based on the model, what would most likely happen when Wind Speed increases from 0 to 30 mph at 30°F?

A. Air Temperature would drop because the wind makes the air colder.
B. Convective Heat Loss would decrease because wind pushes warm air toward the body.
C. Skin Temperature would drop because Convective Heat Loss increases, carrying heat away faster.
D. Perceived Cold would decrease because the body adapts to the wind over time.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: The model shows a positive relationship between Wind Speed and Convective Heat Loss: more wind means more heat lost from the skin. Then the negative relationship between Convective Heat Loss and Skin Temperature means more heat loss causes skin temperature to drop. If you chose A, wind does not change air temperature. The model shows wind affects Convective Heat Loss, not Air Temperature. If you chose B, wind does not push warm air toward the body. It strips warm air away. If you chose D, the model does not include a body adaptation component. Perceived Cold increases as skin temperature drops.

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to explain/predict events) + DCI PS3.B (Energy Transfer) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

Scientists tested wind chill effects at a constant air temperature of 35°F. At 0 mph wind, skin temperature was 91°F and perceived cold was low. At 10 mph wind, skin temperature dropped to 85°F and perceived cold was moderate. At 20 mph wind, skin temperature dropped to 81°F and perceived cold was high. At 40 mph wind, skin temperature dropped to 79°F and perceived cold was very high.

Which observation from the data best supports the idea that wind chill has diminishing returns at higher wind speeds?

A. At 0 mph, skin temperature was 91°F and perceived cold was low.
B. From 0 to 10 mph, skin temperature dropped 6°F, but from 20 to 40 mph, it only dropped 2°F.
C. At 40 mph, perceived cold was very high.
D. As wind speed increased, skin temperature consistently decreased.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Diminishing returns means each additional increase in wind speed produces a smaller cooling effect. From 0 to 10 mph (a 10 mph increase), skin temperature dropped 6 degrees. But from 20 to 40 mph (a 20 mph increase, twice as large), skin temperature only dropped 2 degrees. The thermal boundary layer was already mostly disrupted at moderate wind speeds, so additional wind has less insulation left to remove. If you chose A, this only describes the no-wind baseline and does not show a pattern. If you chose C, this is true but does not show diminishing returns; it only describes one data point. If you chose D, this is true but describes a general trend, not diminishing returns specifically. Diminishing returns is about the rate of change slowing down.

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to predict a scientific event) + DCI PS3.A (Definitions of Energy) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A school is planning outdoor recess rules for winter. The school nurse wants to know when wind chill makes it dangerous for students. The wind chill model shows that higher wind speed increases convective heat loss, which decreases skin temperature, which increases perceived cold. The nurse also knows that clothing insulation can reduce convective heat loss.

Based on the model, which combination of conditions would most likely require canceling outdoor recess?

A. Air Temperature at 40°F, Wind Speed at 5 mph, students wearing winter coats.
B. Air Temperature at 25°F, Wind Speed at 30 mph, students wearing light jackets.
C. Air Temperature at 50°F, Wind Speed at 40 mph, students wearing winter coats.
D. Air Temperature at 35°F, Wind Speed at 0 mph, students wearing light jackets.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: The model shows that wind chill danger comes from the combination of cold air temperature, high wind speed, and inadequate clothing insulation. Choice B has the coldest air temperature (25°F), very high wind speed (30 mph), and minimal clothing insulation (light jackets). This combination would produce high convective heat loss, significant skin temperature drops, and dangerous perceived cold. If you chose A, moderate wind with proper insulation at 40°F would produce minimal wind chill danger. If you chose C, although wind is high, the air temperature of 50°F combined with good insulation makes this low risk. If you chose D, without wind, there is minimal convective heat loss regardless of clothing.
