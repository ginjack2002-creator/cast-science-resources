## Platform

### Title
**Inside the Cell: The Factory That Runs Your Body — How Mitochondria Power Every Move You Make**

### Overview
Students model how cells use nutrients and oxygen to produce ATP energy through cellular respiration. By building a model with dual inputs (food and oxygen), mitochondria activity, cell growth, and waste production, students discover why you need to eat AND breathe, and why oxygen deprivation kills faster than starvation.

### Cover Image
[A stunning photorealistic 3D rendering of the interior of a human cell showing glowing mitochondria producing energy, detailed organelles visible in cross-section, dramatic bioluminescent lighting on dark background]

### Grade
6th (Level 2)

### NGSS Standard
**MS-LS1-1**: Conduct an investigation to provide evidence that living things are made of cells.
**MS-LS1-2**: Develop and use a model to describe the function of a cell as a whole and ways the parts of cells contribute to the function.

### Learning Objectives
- Model how nutrients and oxygen fuel mitochondria to produce ATP energy for the cell
- Explain the relationship between mitochondria activity, cell growth rate, and waste production
- Describe how waste buildup from cellular respiration limits cell growth and function
- Predict what happens to a cell when nutrient or oxygen supply is cut off
- Connect cellular respiration to the whole-body need for food and breathing

### Component List (Starting Model: 5 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Nutrient Input | External | The amount of glucose and other food molecules available to the cell |
| Oxygen Availability | External | The concentration of oxygen reaching the cell for ATP production |
| Mitochondria Activity | Internal | The rate at which mitochondria convert glucose and oxygen into ATP |
| Cell Growth Rate | Internal | How fast the cell can divide, build proteins, and maintain structures |
| Waste Production | Internal | The amount of CO2 and metabolic waste produced as byproducts |

## Step 1: LOCATE — Build Your Cell System

### Text Editor

```
INSIDE THE CELL: THE FACTORY THAT RUNS YOUR BODY

Right now, inside every one of your 37 trillion cells, tiny power
plants called mitochondria are burning fuel and producing energy.
Without them, you would be dead in minutes. You eat food for the
fuel. You breathe for the oxygen. But what happens when they meet
INSIDE your cells? You are about to model it.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Task A: CHOOSE YOUR COMPONENTS
• EXTERNAL (must come from outside the cell):
  ○ "Nutrient Input" — glucose from food, delivered by blood
  ○ "Oxygen Availability" — from breathing, delivered by blood
• INTERNAL (processes inside the cell):
  ○ "Mitochondria Activity" — the power plants running
  ○ "Cell Growth Rate" — how fast the cell grows and divides
  ○ "Waste Production" — CO2 and metabolic waste byproducts

Task B: ADD TO YOUR MODEL
• Click the PLUS (+) button for each component
• You should see 5 components on your canvas

Task C: SORT YOUR COMPONENTS
• EXTERNAL = supplied from outside the cell (nutrients, oxygen)
• INTERNAL = processes and outputs within the cell

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Two inputs feed one engine. Both are required. Remove either one
and the engine stops. Next, we connect them.
```

### Video Script

```
"Right now, inside every single one of your 37 trillion cells,
tiny power plants called mitochondria are burning fuel and producing
energy. Without them, you would be dead in minutes.

You eat food. That is the fuel. Glucose. You breathe. That is the
oxygen. The other reactant. When they meet inside your mitochondria,
something amazing happens: they combine to produce ATP. The energy
currency of life. Plus some waste: carbon dioxide and water.

Let's build this system. Two external inputs. Nutrient Input and
Oxygen Availability. Both come from outside the cell, delivered
by your bloodstream. The cell cannot make glucose. It cannot make
oxygen. These must be supplied.

Three internal components. Mitochondria Activity, the engine speed.
Cell Growth Rate, what the energy is used for. And Waste Production,
the exhaust.

Sort them. Nutrients and oxygen are external. Everything else is
internal, responding to those two inputs.

This model has a key feature: TWO inputs feeding ONE engine. Both
are absolutely required. Remove either one and ATP production
crashes.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

## Step 2: ESTABLISH — Connect the Relationships

### Text Editor

```
TIME TO WIRE THE CELL FACTORY

Nutrients and oxygen flow into mitochondria. ATP energy flows
out to power growth. But waste also flows out. And too much waste
is toxic. Let's map these connections.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Task A: DRAW YOUR RELATIONSHIPS
• Nutrient Input → Mitochondria Activity
• Oxygen Availability → Mitochondria Activity
• Mitochondria Activity → Cell Growth Rate
• Mitochondria Activity → Waste Production
• Waste Production → Cell Growth Rate

Task B: SET POSITIVE OR NEGATIVE
  ○ Nutrient Input → Mitochondria Activity = POSITIVE (+)
    More glucose = MORE fuel for the power plants

  ○ Oxygen Availability → Mitochondria Activity = POSITIVE (+)
    More oxygen = MORE efficient ATP production

  ○ Mitochondria Activity → Cell Growth Rate = POSITIVE (+)
    More ATP = MORE energy for growth, division, repair

  ○ Mitochondria Activity → Waste Production = POSITIVE (+)
    More respiration = MORE CO2 and waste produced

  ○ Waste Production → Cell Growth Rate = NEGATIVE (−)
    More waste = TOXIC buildup that DAMAGES the cell and SLOWS growth

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Notice the tension: mitochondria produce BOTH useful energy AND
harmful waste. More activity means more of BOTH. The cell must
balance production and waste removal.
```

### Video Script

```
"Time to connect the cell factory. Two inputs, one engine, two
outputs. And a hidden tension.

Nutrient Input to Mitochondria Activity: more glucose means more
fuel. POSITIVE. Oxygen Availability to Mitochondria Activity: more
oxygen means the electron transport chain runs efficiently, producing
36 to 38 ATP per glucose instead of just 2 without oxygen. POSITIVE.

Mitochondria Activity to Cell Growth Rate: more ATP means more energy
for everything the cell does. Division, protein building, structure
maintenance. POSITIVE.

Mitochondria Activity to Waste Production: here is the cost. More
respiration means more CO2 and metabolic waste. POSITIVE. More
activity, more exhaust.

And the critical connection: Waste Production to Cell Growth Rate.
NEGATIVE. Too much waste is toxic. CO2 lowers cell pH. Acidic
conditions damage enzymes. If waste removal cannot keep up with
production, the cell poisons itself.

This is the tension in the system. Mitochondria produce both the
energy the cell needs and the waste that can harm it. The cell must
run its power plants hard enough to grow but not so hard that waste
overwhelms the removal systems.

Your breathing rate? That is your body's waste removal system for
CO2. You breathe faster during exercise because your muscle cells
are producing more waste.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

## Step 3: VISUALIZE & EVALUATE — Run Your Cell Model

### Text Editor

```
TIME TO SEE YOUR CELL FACTORY IN ACTION

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Task A: SCENARIO 1 — HEALTHY CELL
• Set Nutrient Input and Oxygen Availability to moderate-high
• Observe balanced cell function: good growth, manageable waste

Task B: SCENARIO 2 — OXYGEN DEPRIVATION
• Lock Oxygen Availability to 10%, keep nutrients normal
• Watch the shutdown cascade
• How fast does the cell crash?

Task C: SCENARIO 3 — NUTRIENT STARVATION
• Lock Nutrient Input to 10%, keep oxygen normal
• Compare to oxygen deprivation
• Is the decline faster or slower?

Task D: COMPARE THE CRASHES
• Which scenario crashes faster: no oxygen or no food?
• Why does this match real life (choking = minutes, fasting = weeks)?

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

REFLECTION QUESTIONS:
• Why do you breathe faster when you exercise?
• Why is choking a medical emergency but skipping lunch is not?
• How is a cell like a factory? What are the inputs and outputs?
```

### Video Script

```
"Let's test the cell factory. Set both inputs to moderate-high.
Healthy cell. Watch the graph. Mitochondria Activity is strong.
Cell Growth Rate is healthy. Waste Production is moderate but
manageable. The cell is humming along.

Now the dramatic test. Lock Oxygen Availability to 10%. This is
what happens when you choke or hold your breath too long.

Watch Mitochondria Activity crash. Without oxygen, the electron
transport chain shuts down. ATP production drops by 95 percent.
Cell Growth Rate crashes rapidly. The cell cannot do ANYTHING
without ATP. And it happens FAST.

Now reset and try the other scenario. Lock Nutrient Input to 10%.
Oxygen is fine but food is gone.

Watch. The decline is more gradual. Why? Because cells have some
stored energy. Glycogen reserves. They can even break down fats
and proteins for fuel. The crash happens but it is slower.

This is why choking is a medical emergency measured in MINUTES
but you can survive WEEKS without food. Without oxygen, ATP
drops by 95 percent instantly. Without food, the cell has backup
fuel sources that buy time.

Both inputs are essential. But oxygen deprivation is the more
immediate crisis because the efficiency drop is catastrophic.

Now connect this to your whole body. Why do you breathe faster
during exercise? Your muscle cells need more oxygen for more ATP
and need to expel more CO2 waste. Your breathing rate is literally
controlled by cellular demand for oxygen and waste removal.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

## Step 4: REVISE & EXTEND — Play, Research, Expand

### Text Editor

```
SET THE CONDITIONS — REFINE YOUR MODEL

Your model shows a relationship between Nutrient Input and Mitochondria Activity,
but right now that relationship applies ALL the time. In the
real system, Nutrient Input only affects Mitochondria Activity when Oxygen Availability is active.

This is called a CONDITIONAL RELATIONSHIP — the connection
between two components depends on a third component's state.

Task A: SET THE CONDITION
   • Click on the connection arrow from Nutrient Input to Mitochondria Activity
   • In the toolbar, click "Conditions"
   • In the conditions panel, set: IF Oxygen Availability is ON
   • Click "Save Conditions"

Task B: VERIFY THE MODEL
   • Run the simulation with Oxygen Availability OFF and Nutrient Input ON
   • Mitochondria Activity should NOT respond to Nutrient Input alone
   • Now turn Oxygen Availability ON — Nutrient Input's effect on Mitochondria Activity activates
   • This matches how the real system behaves

Task C: SET ADDITIONAL CONDITION
   • Click on the arrow from Oxygen Availability to Mitochondria Activity
   • Click "Conditions" and set: IF Nutrient Input is ON
   • Save — Oxygen Availability now only affects Mitochondria Activity when
     Nutrient Input is active

Task D: SET ADDITIONAL CONDITION
   • Click on the arrow from Mitochondria Activity to Cell Growth Rate
   • Click "Conditions" and set: IF Waste Production is ON
   • Save — Mitochondria Activity now only affects Cell Growth Rate when
     Waste Production is active

Your model now captures the conditional logic of the real system.
Not all relationships are always active — some depend on conditions.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

NOW LET'S PLAY AND EXPLORE

YOUR CELL MODEL WORKS — BUT REAL CELLS ARE MORE COMPLEX

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

PLAY TIME CHALLENGES:
1. What happens if BOTH inputs are cut simultaneously?
2. What if you max out both inputs? Is there a waste problem?
3. Can you find the optimal balance between growth and waste?

WHAT'S MISSING?
• ATP Concentration — the actual energy currency amount
• Temperature — enzymes work faster in warmth (to a point)
• Cell Membrane Permeability — how easily stuff crosses the membrane

RESEARCH DIRECTIONS:
• Why do mitochondria have their own DNA? (Endosymbiosis theory)
• Why do muscle cells have more mitochondria than skin cells?
• What happens during anaerobic respiration (without oxygen)?

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

ADD TO YOUR MODEL:
• Pick ONE new component
• Add it, connect it, test it
• Does it make your model more realistic?
```

two outputs, and the tension between useful energy and harmful waste.
But real cells have thousands of components.

Try breaking the system in new ways. Cut BOTH inputs. What happens?

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Question 1
A student sorts cell model components. Which correctly identifies the external components?
A. Nutrient Input only  B. Nutrient Input and Oxygen Availability  C. Mitochondria Activity and Nutrient Input  D. All five are internal
**Correct: B** — Both must be supplied from outside the cell.

### Question 2
Mitochondria Activity positively drives both Cell Growth Rate and Waste Production. What tension does this create?
A. More activity always helps the cell with no drawbacks  B. More activity produces both needed energy AND harmful waste that can damage the cell  C. Waste has no effect on the cell  D. Growth and waste are the same thing
**Correct: B**

### Question 3
A scientist locks Oxygen Availability to 10% while keeping nutrients normal. ATP production drops by approximately 95%. Why?
A. Glucose cannot enter the cell without oxygen  B. Oxygen is needed for the electron transport chain, which produces 34 of the 36 ATP per glucose  C. Mitochondria shut down permanently without oxygen  D. The cell explodes without oxygen
**Correct: B**

### Question 4
Oxygen deprivation crashes a cell in minutes. Nutrient starvation takes weeks. Why the difference?
A. Oxygen is more important than food  B. Without oxygen, ATP efficiency drops by 95% immediately, but without food, cells use stored glycogen and break down fats for backup fuel  C. Cells do not need food  D. Oxygen deprivation affects only brain cells
**Correct: B**

### Question 5
A person exercises vigorously. Their breathing rate and heart rate both increase. Based on the cell model, which explanation connects whole-body responses to cellular demand?
A. Exercise makes lungs grow bigger  B. Muscle cells need more O2 for more ATP and produce more CO2 waste, so breathing increases to deliver O2 and remove CO2, and heart rate increases to transport both faster  C. Exercise produces oxygen, so the body breathes faster to release it  D. Heart rate increases because muscles push the heart
**Correct: B**

### Answer Key
Q1: B | Q2: B | Q3: B | Q4: B | Q5: B
