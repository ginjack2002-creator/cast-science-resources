## Platform

### Title
**Why Does Your Coffee Get Cold? — The Hidden Math of Thermal Energy Transfer**

### Overview
Students model why hot objects cool rapidly at first but then slow down as they approach room temperature. By building a thermal energy model with temperature difference, transfer rate, and exponential decay, students discover Newton's Law of Cooling and why a hot drink can never get colder than the room it sits in.

[A photorealistic thermal imaging photograph of a steaming coffee mug showing heat gradients from deep red at the liquid to blue at the surrounding air, dramatic scientific visualization on dark background]

### Grade
6th (Level 2)

### NGSS Standard
**MS-PS3-3**: Apply scientific principles to design, construct, and test a device that either minimizes or maximizes thermal energy transfer.
**MS-PS3-4**: Plan an investigation to determine the relationships among the energy transferred, the type of matter, the mass, and the change in the average kinetic energy of the particles as measured by the temperature of the sample.

### Learning Objectives
- Explain how the temperature difference between an object and its surroundings drives thermal energy transfer
- Model how transfer rate changes as the temperature gap shrinks over time
- Describe how thermal equilibrium is reached through exponential decay of temperature difference
- Predict how changing room temperature affects the cooling curve of a hot object
- Connect the model to real-world engineering applications like insulation and climate control

### Component List (Starting Model: 5 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Hot Object Temperature | External | The starting temperature of the hot object, which decreases over time as heat transfers out |
| Room Temperature | External | The ambient temperature of the surrounding environment, which the object cannot cool below |
| Temperature Difference | Internal | The gap between the hot object and the room, which drives the rate of heat transfer |
| Transfer Rate | Internal | The speed of heat flow from the object to the surroundings, directly proportional to the temperature difference |
| Object Temperature Over Time | Internal | The changing temperature of the object as it cools toward room temperature following an exponential decay curve |

## Step 2: ESTABLISH — Connect the Relationships

### Text Editor

```
TIME TO DRAW THE INVISIBLE PHYSICS

Those five components are connected by the laws of thermodynamics.
The temperature gap DRIVES the cooling. As the gap shrinks, cooling
SLOWS. Let's map those connections.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Task A: OPEN CONNECTION MODE
• Click the "Connect" icon in the TOP LEFT corner of your screen
• Your cursor is now ready to draw relationship arrows

Task B: DRAW YOUR RELATIONSHIPS
• Click on "Hot Object Temperature" and drag an arrow to "Temperature Difference"
• Click on "Room Temperature" and drag an arrow to "Temperature Difference"
• Click on "Temperature Difference" and drag an arrow to "Transfer Rate"
• Click on "Transfer Rate" and drag an arrow to "Object Temperature Over Time"

Task C: SET POSITIVE OR NEGATIVE
• Look at the +/- toggle in the TOP LEFT corner
• For each connection, ask: "When this goes UP, does the other go UP or DOWN?"

  ○ Hot Object Temperature → Temperature Difference = POSITIVE (+)
    Hotter starting object = BIGGER temperature gap

  ○ Room Temperature → Temperature Difference = NEGATIVE (−)
    Warmer room = SMALLER temperature gap

  ○ Temperature Difference → Transfer Rate = POSITIVE (+)
    Bigger gap = FASTER heat flow (Newton's Law of Cooling!)

  ○ Transfer Rate → Object Temperature Over Time = NEGATIVE (−)
    Faster transfer = object cools MORE quickly

Task D: CHECK YOUR MODEL
• You should have 4 arrows total
• 2 positive relationships, 2 negative relationships
• This is your thermal system with a self-slowing feedback!

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Notice something powerful? As the object cools, the temperature
difference SHRINKS, which SLOWS the transfer rate. The system
slows itself down. That is why the cooling curve is not a straight
line.
```

### Video Script

```
"Your pieces are on the board, but they are not talking to each other
yet. Time to draw the physics that makes coffee cool.

Click the 'Connect' icon in the top left corner. Now you are in
connection mode.

First connection: 'Hot Object Temperature' to 'Temperature Difference.'
A hotter object creates a bigger gap with the room. That is POSITIVE.

Second: 'Room Temperature' to 'Temperature Difference.' A warmer room
means a SMALLER gap. That is NEGATIVE. When room temp goes up, the
difference goes down.

Third: 'Temperature Difference' to 'Transfer Rate.' This is Newton's
Law of Cooling. The bigger the gap, the faster heat flows. POSITIVE.
This is the key insight of the whole lesson.

Fourth: 'Transfer Rate' to 'Object Temperature Over Time.' Faster
transfer means the object cools more quickly. NEGATIVE. A high
transfer rate pushes the temperature down.

Now look at the whole picture. Here is the genius of this system:
as the object cools, the temperature difference SHRINKS. A smaller
difference means a slower transfer rate. A slower transfer rate
means the object cools more slowly. The system SLOWS ITSELF DOWN.

This is called a self-limiting feedback loop. The output (cooling)
reduces the input (temperature difference), which reduces the
output. That is why the cooling curve is NOT a straight line. It
curves. It bends. It flattens out as it approaches room temperature.

And it can NEVER go below room temperature because at that point
the difference is zero and the transfer rate is zero. No driving
force, no cooling.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

"You have built the thermal machine. Now let's see if it creates the
cooling curve that Newton discovered.

Set Hot Object Temperature to 90 degrees C and Room Temperature to
20 degrees C. Click 'Play.' Watch the cooling curve.

It is NOT a straight line! It drops fast at first, then curves, then
flattens out. That is exponential decay. The temperature difference
starts at 70 degrees, driving fast transfer. As the object cools to
50 degrees, the difference is only 30 degrees, and transfer slows.
By 25 degrees, the difference is only 5, and cooling is barely
happening.

Now let's compare. Same hot coffee, but set Room Temperature to 35
degrees. What changes? The curve is shallower! It cools more slowly
the whole time because the maximum temperature difference is only
55 degrees instead of 70. And it stops at a higher final temperature:
35 degrees instead of 20.

One more: set the object to only 40 degrees in a 20-degree room.
Only a 20-degree difference. The cooling is much slower from the
start. Less driving force, less transfer.

Look at all three curves. The pattern is the same every time:
fast at first, then slower, then flattening. Exponential decay.
The only things that change are the steepness and the endpoint.

The endpoint? Always the room temperature. Because when the
difference hits zero, transfer stops. The object literally CANNOT
get colder than the room without a refrigerator pumping heat out.

This pattern is everywhere. Cooling coffee. Draining batteries.
Radioactive decay. Even how your phone battery drops. Same math.
Different systems.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"

Your model shows a relationship between Hot Object Temperature and Temperature Difference,

real system, Hot Object Temperature only affects Temperature Difference when Room Temperature is inactive.

This is called a CONDITIONAL RELATIONSHIP — the connection
between two components depends on a third component's state.

   • Click on the connection arrow from Hot Object Temperature to Temperature Difference
   • In the toolbar, click "Conditions"

   • Click "Save Conditions"

LESSON PURPOSE:
This lesson models Newton's Law of Cooling through computational
simulation, demonstrating how temperature difference drives heat
transfer and produces exponential decay curves. Students discover
the self-limiting feedback mechanism that explains why cooling
slows as objects approach room temperature.

NGSS ALIGNMENT:
MS-PS3-3: Apply scientific principles to design, construct, and
test a device that minimizes or maximizes thermal energy transfer.
MS-PS3-4: Plan an investigation to determine relationships among
energy transferred, matter type, mass, and temperature change.

THREE DIMENSIONS:
• Science Practice: Developing and Using Models — Students develop
  a computational model demonstrating how temperature difference
  drives transfer rate and produces exponential decay cooling curves.
• Disciplinary Core Idea: PS3.A Definitions of Energy / PS3.B
  Conservation of Energy — Temperature measures average kinetic
  energy of particles. Thermal energy transfers from higher to
  lower temperature until equilibrium.
• Crosscutting Concept: Cause and Effect — Temperature difference
  CAUSES heat transfer. As cooling reduces the cause, the rate
  slows. Self-limiting system.

CAST ALIGNMENT:
• Explain how temperature difference drives the rate of thermal
  energy transfer
• Describe why cooling curves are exponential rather than linear
• Predict how changing room temperature affects equilibrium

LEVER FRAMEWORK GUIDE:
L (Locate): Students identify hot object temperature and room
  temperature as external inputs, and temperature difference,
  transfer rate, and object temperature over time as internal.
E (Establish): Students determine that temperature difference
  positively drives transfer rate, transfer rate negatively affects
  object temperature, and as the object cools, the difference
  shrinks, creating a self-slowing system.
V (Visualize): Students build a model showing how the interplay
  between temperature difference and transfer rate produces the
  exponential decay cooling curve.
Ev (Evaluate): Students run hot-in-cold-room vs. hot-in-warm-room
  scenarios and compare cooling curves.
R (Revise): Students add insulation level or object mass to explore
  how additional variables modify the cooling curve shape.

COMPONENT SORTING REASONING:
Hot Object Temperature and Room Temperature are external because
they are the initial conditions the student sets. Temperature
Difference, Transfer Rate, and Object Temperature Over Time are
internal because they are calculated by the system based on the
two external inputs and the relationships between components.

RELATIONSHIP GUIDE:
• Hot Object Temperature → Temperature Difference = POSITIVE (+)
  A hotter starting object creates a larger gap.
• Room Temperature → Temperature Difference = NEGATIVE (−)
  A warmer room means a smaller gap.
• Temperature Difference → Transfer Rate = POSITIVE (+)
  Bigger gap means faster heat flow. Newton's Law of Cooling.
• Transfer Rate → Object Temperature Over Time = NEGATIVE (−)
  Faster transfer causes the object to cool more quickly. As the
  object cools, difference shrinks, slowing the transfer.

SIMULATION ANSWER KEY:
Hot Coffee in Cold Room: The 90-degree C coffee in a 20-degree C
room cools rapidly in the first 5-10 minutes because the 70-degree
temperature difference drives fast transfer. As the coffee
approaches room temperature, the curve flattens dramatically.

Hot Coffee in Warm Room: Same coffee in a 35-degree C room cools
more slowly throughout because the maximum difference is only
55 degrees. It reaches equilibrium at 35 degrees C. Same curve
shape but shallower and shifted upward.

REFLECTION ANSWER KEY:
Q: Why does the cooling curve flatten?
A: The CAUSE of cooling (temperature difference) shrinks as the
EFFECT of cooling (lower temperature) happens. When the coffee is
90 degrees C in a 20-degree C room, the 70-degree gap drives fast
transfer. By 25 degrees C, the gap is only 5, driving very slow
transfer. The system is self-limiting.

Q: Can an object cool below room temperature?
A: No, because at room temperature, the temperature difference is
zero. With zero difference, the transfer rate is zero. To cool
below room temperature requires active heat removal (refrigerator),
which requires energy input. Second Law of Thermodynamics.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
1. "Objects cool at a constant rate"
   CORRECTION: Cooling is NOT linear. Objects cool fast at first
   and slow down as they approach room temperature. The curve is
   exponential.
   STRATEGY: Measure a hot cup every 2 minutes and plot the data.
   Students see the curve flatten in real time.

2. "Cold flows into hot objects"
   CORRECTION: There is no such thing as cold flowing. Only thermal
   energy (heat) moves, always from hot to cold. When your hands
   feel cold touching metal, YOUR heat is flowing into the metal.
   STRATEGY: Put a thermometer in a coat by itself. The temperature
   does not rise. The coat just slows YOUR heat from leaving.

3. "Room temperature is a property of the object"
   CORRECTION: Room temperature is the environment, not the object.
   Different rooms have different temperatures. The same coffee
   would reach different final temperatures in different rooms.
   STRATEGY: Move the same hot cup between rooms. The equilibrium
   temperature changes because the environment changed.

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format, aligned to MS-PS3-3 and MS-PS3-4.

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.1 (Determine components of a system) + DCI PS3.A (Definitions of Energy) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A student is building a model of how a hot cup of water cools in a room. She lists five components: Hot Object Temperature, Room Temperature, Temperature Difference, Transfer Rate, and Object Temperature Over Time. She needs to sort them into external (set by the student) and internal (calculated by the system).

Which list correctly sorts the components?

A. External: Hot Object Temperature, Transfer Rate — Internal: Room Temperature, Temperature Difference, Object Temperature Over Time
B. External: Hot Object Temperature, Room Temperature, Temperature Difference — Internal: Transfer Rate, Object Temperature Over Time
C. External: Hot Object Temperature, Room Temperature — Internal: Temperature Difference, Transfer Rate, Object Temperature Over Time
D. External: Hot Object Temperature — Internal: Room Temperature, Temperature Difference, Transfer Rate, Object Temperature Over Time

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: Hot Object Temperature and Room Temperature are external because the student sets these as initial conditions. Temperature Difference, Transfer Rate, and Object Temperature Over Time are internal because the system calculates them from the external inputs. If you chose B, temperature difference is calculated from the two temperatures, not set by the student. If you chose D, room temperature is set by the student as an environmental condition. If you chose A, transfer rate is calculated based on temperature difference, not set by the student.
---

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships) + DCI PS3.B (Conservation of Energy) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

In a thermal energy model, a student sets the Hot Object Temperature to 90 degrees C and the Room Temperature to 20 degrees C. The Temperature Difference is 70 degrees. The model shows that a larger Temperature Difference causes a faster Transfer Rate.

What type of relationship exists between Temperature Difference and Transfer Rate?

A. Inverse — transfer rate is always the opposite of temperature difference
B. No relationship — temperature difference does not affect transfer rate
C. Positive — when temperature difference increases, transfer rate also increases
D. Negative — when temperature difference increases, transfer rate decreases

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: Newton's Law of Cooling states that the rate of heat transfer is directly proportional to the temperature difference. A bigger gap between the object and the room drives faster heat flow. This is a positive relationship: both increase together. If you chose D, that would mean a larger gap slows cooling, which contradicts the physics. If you chose B, temperature difference is the primary driver of transfer rate. If you chose A, inverse and negative are similar concepts here, but the relationship is positive, not inverse.
---

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships) + DCI PS3.A (Energy) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A student runs two simulations. In Simulation A, a 90 degree C cup of coffee is placed in a 20 degree C room. In Simulation B, the same 90 degree C coffee is placed in a 35 degree C room. Both simulations run for the same amount of time.

Based on Newton's Law of Cooling, which prediction is correct?

A. Cup B will cool faster because 35 degrees is warmer, which speeds up molecular movement.
B. Cup A will cool faster because the temperature difference (70 degrees) is larger than Cup B's (55 degrees).
C. Both cups will cool at the same rate because they start at the same temperature.
D. Neither cup will cool because hot objects cannot lose heat to their surroundings.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Newton's Law of Cooling states that cooling rate is proportional to temperature difference. Cup A has a 70-degree difference (90-20) while Cup B has only a 55-degree difference (90-35). The larger gap drives faster heat flow, so Cup A cools faster. If you chose C, starting temperature alone does not determine cooling rate; the gap matters. If you chose A, a warmer room actually slows cooling because it reduces the driving force. If you chose D, hot objects always transfer heat to cooler surroundings.
---

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to explain events) + DCI PS3.B (Conservation of Energy) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A student measures the temperature of a cup of hot water every 5 minutes. The data shows: 0 min = 90 degrees C, 5 min = 70 degrees C, 10 min = 55 degrees C, 15 min = 45 degrees C, 20 min = 38 degrees C, 25 min = 33 degrees C, 30 min = 28 degrees C. The room is 22 degrees C.

Which pattern best describes the cooling data?

A. Constant — the temperature stays the same throughout the experiment.
B. Linear — the temperature drops by the same amount every 5 minutes.
C. Random — there is no pattern to the temperature changes.
D. Exponential decay — the temperature drops quickly at first, then slows as it approaches room temperature.

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: The data shows drops of 20, 15, 10, 7, 5, and 5 degrees in successive 5-minute intervals. The cooling slows as the object approaches room temperature (22 degrees C). This is exponential decay, driven by the shrinking temperature difference. If you chose B, a linear pattern would show equal drops every interval, but the drops clearly decrease over time. If you chose C, the pattern is very clear and predictable. If you chose A, the data clearly shows temperature changing.

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to predict events) + DCI PS3.B (Conservation of Energy) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A hospital needs to transport medication at exactly 37 degrees C. The delivery van has no refrigeration. The outside temperature is 15 degrees C. A nurse wraps the medication container in thick insulation.

Based on Newton's Law of Cooling, why does insulation help maintain the medication temperature?

A. Insulation slows the transfer rate by reducing how fast heat flows from the medication to the cooler air.
B. Insulation makes the outside temperature warmer so the medication does not need to cool.
C. Insulation eliminates the temperature difference between the medication and the air.
D. Insulation heats the medication to keep it at 37 degrees C.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: Insulation does not create heat or change the temperature difference. It slows the RATE at which heat transfers from the warmer medication to the cooler air. By reducing the transfer rate, the medication maintains its temperature for a longer time before reaching equilibrium with the environment. If you chose D, insulation does not generate heat; it only slows heat loss. If you chose C, the temperature difference still exists; insulation does not change temperatures. If you chose B, insulation does not affect outside air temperature.
