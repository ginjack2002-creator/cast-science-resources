## Platform

### Title
**Who Eats Who? Energy Flow Through Food Webs — Tracing Energy from Sunlight to Top Predators**

### Overview
Students model why ecosystems have far more plants than herbivores and more herbivores than predators. By building a food web energy model with trophic levels, the 10% energy transfer rule, and decomposer recycling, students discover why there are so few top predators on Earth and what happens when species are removed from the web.

### Grade
6th (Level 2)

### NGSS Standard
**MS-LS2-3**: Develop a model to describe the cycling of matter and flow of energy among living and nonliving parts of an ecosystem.
**MS-LS2-4**: Construct an argument supported by empirical evidence that changes to physical or biological components of an ecosystem affect populations.

### Learning Objectives
- Trace the flow of energy from the Sun through producers, primary consumers, and secondary consumers
- Model how energy is lost at each trophic level through metabolism and heat
- Explain the 10% energy transfer rule and its consequences for food web structure
- Predict how removing or adding organisms at one trophic level cascades through the entire web
- Analyze how decomposers recycle matter back to producers

### Component List (Starting Model: 6 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Solar Energy Input | External | The amount of sunlight reaching the ecosystem, which producers capture through photosynthesis |
| Producer Biomass | Internal | The total mass of plants and algae in the ecosystem that convert solar energy to chemical energy |
| Primary Consumer Biomass | Internal | The total mass of herbivores that eat producers and transfer energy to the next level |
| Secondary Consumer Biomass | Internal | The total mass of predators that eat herbivores, representing the third trophic level |
| Available Energy at Each Level | Internal | The energy remaining after metabolic losses at each trophic level, following the 10% rule |
| Decomposer Return Rate | Internal | The speed at which decomposers break down dead organisms and recycle nutrients back to producers |

## Step 2: ESTABLISH — Connect the Relationships

### Text Editor

```
TIME TO DRAW THE ENERGY FLOW

Those six components are connected by invisible energy pathways.
Energy flows from the Sun through each trophic level, and at every
step, 90% is LOST as heat. Let's map those connections.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Task A: OPEN CONNECTION MODE
• Click the "Connect" icon in the TOP LEFT corner of your screen
• Your cursor is now ready to draw relationship arrows

Task B: DRAW YOUR RELATIONSHIPS
• Click on "Solar Energy Input" and drag an arrow to "Producer Biomass"
• Click on "Producer Biomass" and drag an arrow to "Primary Consumer Biomass"
• Click on "Primary Consumer Biomass" and drag an arrow to "Secondary Consumer Biomass"
• Click on "Each Trophic Level" and drag an arrow to "Available Energy"
• Click on "Decomposer Return Rate" and drag an arrow to "Producer Biomass"

Task C: SET POSITIVE OR NEGATIVE
• Look at the +/- toggle in the TOP LEFT corner
• For each connection, ask: "When this goes UP, does the other go UP or DOWN?"

  ○ Solar Energy Input → Producer Biomass = POSITIVE (+)
    More sunlight = MORE photosynthesis = MORE plant growth

  ○ Producer Biomass → Primary Consumer Biomass = POSITIVE (+)
    More plants = MORE food for herbivores (but only 10% transfers!)

  ○ Primary Consumer Biomass → Secondary Consumer Biomass = POSITIVE (+)
    More herbivores = MORE food for predators (again, only 10%)

  ○ Each Trophic Level → Available Energy = NEGATIVE (−)
    At each level, 90% of energy is LOST as heat from metabolism

  ○ Decomposer Return Rate → Producer Biomass = POSITIVE (+)
    Faster decomposition = MORE recycled nutrients for plants

Task D: CHECK YOUR MODEL
• You should have 5 arrows total
• 4 positive relationships, 1 negative relationship
• This is your food web energy system!

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Notice the pattern? Energy flows UP through the levels, but 90%
is lost at every step. That is why there are so few predators.
```

### Video Script

```
"Your pieces are on the board, but they are not connected yet.
Time to draw the energy pathways that explain why ecosystems look
the way they do.

Click the 'Connect' icon in the top left corner. Now you are in
connection mode.

First connection: Click on 'Solar Energy Input' and drag an arrow
to 'Producer Biomass.' When more sunlight hits the ecosystem, what
happens to plant growth? It increases! That is a POSITIVE relationship.

Next: 'Producer Biomass' to 'Primary Consumer Biomass.' More plants
mean more food for herbivores. Positive. But here is the BIG surprise:
only 10% of the energy in plants makes it to herbivores. Ninety percent
is lost as heat from the plants' own metabolism.

Same thing: 'Primary Consumer Biomass' to 'Secondary Consumer Biomass.'
More herbivores support more predators. Positive. But again, only 10%
of herbivore energy reaches predators.

Now the critical one: each trophic level to Available Energy. This is
NEGATIVE. At every level, organisms burn 90% of their consumed energy
just to stay alive. Breathing, moving, maintaining body temperature.
All of that converts usable energy into heat that radiates away.

Last connection: 'Decomposer Return Rate' to 'Producer Biomass.'
Decomposers break down dead organisms and return nutrients to the soil.
More decomposition means more nutrients available for plants. Positive.

Look at your model. Energy flows from the Sun through producers to
consumers, losing 90% at every step. If producers capture 10,000 units
of energy, herbivores get only 1,000, and predators get just 100.

That is why there are more plants than herbivores and more herbivores
than predators. That is why there are so few lions. Energy math.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

### Video Script

```
"You have built the food web energy machine. Now let's flip the switch
and see if ecosystems work the way we predict.

Click 'Play' and set Solar Energy Input to moderate. Watch the graph.
See how each trophic level has LESS energy than the one below it?
Producers have the most, then primary consumers, then secondary
consumers have the least. That is the energy pyramid in action.

Now let's do something dramatic. Lock Solar Energy Input to 10%.
Imagine a volcanic winter or heavy cloud cover blocking the sun.

Resume the simulation. Watch Producer Biomass. It is dropping because
plants cannot photosynthesize without sunlight. And now watch Primary
Consumer Biomass. It is dropping too, but AFTER the producers crashed.
No plants, no food for herbivores.

And finally, Secondary Consumer Biomass collapses. No herbivores,
no food for predators. The whole web crashed from the BOTTOM UP.

Now reset and try something different. Set Solar Energy back to
moderate. But this time, REMOVE secondary consumers entirely.
Take away all the predators.

Watch what happens. Primary Consumer Biomass SURGES. Without
predators eating them, herbivores explode in population. But wait...
now Producer Biomass is CRASHING. Too many herbivores eating too
many plants. The web is destabilizing from the TOP DOWN.

This is exactly what happened in Yellowstone when wolves were removed.
Elk populations exploded, overgrazed the willows, and even changed
how rivers flowed.

Two cascade directions. Bottom-up when energy is cut. Top-down when
predators are removed. Both devastating.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

## Step 4: REVISE & EXTEND — Play, Research, Expand

### Text Editor

```
SET THE CONDITIONS — REFINE YOUR MODEL

Your model shows a relationship between Solar Energy Input and Producer Biomass,
but right now that relationship applies ALL the time. In the
real system, Solar Energy Input only affects Producer Biomass when Decomposer Return Rate is active.

This is called a CONDITIONAL RELATIONSHIP — the connection
between two components depends on a third component's state.

Task A: SET THE CONDITION
   • Click on the connection arrow from Solar Energy Input to Producer Biomass
   • In the toolbar, click "Conditions"
   • In the conditions panel, set: IF Decomposer Return Rate is ON
   • Click "Save Conditions"

Task B: VERIFY THE MODEL
   • Run the simulation with Decomposer Return Rate OFF and Solar Energy Input ON
   • Producer Biomass should NOT respond to Solar Energy Input alone
   • Now turn Decomposer Return Rate ON — Solar Energy Input's effect on Producer Biomass activates
   • This matches how the real system behaves

Your model now captures the conditional logic of the real system.
Not all relationships are always active — some depend on conditions.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

NOW LET'S PLAY AND EXPLORE

YOUR MODEL WORKS — BUT REAL ECOSYSTEMS ARE MORE COMPLEX

You built a food web that explains the energy pyramid and trophic
cascades. But real ecosystems have way more moving parts.

Time to play, explore, and make your model BETTER.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

PLAY TIME CHALLENGES:

2. BREAK THE SYSTEM

   • What if solar energy doubles? Do all levels double?

3. WHAT'S MISSING?
   Your model is simplified. Real food webs involve more. Think about:

   • Tertiary Consumer Biomass — eagles, sharks, apex predators
   • Nutrient Concentration — minerals in the soil for plant uptake
   • Competition Intensity — organisms at the same level fighting for resources
   • Disease — pathogens that can wipe out a trophic level
   • Human Activity — hunting, habitat destruction, pollution

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Don't just guess — find out! Here's what to look for:

IN YOUR TEXTBOOK:

   • What is the difference between energy flow and nutrient cycling?
   • How do humans affect food webs?

QUESTIONS TO INVESTIGATE:
   • Why did removing wolves from Yellowstone change the rivers?
   • How does mercury bioaccumulate through aquatic food webs?
   • What happened to ocean food webs when whales were overhunted?

RELIABLE SOURCES:

ADD TO YOUR MODEL:
```

```
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
```

```
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

LESSON PURPOSE:
This lesson models energy flow through food web trophic levels,
demonstrating the 10% energy transfer rule, trophic cascades,
and the essential role of decomposers. Students discover why
ecosystem structure follows predictable energy patterns.

NGSS ALIGNMENT:
MS-LS2-3: Develop a model to describe the cycling of matter and
flow of energy among living and nonliving parts of an ecosystem.
MS-LS2-4: Construct an argument supported by empirical evidence
that changes to physical or biological components of an ecosystem
affect populations.

THREE DIMENSIONS:
• Science Practice: Developing and Using Models — Students develop
  a computational model showing how solar energy flows through
  trophic levels with 90% loss at each transfer.
• Disciplinary Core Idea: LS2.B Cycles of Matter and Energy
  Transfer in Ecosystems — Food webs demonstrate how matter and
  energy are transferred between producers, consumers, and
  decomposers.
• Crosscutting Concept: Energy and Matter: Flows, Cycles, and
  Conservation — Students trace energy flow (one-way, Sun through
  organisms as heat) and matter cycling (nutrients recycled by
  decomposers).

CAST ALIGNMENT:
• Trace energy flow from sunlight through multiple trophic levels
  in a food web
• Explain why energy decreases at each trophic level using the
  10% rule
• Predict the effects of removing a species from a food web on
  other populations

  decomposer, trophic cascade

LEVER FRAMEWORK GUIDE:
L (Locate): Students identify solar energy input, producer biomass,
  primary consumer biomass, secondary consumer biomass, available
  energy, and decomposer return rate as the key components.
E (Establish): Students determine that solar energy positively
  drives producer biomass, each trophic level positively feeds
  the next but with 90% energy loss, and decomposers positively
  recycle nutrients back to producers.
V (Visualize): Students build a model showing energy flow from
  sunlight through three trophic levels with metabolic losses and
  nutrient recycling by decomposers.
Ev (Evaluate): Students run solar shutdown and predator removal
  scenarios to observe bottom-up and top-down cascade effects.
R (Revise): Students add tertiary consumers or competition
  intensity to explore more complex food web dynamics.

COMPONENT SORTING REASONING:
Solar Energy Input is external because it comes from outside the
ecosystem entirely. Producer Biomass, Primary Consumer Biomass,
Secondary Consumer Biomass, Available Energy at Each Level, and
Decomposer Return Rate are all internal because they are properties
of the ecosystem that respond to solar input and to each other
through feeding relationships.

RELATIONSHIP GUIDE:
• Solar Energy Input → Producer Biomass = POSITIVE (+)
  More sunlight allows producers to photosynthesize more, building
  more biomass. Without sunlight, producers cannot make food.
• Producer Biomass → Primary Consumer Biomass = POSITIVE (+)
  More plant biomass supports more herbivores. But only about 10%
  of the energy in producers transfers to primary consumers.
• Primary Consumer Biomass → Secondary Consumer Biomass = POSITIVE (+)
  More herbivore biomass supports more predators. Again, only 10%
  of herbivore energy reaches predators.
• Each Trophic Level → Available Energy = NEGATIVE (−)
  At each level, organisms use 90% of consumed energy for
  metabolism. Only 10% is stored as biomass.
• Decomposer Return Rate → Producer Biomass = POSITIVE (+)
  Faster decomposition releases nutrients back into the soil
  sooner, allowing producers to grow more.

SIMULATION ANSWER KEY:
Solar Shutdown Scenario: When Solar Energy Input is locked at 10%,
Producer Biomass drops sharply because plants cannot photosynthesize
enough. Primary Consumer Biomass then crashes because there is not
enough food. Secondary Consumer Biomass collapses last. The model
shows a bottom-up cascade: energy starvation propagates upward.

Predator Removal Scenario: When secondary consumers are removed,
Primary Consumer Biomass surges because nothing is eating the
herbivores. This exploding herbivore population then overgrazes
producers, causing Producer Biomass to crash. The model shows a
top-down cascade: removing predators destabilizes the entire web.

REFLECTION ANSWER KEY:
Q: Why can't a food web have infinite trophic levels?
A: Each trophic level only retains 10% of the energy from the level
below. After 4-5 levels, there simply is not enough energy left to
support another population. If producers capture 10,000 kJ, the
fourth level gets only 10 kJ. Energy loss sets a hard limit.

Q: What happens if decomposers disappear?
A: Without decomposers, dead organisms would pile up and nutrients
would be locked in dead matter forever. Producers would run out of
soil nutrients. Without producers, the entire food web collapses.
Decomposers are the recyclers that keep the nutrient cycle running.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
1. "Energy recycles through ecosystems like nutrients do"
   CORRECTION: Energy does NOT cycle. It flows one way: in from
   the Sun, through organisms, and out as heat. Nutrients cycle,
   but energy flows.
   STRATEGY: Draw two diagrams side by side: nutrient cycle (circle)
   vs. energy flow (straight arrow from Sun to heat).

2. "Removing predators helps prey populations"
   CORRECTION: In the short term, prey increases. But without
   predators, herbivores overpopulate and overgraze, destroying
   the producer base. Then herbivores starve. Predators stabilize
   prey populations.
   STRATEGY: Show Yellowstone data on elk populations after wolf
   removal.

3. "Decomposers are not important to the food web"
   CORRECTION: Decomposers are arguably the most critical organisms.
   Without them, nutrients would be permanently locked in dead
   matter and producers would starve.
   STRATEGY: Seal a terrarium with and without decomposers. The
   one without will fail.
```

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format. Each item is three-dimensional, assessing a Science and Engineering Practice (SEP), Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and Crosscutting Concept (CCC) simultaneously, aligned to MS-LS2-3 and MS-LS2-4.

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.1 (Determine components of a system) + DCI LS2.B (Cycles of Matter and Energy Transfer) + CCC5 (Energy and Matter)

An ecologist is studying a grassland ecosystem. She lists the key factors: solar energy input, producer biomass, primary consumer biomass, secondary consumer biomass, available energy at each level, and decomposer return rate. She needs to sort them into external (from outside the ecosystem) and internal (changing within the ecosystem).

Which list correctly sorts the components?

A. External: Solar Energy Input, Producer Biomass — Internal: Primary Consumer Biomass, Secondary Consumer Biomass, Available Energy, Decomposer Return Rate
B. External: Solar Energy Input, Decomposer Return Rate — Internal: Producer Biomass, Primary Consumer Biomass, Secondary Consumer Biomass, Available Energy
C. External: Solar Energy Input — Internal: Producer Biomass, Primary Consumer Biomass, Secondary Consumer Biomass, Available Energy, Decomposer Return Rate
D. External: Solar Energy Input, Available Energy — Internal: Producer Biomass, Primary Consumer Biomass, Secondary Consumer Biomass, Decomposer Return Rate

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: Solar Energy Input is the only external component because it comes from the Sun, completely outside the ecosystem. All other components are internal because they are properties of the ecosystem that respond to solar input and to each other. If you chose A, producer biomass is an internal property of the ecosystem, not an external input. If you chose B, decomposer return rate is an internal process within the ecosystem. If you chose D, available energy is an internal property calculated from trophic level interactions.
---

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships among components) + DCI LS2.B (Energy Transfer) + CCC5 (Energy and Matter)

In a food web model, a student draws a positive arrow from Producer Biomass to Primary Consumer Biomass. The student says this means that when producer biomass increases, primary consumer biomass also increases. Another student asks why only 10% of the energy transfers to the next level.

Which explanation best describes why only 10% of energy transfers between trophic levels?

A. Organisms at each level use 90% of their consumed energy for metabolism (breathing, moving, body heat), losing it as heat.
B. Consumers only eat 10% of the producers because they are not hungry enough to eat more.
C. The Sun only provides 10% of the energy that producers need, so they can only pass on 10%.
D. Producers are 10 times bigger than consumers, so consumers can only eat 10% of them.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: At every trophic level, organisms use approximately 90% of their energy intake for metabolic processes like breathing, moving, growing, and maintaining body temperature. This energy is converted to heat and radiates away. Only the remaining 10% is stored as biomass available to the next trophic level. If you chose D, the size difference between producers and consumers is not the reason for the 10% rule. If you chose B, appetite has nothing to do with energy transfer efficiency. If you chose C, the Sun provides abundant energy; the loss happens at each transfer, not at the input.

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships among components) + DCI LS2.C (Ecosystem Dynamics) + CCC5 (Energy and Matter)

A student builds a food web model showing solar energy flowing through producers, primary consumers, and secondary consumers. The model shows that when Solar Energy Input is locked at 10% (simulating heavy cloud cover), the entire food web collapses from the bottom up.

Based on the model, which sequence correctly describes the order of the cascade?

A. Producers crash first, then secondary consumers, then primary consumers.
B. Producers crash first, then primary consumers, then secondary consumers.
C. All three levels crash at exactly the same time because they are all connected.
D. Secondary consumers crash first, then primary consumers, then producers.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: In a bottom-up cascade, the base of the food web is affected first. Without sunlight, producers cannot photosynthesize, so their biomass drops first. Then primary consumers lose their food source and decline. Finally, secondary consumers at the top lose their prey and collapse last. Energy starvation propagates upward through the web. If you chose D, top-down cascades start from predator removal, not energy reduction. If you chose A, primary consumers would crash before secondary consumers because they directly depend on producers. If you chose C, the cascade is sequential, not simultaneous, because each level depends on the one below it.
---

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to predict events) + DCI LS2.C (Ecosystem Dynamics) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

Scientists removed all wolves from a national park in the 1920s. Over the next 70 years, they observed that elk populations increased dramatically, willow and aspen trees along riverbanks were heavily overgrazed, riverbank erosion increased, and several small mammal and bird species declined.

Based on a food web model, which explanation best accounts for ALL of these observations?

A. Elk populations increased because there was more food available after the wolves stopped eating the grass.
B. The changes were caused by climate change, not wolf removal, because wolves do not affect plants or rivers.
C. Removing wolves made the ecosystem healthier because elk could roam freely and eat whatever they needed.
D. Without wolves, elk had no predators, so they overpopulated, overgrazed vegetation, which caused erosion and habitat loss for other species.

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: This is a classic top-down trophic cascade. Removing the apex predator (wolves) allowed herbivores (elk) to increase unchecked. More elk meant more grazing pressure on willows and aspens, which destabilized riverbanks. The cascade rippled through the entire ecosystem, affecting birds and mammals that depended on the riverbank vegetation. If you chose C, overpopulation is not healthier; it leads to overgrazing and ecosystem collapse. If you chose B, the timing and pattern of changes directly correlate with wolf removal, not gradual climate trends. If you chose A, wolves are carnivores and do not eat grass; they eat elk.
---

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to predict events) + DCI LS2.B (Energy Transfer) + CCC5 (Energy and Matter)

A marine biologist measures the energy at each trophic level in an ocean food web. Phytoplankton (producers) capture 100,000 kJ of solar energy. Zooplankton (primary consumers) contain 10,000 kJ. Small fish (secondary consumers) contain 1,000 kJ. Tuna (tertiary consumers) contain 100 kJ.

Based on the 10% energy transfer rule, how much energy would be available to support a fourth-level predator like a shark?

A. 100 kJ — the same as tuna because sharks are the same size
B. 1,000 kJ — because sharks are more efficient hunters
C. 0 kJ — because energy runs out completely after three levels
D. 10 kJ — following the 10% transfer from tuna to sharks

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: Following the 10% rule, each trophic level retains only 10% of the energy from the level below. Tuna contain 100 kJ, so sharks would have access to 10% of that, which is 10 kJ. This explains why apex predators are rare: there is very little energy left at the top of the food web. If you chose A, body size does not determine energy availability; the 10% rule applies regardless of size. If you chose B, hunting efficiency does not override the thermodynamic losses at each trophic level. If you chose C, energy does not run out completely; it just becomes too scarce to support large populations.

Question 3: B (Cognitive Level: Reason — SEP 2.1.2, DCI LS2.C, CCC5)
Question 4: D (Cognitive Level: Reason + Evidence — SEP 2.1.4, DCI LS2.C, CCC2)
