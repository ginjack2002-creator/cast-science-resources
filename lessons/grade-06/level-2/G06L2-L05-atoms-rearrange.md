## Platform

### Title
**Atoms Rearrange: Modeling Chemical Reactions — Conservation of Mass in Action**

### Overview
Students model chemical reactions to discover conservation of mass. By tracking reactant concentration, temperature, reaction rate, product formation, and total mass, students prove that atoms are never created or destroyed. They explain why things SEEM to disappear in reactions (the products are often invisible gases) and connect to Lavoisier's foundational chemistry.

### Cover Image
[A photorealistic close-up of a dramatic chemical reaction in a beaker with colorful precipitates forming, dry ice fog, and vibrant liquid layers, laboratory setting with scientific precision]

### Grade
6th (Level 2)

### NGSS Standard
**MS-PS1-2**: Analyze and interpret data on the properties of substances before and after the substances interact to determine if a chemical reaction has occurred.
**MS-PS1-5**: Develop and use a model to describe how the total number of atoms does not change in a chemical reaction and thus mass is conserved.

### Learning Objectives
- Model how reactant concentration and temperature affect the rate of a chemical reaction
- Demonstrate conservation of mass by tracking total mass as reactants convert to products
- Explain why reactants decrease and products increase as mirror images of each other
- Predict how changing temperature or concentration affects the speed of product formation
- Connect the conservation of mass model to real-world chemical reactions like rusting and combustion

### Component List (Starting Model: 5 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Reactant Concentration | External | The amount of starting materials available for the reaction |
| Temperature | External | The thermal energy applied to the reaction system |
| Reaction Rate | Internal | How fast reactants are being converted into products |
| Product Formation | Internal | The amount of new substance being created as atoms rearrange |
| Total Mass | Internal | The combined mass of all reactants and products (ALWAYS CONSTANT) |

### Video Script

```
"When you burn a match, the wood seems to vanish. A 10-pound log
burns down to a few ounces of ash. Where did the other 9+ pounds
go? Did they just stop existing?

Here is the truth: not one atom was destroyed. Not one. The atoms
are all still there. They just rearranged into invisible gases
that floated away. Carbon dioxide. Water vapor. You cannot see
them, but they carry all the missing mass.

Today you are going to prove this with a model. Let's build it.

Two external components you control. Reactant Concentration: how

the combined mass of reactants AND products.

Here is the teaser. When you run the simulation, watch the Total
Mass line. It is internal but it will never move. Flat. Perfectly
flat. No matter what you do to the other variables. That flat line
is the law of conservation of mass.

Lavoisier proved this 250 years ago. Every reaction in the universe
obeys it. Today, you prove it too.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

## Step 2: ESTABLISH — Connect the Relationships

### Text Editor

```
TIME TO DRAW THE REACTION PATHWAYS

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Task A: DRAW YOUR RELATIONSHIPS
• Reactant Concentration → Reaction Rate
• Temperature → Reaction Rate
• Reaction Rate → Product Formation
• Reaction Rate → Reactant Concentration (FEEDBACK!)
• Total Mass: CONSTANT — connected to everything but never changes

Task B: SET POSITIVE OR NEGATIVE
  ○ Reactant Concentration → Reaction Rate = POSITIVE (+)
    More reactant molecules = MORE collisions = FASTER reaction

  ○ Temperature → Reaction Rate = POSITIVE (+)
    Higher temperature = FASTER molecules = MORE energetic collisions

  ○ Reaction Rate → Product Formation = POSITIVE (+)
    Faster reaction = MORE products created

  ○ Reaction Rate → Reactant Concentration = NEGATIVE (−)
    As reaction proceeds, it CONSUMES reactants. Self-slowing!

  ○ Total Mass: CONSERVATION — never changes. As reactant mass
    decreases, product mass increases by EXACTLY the same amount.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Watch for two things: the reactant and product lines will be
PERFECT MIRROR IMAGES, and the total mass line will be FLAT.
```

### Video Script

```
"Let's connect the chemistry. Reactant Concentration to Reaction
Rate: more molecules in the same space means more collisions per
second. Faster reaction. POSITIVE.

Temperature to Reaction Rate: hotter molecules move faster, collide
more often, and collide with more energy. Faster reaction. POSITIVE.

Reaction Rate to Product Formation: a faster reaction creates
products more quickly. POSITIVE.

Now the self-slowing feedback: Reaction Rate back to Reactant
Concentration. NEGATIVE. As the reaction runs, it USES UP
reactants. Fewer reactant molecules means fewer collisions
means slower reaction. The reaction slows itself down as it
consumes its own fuel. Sound familiar? Same pattern as the
cooling curve!

And Total Mass. This is the conservation law. As reactant mass
decreases, product mass increases by the exact same amount. The
sum never changes. It cannot. Atoms are not created or destroyed.
They just rearrange from one molecular configuration to another.

When you run the simulation, the reactant line will go DOWN while
the product line goes UP. They will be mirror images. And the
total mass line? Perfectly flat. That flat line IS conservation
of mass. Lavoisier proved it. Your model proves it too.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

## Step 3: VISUALIZE & EVALUATE — Run Your Reaction Model

### Text Editor

```
TIME TO PROVE CONSERVATION OF MASS

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Task A: SCENARIO 1 — FAST REACTION
• Set Reactant Concentration HIGH and Temperature HIGH
• Watch the reactant line plunge and the product line soar
• Watch the Total Mass line — does it move?

Task B: SCENARIO 2 — SLOW REACTION
• Set Temperature LOW, Reactant Concentration HIGH
• Compare the rate to Scenario 1
• Does Total Mass change?

Task C: SCENARIO 3 — RUNNING OUT
• Start with high reactants, observe over time
• Watch the reaction rate slow as reactants are consumed
• Does the rate stay constant or does it change?

Task D: THE KEY OBSERVATION
• In every scenario, the Total Mass line is FLAT
• Reactant and product lines are mirror images
• THIS IS CONSERVATION OF MASS

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

REFLECTION:
• Why are reactant and product lines mirror images?
• Why does the reaction slow down even if temperature stays high?
• If mass is conserved, why does a burned log seem lighter?
```

### Video Script

```
"Let's prove conservation of mass. Set both inputs high. Click Play.

Watch the graph. The Reactant line drops steeply. The Product line
rises steeply. They are mirror images. As one goes down, the other
goes up by exactly the same amount.

And the Total Mass line? Flat. Perfectly, beautifully flat. No
matter how fast the reaction runs, total mass never changes.
That is conservation of mass in action.

Now slow it down. Set Temperature low. The reaction runs slower.
The lines change more gradually. But Total Mass? Still flat. The
RATE changes but mass is ALWAYS conserved.

One more. Start with high reactants and just watch over time.
The reaction starts fast because lots of molecules are colliding.
But as reactants get used up, fewer molecules remain, collisions
become rarer, and the rate slows. The curve flattens. Same self-
slowing pattern we saw in the cooling curve!

Three scenarios. Different rates. Different speeds. But in EVERY
one, the total mass line is flat. Lavoisier proved this 250 years
ago and it has never been violated. Not once. Every chemical
reaction in the universe conserves mass.

So why does a burned log seem lighter? Because the products are
GASES. CO2 and water vapor float away into the air. The ash that
remains is only the minerals. If you could capture and weigh ALL
the gases too, the total would equal the original log plus the
oxygen consumed. Mass did not disappear. It changed form.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

## Step 4: REVISE & EXTEND — Play, Research, Expand

### Text Editor

```
SET THE CONDITIONS — REFINE YOUR MODEL

Your model shows a relationship between Reactant Concentration and Reaction Rate,
but right now that relationship applies ALL the time. In the
real system, Reactant Concentration only affects Reaction Rate when Temperature is active.

This is called a CONDITIONAL RELATIONSHIP — the connection
between two components depends on a third component's state.

Task A: SET THE CONDITION
   • Click on the connection arrow from Reactant Concentration to Reaction Rate
   • In the toolbar, click "Conditions"
   • In the conditions panel, set: IF Temperature is ON
   • Click "Save Conditions"

Task B: VERIFY THE MODEL
   • Run the simulation with Temperature OFF and Reactant Concentration ON
   • Reaction Rate should NOT respond to Reactant Concentration alone
   • Now turn Temperature ON — Reactant Concentration's effect on Reaction Rate activates
   • This matches how the real system behaves

Task C: SET ADDITIONAL CONDITION
   • Click on the arrow from Temperature to Reaction Rate
   • Click "Conditions" and set: IF Reactant Concentration is ON
   • Save — Temperature now only affects Reaction Rate when
     Reactant Concentration is active

Your model now captures the conditional logic of the real system.
Not all relationships are always active — some depend on conditions.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

NOW LET'S PLAY AND EXPLORE

YOUR MODEL PROVES CONSERVATION — NOW EXPLORE FURTHER

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

PLAY TIME CHALLENGES:
1. Can you make Total Mass change? (Hint: you cannot!)
2. What if reactants start at zero? What happens?
3. Can you make the reaction go backwards?

WHAT'S MISSING?
• Surface Area — crushed reactants react faster
• Catalyst Presence — speeds up without being consumed
• Equilibrium Point — forward and reverse rates balance

RESEARCH DIRECTIONS:
• How did Lavoisier prove conservation of mass?
• Why does iron gain mass when it rusts?
• What about nuclear reactions? Does E=mc2 break conservation?

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

ADD ONE COMPONENT. Connect it. Test it. Does it change the
rate but NOT the total mass? That is the pattern.
```

### Video Script

```
"Your model proves conservation of mass. Now push it further.

Try to make Total Mass change. You cannot. No matter what you
do to temperature, concentration, or rate, that line stays flat.

Think about what is missing. Surface Area: if you crush a
reactant into powder, it reacts faster because more molecules
are exposed. How would you model that? It is a POSITIVE
relationship to Reaction Rate.

A catalyst is fascinating. It speeds up a reaction without being
consumed. Enzymes in your body are catalysts. They lower the
energy barrier for reactions.

And here is a mind-bending research question: in nuclear reactions,
mass actually IS converted to energy. E=mc2. A tiny amount of mass
becomes enormous energy. But in chemical reactions? Mass is always
perfectly conserved.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Question 1
Which correctly identifies external components? A. Reactant Concentration only  B. Reactant Concentration and Temperature  C. Reaction Rate and Temperature  D. Total Mass and Reactant Concentration
**Correct: B**

### Question 2
In the simulation, as the Reactant line drops from 100% to 0%, the Product line rises from 0% to 100%. What does the Total Mass line do?
A. Drops with reactants  B. Rises with products  C. Stays perfectly flat at 100%  D. Fluctuates randomly
**Correct: C** — Conservation of mass.

### Question 3
A student mixes 50g baking soda with 100g vinegar in an OPEN beaker. After the reaction, the beaker weighs 140g. What explains the missing 10g?
A. The reaction destroyed 10g of matter  B. CO2 gas escaped into the air, carrying 10g of mass with it  C. The scale was wrong  D. The vinegar evaporated
**Correct: B**

### Question 4
A student increases temperature but not reactant concentration. What happens?
A. Reaction rate increases AND total mass increases  B. Reaction rate increases but total mass stays the same  C. Reaction rate stays the same  D. Total mass decreases
**Correct: B**

### Question 5
Iron rusts and gains mass. A student says this violates conservation of mass. Why is the student wrong?
A. Rusting is not a chemical reaction  B. The iron combined with oxygen from the air; the total mass of iron + air is conserved  C. Conservation of mass only applies to burning, not rusting  D. The scale must be wrong
**Correct: B**

### Answer Key
Q1: B | Q2: C | Q3: B | Q4: B | Q5: B
