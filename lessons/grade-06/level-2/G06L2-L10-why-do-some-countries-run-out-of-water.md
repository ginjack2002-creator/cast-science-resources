## Platform

### Title
**Why Do Some Countries Run Out of Water? — Modeling Water Stress from Rainfall, Population, and Consumption**

### Overview
Students model why some countries face water crises despite Earth having the same total amount of water it has always had. By building a cause-and-effect model of rainfall patterns, population density, aquifer recharge, and water demand, students discover that water stress is about the balance between local supply and local demand, not global shortage. This lesson serves as a hook for deeper inquiry into natural resource distribution, human impacts on Earth systems, and sustainability.

### NGSS Standard
**MS-ESS3-1**: Construct a scientific explanation based on evidence for how the uneven distributions of Earth's mineral, energy, and groundwater resources are the result of past and current geoscience processes.
**MS-ESS3-4**: Construct an argument supported by evidence for how increases in human population and per-capita consumption of natural resources impact Earth's systems.

### Learning Objectives
- Model how rainfall patterns and population density interact to create water stress
- Explain the relationship between aquifer recharge rates and water demand
- Predict which conditions lead to sustainable water use versus water crisis
- Analyze how the Water Stress Index reflects the balance between supply and demand
- Evaluate how human population growth and consumption patterns affect groundwater systems

### Component List (Starting Model: 5 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Rainfall Pattern | External | The amount, timing, and distribution of precipitation that provides the primary input of freshwater to a region |
| Population Density | External | The number of people per unit area, which drives total water demand through domestic, agricultural, and industrial use |
| Aquifer Recharge | Internal | The rate at which groundwater supplies are naturally replenished by rainfall infiltration through soil and rock |
| Water Demand | Internal | The total amount of water withdrawn for human use including drinking, agriculture, industry, and sanitation |
| Water Stress Index | Internal | A measure of the gap between available water supply and human water demand, indicating sustainability or crisis |

• Click on "Aquifer Recharge" and drag an arrow to "Water Stress Index"
• Click on "Water Demand" and drag an arrow to "Water Stress Index"

• For each connection, ask: "When this goes UP, does the other go UP or DOWN?"

  ○ Rainfall Pattern → Aquifer Recharge = POSITIVE (+)
    More rainfall = MORE water seeping into underground reserves

  ○ Population Density → Water Demand = POSITIVE (+)
    More people = MORE water consumed for drinking, farming, industry

  ○ Aquifer Recharge → Water Stress Index = NEGATIVE (−)
    MORE recharge = LESS stress (supply is keeping up with demand)

  ○ Water Demand → Water Stress Index = POSITIVE (+)
    MORE demand = MORE stress (humans are using it faster)

Task D: CHECK YOUR MODEL
• You should have 4 arrows total
• 2 positive relationships, 2 negative relationships
• Notice: SUPPLY (rainfall → recharge) REDUCES stress
• Notice: DEMAND (population → demand) INCREASES stress

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

This is a BALANCE system. When supply keeps up with demand,
the index stays low. When demand outpaces supply, crisis.
```

need to drink, farm, and manufacture.

Three years of low rainfall combined with a growing population.
The gap between supply and demand widened until they were counting
down to Day Zero.

Your model just explained a crisis that affected four million
real people. And the lesson is this: countries do not run out of
water because Earth is drying up. They run out because local
demand exceeds local supply.

## Step 4: REVISE & EXTEND — Play, Research, Expand

### Text Editor

```
SET THE CONDITIONS — REFINE YOUR MODEL

Your model shows a relationship between Aquifer Recharge and Water Stress Index,
but right now that relationship applies ALL the time. In the
real system, Aquifer Recharge only affects Water Stress Index when Water Demand is active.

This is called a CONDITIONAL RELATIONSHIP — the connection
between two components depends on a third component's state.

Task A: SET THE CONDITION
   • Click on the connection arrow from Aquifer Recharge to Water Stress Index
   • In the toolbar, click "Conditions"
   • In the conditions panel, set: IF Water Demand is ON
   • Click "Save Conditions"

Task B: VERIFY THE MODEL
   • Run the simulation with Water Demand OFF and Aquifer Recharge ON
   • Water Stress Index should NOT respond to Aquifer Recharge alone
   • Now turn Water Demand ON — Aquifer Recharge's effect on Water Stress Index activates
   • This matches how the real system behaves

Task C: SET ADDITIONAL CONDITION
   • Click on the arrow from Water Demand to Water Stress Index
   • Click "Conditions" and set: IF Aquifer Recharge is ON
   • Save — Water Demand now only affects Water Stress Index when
     Aquifer Recharge is active

Your model now captures the conditional logic of the real system.
Not all relationships are always active — some depend on conditions.

□ Review vocabulary: aquifer, recharge, water stress, per-capita

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Earth is running out of water" → Earth has the same amount of
   water it has always had. The problem is local distribution and
   the rate of human extraction versus natural recharge.
• "Rainfall alone determines water stress" → Demand matters just
   as much. Moderate rainfall with massive population can be worse
   than low rainfall with tiny population.
• "Groundwater is unlimited because it refills from rain" → Aquifer
   recharge can take years to centuries. Humans can drain an aquifer
   in decades. This time mismatch is the root of many crises.

FACILITATION TIPS:
• Step 1: The Cape Town story is a powerful hook. Show the
   countdown clock they displayed in the city.
• Step 2: Draw the balance scale on the board. Supply on one side,
   demand on the other. The Water Stress Index is the tilt.
• Step 3: The growing demand scenario is the most impactful. Let
   students see the index climb as they increase population.
• Step 4: Push students toward solutions. If they can add water
   recycling or conservation to their model, they are thinking
   like real water engineers.

DISCUSSION PROMPTS:
• "Where does the water in your tap actually come from?"
• "If Earth is 70% water, why would any city run out?"
• "What if a country doubled its population but cut per-capita
   water use in half? What would happen to the stress index?"
• "Why is the Aral Sea almost gone and can it ever come back?"

EXTENSION CONNECTIONS:
• Water cycle → Aquifers as part of the larger hydrological cycle
• Human impact → Agricultural, industrial, and domestic water use
• Climate change → Shifting rainfall patterns and drought
• Engineering → Desalination, recycling, and conservation technology

ASSESSMENT:
• Can student explain water stress as a supply-demand balance?
• Does student's model show correct positive/negative relationships?
• Can student explain why local conditions matter more than global totals?
• Did student add at least one researched component?

DIFFERENTIATION:
Support: Pre-label relationships; use the bucket demonstration
(small hole = recharge, cup = demand); sentence starters
Challenge: Calculate Water Stress Index for real cities; research
Israel's water management; design a 50-year management plan
ELL: Provide visual vocabulary cards; pair with English-proficient
partner; use concrete demonstrations with water and containers

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format. Each item is three-dimensional, assessing a Science and Engineering Practice (SEP), Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and Crosscutting Concept (CCC) simultaneously, aligned to MS-ESS3-1 and MS-ESS3-4.

---

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.1 (Determine components of a system) + DCI ESS3.A (Natural Resources) + CCC7 (Stability and Change)

A student is building a model to explain water stress. She identifies five things that matter: rainfall pattern, population density, aquifer recharge, water demand, and the Water Stress Index. She needs to sort them into two groups: things determined by geography and demographics (external) and things that respond within the system (internal).

Which list correctly sorts the components of the water stress system?

A. External: Rainfall Pattern, Aquifer Recharge — Internal: Population Density, Water Demand, Water Stress Index
B. External: Rainfall Pattern, Population Density, Water Demand — Internal: Aquifer Recharge, Water Stress Index
C. External: Water Stress Index, Rainfall Pattern — Internal: Population Density, Aquifer Recharge, Water Demand
D. External: Rainfall Pattern, Population Density — Internal: Aquifer Recharge, Water Demand, Water Stress Index

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: Rainfall Pattern is determined by geography and climate, and Population Density is determined by demographic trends. Both are external inputs that the water system cannot control. Aquifer Recharge, Water Demand, and the Water Stress Index all respond to those inputs through cause-and-effect relationships within the system. If you chose A, Aquifer Recharge is not external; it responds to rainfall. If you chose C, the Water Stress Index is the final output of the system, not an external input. If you chose B, Water Demand is generated by the population's interaction with the water system, making it internal.
---

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships among components) + DCI ESS3.A (Natural Resources) + CCC7 (Stability and Change)

In a water stress model, a student draws an arrow from Aquifer Recharge to the Water Stress Index and labels it as a negative relationship. Another student says it should be positive because when the aquifer recharges, the Water Stress Index number should go up.

Which explanation best supports why the relationship between Aquifer Recharge and the Water Stress Index is negative?

A. When aquifer recharge increases, more water is available underground, so the Water Stress Index goes up to reflect more water.
B. When aquifer recharge increases, population grows because water is available, increasing stress.
C. When aquifer recharge increases, more water supply is available, which reduces the gap between supply and demand, lowering the Water Stress Index.
D. When aquifer recharge decreases, people use less water because they know supply is limited.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: A negative relationship means when one goes up, the other goes down. The Water Stress Index measures how close demand is to exceeding supply. When Aquifer Recharge increases, supply grows, the gap between supply and demand narrows, and stress decreases. If you chose A, the Water Stress Index measures stress, not water amount. More supply means less stress, not more. If you chose D, this describes a possible behavioral response, not the direct relationship between recharge and stress. If you chose B, this describes an indirect potential effect, but the direct relationship is that more recharge reduces stress.
---

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships among components) + DCI ESS3.C (Human Impacts on Earth Systems) + CCC7 (Stability and Change)

A student builds a water stress model. Rainfall Pattern positively drives Aquifer Recharge. Population Density positively drives Water Demand. Aquifer Recharge negatively affects the Water Stress Index (more recharge means less stress). Water Demand positively affects the Water Stress Index (more demand means more stress). A city's population triples over 30 years while rainfall stays the same.

Based on the model, what would most likely happen to the Water Stress Index?

A. It would decrease because a larger population can dig more wells to increase supply.
B. It would increase because Water Demand rises while Aquifer Recharge stays constant, widening the gap.
C. It would decrease because more people means more tax revenue to build water infrastructure.
D. It would stay the same because rainfall has not changed.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: The model shows that Population Density positively drives Water Demand, and Water Demand positively increases the Water Stress Index. When population triples, demand triples. But rainfall has not changed, so Aquifer Recharge stays the same. Supply stays flat while demand rises. The gap between them grows, pushing the Water Stress Index higher. If you chose C, the model does not include economic factors like tax revenue. It models the physical balance of water supply and demand. If you chose D, rainfall being constant means supply is unchanged, but demand has tripled. The stress index depends on both. If you chose A, digging more wells extracts water faster but does not increase the rate of recharge. It would actually deplete the aquifer faster.
---

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to explain/predict events) + DCI ESS3.A (Natural Resources) + CCC7 (Stability and Change)

Scientists compared water data for four cities. City A has high rainfall and low population, with a Water Stress Index of 0.1 (low stress). City B has high rainfall and high population, with a Water Stress Index of 0.5 (moderate stress). City C has low rainfall and low population, with a Water Stress Index of 0.3 (moderate stress). City D has low rainfall and high population, with a Water Stress Index of 0.9 (crisis).

Which comparison best demonstrates that water stress depends on the BALANCE between supply and demand, not just rainfall alone?

A. City B has high rainfall but moderate stress because its large population drives high demand, while City C has low rainfall but only moderate stress because its small population keeps demand low.
B. City D has crisis-level stress because it has low rainfall.
C. City A and City B both have high rainfall, proving rainfall is the most important factor.
D. City A has low stress because it has high rainfall.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: This comparison shows that rainfall alone does not determine water stress. City B has high rainfall (good supply) but high population (high demand), resulting in moderate stress. City C has low rainfall (limited supply) but low population (low demand), also resulting in moderate stress. The stress level depends on the ratio of supply to demand, not just one side. If you chose D, this is true but does not show the balance. It only considers supply. If you chose B, this is partially true but misses that low population could offset low rainfall (as in City C). If you chose C, City A and B have the same rainfall but different stress levels because of different populations. This actually disproves that rainfall alone determines stress.
---

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to predict a scientific event) + DCI ESS3.C (Human Impacts on Earth Systems) + CCC7 (Stability and Change)

A growing desert city currently has a Water Stress Index of 0.7 (high stress). Population is projected to double in 25 years and climate models predict 20% less rainfall. City planners are debating what to do.

Based on the water stress model, which strategy would most effectively reduce the Water Stress Index?

A. Move some of the population to a different city to reduce local density.
B. Build more wells to extract water from the aquifer faster.
C. Wait for climate patterns to shift and bring more rainfall to the region.
D. Implement water recycling, reduce per-capita consumption through conservation, and build desalination infrastructure.

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: The model shows that water stress is driven by the gap between supply (Aquifer Recharge) and demand (Water Demand). The most effective strategy attacks both sides: water recycling and desalination increase effective supply, while conservation reduces demand. Together, these narrow the gap and lower the Water Stress Index. If you chose B, extracting water faster does not increase supply; it depletes the aquifer faster. This would increase stress, not reduce it. Aquifer recharge is limited by rainfall, not by pump capacity. If you chose C, rainfall is an external factor the city cannot control. Climate models predict LESS rainfall, making this strategy worse than doing nothing. If you chose A, while this would reduce local demand, relocating population is not a realistic water management strategy and does not address the fundamental supply-demand imbalance.

Question 3: B (Cognitive Level: Reason — SEP 2.1.2, DCI ESS3.C, CCC7)
