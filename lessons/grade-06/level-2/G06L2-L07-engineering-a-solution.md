## Platform

### Title
**Engineering a Solution: The Design Process — Modeling How Iteration Turns Failure into Innovation**

### Overview
Students model the engineering design process itself, discovering how problem complexity, research depth, design quality, prototype performance, and iteration count interact. The key insight is the positive feedback loop between testing and redesign: each prototype test reveals problems that improve the next version. Students learn that iteration is not failure but the most powerful tool in engineering.

### Grade
6th (Level 2)

### NGSS Standard
**MS-ETS1-1**: Define the criteria and constraints of a design problem with sufficient precision to ensure a successful solution, taking into account relevant scientific principles and potential impacts on people and the natural environment.
**MS-ETS1-2**: Evaluate competing design solutions using a systematic process to determine how well they meet the criteria and constraints of the problem.

### Learning Objectives
- Model how problem complexity, research depth, design quality, prototype performance, and iteration count interact
- Explain the positive feedback loop between testing prototypes and improving design quality
- Predict how increasing research depth improves first-attempt design quality for complex problems
- Demonstrate that iteration (test-improve cycles) is the single most powerful variable for improving outcomes
- Connect the engineering design process model to real-world innovation stories

### Component List (Starting Model: 5 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Problem Complexity | External | How difficult the engineering challenge is |
| Research Depth | External | How much background investigation is done before designing |
| Design Quality | Internal | How well the proposed solution addresses the problem |
| Prototype Performance | Internal | How well the physical build works when tested |
| Iteration Count | Internal | The number of test-improve cycles completed |

### Video Script

```
"Three scenarios. Three strategies. Let's see which wins.

Scenario 1: No Research Rush. Problem Complexity high, Research
Depth zero. Jump straight to building. Watch. Design Quality is
terrible. Prototype Performance is terrible. Even with iteration,
improvement is slow because the team does not understand the
fundamentals. They are fixing symptoms, not root causes.

Scenario 2: Research First. Same hard problem, but 90% research.
The first design is MUCH better. The first prototype actually
works partially. Fewer iterations needed. But still not perfect
on the first try because research cannot predict everything that
happens in the real world.

Scenario 3: Iterate to Excellence. Moderate research, then multiple
test-improve cycles. Watch the performance curve. Each iteration
makes the prototype better. The improvement is fastest at the
beginning (biggest problems found first) and gradually slows as
the design approaches optimization.

Now compare. The team that does maximum research with one test gets
a decent result. The team that does moderate research with many
tests gets a BETTER result. The optimal strategy? Research first,
THEN iterate. Research gives a better starting point. Iteration
refines from there.

This is exactly what the best engineering companies do. SpaceX
researches rocket science deeply, THEN tests rapidly. Apple studies
user needs intensely, THEN prototypes dozens of versions. The
combination wins.

Iteration is the most powerful variable in the model. You can
overcome a bad start by testing and improving. But you cannot
overcome skipping the testing loop.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

```
Q: How is the engineering loop like the scientific method?
```

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Question 1
A student sorts engineering design process components. Problem Complexity and Research Depth are:
A. Both internal  B. Both external  C. Problem Complexity is external, Research Depth is internal  D. Problem Complexity is internal, Research Depth is external
**Correct: B** — Both are set before the design process begins.

### Question 2
In the model, Problem Complexity has a NEGATIVE relationship with Design Quality. Research Depth has a POSITIVE relationship. If a team faces a highly complex problem, what is their best strategy?
A. Give up because complex problems always have bad designs  B. Increase Research Depth to offset the negative effect of complexity  C. Skip research and start building immediately  D. Reduce problem complexity by ignoring some constraints
**Correct: B**

### Question 3
Team A spends 80% of time researching and 20% building one prototype. Team B spends 20% researching and 80% building and testing three prototypes. Based on the model, which is more likely to succeed?
A. Team A, because research always beats testing  B. Team B, because iteration with the feedback loop progressively improves the design through real-world testing data  C. Both perform equally because the total time is the same  D. Neither, because engineering success is random
**Correct: B**

### Question 4
The feedback loop shows that Prototype Performance feeds back to improve Design Quality. Why is this positive feedback the most important arrow in the model?
A. It makes prototypes perform worse each time  B. Testing reveals real-world problems that were invisible on paper, providing information that directly improves the next design  C. It eliminates the need for research  D. It means every first design is perfect
**Correct: B**

### Question 5
SpaceX crashed rockets multiple times before successfully landing one. A student says SpaceX failed multiple times. Using the engineering model, what is a more accurate interpretation?
A. The student is correct; SpaceX failed because the rockets crashed  B. Each crash was a successful TEST that provided data for the feedback loop, progressively improving the design until it succeeded  C. SpaceX should have researched more and tested less  D. The crashes were random and not part of a process
**Correct: B**
