## Platform

### Title
**Your Music Is a Wave — How Sound, Light, and WiFi All Follow the Same Rules**

### Overview
Every sound you hear, every color you see, every WiFi signal your phone receives — they're all waves. Understanding wave properties reveals the physics behind music, communication, and even medical imaging. Students investigate the driving question: How does a wave carry your favorite song from a speaker to your ear? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

**MS-PS4-1, MS-PS4-2**: Use mathematical representations to describe a simple model for waves that includes how the amplitude of a wave is related to the energy in a wave. Develop and use a model to describe that waves are reflected, absorbed, or transmitted through various materials.

### Learning Objectives
- Explain the relationship between wave properties (amplitude, frequency, wavelength)
- Model how changing amplitude and frequency affects the energy and pitch of sound
- Predict how waves behave when they encounter different materials
- Evaluate real-world applications of wave science in music and technology

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• Wave Basics:
  A wave is a repeating disturbance that transfers energy without transferring matter. The key properties are: amplitude (height from baseline), frequency (cycles per second, measured in Hertz), wavelength (distance between identical points), and speed (depends on the medium).

• Amplitude and Energy:
  Wave energy is proportional to amplitude SQUARED. This means doubling the amplitude quadruples the energy. In sound, amplitude determines loudness. In light, amplitude determines brightness. This squared relationship is why a small increase in volume requires a large increase in speaker power.

• Frequency and Perception:
  Humans can hear frequencies from about 20 Hz (deep bass) to 20,000 Hz (high pitch). Frequency determines the pitch of sound and the color of light. Musical notes correspond to specific frequencies: middle C is 262 Hz, the A above it is 440 Hz.

• Wave Interactions with Materials:
  When waves encounter a new material, they can be: reflected (bounced back — echo), absorbed (converted to heat — sound dampening), or transmitted (passed through). Most materials do all three to varying degrees. Hard surfaces reflect more sound; soft surfaces absorb more.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Sound can travel through empty space"
  Reality: Sound requires a medium (matter) to travel through. Sound travels through air, water, and solids, but NOT through a vacuum. In space, no one can hear you scream!
  Strategy: Ask: If sound needs air molecules to vibrate, what happens when there are no air molecules?

• "Loud sounds travel faster"
  Reality: Volume (amplitude) does NOT affect the speed of sound. A whisper and a scream travel at the same speed (~343 m/s in air). Amplitude only affects energy, not speed.
  Strategy: Analogy: A big wave and a small wave in a pool move at the same speed — one is just taller.

• "Higher frequency means more energy"
  Reality: Frequency affects pitch, not necessarily total energy. Amplitude is the primary driver of wave energy. A loud bass note (high amplitude, low frequency) can carry more energy than a quiet high note (low amplitude, high frequency).
  Strategy: Compare: Which carries more energy — a whispered scream or a felt bass drop?

• Screaming High Note: At maximum amplitude AND frequency, Wave Energy is at its peak (amplitude squared). Sound Perception shows maximum loudness AND highest pitch. This represents the most energetic sound wave in our model.
• Bass Drop: At maximum amplitude but minimum frequency, Wave Energy is still high (amplitude drives energy). Sound Perception shows maximum loudness but lowest pitch. The deep bass vibration carries a lot of energy but at a low frequency — that's why you can FEEL bass in your chest.

Reflection Exemplars:
• Q: Why does bass feel different from treble?
  A: Bass waves have low frequency (long wavelength) and can actually vibrate your body because the wavelengths are long enough to physically move objects. High-frequency waves have shorter wavelengths that primarily affect your eardrums. That's why you feel bass in your chest at concerts.
• Q: Why does a room's material affect sound?
  A: Hard surfaces (concrete, glass) reflect most sound waves back into the room, creating echoes and reverb. Soft surfaces (carpet, curtains, foam) absorb sound energy by converting it to tiny amounts of heat. Good acoustics require the right balance of reflection and absorption.

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 5.1 (Use mathematical representations) + DCI PS4.A.1 (Amplitude is related to energy in a wave) + CCC3 (Scale, Proportion, and Quantity)

A student runs a wave model and records the following data. When amplitude is set to 1 unit, wave energy reads 1 unit. When amplitude is doubled to 2 units, wave energy reads 4 units. When amplitude is tripled to 3 units, wave energy reads 9 units.

What mathematical relationship between amplitude and wave energy does this data demonstrate?

A. Energy and amplitude are inversely proportional, meaning that as amplitude increases, energy decreases.
B. Energy is proportional to the square of amplitude (E is proportional to A squared). Doubling amplitude quadruples energy, and tripling amplitude produces nine times the energy.
C. Energy and amplitude are directly proportional, meaning doubling amplitude should double energy.
D. Energy and amplitude are completely unrelated, and the pattern in the data is coincidental.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: The data shows a perfect squared relationship. Amplitude 1 gives energy 1 (1 squared = 1). Amplitude 2 gives energy 4 (2 squared = 4). Amplitude 3 gives energy 9 (3 squared = 9). This squared relationship means small increases in amplitude produce large increases in energy. This is a fundamental property of waves and explains why very loud sounds carry far more energy than moderately loud sounds. If you chose A, the data shows energy increasing with amplitude, not decreasing. An inverse relationship would show energy dropping as amplitude rises. If you chose C, direct proportionality would predict energy of 2 at amplitude 2 and energy of 3 at amplitude 3. The actual values (4 and 9) are much larger, indicating a squared, not linear, relationship. If you chose D, the pattern (1, 4, 9) exactly matches the squares of (1, 2, 3). This is a consistent mathematical relationship, not a coincidence.

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 4.1 (Analyze data) + DCI PS4.A.1 (Wave properties) + CCC5 (Energy and Matter)

A student tests two scenarios in the wave model. In the "Bass Drop" scenario, amplitude is set to maximum but frequency is set to minimum, producing high Wave Energy. In the "Screaming High Note" scenario, both amplitude and frequency are set to maximum, producing even higher Wave Energy. In a third scenario, amplitude is minimum and frequency is maximum, producing moderate Wave Energy.

What do these three scenarios reveal about what determines total wave energy?

A. Both amplitude and frequency contribute to total wave energy. A wave can carry significant energy from high amplitude, high frequency, or a combination of both.
B. Wave energy is constant and cannot be changed by adjusting any property.
C. Only amplitude determines wave energy. Frequency has no effect whatsoever.
D. Only frequency determines wave energy. Amplitude has no effect whatsoever.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: The three scenarios isolate the contributions. The "Bass Drop" (high amplitude, low frequency) has high energy, showing amplitude contributes. The third scenario (low amplitude, high frequency) has moderate energy, showing frequency contributes. The "Screaming High Note" (both high) has the highest energy, showing the contributions combine. Both properties independently add to total wave energy. If you chose C, if only amplitude mattered, the third scenario (low amplitude, high frequency) would show near-zero energy instead of moderate energy. Frequency clearly contributes. If you chose D, if only frequency mattered, the "Bass Drop" (high amplitude, minimum frequency) would show near-zero energy instead of high energy. Amplitude clearly contributes. If you chose B, the three scenarios show three different energy levels, proving that wave energy changes with amplitude and frequency adjustments.

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.3 (Develop and use a model) + DCI PS4.A.2 (Sound, light, and other waves have common properties) + CCC1 (Patterns)

The model shows that in sound waves, amplitude determines loudness and frequency determines pitch. The student knows that sound and light are both waves, even though they are different types (mechanical vs. electromagnetic).

If amplitude controls loudness in sound waves, what does amplitude control in light waves?

A. The speed at which light travels through space.
B. The color of the light, because color is the visual equivalent of sound.
C. Whether light can pass through glass or is blocked by solid objects.
D. The brightness (intensity) of the light, because amplitude represents the intensity of energy in any wave, manifesting as loudness in sound and brightness in light.

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: Amplitude is the intensity property of all waves. In sound, higher amplitude means greater pressure variation, which we perceive as louder. In light, higher amplitude means greater electromagnetic field strength, which we perceive as brighter. The same wave property (amplitude = energy intensity) manifests differently depending on wave type but plays the same fundamental role. If you chose B, color in light is determined by frequency, just as pitch in sound is determined by frequency. Both are frequency-dependent properties, not amplitude-dependent. If you chose A, the speed of light in a vacuum is constant (approximately 300 million meters per second) and does not depend on amplitude, frequency, or any other wave property. If you chose C, whether light passes through or is blocked by a material depends on the material's properties and the light's frequency, not its amplitude. Transparent, translucent, and opaque are material properties.

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 6.5 (Apply scientific ideas to design solutions) + DCI PS4.B.1 (Waves are reflected, absorbed, or transmitted) + CCC4 (Systems and System Models)

In the STEM challenge, students designed a concert venue. A student proposes building all interior walls from hard, smooth materials like glass and polished concrete to create a modern aesthetic.

Using the model's principles about how waves interact with different materials, what acoustic problem would this design create?

A. Glass walls would completely block all sound from entering the venue, creating silence inside.
B. Glass walls would amplify sound waves to dangerously loud levels through a resonance effect.
C. Hard, smooth surfaces reflect sound waves rather than absorbing them. Multiple reflections would create excessive echoes and reverberation, causing direct sound and reflected sound to overlap and distort the music quality.
D. Smooth walls would cause sound to travel faster, raising the pitch of all music performed in the venue.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: When sound waves encounter a surface, they can be reflected, absorbed, or transmitted. Hard, smooth surfaces primarily reflect sound. In a room made entirely of reflective surfaces, sound waves bounce repeatedly off walls, ceiling, and floor, arriving at the listener's ears at slightly different times. These overlapping reflections create reverberation and muddy the audio quality. Concert venues use a strategic mix of reflective and absorptive materials to control sound. If you chose A, glass transmits some sound and reflects the rest, but it does not block all sound. Sound waves are pressure waves that can also vibrate the glass itself, transmitting energy through it. If you chose B, while resonance can amplify specific frequencies in enclosed spaces, the primary problem with all-reflective walls is echo and reverberation, not amplification. Sound energy is conserved, not multiplied by reflection. If you chose D, the speed of sound in air depends on temperature and the medium, not on wall surfaces. Smooth walls do not change the speed of sound or the pitch of music.

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 7.1 (Engage in argument from evidence) + DCI PS4.A.1 (Amplitude and energy relationship) + CCC3 (Scale, Proportion, and Quantity)

A student reads that prolonged exposure to sounds above 85 decibels can cause permanent hearing damage. A concert speaker produces sound at 100 decibels at the front row (10 meters away). The student calculates that at 40 meters away, the sound level drops to approximately 88 decibels due to the inverse square law (energy spreads over a larger area with distance).

A friend argues that sitting at 40 meters is safe because 88 decibels is "close to" the 85 dB safety threshold. Using the model's squared relationship between amplitude and energy, which argument best evaluates this claim?

A. The friend is correct. Since 88 is only 3 more than 85, the difference in energy is negligible.
B. Distance from the speaker has no effect on hearing safety because sound energy stays the same regardless of where you sit.
C. The friend underestimates the risk. Because energy is proportional to amplitude squared, the difference between 85 dB and 88 dB represents approximately double the energy intensity, not just a 3.5% increase. At 88 dB for a multi-hour concert, the cumulative energy exposure exceeds safe levels for hearing.
D. The only way to be safe at a concert is to wear earplugs that block all sound completely.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: The decibel scale is logarithmic. Each 3 dB increase represents approximately a doubling of sound energy intensity. So 88 dB delivers roughly double the energy per unit area compared to 85 dB. Combined with the squared relationship between amplitude and energy, a seemingly small numerical difference (3 dB) represents a significant increase in the energy hitting the listener's eardrums. Over a multi-hour concert, this cumulative exposure can cause permanent hearing damage. If you chose A, the linear appearance of "only 3 more" is deceptive because the decibel scale is logarithmic. A 3 dB difference doubles the energy intensity, which is physiologically significant for hearing damage. If you chose B, the inverse square law shows that sound energy does decrease with distance. Moving farther from the source reduces the energy reaching your ears. Distance absolutely affects hearing safety. If you chose D, complete sound blocking would defeat the purpose of attending a concert. Many concert-goers use attenuating earplugs that reduce volume by 15-25 dB while preserving music quality, which is a practical harm-reduction strategy.
