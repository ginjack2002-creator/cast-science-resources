## Platform

### Title
**Why Hurricanes Are Getting Angrier — The Science Behind Supercharged Storms**

### Overview
Hurricanes are the most powerful weather events on Earth. A single hurricane can release energy equivalent to 10,000 nuclear bombs. Understanding the science behind their formation and intensification is critical as climate change alters ocean temperatures. Students investigate the driving question: Why are hurricanes getting stronger and more destructive? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

**MS-ESS2-5, MS-ESS2-6**: Collect data to provide evidence for how the motions and complex interactions of air masses results in changes in weather conditions. ### Learning Objectives
- Explain how warm ocean water provides energy for hurricane formation
- Model how rising sea surface temperatures intensify hurricanes
- Predict how climate patterns affect hurricane strength and frequency
- Evaluate the relationship between ocean temperature and storm damage

Hurricanes are the most powerful weather events on Earth. A single hurricane can release energy equivalent to 10,000 nuclear bombs. Understanding the science behind their formation and intensification is critical as climate change alters ocean temperatures.

NGSS ALIGNMENT:
MS-ESS2-5, MS-ESS2-6: Collect data to provide evidence for how the motions and complex interactions of air masses results in changes in weather conditions. THREE-DIMENSIONAL LEARNING:
• Science Practice: Developing and Using Models
  Students develop a model showing how ocean temperature and atmospheric conditions determine hurricane intensity.
• Disciplinary Core Idea: ESS2.D Weather and Climate
  Complex interactions of air masses, ocean temperatures, and atmospheric conditions drive weather patterns.
• Crosscutting Concept: Energy and Matter
  Students trace the flow of thermal energy from ocean water through evaporation into the kinetic energy of hurricane winds.

PACING GUIDE:
• Step 1 (Locate): 8-10 minutes
• Step 2 (Establish): 8-10 minutes
• Step 3 (Visualize & Evaluate): 10-12 minutes
• Step 4 (Revise & Extend): 10-15 minutes
• Total: 50-70 minutes (or split across 2 class periods)

PRE-LESSON PREP:
□ Test ModelIt access on student devices
□ Review vocabulary: Sea Surface Temperature, Rapid Intensification, Storm Surge, Convection
□ Prepare Why are hurricanes getting stronger and more destructive discussion hook
□ Have images or video ready for phenomenon introduction

LEVER FRAMEWORK:
• Locate: Students identify sea surface temperature, atmospheric moisture, hurricane wind speed, and storm surge height as system components.
• Establish: Students determine that warmer water and more moisture both increase wind speed (positive), and wind speed increases storm surge (positive).
• Visualize: Students build the hurricane model showing how ocean heat translates to storm intensity.
• Evaluate: Students compare normal, supercharged, and cool water scenarios to see the energy relationship.
• Revise: Students add wind shear or coastal population to explore additional factors.

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• Hurricane Formation:
  Hurricanes form over warm ocean water (above 80°F/26.5°C). Warm water evaporates, and the moist air rises rapidly through convection. As it rises, it cools and condenses, releasing latent heat that further fuels the rising air. The Coriolis effect from Earth's rotation causes the system to spin.

• The Energy Connection:
  A hurricane is essentially a heat engine. The warmer the ocean surface, the more water evaporates, and the more energy is available to fuel the storm. The relationship between sea surface temperature and maximum potential hurricane intensity is well-established in atmospheric science.

• Rapid Intensification:
  Rapid intensification occurs when a hurricane's maximum sustained winds increase by 35+ mph in 24 hours. Hurricane Patricia (2015) went from tropical storm to Category 5 in just 24 hours. Studies show RI events have increased in frequency as oceans warm, making hurricanes harder to forecast and evacuate for.

• Climate Change Connection:
  Global average sea surface temperatures have risen about 1.5°F since 1900. The proportion of Category 4 and 5 hurricanes has increased significantly since 1980. Climate models predict continued intensification as oceans absorb more heat, with the strongest storms getting even stronger.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Hurricanes are getting more frequent"
  Reality: The total NUMBER of hurricanes hasn't clearly increased, but the PROPORTION of intense storms (Category 4-5) has increased significantly. Fewer storms, but the ones that form are stronger.
  Strategy: Show data comparing total hurricane counts vs. major hurricane counts over time.

• "Wind is the most dangerous part"
  Reality: Storm surge actually causes the most deaths and property damage during hurricanes. A 15-foot wall of ocean water pushed inland is more destructive than wind alone.
  Strategy: Show storm surge vs. wind damage statistics from recent hurricanes.

• "Climate change creates hurricanes"
  Reality: Climate change doesn't CREATE hurricanes — they've always existed. But warmer oceans provide more FUEL for storms that do form, making them stronger and more likely to rapidly intensify.
  Strategy: Analogy: A bigger gas tank doesn't start the car, but it lets the engine run harder and longer.

FACILITATION TIPS:
• Step 1: Let students explore the interface. Don't over-explain.
  Let them discover. Circulate and support, don't lecture.
• Step 2: Ask "When this goes up, what happens to that?" to
  guide positive/negative relationship decisions. Let students debate.
• Step 3: Give time for students to "break" the model — turn
  things on/off and observe. This is where real insight happens.
• Step 4: Don't give answers. Ask questions. Let curiosity drive
  the research. Celebrate when students' additions don't work as
  expected — that's authentic science.

ANSWER KEY:
Big Question: Why are hurricanes getting stronger and more destructive?
Answer: Warmer ocean water provides more energy for hurricanes through evaporation and convection. As climate change heats the oceans, hurricanes have more fuel to intensify rapidly and produce devastating storm surges!

Simulation Answers:
• Supercharged Storm Scenario: At 90°F sea surface temperature, Hurricane Wind Speed rapidly increases as the abundant warm water provides massive energy through evaporation. Storm Surge Height climbs proportionally. The storm intensifies much faster than at lower temperatures.
• Cool Water Encounter: When temperature drops to 75°F, the hurricane's energy source is cut off. Wind speed decreases rapidly as the storm can no longer sustain itself. Storm surge drops accordingly. This is why hurricanes weaken over land — no warm water fuel.

Reflection Exemplars:
• Q: Why does rapid intensification make forecasting harder?
  A: If a storm can go from Category 1 to Category 5 in 24 hours, there may not be enough time to issue warnings and evacuate. Traditional forecasting assumes more gradual changes. Rapid intensification is essentially 'surprise strength' that catches communities off guard.
• Q: Should people build in hurricane zones?
  A: This is a complex question involving risk assessment, economics, and equity. Many coastal communities have existed for centuries. The key is building WITH hurricane awareness: elevated structures, storm-resistant designs, robust evacuation plans, and acknowledging that stronger storms are coming.

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format. Each item is three-dimensional, assessing a Science and Engineering Practice (SEP), Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and Crosscutting Concept (CCC) simultaneously, aligned to MS-ESS2-5 and MS-ESS2-6.

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.3 (Develop and use a model) + DCI ESS2.D.1 (Weather and climate are driven by interactions of energy) + CCC5 (Energy and Matter)

A student builds a model of hurricane formation. In the model, Sea Surface Temperature is an external component. When the student increases Sea Surface Temperature from 80 degrees Fahrenheit to 90 degrees Fahrenheit, Hurricane Wind Speed increases dramatically. When the student decreases Sea Surface Temperature below 79 degrees Fahrenheit, the hurricane weakens and eventually dissipates.

Based on the model, which statement best describes how hurricanes function as energy systems?

A. Hurricanes are random weather events with no predictable relationship to ocean temperature.
B. Hurricanes convert cold ocean water into wind energy through freezing processes.
C. Hurricanes are electrical generators that produce lightning from ocean salt and convert it to wind.
D. Hurricanes function as heat engines that convert thermal energy stored in warm ocean water into the kinetic energy of powerful winds through evaporation and convection.

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: The model demonstrates a clear energy conversion process. Warm ocean water evaporates, carrying thermal energy into the atmosphere. This energy drives convection (rising warm air, sinking cool air), which creates the rotating wind pattern of the hurricane. Warmer water means more evaporation and more thermal energy available to convert into wind. If you chose B, hurricanes require warm water, not cold water. No freezing occurs in hurricane formation. The process is driven by evaporation of warm water, not by cooling or ice formation. If you chose C, while hurricanes do produce lightning, the primary energy source is thermal energy from warm ocean water, not electrical energy from salt. Lightning is a byproduct, not the driving mechanism. If you chose A, the model shows a clear, predictable relationship: warmer water equals stronger storms. This is not random but follows the physics of energy transfer.
---

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 4.1 (Analyze data to provide evidence) + DCI ESS2.D.1 (Complex interactions of atmosphere and ocean) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A hurricane tracker records the following data as Hurricane Maria crosses a stretch of ocean. At Hour 0, the sea surface temperature is 90 degrees Fahrenheit and wind speed is 85 mph. At Hour 12, sea surface temperature is still 90 degrees Fahrenheit and wind speed has rapidly increased to 130 mph. At Hour 24, sea surface temperature has dropped to 78 degrees Fahrenheit as the storm moves over cooler water, and wind speed has dropped to 75 mph.

What does the rapid change from Hour 0 to Hour 12 demonstrate about the relationship between ocean temperature and hurricane behavior?

A. The rapid intensification was impossible and must be a measurement error.
B. Sustained warm ocean temperatures can fuel disproportionately rapid increases in storm intensity, making hurricanes harder to predict and more dangerous than gradual strengthening would suggest.
C. Wind speed always increases proportionally to time, regardless of ocean temperature.
D. Rapid intensification only occurs when two hurricanes merge together.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Between Hour 0 and Hour 12, the sea surface temperature remained constant at 90 degrees Fahrenheit, yet wind speed increased by 45 mph. This non-linear response shows that sustained exposure to very warm water can trigger rapid intensification, where the storm strengthens much faster than a simple proportional relationship would predict. The weakening over cooler water at Hour 24 confirms that ocean temperature is the controlling factor. If you chose A, rapid intensification is well-documented in real hurricanes. Hurricane Maria in 2017 intensified from Category 1 to Category 5 in less than 24 hours. If you chose C, the data directly contradicts proportional increase with time. Wind speed jumped 45 mph in the first 12 hours over warm water, then dropped 55 mph in the next 12 hours over cool water. Time alone does not predict wind speed. If you chose D, only one hurricane is described in this scenario. Rapid intensification occurs when a single storm has sustained access to very warm ocean water.

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 6.2 (Construct an explanation using evidence) + DCI ESS2.D.1 (Weather results from interactions of atmosphere and ocean) + CCC5 (Energy and Matter)

In the model's "Cool Water Encounter" scenario, a hurricane moves from ocean water at 90 degrees Fahrenheit to water at 75 degrees Fahrenheit. Within 12 hours, hurricane wind speed drops from 130 mph to 65 mph and the storm structure begins to break apart.

Which explanation best accounts for why the hurricane weakened rapidly over cooler water?

A. The hurricane lost its energy source because cooler water provides significantly less evaporation and therefore less thermal energy to drive the convection cycle that maintains wind speed.
B. Cool water created ice crystals inside the hurricane that blocked the rotation of the wind pattern.
C. The atmospheric moisture increased over cool water, making the storm too saturated to continue circulating.
D. Cool water added extra mass to the storm system, making it too heavy to sustain high wind speeds.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: Hurricanes depend on warm ocean water as their energy source. Evaporation from warm water carries thermal energy into the atmosphere, driving the convection cycle that creates and maintains the wind pattern. Over cooler water, evaporation decreases dramatically, cutting off the energy supply. Without sufficient energy input, the convection weakens and wind speeds drop. If you chose D, water does not add mass to the hurricane in a way that slows it. The issue is energy supply, not weight. Hurricanes contain enormous amounts of water already. If you chose B, ice crystals do not form in the lower atmosphere where hurricanes operate. Hurricane formation requires warm, tropical conditions, and the wind pattern is not blocked by ice. If you chose C, cooler water actually provides less atmospheric moisture (less evaporation), not more. The storm weakens from insufficient moisture and energy, not from excess.

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.3 (Use a model to predict) + DCI ESS2.C.1 (Patterns of atmospheric and oceanic circulation) + CCC7 (Stability and Change)

Climate data shows that average sea surface temperatures in hurricane formation zones have increased by 1.5 degrees Fahrenheit over the past 40 years. During the same period, the number of Category 4 and Category 5 hurricanes (the most powerful storms) has increased by approximately 25-30%. The model shows a positive relationship between Sea Surface Temperature and Hurricane Wind Speed, with a particularly strong effect above 86 degrees Fahrenheit.

Based on the model and this data, which prediction is best supported for the next 50 years if ocean temperatures continue to rise?

A. Ocean temperature will have no effect on long-term hurricane patterns because other weather factors are more important.
B. Hurricanes will become weaker because warmer air above the ocean will create more atmospheric stability.
C. Hurricanes will have access to more thermal energy, increasing the likelihood of stronger storms, higher storm surges, and more instances of rapid intensification.
D. Hurricane frequency will decrease because warm water prevents the temperature differences that help storms form.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: The model shows a positive relationship between ocean temperature and storm intensity, and the 40-year data confirms this relationship in the real world. Continued ocean warming would increase the thermal energy available to fuel hurricanes, supporting predictions of more powerful storms. Higher wind speeds also push more water onshore, increasing storm surge height. If you chose B, warmer air actually destabilizes the atmosphere by increasing convection, which is the mechanism that drives hurricanes. Atmospheric instability fuels storms rather than suppressing them. If you chose D, hurricanes form from warm water, not from temperature differences. Warmer ocean surfaces provide more energy for storm formation, not less. If you chose A, while multiple factors influence hurricanes, the data clearly shows a correlation between rising sea surface temperatures and increasing storm intensity over 40 years, and the model explains the physical mechanism behind this pattern.

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 7.1 (Engage in argument from evidence) + DCI ESS2.D.1 (Role of ocean in weather and climate) + CCC5 (Energy and Matter)

A city on the Gulf Coast experiences a hurricane that causes record storm surge flooding. City planners review the damage and discover that the storm surge was 18 feet, compared to an average of 10 feet for storms of similar wind speed 30 years ago. Sea levels in the area have risen 8 inches over the same period due to thermal expansion and ice melt.

A city council member argues that the increased storm surge is caused entirely by sea level rise. Using the model, which argument best evaluates this claim?

A. The historical comparison is invalid because storms 30 years ago were measured with different instruments.
B. Storm surge has nothing to do with sea level rise or wind speed. It is caused entirely by the gravitational pull of the moon during hurricanes.
C. The council member is correct. Sea level rise is the only factor that affects storm surge height.
D. Sea level rise contributes to higher storm surges by raising the baseline water level, but the model also shows that warmer ocean water produces stronger hurricane winds, which push more water onshore. The 8-foot increase in surge cannot be explained by 8 inches of sea level rise alone. Both factors, elevated baseline water level and stronger wind-driven surge, work together.

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: The math reveals that sea level rise (8 inches) accounts for only a small fraction of the 8-foot surge increase. The model shows that warmer ocean water increases wind speed, and stronger winds push more water onshore. Both factors contribute: the baseline water level is higher due to sea level rise, AND the wind-driven surge is stronger due to warmer water fueling more intense storms. If you chose C, while sea level rise does contribute, 8 inches of rise cannot explain an 8-foot increase in storm surge. The model identifies wind speed as a major driver of surge height, and warmer water increases wind speed. If you chose B, tidal forces from the moon do affect water levels, but storm surge is specifically caused by hurricane winds and low atmospheric pressure pushing ocean water onto shore. Gravity does not cause storm surge. If you chose A, while measurement methods have improved, the core measurement of water height above normal tide levels has been reliable for over a century using tide gauges.
