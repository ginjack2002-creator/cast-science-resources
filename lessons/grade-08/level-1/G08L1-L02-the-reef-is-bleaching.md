## Platform

### Title
**The Reef Is Bleaching — Why the World's Most Colorful Ecosystems Are Turning White**

### Overview
Coral reefs cover less than 1% of the ocean floor but support approximately 25% of all marine species. Often called 'rainforests of the sea,' they are among the most biodiverse and economically valuable ecosystems on Earth. Students investigate the driving question: Why are coral reefs turning white and dying? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

### NGSS Standard
**MS-LS2-1, MS-LS2-4**: Analyze and interpret data to provide evidence for the effects of resource availability on organisms and populations of organisms in an ecosystem. Construct an argument supported by empirical evidence that changes to physical or biological components of an ecosystem affect populations.

### Learning Objectives
- Explain the symbiotic relationship between coral and zooxanthellae algae
- Model how rising ocean temperature disrupts the coral reef ecosystem
- Predict cascade effects when one component of an ecosystem is disrupted
- Evaluate human impacts on marine ecosystems using model evidence

Coral reefs cover less than 1% of the ocean floor but support approximately 25% of all marine species. Often called 'rainforests of the sea,' they are among the most biodiverse and economically valuable ecosystems on Earth.

NGSS ALIGNMENT:

THREE-DIMENSIONAL LEARNING:

• Disciplinary Core Idea: LS2.C Ecosystem Dynamics, Functioning, and Resilience

• Crosscutting Concept: Stability and Change
  Students identify conditions that maintain reef stability vs. changes that push the system toward collapse.

• Step 2 (Establish): 8-10 minutes

LEVER FRAMEWORK:
• Locate: Students identify ocean temperature, ocean acidity, zooxanthellae population, and coral health as system components.
• Establish: Students determine that temperature and acidity both negatively affect zooxanthellae, which positively affects coral health.
• Visualize: Students build the coral reef model showing cascading effects from environmental stressors to ecosystem collapse.
• Evaluate: Students compare healthy reef, heat wave, and double threat scenarios.
• Revise: Students add fish population or algae overgrowth to model trophic cascades.

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• The Coral-Algae Symbiosis:
  Coral polyps have a mutualistic relationship with microscopic algae called zooxanthellae. The algae live inside coral tissue, photosynthesizing and providing up to 90% of the coral's energy. In return, coral provides shelter and nutrients. The algae also give coral its vibrant colors.

• The Bleaching Process:
  When water temperatures rise 1-2°C above the normal summer maximum for 4+ weeks, coral becomes stressed and expels its zooxanthellae. Without the algae, the coral's white skeleton shows through — hence 'bleaching.' Bleached coral isn't dead yet, but without its algae it starves and becomes vulnerable to disease.

• Ocean Acidification:
  The ocean absorbs about 30% of atmospheric CO2. When CO2 dissolves in seawater, it forms carbonic acid, lowering pH. More acidic water makes it harder for coral to build its calcium carbonate skeleton. This is sometimes called 'osteoporosis of the sea.'

• Global Reef Status:
  The Great Barrier Reef experienced mass bleaching events in 2016, 2017, 2020, 2022, and 2024. Scientists estimate that 50% of the world's coral reefs have already been lost since the 1950s. If current trends continue, 90% could be gone by 2050.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Coral is a plant or rock"
  Reality: Coral is an ANIMAL — specifically, colonies of tiny polyps related to jellyfish. They build calcium carbonate skeletons but are very much alive.
  Strategy: Show video of coral polyps feeding at night. Ask: Do rocks eat?

• "Bleaching means the coral is dead"
  Reality: Bleached coral is stressed and starving but not necessarily dead. If conditions improve within weeks, zooxanthellae can recolonize and the coral can recover. But prolonged bleaching leads to death.
  Strategy: Analogy: Bleaching is like a high fever — dangerous but potentially survivable if treated.

• "Climate change only affects land"
  Reality: The ocean absorbs 90% of excess heat from global warming and 30% of CO2. Marine ecosystems are being dramatically affected by rising temperatures and acidification.
  Strategy: Show ocean temperature data alongside atmospheric temperature data.

• Step 1: Let students explore the interface. Don't over-explain.
  Let them discover. Circulate and support, don't lecture.
• Step 2: Ask "When this goes up, what happens to that?" to
  guide positive/negative relationship decisions. Let students debate.
• Step 3: Give time for students to "break" the model — turn
  things on/off and observe. This is where real insight happens.
• Step 4: Don't give answers. Ask questions. Let curiosity drive
  the research. Celebrate when students' additions don't work as
  expected — that's authentic science.

ANSWER KEY:
Big Question: Why are coral reefs turning white and dying?
Answer: Rising ocean temperatures cause coral to expel the symbiotic algae they depend on for food, turning white (bleaching) and eventually starving to death!

Simulation Answers:
• Heat Wave Scenario: When Ocean Temperature increases +3°C, Zooxanthellae Population drops rapidly as coral expels its algae. Coral Health then crashes because it loses its primary food source. This is the bleaching cascade.
• Double Threat Scenario: When BOTH temperature and acidity increase, the effects are compounded. Zooxanthellae are expelled AND coral can't rebuild its skeleton. Recovery becomes nearly impossible because both energy supply and structural integrity are compromised.

Reflection Exemplars:
• Q: Why is it called bleaching?
  A: The vibrant colors of coral come from the zooxanthellae algae living inside them. When stress causes coral to expel these algae, the transparent coral tissue reveals the white calcium carbonate skeleton underneath. It looks 'bleached' but it's actually starving.
• Q: What can an 8th grader do?
  A: Reducing carbon footprint helps slow ocean warming and acidification. Supporting reef-safe sunscreen, reducing plastic waste, and advocating for marine protected areas all help. Understanding the science — like you're doing now — means you can explain the urgency to others.

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format. Each item is three-dimensional, assessing a Science and Engineering Practice (SEP), Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and Crosscutting Concept (CCC) simultaneously, aligned to MS-LS2-1 and MS-LS2-4.

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 4.1 (Analyze and interpret data) + DCI LS2.A.1 (Organisms depend on interactions with living and nonliving parts of their environment) + CCC4 (Systems and System Models)

Scientists monitor a coral reef ecosystem and record the following observations. In Year 1, ocean temperature is 26 degrees Celsius, zooxanthellae population is high, and coral health is excellent. In Year 2, ocean temperature rises to 28 degrees Celsius, zooxanthellae population drops to medium, and coral health declines to fair. In Year 3, ocean temperature reaches 30 degrees Celsius, zooxanthellae population is critically low, and coral health is poor with visible bleaching across 70% of the reef.

Based on this data, which statement best describes the relationship between ocean temperature and coral health?

A. Rising ocean temperature causes coral to expel zooxanthellae, which disrupts the symbiotic food supply that coral depends on for survival, leading to declining health.
B. Warmer water causes coral to grow faster, which uses up zooxanthellae and leads to temporary bleaching.
C. Ocean temperature affects only zooxanthellae, and coral health declined for unrelated reasons during the same time period.
D. Ocean temperature directly kills coral by dissolving its skeleton, so warmer water means weaker coral structures.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: The data shows a cascading sequence: temperature rise leads to zooxanthellae loss, which leads to coral health decline. Coral depends on zooxanthellae for up to 90% of its energy through a mutualistic symbiosis. When heat stress causes coral to expel these algae, the coral loses its primary food source and bleaches. If you chose D, ocean temperature does not dissolve coral skeletons. That process is caused by acidification, not warming. The data shows zooxanthellae declining first, indicating an indirect pathway. If you chose B, warmer water does not accelerate coral growth. The zooxanthellae are being expelled due to heat stress, not consumed by fast-growing coral. If you chose C, the year-by-year correlation between all three variables shows a connected system, not coincidental changes.

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.3 (Develop and use a model to describe phenomena) + DCI LS2.C.1 (Disruptions to any component of an ecosystem can lead to shifts in populations) + CCC4 (Systems and System Models)

A student builds a model of a coral reef ecosystem with Ocean Temperature and Ocean Acidity as external components and Zooxanthellae Population and Coral Health as internal components. The model shows that increasing temperature causes zooxanthellae to decline (negative relationship) and increasing acidity weakens coral skeletons (negative relationship). In the "Double Threat" scenario, both temperature and acidity increase simultaneously, and coral health drops far below what either stressor causes alone.

Which explanation best accounts for why the combined stressors cause worse damage than either one alone?

A. Acidic water directly kills all zooxanthellae, so both stressors target the same pathway.
B. The two stressors cancel each other out chemically but produce a new toxic compound that poisons the coral.
C. The model exaggerates the combined effect because in nature these two factors never occur together.
D. Temperature causes coral to lose its energy source (zooxanthellae expelled), while acidity weakens its physical structure (skeleton dissolves). These simultaneous attacks through different pathways overwhelm the coral's ability to recover.

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: The model reveals two distinct pathways of harm. Temperature disrupts the energy supply by causing bleaching (zooxanthellae loss). Acidity disrupts the structural integrity by dissolving the calcium carbonate skeleton. When both pathways are attacked simultaneously, the coral cannot recover from energy loss while also losing its physical framework. If you chose A, acidity affects the coral skeleton, not zooxanthellae directly. The two stressors work through different components of the system. If you chose B, there is no new toxic compound formed. The combined damage results from two independent pathways being disrupted at the same time. If you chose C, rising CO2 drives both ocean warming and acidification simultaneously, so these stressors co-occur in nature as a direct consequence of climate change.

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 6.2 (Construct an explanation using evidence) + DCI LS2.A.3 (Growth of organisms is affected by interactions with other organisms) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A marine biologist observes that a reef section bleached during a summer heat wave but recovered six months later when water temperatures returned to normal. Nearby, another reef section experienced the same heat wave plus increased acidification from coastal runoff. That section did not recover, even after temperatures normalized.

Using the model of coral reef systems, which explanation best accounts for the difference in recovery between the two reef sections?

A. The second reef failed to recover because it had fewer fish species, and fish are needed to feed coral during bleaching events.
B. The first reef's zooxanthellae were able to recolonize coral tissue once temperatures dropped, restoring the energy supply. The second reef lost both its energy source (bleaching) and its skeletal structure (acidification), so even when temperatures normalized, the physical framework for recovery no longer existed.
C. Ocean currents carried healthy zooxanthellae away from the second reef but not the first.
D. The first reef was younger and therefore more resistant to bleaching than the older reef.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Bleached coral is stressed but not necessarily dead. If conditions return to normal quickly enough, zooxanthellae can recolonize the coral tissue and restore the mutualistic energy supply. However, the second reef suffered both bleaching and skeletal damage from acidification. Without intact skeletal structure, there is no framework for zooxanthellae to recolonize, making recovery impossible even after temperature normalizes. If you chose D, the age of the reef was not mentioned in the observations. The key difference is the presence of acidification, not reef age. If you chose A, fish do not feed coral. Coral gets energy from zooxanthellae through photosynthesis. Fish diversity is a result of reef health, not a cause of coral feeding. If you chose C, zooxanthellae recolonize from remaining populations in the water and on nearby coral. Currents were not identified as different between the two reef sections.

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 7.1 (Construct an argument supported by evidence) + DCI LS2.C.1 (Changes to physical components of an ecosystem affect populations) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A coastal city proposes three plans to protect a nearby coral reef. Plan A involves building shade structures over the reef to block sunlight. Plan B involves reducing industrial CO2 emissions in the region. Plan C involves introducing heat-resistant zooxanthellae species bred in a laboratory. A scientist reviews the model showing that CO2 drives both ocean warming and ocean acidification, and that both of these external factors damage coral through different pathways.

Based on the model, which plan addresses the root cause of reef decline most effectively?

A. Plan C, because heat-resistant zooxanthellae will make the reef immune to all future temperature increases.
B. Plan A, because blocking sunlight will cool the water around the reef and stop bleaching.
C. Plan B, because reducing CO2 emissions slows both ocean warming and acidification, addressing the root cause of both external stressors in the model.
D. All three plans are equally effective because they all reduce temperature stress on coral.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: The model shows that CO2 is the upstream driver of both external stressors (ocean warming and acidification). Reducing CO2 emissions addresses the root cause, slowing both warming and acidification simultaneously. This is the only plan that targets the source of both pathways of damage. If you chose B, shade structures would provide local cooling but would also block sunlight needed for zooxanthellae photosynthesis, potentially harming the very organisms coral depends on for energy. This plan does not address acidification. If you chose A, heat-resistant zooxanthellae could help with bleaching from temperature stress but would not protect the coral skeleton from acidification damage. It addresses one pathway but not both. If you chose D, the three plans are not equally effective. Only Plan B targets the root cause of both stressors, while the others address symptoms or single pathways.

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 4.1 (Analyze data to provide evidence) + DCI LS2.A.1 (Resource availability affects organisms in ecosystems) + CCC7 (Stability and Change)

Scientists track two coral reef systems over 20 years. Reef A is in a marine protected area with strict pollution controls. Reef B is near an unregulated coastal city. Both reefs experience the same ocean temperature increases over the period. After 20 years, Reef A shows 30% bleaching and moderate recovery during cool periods. Reef B shows 80% bleaching with almost no recovery.

What does this comparison best demonstrate about the stability of coral reef ecosystems?

A. The 20-year period was too short to draw any conclusions about long-term reef stability.
B. Marine protected areas prevent ocean temperatures from rising, which is why Reef A bleached less.
C. Reef B bleached more because it had naturally weaker coral species from the beginning.
D. Multiple stressors reduce a reef's ability to recover. Reef B likely experienced additional stressors (pollution, runoff, overfishing) that combined with temperature stress to overwhelm the system's ability to maintain stability.

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: Both reefs experienced the same temperature increase, yet outcomes differed dramatically. The key variable is the additional stressors present at Reef B. Pollution, runoff, and overfishing weaken the reef system's resilience, making it less able to absorb and recover from temperature stress. Reef A's protected status reduced these additional stressors, allowing the ecosystem to maintain greater stability despite the same warming. If you chose B, marine protected areas do not control ocean temperature. Both reefs experienced identical warming. The difference is in the additional stressors, not temperature. If you chose C, no information suggests inherently weaker coral species at Reef B. The comparison is designed to isolate the effect of additional human-caused stressors. If you chose A, 20 years of data with clear divergent trends provides meaningful evidence about ecosystem stability and resilience.
