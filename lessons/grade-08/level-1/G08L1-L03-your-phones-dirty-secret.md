## Platform

### Title
**Your Phone's Dirty Secret — The Hidden Environmental Cost of the Device in Your Pocket**

### Overview
The average smartphone contains over 30 different elements, many classified as rare earth minerals. The environmental cost of extracting, processing, manufacturing, and disposing of these devices is enormous — and growing as global demand increases. Students investigate the driving question: What is the true environmental cost of the phone in your pocket? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

### Learning Objectives
- Trace the lifecycle of a smartphone from raw materials to e-waste
- Model how increased demand for rare earth minerals affects Earth's systems
- Evaluate the environmental trade-offs of technology consumption
- Design solutions to minimize the environmental impact of electronics

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• What's Inside Your Phone:
  A typical smartphone contains: lithium (battery), cobalt (battery), gold and silver (circuit connections), rare earth elements like neodymium (speakers/vibration motor), indium (touchscreen), tantalum from coltan (capacitors), and silicon (processor). Many of these are mined in environmentally sensitive areas.

• The Mining Problem:
  Rare earth mineral mining is extremely destructive. It involves stripping large areas of land, using toxic chemicals to extract minerals, generating radioactive waste, and contaminating water supplies. The Democratic Republic of Congo produces 60% of the world's cobalt, often using child labor in dangerous conditions.

• The E-Waste Crisis:
  The world generates approximately 50 million tons of e-waste per year. Only about 20% is formally recycled. The rest ends up in landfills or is shipped to developing countries where it's dismantled by hand, exposing workers to lead, mercury, cadmium, and other toxins.

• Solutions and Alternatives:
  Strategies include: extending device lifespan (repair instead of replace), improving recycling technology, designing devices for disassembly, using recycled materials in new devices, and developing alternative materials. The 'circular economy' model aims to eliminate waste entirely.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Recycling solves the e-waste problem"
  Reality: Only 20% of e-waste is properly recycled. Even recycled electronics lose material in the process. REDUCING consumption (keeping devices longer) is far more effective than recycling alone.
  Strategy: Show the 'reduce, reuse, recycle' hierarchy — reduce is FIRST for a reason.

• "My phone doesn't affect the environment"
  Reality: Every phone requires mining tons of rock, uses energy from fossil fuels to manufacture, and generates toxic waste at end of life. Multiply by 1.5 billion phones sold per year and the impact is massive.
  Strategy: Calculate: If each phone requires 75 lbs of raw materials, how much is that for all phones sold in a year?

• "Technology is always progress"
  Reality: Technology provides amazing benefits, but it also has environmental costs that we need to account for. The goal isn't to stop using technology, but to use it more responsibly and demand sustainable practices.
  Strategy: Discuss: What's the difference between progress and unsustainable consumption?

FACILITATION TIPS:
• Step 1: Let students explore the interface. Don't over-explain.

• Step 2: Ask "When this goes up, what happens to that?" to

ANSWER KEY:
Big Question: What is the true environmental cost of the phone in your pocket?
Answer: Every phone requires mining dozens of minerals from the Earth, creating environmental destruction. When billions of phones are discarded, the toxic e-waste accumulates faster than we can recycle it!

Simulation Answers:
• Maximum Consumption Scenario: When Consumer Demand is maximized, Mining Intensity increases dramatically, driving Environmental Damage to critical levels. E-Waste Accumulation accelerates as phones are discarded annually. The system is clearly unsustainable.
• Reduced Demand Scenario: When Consumer Demand is cut by 50% (keeping phones twice as long), all downstream effects decrease significantly. This shows that reducing consumption has a larger impact than any other single intervention.

Reflection Exemplars:
• Q: Why is only 20% of e-waste recycled?
  A: Recycling electronics is expensive and complex — a single phone contains 30+ different materials bonded together. Many countries lack the technology. It's often cheaper to mine new materials than to recover them from old devices. And many people don't know how to properly dispose of electronics.
• Q: Can we have tech without destruction?
  A: A circular economy approach could dramatically reduce impact: design phones for easy repair and disassembly, use recycled materials, extend product lifespans, and ensure 100% recycling. Companies like Fairphone are already attempting this, but the economics need to change.

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format. Each item is three-dimensional, assessing a Science and Engineering Practice (SEP), Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and Crosscutting Concept (CCC) simultaneously, aligned to MS-ESS3-3 and MS-ESS3-4.

---

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 7.1 (Construct an argument supported by evidence) + DCI ESS3.C.1 (Human activities affect Earth's systems) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A student examines a model of the smartphone lifecycle. The model shows that Consumer Demand drives Mining Intensity (positive relationship), Mining Intensity drives Environmental Damage (positive relationship), and at the end of the lifecycle, discarded phones increase E-Waste Accumulation (positive relationship). When Consumer Demand is set to maximum, both Environmental Damage and E-Waste Accumulation rise significantly.

What does this chain of relationships demonstrate about the connection between human behavior and Earth's systems?

A. Human consumption patterns have direct, measurable impacts on Earth's systems through a chain of cause-and-effect relationships linking demand to extraction to environmental damage.
B. Mining has no significant environmental effects if companies follow proper regulations.
C. Environmental damage occurs only in the countries where minerals are mined, not where phones are used.
D. Technology development always improves the environment because newer phones use fewer materials.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: The model traces a clear causal chain from human behavior (consumer demand) through industrial processes (mining) to environmental outcomes (damage and waste). Each link shows a positive relationship, meaning increased demand amplifies impacts at every stage. If you chose D, the model shows that higher demand increases environmental damage, not that newer technology reduces it. Even if phones become more efficient, growing total demand can still increase total environmental impact. If you chose B, the model shows that environmental damage increases with mining intensity regardless of regulation. Mining inherently disturbs land, uses water, and generates emissions. If you chose C, environmental damage from mining affects the extraction site, but e-waste and carbon emissions affect the global environment. The impacts are not confined to one location.
---

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 5.1 (Use mathematics to support explanations) + DCI ESS3.A.1 (Humans depend on Earth's resources and alter them) + CCC3 (Scale, Proportion, and Quantity)

A single smartphone contains over 30 different minerals and weighs about 175 grams. In 2023, approximately 1.4 billion smartphones were produced worldwide. A student argues that smartphones have no significant environmental impact because each individual phone is small and lightweight.

Using evidence about scale, which response best refutes this argument?

A. Phone production affects only the air through factory emissions, not land or water systems.
B. While each phone is small, multiplying 30+ mined minerals across 1.4 billion phones annually creates massive cumulative impacts. The total mineral extraction, energy use for manufacturing, and eventual e-waste are substantial when considered at global production scale.
C. The environmental impact comes entirely from packaging materials, not the phone components themselves.
D. The student is correct because small devices require very few materials per unit.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Scale transforms individual small impacts into massive cumulative ones. Each phone requires mining in multiple countries, energy-intensive manufacturing, global shipping, and eventually becomes e-waste containing toxic materials. At 1.4 billion units per year, even tiny per-unit impacts become globally significant. If you chose D, the argument fails at scale. A single phone uses small amounts of many minerals, but billions of phones require enormous total extraction from Earth's systems. If you chose A, smartphone production affects land (mining creates open pits, removes topsoil), water (mining runoff contaminates rivers, manufacturing uses water), and air (factory emissions, shipping). All Earth systems are impacted. If you chose C, the phone itself contains far more environmentally impactful materials than its packaging. The rare earth minerals, metals, and electronics inside the device require far more resource extraction than cardboard or plastic packaging.
---

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.3 (Develop a model to predict) + DCI ESS3.C.1 (Monitor and minimize human impact) + CCC4 (Systems and System Models)

A student runs two scenarios in the smartphone lifecycle model. In Scenario 1, Consumer Demand is reduced by 50% while recycling stays at current levels. In Scenario 2, Consumer Demand stays at current levels while recycling rates are doubled. The results show that Scenario 1 (reduced demand) decreased Environmental Damage by 45%, while Scenario 2 (doubled recycling) decreased Environmental Damage by only 20%.

What is the best scientific explanation for why reducing demand had a larger positive impact than increasing recycling?

A. Recycling creates more pollution than manufacturing new devices from raw materials.
B. Consumers are unable to reduce their demand for electronics, so this scenario is unrealistic.
C. Recycling is worthless and provides no environmental benefits at all.
D. Reducing demand prevents environmental damage from mining and manufacturing at the source, while recycling can only recover some materials after the damage from extraction and production has already occurred.

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: Reducing demand is a source-level intervention. When fewer phones are demanded, less mining occurs, less energy is consumed in manufacturing, and less transportation is needed. These impacts are prevented entirely. Recycling is valuable but occurs at the end of the lifecycle, after extraction, manufacturing, and transportation damage have already happened. It can recover some materials but cannot undo prior damage. If you chose C, the model shows that recycling did reduce Environmental Damage by 20%, proving it provides real benefits. It is simply less effective than demand reduction. If you chose B, the question asks for a scientific explanation of the model results, not whether the scenario is realistic. Many countries have demonstrated that policy changes can reduce per-capita consumption. If you chose A, recycling does require energy and can produce some pollution, but it produces significantly less environmental damage than mining and manufacturing from raw materials.

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 6.5 (Apply scientific principles to design solutions) + DCI ESS3.A.1 (Uneven distribution and sustainability of resources) + CCC7 (Stability and Change)

An electronics company wants to create a more sustainable product lifecycle. Their current model shows: high Consumer Demand leads to high Mining Intensity, which leads to high Environmental Damage, and short device lifespan leads to high E-Waste Accumulation. The company can modify design, manufacturing, and end-of-life practices but cannot control consumer purchasing decisions.

Based on the model, which approach would create the most sustainable lifecycle?

A. A circular economy approach: extend device lifespan through durable design, make components easy to disassemble for recycling, and recover rare minerals from old devices to reduce the need for new mining.
B. Ship all e-waste to developing countries to avoid domestic landfill problems.
C. Stop using all rare earth minerals and build phones from wood and biodegradable plastics.
D. Design devices to be replaced every six months so consumers always have the newest features.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: A circular economy addresses multiple points in the model. Extending device lifespan reduces the rate of Consumer Demand (fewer new purchases needed). Designing for disassembly makes recycling more effective at recovering materials. Recovering minerals from old devices reduces Mining Intensity by providing an alternative material source. Together, these interventions reduce both Environmental Damage and E-Waste Accumulation. If you chose D, faster replacement cycles would increase both Consumer Demand and E-Waste Accumulation, worsening every negative outcome in the model. If you chose B, moving e-waste to another location does not reduce its environmental impact. It shifts the harm to communities with fewer protections, and the toxic materials still damage Earth's systems. If you chose C, current technology cannot function without rare earth minerals. Phones require specific electrical, magnetic, and optical properties that only these materials provide.
---

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 7.1 (Construct an argument from evidence) + DCI ESS3.C.1 (Increases in per-capita consumption impact Earth's systems) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

Global smartphone production has increased from 300 million units in 2010 to 1.4 billion units in 2023. During the same period, the average smartphone lifespan decreased from 4.5 years to 2.5 years. Meanwhile, only about 20% of discarded electronics are properly recycled worldwide.

Using this data, which argument best explains why e-waste is one of the fastest-growing waste categories on Earth?

A. E-waste growth is caused entirely by manufacturing defects that make devices stop working after a few months.
B. The data shows that e-waste growth has slowed because smartphones are becoming smaller and lighter over time.
C. Production volume has increased nearly fivefold while device lifespan has nearly halved, meaning more devices enter the waste stream faster. With only 20% recycling, the remaining 80% accumulates as e-waste, creating an accelerating cycle of consumption and disposal.
D. E-waste is growing because recycling technology has gotten worse over time and can no longer process electronic materials.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: Three factors compound to accelerate e-waste growth. Production volume increased (more devices entering the system), device lifespan decreased (each device reaches end-of-life sooner), and recycling rates remain low (most discarded devices are not recovered). Together, these factors create an accelerating flow of electronics into the waste stream. If you chose D, recycling technology has actually improved over the past decade. The problem is that recycling rates have not kept pace with production growth and shortened device lifespans. If you chose A, while some devices do fail, the primary driver is planned obsolescence and consumer demand for upgrades, not manufacturing defects. The data shows a systemic pattern across the entire industry. If you chose B, smaller and lighter devices still contain toxic materials and rare minerals. The total weight of e-waste continues to grow because the number of devices produced far exceeds any per-unit weight reduction.
