## Platform

### Title
**The Concussion Problem — Why Helmets Can't Stop Your Brain from Slamming Into Your Skull**

### Overview
Concussions are one of the most significant health concerns in contact sports. Understanding the physics of collisions — force, impulse, and deceleration — is essential for designing better protective equipment and safer sports rules. Students investigate the driving question: Why do football players still get concussions even while wearing helmets? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

### NGSS Standard
**MS-PS2-1, MS-PS2-2**: Apply Newton's Third Law to design a solution to a problem involving the motion of two colliding objects. Plan an investigation to provide evidence that the change in an object's motion depends on the sum of the forces acting on the object and the mass of the object.

### Learning Objectives
- Apply Newton's Laws of Motion to explain what happens during a collision
- Model how force, mass, and acceleration are related in impact scenarios
- Predict how changing collision speed or mass affects the force of impact
- Evaluate helmet designs based on force reduction principles

LESSON PURPOSE:
Concussions are one of the most significant health concerns in contact sports. Understanding the physics of collisions — force, impulse, and deceleration — is essential for designing better protective equipment and safer sports rules.

NGSS ALIGNMENT:

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• Newton's Laws in Collisions:
  First Law (Inertia): The brain tends to keep moving when the skull suddenly stops. Second Law (F=ma): Force equals mass times acceleration — more speed means more deceleration force. Third Law: During a collision, both objects experience equal and opposite forces, regardless of size difference.

• The Concussion Mechanism:
  The brain floats in cerebrospinal fluid inside the skull. During a sudden deceleration, the brain continues moving forward and impacts the inside of the skull. This causes bruising, stretching of nerve fibers, and chemical changes that constitute a concussion. The brain can also bounce and hit the back of the skull (coup-contrecoup injury).

• Impulse and Helmets:
  Impulse = Force × Time. A given change in momentum can happen with a BIG force over a SHORT time, or a SMALLER force over a LONGER time. Helmets work by crushing or deforming, which EXTENDS the collision time. This reduces the peak force. It's the same physics behind crumple zones in cars and air bags.

• The Speed Problem:
  Kinetic energy = ½mv². Because speed is SQUARED, doubling speed QUADRUPLES the kinetic energy that must be absorbed during the collision. A player running at 20 mph has 4x the kinetic energy of one running at 10 mph. This is why high-speed collisions are so much more dangerous.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Helmets prevent concussions"
  Reality: Helmets reduce skull fractures and some force, but CANNOT prevent concussions because the brain still decelerates inside the skull. No amount of external padding can stop internal brain movement.
  Strategy: Demo: Shake a ball in a jar of water. The jar stops but the ball keeps moving. That's your brain.

• "Bigger players get fewer concussions"
  Reality: Bigger players generate and receive LARGER forces due to greater mass and momentum. While a bigger neck may help stabilize the head, overall concussion risk isn't lower for larger players.
  Strategy: Show that F=ma means more mass = more force at the same speed.

• "Speed and force are directly proportional"
  Reality: Force in a collision relates to KINETIC ENERGY, which scales with speed SQUARED. Doubling speed quadruples the force, not just doubles it. This non-linear relationship is why high-speed collisions are so much more dangerous.
  Strategy: Graph KE vs. speed: 5 mph = 25 units, 10 mph = 100 units, 20 mph = 400 units.

• Step 2: Ask "When this goes up, what happens to that?" to
  guide positive/negative relationship decisions. Let students debate.
• Step 3: Give time for students to "break" the model — turn
  things on/off and observe. This is where real insight happens.
• Step 4: Don't give answers. Ask questions. Let curiosity drive
  the research. Celebrate when students' additions don't work as
  expected — that's authentic science.

ANSWER KEY:
Big Question: Why do football players still get concussions even while wearing helmets?
Answer: During a collision, the skull stops suddenly but the brain keeps moving (Newton's First Law), slamming into the skull. Helmets extend the collision time to reduce force, but can't prevent the brain from decelerating inside the skull!

Simulation Answers:
• Full Sprint Scenario: At 20 mph, Impact Force is dramatically higher than at 5 mph — roughly 16 times higher because kinetic energy scales with speed squared. Brain Acceleration reaches dangerous levels. This shows why high-speed collisions are so much more dangerous.
• Heavy Player Scenario: Doubling mass at the same speed doubles the Impact Force. However, the relationship between speed and force is much steeper than between mass and force. Reducing speed is more effective than reducing mass for preventing concussions.

Reflection Exemplars:
• Q: Why can't helmets prevent concussions?
  A: A helmet can reduce the force on the skull by extending the collision time (impulse). But the brain is floating INSIDE the skull. When the skull suddenly decelerates, the brain keeps moving (Newton's First Law) and impacts the skull wall. No external padding can prevent this internal deceleration.
• Q: Why is doubling speed worse than doubling mass?
  A: Kinetic energy = ½mv². Speed is SQUARED, so doubling speed quadruples kinetic energy. Doubling mass only doubles KE. In a collision at 20 mph vs. 10 mph, the force isn't just twice as bad — it's roughly 4 times worse. This is why speed reduction rules are more effective than weight limits.

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 6.2 (Construct an explanation using evidence) + DCI PS2.A.1 (Newton's Third Law: forces come in pairs) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A student runs a model showing that during a football collision at 20 mph, even with a helmet, Brain Acceleration remains significant. The model tracks four components: Collision Speed and Player Mass (external), and Impact Force and Brain Acceleration (internal). The helmet reduces Impact Force by extending collision time, but Brain Acceleration stays high.

Using Newton's Laws, which explanation best accounts for why helmets cannot eliminate concussions?

A. The brain floats in cerebrospinal fluid inside the skull. During a sudden stop, the skull decelerates but the brain continues moving forward due to inertia (Newton's First Law). No external helmet can prevent the brain from shifting inside the skull.
B. Helmets make players overconfident and cause them to hit harder, which is the real cause of concussions.
C. Helmets are not made of strong enough materials to withstand high-speed impacts.
D. Concussions are caused by damage to the neck, not brain movement, so helmets protect the wrong body part.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: The fundamental physics problem is inertia. Newton's First Law states that an object in motion stays in motion unless acted upon by a force. The brain, floating in fluid inside the skull, continues moving when the skull suddenly stops during impact. The brain then strikes the inside of the skull. Helmets protect the skull and reduce Impact Force, but they cannot prevent the brain's inertial motion inside the skull. If you chose C, modern helmets are engineered to withstand high impacts. The issue is not material strength but the physics of internal brain motion that no external device can prevent. If you chose B, while risk compensation is a real behavioral phenomenon, the model's physics show that even without behavioral changes, brain acceleration remains significant due to inertia. The cause is physics, not psychology. If you chose D, concussions are specifically brain injuries caused by the brain moving inside the skull, not neck injuries. The model tracks Brain Acceleration as the relevant internal component.

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 5.1 (Use mathematics to support explanations) + DCI PS2.A.3 (Larger forces cause larger changes in motion) + CCC3 (Scale, Proportion, and Quantity)

In the model, a helmet extends the collision time from 5 milliseconds (no helmet) to 50 milliseconds (with helmet). The total momentum change remains the same in both cases because the player goes from 20 mph to 0 mph either way.

Using the impulse relationship (Impulse = Force x Time), what happens to the peak force when collision time is extended from 5 ms to 50 ms?

A. Peak force is reduced to approximately one-tenth because the same momentum change is distributed over 10 times longer, so less force is needed at each instant.
B. Peak force stays exactly the same because the total momentum change is conserved regardless of time.
C. Peak force is completely eliminated because 50 milliseconds is long enough for the collision to become harmless.
D. Peak force increases tenfold because the collision lasts longer and delivers more total force.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: Impulse equals Force times Time, and the total impulse (momentum change) is fixed. If time increases by a factor of 10 (from 5 ms to 50 ms), force must decrease by a factor of 10 to maintain the same impulse. This is exactly how padding works in all protective equipment. Spreading the same momentum change over more time requires less force at each moment. If you chose D, extending time does not increase total force. The total impulse (momentum change) is fixed. A longer collision means the same total impulse delivered at lower peak force, not higher. If you chose B, total momentum change (impulse) is conserved, but peak force is not. The force at each instant decreases when the same impulse is spread over more time. Conservation applies to momentum, not to instantaneous force. If you chose C, while reducing peak force by 90% is significant, it does not eliminate force entirely. The brain still decelerates, and at high speeds, even the reduced force can cause injury.

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 4.1 (Analyze data) + DCI PS2.A.2 (Change in motion depends on sum of forces and mass) + CCC3 (Scale, Proportion, and Quantity)

A student runs two comparisons in the model. In Comparison 1, Player Mass is doubled (from 80 kg to 160 kg) at a constant speed of 15 mph, and Impact Force increases by 100%. In Comparison 2, Collision Speed is doubled (from 15 mph to 30 mph) at constant mass of 80 kg, and Impact Force increases by 300%.

What does this comparison reveal about the relative effects of mass and speed on collision force?

A. The model must contain an error because mass and speed should produce identical changes in impact force.
B. Mass and speed contribute equally to impact force in all collisions.
C. Mass has no effect on impact force; only speed matters in determining collision severity.
D. Speed has a greater effect on impact force than mass because kinetic energy depends on velocity squared (v squared) but only linearly on mass, so doubling speed quadruples kinetic energy while doubling mass only doubles it.

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: The kinetic energy equation KE = 1/2 mv squared reveals why speed has a disproportionate effect. Mass appears as a linear factor (doubling mass doubles KE). Speed appears as a squared factor (doubling speed quadruples KE). Since the kinetic energy must be absorbed during the collision, quadrupled energy produces more than double the impact force. This is why speed is the most dangerous variable in collisions. If you chose B, the mathematical relationship shows they are not equal. The v squared term makes speed disproportionately more influential than mass. If you chose C, the data shows that doubling mass did increase impact force by 100%. Mass clearly has an effect, just a smaller one than speed due to the mathematical relationship. If you chose A, the data is consistent with the kinetic energy equation. The asymmetry between mass and speed effects is predicted by physics, not an error.

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 6.5 (Apply Newton's Third Law to design a solution) + DCI PS2.A.1 (Forces in collisions) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A sports equipment company analyzes the model data and wants to design a helmet that better reduces concussion risk. The model shows that extending collision time reduces peak force, and that peak force is the primary driver of Brain Acceleration.

Which design principle is best supported by the model evidence?

A. Make the helmet shell as hard and rigid as possible to completely block all incoming force from reaching the skull.
B. Use multi-layered materials that compress slowly and progressively during impact, maximizing the time over which deceleration occurs and thereby reducing peak force transmitted to the brain.
C. Add a pointed outer surface to deflect opponents and prevent direct impacts entirely.
D. Make the helmet as light as possible to minimize the total mass involved in the collision.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: The model demonstrates the impulse principle: spreading the same momentum change over more time reduces peak force. Materials that compress progressively extend the deceleration time. Each layer absorbs energy over a longer period, reducing the instantaneous force peak. This directly reduces Brain Acceleration, the component most closely linked to concussion. If you chose A, a perfectly rigid helmet would transfer force instantaneously with zero compression time, maximizing peak force rather than reducing it. Rigidity is the opposite of what the impulse principle recommends. If you chose D, while a lighter helmet is more comfortable, the model shows that collision time (not helmet mass) is the key factor in reducing peak force. A light but rigid helmet would perform worse than a heavier but compressible one. If you chose C, a pointed helmet could increase the severity of injury to other players and would not address the physics of brain deceleration inside the wearer's skull.

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 7.1 (Engage in argument from evidence) + DCI PS2.A.2 (Forces and acceleration) + CCC4 (Systems and System Models)

A school board reviews data showing that youth football players (ages 12-14) experience an average of 240 head impacts per season. A parent argues that the impacts are safe because the players move slower than professional athletes. The model shows that Brain Acceleration at 10 mph still reaches levels associated with mild concussion, and that repeated sub-concussive impacts accumulate damage over time.

Using the model evidence, which argument best evaluates the parent's claim?

A. The data is irrelevant because concussions only occur in single catastrophic impacts, not from accumulated smaller impacts.
B. The parent is correct. Slower speeds mean zero risk of brain injury at the youth level.
C. While individual impacts at lower speeds produce less force than professional-level collisions, the model shows that even 10 mph impacts cause measurable Brain Acceleration. Combined with 240 impacts per season and developing brains that are more vulnerable to damage, the cumulative effect creates significant concussion risk despite lower speeds.
D. Youth players should simply wear better helmets, which would eliminate all risk regardless of impact frequency.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: The parent's argument focuses only on individual impact severity. The model reveals two critical factors the argument misses. First, even lower-speed impacts produce Brain Acceleration above safe thresholds. Second, 240 impacts per season means cumulative exposure. Additionally, adolescent brains (ages 12-14) are still developing and are more susceptible to damage from repeated impacts. If you chose B, the model directly contradicts this claim by showing measurable Brain Acceleration at 10 mph. Lower speed reduces risk per impact but does not eliminate it, especially with high-frequency repeated exposure. If you chose D, the model shows that helmets reduce peak force but cannot eliminate Brain Acceleration due to inertia (Newton's First Law). Better helmets reduce risk but cannot eliminate it, especially with 240 repeated impacts. If you chose A, medical research and the model both show that repeated sub-concussive impacts accumulate damage to brain tissue over time. Concussion risk is not limited to single catastrophic events.
