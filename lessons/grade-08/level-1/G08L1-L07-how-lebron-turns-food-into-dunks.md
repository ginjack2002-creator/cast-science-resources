## Platform

### Title
**How LeBron Turns Food Into Dunks — The Cellular Science Behind Athletic Performance**

### Overview
Every movement you make — every heartbeat, every thought, every slam dunk — is powered by ATP, a molecule produced through cellular respiration. Understanding this process reveals how food literally becomes action. Students investigate the driving question: How does a chicken sandwich become a slam dunk? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

### NGSS Standard
**MS-LS1-5, MS-LS1-7**: Construct a scientific explanation based on evidence for how environmental and genetic factors influence the growth of organisms. ### Learning Objectives
- Trace the path of food molecules from digestion through cellular respiration
- Model how cells convert glucose and oxygen into ATP energy for movement
- Predict how exercise intensity affects energy demand and oxygen consumption
- Evaluate how nutrition choices affect athletic performance

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• From Food to Glucose:
  When you eat, digestion breaks complex food molecules into simpler ones. Carbohydrates are broken down into glucose (C6H12O6). This glucose enters the bloodstream and is delivered to every cell in your body. An average person's body contains about 4 grams of glucose in their blood at any time.

• Cellular Respiration:
  The equation: C6H12O6 + 6O2 → 6CO2 + 6H2O + ~36 ATP. This happens in the mitochondria of every cell. The process has three stages: glycolysis (splitting glucose), the Krebs cycle (extracting electrons), and the electron transport chain (making most of the ATP).

• ATP: The Energy Currency:
  ATP is like cash for your cells. Every muscle contraction, nerve impulse, and chemical reaction is paid for with ATP. Your body produces and uses about 40 kg of ATP per day — roughly your body weight! But you only have about 250 grams at any given time, so it's constantly being recycled.

• Aerobic vs. Anaerobic:
  With oxygen (aerobic): 1 glucose → ~36 ATP (efficient). Without enough oxygen (anaerobic): 1 glucose → 2 ATP + lactic acid (inefficient). During intense exercise, muscles outpace oxygen delivery and switch to anaerobic respiration. The lactic acid buildup causes the burning feeling in your muscles.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Food gives you energy directly"
  Reality: Food doesn't power muscles directly. Digestion breaks food into glucose, which cells convert to ATP through respiration. ATP is the actual energy molecule that powers all cellular work.
  Strategy: Analogy: You can't pour crude oil into a car engine. It must be refined into gasoline first. Similarly, food must be 'refined' into ATP.

• "You only breathe to get oxygen"
  Reality: You breathe for TWO reasons: to bring in O2 (input for respiration) AND to expel CO2 (waste product of respiration). Both are equally important. Too much CO2 is toxic.
  Strategy: Show the full respiration equation — both sides matter.

• "Energy is created when you eat"
  Reality: Energy is TRANSFORMED, not created. The chemical energy stored in food molecules (glucose) is converted to ATP energy through respiration. The total energy is conserved.
  Strategy: Connect to conservation of energy from physics.

• Maximum Effort Scenario: With high glucose and maximum oxygen, ATP Production is maximized. Athletic Output reaches peak levels. The model shows the body is efficiently converting food energy into motion through aerobic cellular respiration.
• No Fuel Scenario: Without food (glucose), ATP Production drops dramatically even if oxygen is plentiful. Athletic Output crashes. You can breathe all you want — without fuel, there's nothing to burn. This models what happens when an athlete 'hits the wall.'

Reflection Exemplars:
• Q: Why do marathon runners hit the wall?
  A: After about 90-120 minutes of intense exercise, the body depletes its stored glycogen (glucose reserves). Without glucose, ATP production drops dramatically. The runner suddenly feels exhausted, weak, and may have difficulty thinking clearly because the brain also needs glucose.
• Q: Why do you breathe harder during exercise?
  A: Exercise increases the demand for ATP, which increases the need for oxygen (a required input for efficient cellular respiration). Your brain detects rising CO2 levels (a waste product of respiration) and signals you to breathe faster and deeper to bring in more O2 and expel more CO2.

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format. Each item is three-dimensional, assessing a Science and Engineering Practice (SEP), Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and Crosscutting Concept (CCC) simultaneously, aligned to MS-LS1-5 and MS-LS1-7.

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.3 (Develop and use a model) + DCI LS1.C.1 (Food provides molecules that serve as fuel and building material) + CCC5 (Energy and Matter)

A student builds a model of athletic energy production with Food Intake (Glucose) and Oxygen Supply as external components and ATP Production and Athletic Output as internal components. When Food Intake is set to zero while Oxygen Supply remains at maximum, ATP Production drops to near zero and Athletic Output collapses completely.

What does this result demonstrate about the role of glucose in the body's energy system?

A. Oxygen alone is sufficient to power muscle contraction when glucose is unavailable.
B. Glucose is a required reactant in cellular respiration. Without it, cells cannot produce ATP, regardless of how much oxygen is available, because the chemical reaction requires both inputs.
C. When glucose is unavailable, the body produces ATP from water molecules instead.
D. Athletes do not actually need food to perform. They only need rest and hydration.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Cellular respiration is a chemical reaction: Glucose + Oxygen leads to Carbon Dioxide + Water + ATP (energy). Both reactants are required. Without glucose, the reaction cannot proceed, even with abundant oxygen. This is like having a car with a full battery but no gasoline. One input alone is insufficient. If you chose A, oxygen is necessary but not sufficient. It serves as the other reactant in cellular respiration. Without glucose to react with, oxygen alone cannot produce ATP energy. If you chose D, the model directly disproves this. Without food (glucose), ATP production drops to near zero, which means no energy for muscle contraction or any other cellular activity. If you chose C, water is a product of cellular respiration, not a substitute reactant. The body cannot produce ATP from water alone. It requires the chemical energy stored in glucose bonds.

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 6.2 (Construct a scientific explanation) + DCI LS1.C.2 (Chemical reactions release energy from food molecules) + CCC5 (Energy and Matter)

The chemical equation for cellular respiration is: Glucose + Oxygen leads to Carbon Dioxide + Water + ATP (energy). A student says, "Food gives you energy." A classmate responds, "That is not scientifically precise."

Based on the model and the chemical equation, which statement is the most scientifically accurate description of how food provides energy?

A. The energy comes from the oxygen you breathe, not from the food itself. Food just helps oxygen work faster.
B. Food molecules (glucose) are rearranged through chemical reactions. The energy stored in the chemical bonds of glucose is released and transferred to ATP molecules, which cells use to power activities.
C. Food creates brand new energy that did not exist before you ate it.
D. Food is directly burned in the stomach like fuel in a furnace, producing heat energy that powers muscles.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: The scientifically precise description involves energy transfer, not energy creation. Chemical energy stored in the bonds of glucose molecules is released when those bonds are broken during cellular respiration. This energy is transferred to ATP molecules, which serve as the universal energy currency for cells. The atoms from glucose are rearranged into CO2 and H2O. If you chose C, the law of conservation of energy states that energy cannot be created or destroyed. The energy in food originally came from the sun through photosynthesis. Cellular respiration transfers that energy to ATP, not creates new energy. If you chose D, food is not burned in the stomach. Digestion in the stomach is a mechanical and chemical breakdown process. Cellular respiration occurs in the mitochondria of cells throughout the body, not in the stomach. If you chose A, both glucose and oxygen are required reactants. The chemical energy comes from the glucose bonds, not from oxygen. Oxygen is the electron acceptor in the reaction, but the energy is stored in the glucose.

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.3 (Use a model to predict) + DCI LS1.C.1 (Body processes to obtain and release energy) + CCC4 (Systems and System Models)

In the model's "Maximum Effort" scenario, both Food Intake and Oxygen Supply are set to high levels, producing peak ATP Production and maximum Athletic Output. A student wants to predict what would happen if an athlete trains at high altitude where oxygen levels are 30% lower than at sea level.

Based on the model, which prediction about the athlete's performance at high altitude is best supported?

A. ATP production would decrease because oxygen is a required reactant in aerobic cellular respiration. With 30% less oxygen available, cells cannot fully process glucose, limiting energy production and reducing athletic output.
B. ATP production would increase because cells would work harder to compensate for lower oxygen.
C. Athletic output would stay the same because food provides enough chemical energy on its own to power performance.
D. The body would immediately switch to using carbon dioxide as a replacement for oxygen in cellular respiration.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: The model shows that both external inputs (glucose and oxygen) must be present for maximum ATP production. When oxygen is limited, the aerobic pathway of cellular respiration is restricted. Cells can partially compensate through anaerobic respiration, but this produces far less ATP per glucose molecule and generates lactic acid as a byproduct, causing fatigue. If you chose B, cells cannot increase their ATP production when a required reactant is limited. The chemical reaction requires a specific ratio of glucose to oxygen. Less oxygen means less complete reactions. If you chose C, the model clearly shows that both inputs are required. Even with maximum food intake, reduced oxygen limits the rate of cellular respiration and therefore ATP production. If you chose D, CO2 is a waste product of cellular respiration, not a substitute reactant. The body has no mechanism to use CO2 in place of oxygen for energy production.

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 6.2 (Construct an explanation using evidence) + DCI LS1.C.2 (Food rearranged through chemical reactions to release energy) + CCC5 (Energy and Matter)

An athlete eats a large pasta meal and then immediately tries to run a 400-meter race but performs poorly, despite having consumed ample calories. Later that evening, after the meal has been digested, the athlete runs the same distance at practice and performs significantly better.

Using the model's representation of the food-to-energy pathway, which explanation best accounts for the difference in performance?

A. The athlete performed better in the evening because cooler temperatures make muscles work more efficiently.
B. The food was too heavy and physically weighed the athlete down during the first race.
C. Eating food temporarily removes oxygen from the bloodstream, leaving less oxygen available for muscle cells during exercise.
D. Digestion must first break food down into glucose and transport it via the bloodstream to muscle cells before cellular respiration can use it as a reactant. This process takes time, so the glucose was not yet available to muscles during the immediate race.

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: The model uses glucose as the food input to cellular respiration, but whole food must first undergo digestion to become glucose. The digestive process (mechanical breakdown in the stomach, chemical breakdown by enzymes, absorption through the intestinal wall, transport via bloodstream to cells) takes one to three hours. During the immediate race, the pasta had not yet been converted to glucose and delivered to muscle cells. If you chose B, while a full stomach can cause discomfort, the primary issue is biochemical, not gravitational. The food's mass does not significantly affect running performance. The problem is that the chemical energy in the food has not yet been converted to usable glucose. If you chose C, eating does not remove oxygen from the blood. While digestion does increase blood flow to the digestive organs, the primary issue is glucose availability, not oxygen deprivation. If you chose A, the model focuses on food and oxygen as inputs. Temperature was not a variable, and the key difference is the time needed for digestion and glucose delivery.

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 7.1 (Engage in argument from evidence) + DCI LS1.C.1 (Matter and energy flow through organisms) + CCC5 (Energy and Matter)

A sports nutritionist claims that elite athletes need to consume 6,000-8,000 calories per day during intense training, compared to 2,000-2,500 for the average person. A skeptic argues this is impossible because "the human body can only process a fixed amount of food per day."

Using the model's representation of energy flow, which argument best evaluates the skeptic's claim?

A. The nutritionist is wrong because excess calories are always stored as fat and never used for energy.
B. Elite athletes need more calories only because they are physically larger, not because of their activity level.
C. The model shows that Athletic Output depends on ATP Production, which depends on Glucose (from food) and Oxygen inputs. During intense training, the body's ATP demand increases dramatically, requiring proportionally more glucose from food. The body adapts to process more food by increasing digestive efficiency, blood flow, and metabolic rate.
D. The skeptic is correct. All humans process the same amount of food regardless of activity level.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: The model shows a direct chain from food intake through ATP production to athletic output. Intense training dramatically increases ATP demand in muscle cells. To meet this demand, cells need more glucose and oxygen. More glucose requires more food intake. The body adapts to high training loads by increasing digestive capacity, blood flow to muscles, and metabolic efficiency, allowing it to process significantly more food than a sedentary person. If you chose D, the model directly contradicts this. Different activity levels produce different ATP demands, which require different amounts of glucose input. The body's processing capacity adapts to demand. If you chose B, while larger body size does increase baseline caloric needs, the primary driver of the 3x-4x caloric increase is the enormous energy expenditure of intense athletic training, not body size alone. If you chose A, excess calories can be stored as fat, but during intense training, the calories are being consumed in real-time by cellular respiration. The 6,000-8,000 calories are needed to match the rate of energy expenditure, not stored.
