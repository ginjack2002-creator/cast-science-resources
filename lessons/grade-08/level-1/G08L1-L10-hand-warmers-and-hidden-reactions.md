## Platform

### Title
**Hand Warmers and Hidden Reactions — How Atoms Rearrange to Create Heat, Light, and Everything You Use**

### Overview
Hand warmers are elegant examples of chemistry in action. The simple exothermic reaction of iron oxidation demonstrates both conservation of mass and energy transformation — making abstract chemistry concepts tangible and practical. Students investigate the driving question: How can a small packet create heat without fire, batteries, or electricity? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

### NGSS Standard
**MS-PS1-4, MS-PS1-5**: Develop a model that predicts and describes changes in particle motion, temperature, and state of a pure substance when thermal energy is added or removed. Develop and use a model to describe how the total number of atoms does not change in a chemical reaction and thus mass is conserved.

### Learning Objectives
- Explain how chemical reactions rearrange atoms to form new substances
- Model how exothermic reactions release energy as heat
- Apply the law of conservation of mass to chemical reactions
- Predict whether a reaction is exothermic or endothermic based on temperature change

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• The Chemistry of Hand Warmers:
  Hand warmers contain: iron powder (reactant), salt (catalyst to speed the reaction), activated carbon (distributes heat and retains moisture), and vermiculite (insulation). When exposed to air, the iron reacts with oxygen: 4Fe + 3O2 → 2Fe2O3 + heat. This is the same reaction as rusting, but engineered to happen faster.

• Exothermic vs. Endothermic:
  Exothermic reactions release energy to the surroundings (hand warmers, combustion, cellular respiration). Endothermic reactions absorb energy from the surroundings (instant cold packs, photosynthesis, cooking an egg). The direction of energy flow determines whether you feel heat or cold.

• Conservation of Mass:
  Antoine Lavoisier discovered that mass is conserved in chemical reactions. In a hand warmer: the mass of iron + the mass of oxygen that reacts = the mass of iron oxide produced. No atoms are created or destroyed — they simply rearrange. If you weigh a sealed hand warmer before and after, the mass is identical.

• Reaction Rates:
  Several factors affect how fast a reaction occurs: surface area (powder reacts faster than a solid chunk), temperature (higher temp = faster), concentration of reactants (more oxygen = faster), and catalysts (salt speeds up the iron oxidation). Hand warmers are engineered to balance these factors for consistent, safe heat.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Chemical reactions destroy matter"
  Reality: No atoms are created or destroyed in a chemical reaction — they only rearrange. The total mass of reactants ALWAYS equals the total mass of products. This is the law of conservation of mass.
  Strategy: Weigh a sealed hand warmer before and after activation — same mass!

• "Heat means something is burning"
  Reality: Exothermic reactions release heat but don't require visible combustion. Rusting, dissolving, and neutralization all release heat without flames. 'Burning' is just one type of exothermic reaction (combustion).
  Strategy: Ask: Is a hand warmer on fire? No, but it's hot. Chemistry creates heat in many ways.

• "The hand warmer creates energy"
  Reality: The hand warmer doesn't CREATE energy — it TRANSFORMS chemical potential energy stored in the bonds of iron and oxygen into thermal energy. Energy is conserved, just like mass.
  Strategy: Connect to conservation of energy from the roller coaster lesson (L05).

FACILITATION TIPS:
• Step 1: Let students explore the interface. Don't over-explain.
  Let them discover. Circulate and support, don't lecture.
• Step 2: Ask "When this goes up, what happens to that?" to
  guide positive/negative relationship decisions. Let students debate.
• Step 3: Give time for students to "break" the model — turn
  things on/off and observe. This is where real insight happens.
• Step 4: Don't give answers. Ask questions. Let curiosity drive
  the research. Celebrate when students' additions don't work as
  expected — that's authentic science.

ANSWER KEY:
Big Question: How can a small packet create heat without fire, batteries, or electricity?
Answer: Hand warmers contain iron powder that reacts with oxygen in the air. This exothermic chemical reaction releases heat energy as atoms rearrange from iron and oxygen into iron oxide (rust) — the same total atoms, just in a new arrangement!

Simulation Answers:
• Sealed (No Oxygen): With Oxygen Exposure at 0%, Reaction Rate stays at zero — no reaction can occur. Heat Output remains at baseline (room temperature). This is why hand warmers come in sealed packages — blocking oxygen prevents the reaction from starting early.
• Maximum Exposure: With maximum iron AND oxygen, Reaction Rate peaks rapidly and Heat Output is very high initially. However, the iron is consumed faster, so the total duration is shorter. More isn't always better — the company wants CONSISTENT heat over 8 hours, not a burst of heat followed by nothing.

Reflection Exemplars:
• Q: Why does the hand warmer stop?
  A: The hand warmer stops producing heat when one of the reactants is used up — usually the iron powder. This is the concept of a limiting reactant. All the iron has been converted to iron oxide (rust). The atoms still exist; they're just in a new arrangement that doesn't produce more heat.
• Q: Where does the heat come from?
  A: The heat comes from the REARRANGEMENT of chemical bonds. When iron atoms bond with oxygen atoms to form iron oxide, the new bonds are at a lower energy state than the original separate elements. The difference in energy is released as heat. No atoms are created or destroyed — only rearranged.

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.3 (Develop and use a model) + DCI PS1.B.1 (Substances react to form new substances with different properties) + CCC5 (Energy and Matter)

A student builds a model of a hand warmer with Iron Powder Amount and Oxygen Exposure as external components and Reaction Rate and Heat Output as internal components. When Oxygen Exposure is set to zero, the iron powder remains completely unchanged and Heat Output stays at zero, even though Iron Powder Amount is at maximum.

What does this result demonstrate about chemical reactions?

A. The model contained an error because iron should generate heat spontaneously without oxygen.
B. Iron powder does not contain any chemical energy, so it cannot produce heat under any conditions.
C. Oxygen plays no role in the iron oxidation reaction and can be excluded without consequence.
D. Both reactants (iron and oxygen) must be present for the chemical reaction to occur. Removing one reactant prevents the reaction entirely, regardless of how much of the other reactant is available.

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: The chemical reaction in a hand warmer is: Iron + Oxygen leads to Iron Oxide + Heat. Both reactants are required. Without oxygen, iron atoms have nothing to react with, so no new substance forms and no energy is released. This is why hand warmers are sealed in airtight packaging until use. Opening the package exposes iron to atmospheric oxygen, starting the reaction. If you chose B, iron does contain chemical energy in its bonds. That energy is released when iron reacts with oxygen. The point is that a reaction partner (oxygen) is needed to release it. If you chose A, iron does not spontaneously generate heat. It requires oxygen as a reactant. The model correctly represents the chemistry. If you chose C, the model result directly contradicts this. Without oxygen, no reaction occurred. Oxygen is essential as the second reactant in the oxidation reaction.

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 6.2 (Construct an explanation) + DCI PS1.B.1 (Chemical reactions conserve mass) + CCC3 (Scale, Proportion, and Quantity)

In the model, a hand warmer eventually stops producing heat even though oxygen is still available in the air. The iron powder has been fully consumed and converted to iron oxide. The student notes that no more iron remains to react.

This result is an example of which chemical concept?

A. A limiting reactant: the iron was completely consumed, so the reaction cannot continue even with excess oxygen. The amount of the limiting reactant determines the maximum amount of product and heat that can be produced.
B. The reaction lost energy over time due to entropy, causing it to stop spontaneously.
C. Oxygen became too concentrated for the reaction to continue, poisoning the system.
D. The reaction reached equilibrium where forward and reverse reactions are balanced equally.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: Iron is the limiting reactant in this system. Once all iron atoms have reacted with oxygen to form iron oxide, no more reactant is available to sustain the reaction, even with excess oxygen in the air. The limiting reactant controls the total output of the reaction. This concept explains why hand warmers produce heat for a fixed duration, they contain a finite amount of iron. If you chose D, this is not an equilibrium situation. The reaction stopped because a reactant was fully consumed, not because forward and reverse rates balanced. Iron oxidation at room temperature is effectively irreversible. If you chose B, while entropy is a real thermodynamic concept, the reaction stopped because a reactant was used up, not because of energy loss. The reaction would continue if more iron were added. If you chose C, excess oxygen does not poison or inhibit the iron oxidation reaction. In fact, more oxygen would increase reaction rate. The reaction stopped because the other reactant (iron) was fully consumed.

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 4.1 (Analyze data to provide evidence) + DCI PS1.B.1 (Total number of atoms does not change; mass is conserved) + CCC5 (Energy and Matter)

A student weighs a sealed hand warmer before activation (25.0 grams), activates it by opening the package and allowing the reaction to proceed to completion, then seals it in a bag and weighs it again. The mass reads 25.3 grams. The student is confused because new substances (iron oxide) formed but the hand warmer gained mass instead of losing it.

Which law explains this result, and why did the mass increase?

A. The law of conservation of mass: atoms are neither created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction. The mass increased because the iron reacted with oxygen from the air, and the oxygen atoms added to the iron's mass to form heavier iron oxide. Total mass of reactants equals total mass of products.
B. The law of gravity increased the mass because the chemical reaction made the iron oxide denser than the original iron powder.
C. Newton's Third Law caused an equal and opposite mass reaction, adding mass to balance the energy released.
D. The mass change was a measurement error because chemical reactions cannot change mass.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: Conservation of mass explains both the principle and the specific observation. Iron (Fe) combined with oxygen (O2) from the air to form iron oxide (Fe2O3). The oxygen atoms that bonded with the iron added their mass to the product. The sealed bag captured the iron oxide (including the oxygen atoms it incorporated), so the final mass is the original iron mass plus the mass of oxygen consumed from the air. If you chose B, density does not change total mass. Even if iron oxide is denser, the total mass change comes from oxygen atoms joining the system, not from gravitational effects. If you chose C, Newton's Third Law describes force pairs in physics, not mass changes in chemistry. Conservation of mass is a chemistry principle unrelated to Newton's Laws of Motion. If you chose D, the mass change is real and predicted by chemistry. When a system is open to air (oxygen can enter), the products can be heavier than the original reactant alone because oxygen atoms from the air are incorporated.

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 6.5 (Apply scientific principles to design a solution) + DCI PS1.A.1 (Substances have characteristic properties including reaction with other substances) + CCC7 (Stability and Change)

A student needs to design a hand warmer that lasts exactly 8 hours. The current prototype uses 10 grams of iron powder and lasts only 4 hours. The model shows that Iron Powder Amount directly determines how long the reaction produces heat, and that Oxygen Exposure rate controls how fast the iron reacts.

Which modification is best supported by the model evidence to achieve the 8-hour target?

A. Use a different metal that does not react with oxygen, so the reaction lasts longer.
B. Double the iron powder to 20 grams, providing twice as much reactant for the exothermic reaction, and adjust oxygen exposure holes to maintain a steady reaction rate over the longer period.
C. Make the packet smaller to concentrate the heat into a tighter space.
D. Remove the oxygen exposure holes completely to trap more heat inside the hand warmer.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: The model shows two key relationships. Iron Powder Amount determines total heat capacity (more reactant = more total reaction possible). Oxygen Exposure rate controls how fast the iron reacts. Doubling the iron doubles the available reactant, and adjusting oxygen holes regulates the rate to distribute the reaction evenly over 8 hours instead of consuming it all in 4. If you chose A, a metal that does not react with oxygen would not produce heat through oxidation. The entire hand warmer mechanism depends on the exothermic iron-oxygen reaction. If you chose D, removing oxygen holes would stop the reaction entirely because oxygen is a required reactant. No oxygen means no reaction and no heat. If you chose C, making the packet smaller does not increase the amount of reactant or the duration of the reaction. It would not change when the iron runs out. The total heat produced depends on the amount of iron, not the packet size.

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 7.1 (Engage in argument from evidence) + DCI PS1.B.1 (Conservation of mass in chemical reactions) + CCC5 (Energy and Matter)

A student burns a piece of wood on a scale and observes that the ash weighs significantly less than the original wood. The student concludes, "Mass was destroyed during the burning reaction because the ash weighs less than the wood."

Using conservation of mass and evidence from the hand warmer model, which argument best evaluates this claim?

A. Conservation of mass only applies to reactions in sealed containers, not to open-air burning.
B. The mass was converted entirely into heat energy, which is why it appears to be missing from the scale.
C. The student's conclusion is incorrect. Mass was not destroyed. Burning is a chemical reaction (combustion) where carbon and hydrogen in the wood react with oxygen to form carbon dioxide gas and water vapor, which escaped into the air. If all products (ash + CO2 gas + water vapor) were captured and weighed, the total would equal the original wood plus the oxygen consumed. The hand warmer model shows this principle: when the sealed system gained mass, it was because oxygen entered. In the wood example, gaseous products left the system.
D. The student is correct. Burning destroys mass, which is why the ash weighs less than the original wood.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: Conservation of mass applies to ALL chemical reactions, open or closed. In the hand warmer, the system gained mass because oxygen atoms entered (from air into the iron). In the wood burning, the system lost apparent mass because carbon and hydrogen atoms left (as CO2 and H2O gases escaping into the air). Both cases conserve mass. The total atoms before and after are identical. The difference is whether products stay in the system or escape. If you chose D, mass cannot be destroyed in any chemical reaction. The atoms that made up the wood still exist, rearranged as ash, carbon dioxide, and water vapor. If you chose B, while the reaction does release heat energy, mass and energy are separate in chemical reactions at everyday scales. The missing mass is in the gaseous products, not converted to energy. Mass-energy equivalence (E=mc squared) applies only at nuclear scales. If you chose A, conservation of mass is a universal law that applies to all chemical reactions regardless of whether the system is open or closed. In an open system, products may escape, but they still exist and have mass.
