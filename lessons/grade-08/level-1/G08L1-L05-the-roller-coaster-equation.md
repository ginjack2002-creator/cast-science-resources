## Platform

### Title
**The Roller Coaster Equation — How Physics Turns Height Into Speed and Screams Into Science**

### Overview
Roller coasters are elegant demonstrations of energy conservation. Every scream, every thrill, and every stomach drop is the result of energy transforming between two forms: potential and kinetic. Students investigate the driving question: How does a roller coaster turn height into speed without an engine? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

### NGSS Standard
**MS-PS3-1, MS-PS3-2**: Construct and interpret graphical displays of data to describe the relationships of kinetic energy to the mass of an object and to the speed of an object. Develop a model to describe that when the arrangement of objects interacting at a distance changes, different amounts of potential energy are stored in the system.

### Learning Objectives
- Explain the relationship between potential energy and kinetic energy
- Model how height, mass, and speed are connected in energy transformations
- Predict the speed of a coaster at any point on the track using energy conservation
- Evaluate real roller coaster designs using energy transformation principles

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

LESSON PURPOSE:
Roller coasters are elegant demonstrations of energy conservation. Every scream, every thrill, and every stomach drop is the result of energy transforming between two forms: potential and kinetic.

NGSS ALIGNMENT:
MS-PS3-1, MS-PS3-2: Construct and interpret graphical displays of data to describe the relationships of kinetic energy to the mass of an object and to the speed of an object. Develop a model to describe that when the arrangement of objects interacting at a distance changes, different amounts of potential energy are stored in the system.

THREE-DIMENSIONAL LEARNING:
• Science Practice: Developing and Using Models
  Students develop a model to describe energy transformations between potential and kinetic energy.
• Disciplinary Core Idea: PS3.A Definitions of Energy / PS3.B Conservation of Energy
  Kinetic energy is proportional to mass and speed squared. Energy is conserved in transformations.
• Crosscutting Concept: Energy and Matter
  Students trace energy as it flows and transforms between potential and kinetic forms throughout the system.

PACING GUIDE:
• Step 1 (Locate): 8-10 minutes
• Step 2 (Establish): 8-10 minutes
• Step 3 (Visualize & Evaluate): 10-12 minutes
• Step 4 (Revise & Extend): 10-15 minutes
• Total: 50-70 minutes (or split across 2 class periods)

PRE-LESSON PREP:
□ Test ModelIt access on student devices
□ Review vocabulary: Kinetic Energy, Potential Energy, Energy Conservation, Energy Transformation
□ Prepare How does a roller coaster turn height into speed without an engine discussion hook
□ Have images or video ready for phenomenon introduction

LEVER FRAMEWORK:
• Locate: Students identify coaster height, coaster mass, potential energy, and kinetic energy as system components.
• Establish: Students determine that height positively affects PE, and as PE decreases (going downhill), KE increases — they trade back and forth.
• Visualize: Students build the energy transformation model showing the PE-KE seesaw.
• Evaluate: Students compare different heights and masses to see how they affect the energy balance.
• Revise: Students add friction to see why real coasters lose energy and eventually stop.

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• Potential Energy:
  Gravitational potential energy depends on height and mass: PE = mgh (mass × gravity × height). At the top of the first hill, the coaster has maximum PE. The chain lift does work against gravity to give the coaster this stored energy. A 500 kg coaster at 50 meters has PE = 500 × 9.8 × 50 = 245,000 Joules.

• Kinetic Energy:
  Kinetic energy depends on mass and speed: KE = ½mv² (half × mass × speed squared). Notice that speed is SQUARED — doubling speed quadruples kinetic energy. This is why the first drop feels so intense: small changes in height create large changes in speed.

• Conservation of Energy:
  In an ideal system (no friction), total energy = PE + KE = constant. At the top: high PE, low KE. At the bottom: low PE, high KE. The energy doesn't disappear — it transforms. In real coasters, some energy is lost to friction and air resistance as heat.

• Real Coaster Design:
  Engineers use these equations to design every hill, loop, and turn. The first hill must be the tallest because the coaster can never regain that height without additional energy input. Loop-the-loops require a minimum speed at the top to maintain contact with the track.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Heavier coasters go faster"
  Reality: Mass cancels out in energy calculations! A heavy coaster and a light coaster dropped from the same height reach the same speed (ignoring friction). They have different amounts of energy, but the speed is identical.
  Strategy: Demo: Drop a heavy and light ball from the same height — they hit the ground at the same time.

• "The coaster has no energy at the top"
  Reality: At the top, the coaster is moving slowly (low KE) but has MAXIMUM potential energy stored in its height. It's like a fully charged battery — the energy is there, just stored.
  Strategy: Ask: Which has more energy — a rock on the ground or the same rock on top of a building?

• "Energy is used up on the ride"
  Reality: Energy is never created or destroyed — only transformed. 'Used up' energy actually became heat through friction. The total energy of the universe stays the same.
  Strategy: Calculate: Track the total energy (PE + KE) at each point — it should be constant in an ideal system.

FACILITATION TIPS:
• Step 1: Let students explore the interface. Don't over-explain.
  Let them discover. Circulate and support, don't lecture.
• Step 2: Ask "When this goes up, what happens to that?" to
  guide positive/negative relationship decisions. Let students debate.
• Step 3: Give time for students to "break" the model — turn
  things on/off and observe. This is where real insight happens.
• Step 4: Don't give answers. Ask questions. Let curiosity drive
  the research. Celebrate when students' additions don't work as
  expected — that's authentic science.

ANSWER KEY:
Big Question: How does a roller coaster turn height into speed without an engine?
Answer: A roller coaster converts potential energy (height) into kinetic energy (speed). The higher the first hill, the faster the coaster goes at the bottom — no engine needed!

Simulation Answers:
• First Drop Scenario: Starting at maximum height, PE is at its maximum and KE is near zero. As the coaster descends, PE decreases smoothly while KE increases by exactly the same amount. At the bottom, KE is maximized — this is where the coaster is fastest.
• Heavy vs. Light Coaster: Doubling the mass doubles BOTH PE at the top and KE at the bottom. However, since KE = ½mv², the speed at the bottom is the same! More mass means more energy, but the speed doesn't change — a key insight.

Reflection Exemplars:
• Q: Why must the first hill be tallest?
  A: The chain lift gives the coaster all of its energy at the start by raising it to maximum height. After that, the total energy can only decrease (due to friction). Since PE = mgh, the coaster can never reach a height higher than it started because it doesn't have enough energy.
• Q: Why does the coaster eventually stop?
  A: In the real world, friction and air resistance convert kinetic energy into heat. This 'lost' energy reduces the total available for PE-KE transformation. The energy isn't destroyed — it becomes thermal energy — but it's no longer useful for motion.

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.3 (Develop and use a model) + DCI PS3.A.2 (Motion energy depends on mass and speed) + CCC5 (Energy and Matter)

A student builds a model of a roller coaster. At the top of the first hill (30 meters high), the model shows Potential Energy at maximum and Kinetic Energy near zero. At the bottom of the drop (ground level), Potential Energy is near zero and Kinetic Energy is at maximum. The student notices that the total energy (Potential + Kinetic) remains the same at every point on the track.

What fundamental principle does this constant total energy demonstrate?

A. The coaster used a hidden engine at the bottom of the hill to reach maximum speed.
B. The law of conservation of energy: energy transforms between potential and kinetic forms, but the total amount in the system stays the same.
C. Potential energy is always more powerful than kinetic energy, which is why the coaster slows down going up hills.
D. Energy was created at the bottom of the hill by the force of gravity pulling the coaster down.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: The model demonstrates conservation of energy. At the top, energy exists as gravitational potential energy (stored due to height). During the descent, potential energy transforms into kinetic energy (motion). At the bottom, nearly all energy is kinetic. At no point was energy created, destroyed, or added from outside the system. The total was constant throughout. If you chose D, gravity does not create energy. It provides the force that converts potential energy (from height) into kinetic energy (motion). The energy existed all along in potential form. If you chose C, potential and kinetic energy are not ranked by power. They are different forms of the same total energy. The coaster slows going uphill because kinetic energy is converting back to potential energy, not because one form is weaker. If you chose A, the model shows no engine or external energy input. The coaster's energy at every point traces back to the potential energy it had at the top of the first hill.

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 5.1 (Use mathematics to describe relationships) + DCI PS3.A.2 (Kinetic energy depends on mass and speed) + CCC3 (Scale, Proportion, and Quantity)

In the "Heavy Coaster" scenario, a student doubles the coaster's mass from 500 kg to 1000 kg while keeping the starting height at 30 meters. The model shows that both potential energy and kinetic energy doubled at every point on the track. However, the speed at the bottom was identical to the lighter coaster.

Using the relationship KE = 1/2 mv squared, which explanation accounts for the same speed despite doubled energy?

A. When mass doubles, KE doubles even at the same speed because KE depends on both mass and velocity squared. The height determines the speed (not mass), because both PE and KE scale equally with mass, so mass cancels out in the conversion.
B. All objects always move at exactly the same speed regardless of mass, weight, or height.
C. The extra mass created friction that slowed the heavier coaster to match the lighter one's speed.
D. The model was inaccurate because heavier objects should always move faster than lighter ones.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: The mathematics explain this precisely. PE = mgh and KE = 1/2 mv squared. When converting from PE to KE: mgh = 1/2 mv squared. Mass (m) appears on both sides and cancels, leaving gh = 1/2 v squared. Speed depends only on height, not mass. The doubled mass means doubled energy at every point, but the speed at each point remains the same. If you chose D, Galileo demonstrated that objects of different mass fall at the same rate (ignoring air resistance). The model correctly shows this principle applied to a coaster on a frictionless track. If you chose B, this is too broad. Speed does depend on height and the track path. The specific insight is that mass does not affect speed in this energy conversion, not that all objects always move at the same speed. If you chose C, the model operates without friction. Even in the real world, friction's effect on speed is minimal compared to the fundamental physics of energy conversion. The equal speeds result from mathematics, not friction.

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.3 (Use a model to predict) + DCI PS3.B.1 (Conservation of energy) + CCC5 (Energy and Matter)

A roller coaster starts at the top of a 30-meter hill. It then enters a vertical loop that is 20 meters tall. At the top of the loop, the model shows the coaster still has significant kinetic energy and is moving fast enough to maintain contact with the track.

Using conservation of energy, why does the coaster still have kinetic energy at the top of the 20-meter loop?

A. The coaster started with 30 meters worth of potential energy. Climbing to 20 meters converted only 20 meters worth back to potential energy, leaving 10 meters worth of energy still in kinetic form (speed).
B. Potential energy is always greater than kinetic energy inside a loop, so kinetic energy must exist at the top.
C. The loop generated extra energy that did not exist when the coaster was at the starting hill.
D. The coaster accelerated inside the loop using centrifugal force, which added energy to the system.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: Conservation of energy provides a precise accounting. At 30 meters, the coaster has 30 units of PE. At the top of the 20-meter loop, 20 units have converted back to PE (due to height), leaving 10 units as KE (motion). The coaster is still moving because it has not used all its original energy to climb. If you chose C, no energy was generated. The loop is part of the track and does not add energy. Every bit of energy at any point on the track can be traced back to the original PE at the top of the first hill. If you chose D, centrifugal force is not a real force (it is an apparent effect of circular motion). No energy is added by circular motion. The coaster's energy at the loop top comes from leftover PE from the starting height. If you chose B, there is no rule that PE must exceed KE in a loop. The relationship depends on the height at each point. At the loop bottom, KE exceeds PE. At the loop top, PE is high but KE is still present because the starting height exceeded the loop height.

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 6.5 (Apply scientific principles to design solutions) + DCI PS3.B.1 (Energy is conserved in a closed system) + CCC5 (Energy and Matter)

A theme park engineer proposes a roller coaster design where the second hill (35 meters) is taller than the first hill (30 meters). The coaster has no engines after the initial chain lift to the first hill.

Based on conservation of energy, why is this design impossible without an additional engine?

A. Taller hills are structurally unsafe and violate building codes for amusement parks.
B. Gravity only provides force on the first descent and stops affecting the coaster afterward.
C. The coaster would be moving too fast to climb a taller hill safely.
D. The total energy of the system is set by the first hill's height. A 35-meter second hill would require more potential energy than the system contains, which violates conservation of energy. Some energy is also always lost to friction, making the situation even more impossible.

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: The first hill sets the total energy budget. At 30 meters, the coaster has a fixed amount of PE. To reach 35 meters, it would need more PE than it started with. Since energy cannot be created (conservation of energy), and some energy is always lost to friction (converted to heat), the coaster cannot reach a height equal to or greater than its starting height. This is why the first hill must always be the tallest on any unpowered coaster. If you chose C, speed is not the issue. The coaster would actually be slowing down as it climbs, converting KE to PE. The problem is that it runs out of energy before reaching the top. If you chose A, while safety codes exist, the fundamental constraint is physics. Even a perfectly safe structure cannot overcome conservation of energy. If you chose B, gravity acts on the coaster at every point on the track, not just the first hill. The constraint is energy, not gravity's reach.

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 4.1 (Analyze graphical displays of data) + DCI PS3.A.2 (Potential energy depends on arrangement of objects) + CCC3 (Scale, Proportion, and Quantity)

A student collects data from three roller coaster runs with different starting heights. Run A starts at 10 meters and reaches 14 m/s at the bottom. Run B starts at 20 meters and reaches 20 m/s at the bottom. Run C starts at 40 meters and reaches 28 m/s at the bottom. The student notices that when height doubles (10 to 20 meters), speed does not double (14 to 20 m/s is only a 1.4x increase).

What mathematical relationship between height and speed does this data reveal?

A. Height and speed are directly proportional: doubling height should double speed, so the data must contain measurement errors.
B. Height has no effect on speed. The differences in the data are caused by variations in friction between runs.
C. Speed is proportional to the square root of height. Doubling height increases speed by a factor of approximately 1.4 (the square root of 2), which matches the data exactly.
D. Speed increases exponentially with height, meaning each additional meter of height produces dramatically more speed.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: The mathematical derivation from conservation of energy gives v = square root of (2gh). Speed is proportional to the square root of height, not height itself. Doubling height from 10 to 20 meters should increase speed by the square root of 2, which is approximately 1.414. The data confirms this: 14 m/s times 1.414 equals approximately 20 m/s. Quadrupling height (10 to 40 meters) should double speed: 14 times 2 equals 28 m/s, which also matches. If you chose A, direct proportionality would mean 28 m/s at 20 meters, not 20 m/s. The data is consistent and accurate. The relationship is square root, not linear. If you chose B, the data shows a clear systematic relationship between height and speed. If height had no effect, all three speeds would be similar, which they are not. If you chose D, an exponential relationship would produce much larger speed increases at higher heights. The data shows a diminishing relationship (each additional meter produces less additional speed), which is characteristic of a square root function, not an exponential one.
