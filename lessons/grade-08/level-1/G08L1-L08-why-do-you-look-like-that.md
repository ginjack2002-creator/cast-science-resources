## Platform

### Title
**Why Do You Look Like That? — The Genetic Lottery That Made You, You**

### Overview
Every human has approximately 20,000-25,000 genes on 23 pairs of chromosomes. The specific combination of alleles you inherit makes you genetically unique — unless you have an identical twin. Understanding inheritance is key to medicine, agriculture, and evolution. Students investigate the driving question: Why do you look similar to your parents but not exactly like them? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

### NGSS Standard
**MS-LS3-1, MS-LS3-2**: Develop and use a model to describe why structural changes to genes (mutations) located on chromosomes may affect proteins and may result in harmful, beneficial, or neutral effects to the structure and function of the organism. Develop and use a model to describe why asexual reproduction results in offspring with identical genetic information and sexual reproduction results in offspring with genetic variation.

### Learning Objectives
- Explain how genes are passed from parents to offspring through chromosomes
- Model how sexual reproduction creates unique genetic combinations
- Predict trait inheritance patterns using dominant and recessive alleles
- Evaluate why genetic variation is important for species survival

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• Chromosomes and Genes:
  Humans have 23 pairs of chromosomes — one set from each parent. Each chromosome contains hundreds to thousands of genes. Each gene has a specific location (locus) on a chromosome. You have two copies of every gene (one from each parent), and these copies may be the same or different versions (alleles).

• Dominant and Recessive:
  Dominant alleles (represented by capital letters, like B) are expressed when at least one copy is present. Recessive alleles (lowercase, like b) are only expressed when two copies are present (bb). Heterozygous individuals (Bb) carry the recessive allele but show the dominant trait — they are 'carriers.'

• Sexual vs. Asexual Reproduction:
  Sexual reproduction mixes genetic material from two parents, creating unique offspring through random allele combination. Asexual reproduction produces genetically identical offspring (clones). Genetic variation from sexual reproduction is crucial for species survival because it provides the raw material for natural selection.

• Beyond Simple Inheritance:
  Most human traits aren't controlled by a single gene with simple dominant/recessive patterns. Traits like skin color, height, and intelligence are influenced by multiple genes (polygenic inheritance) AND environmental factors. This is why human diversity is so rich and complex.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Traits blend like paint"
  Reality: Traits don't blend — alleles maintain their identity. A Bb individual doesn't have 'medium' expression. The dominant allele is expressed and the recessive is masked but preserved.
  Strategy: Show that blue-eyed children can come from brown-eyed parents — the blue allele was preserved, not blended away.

• "You look exactly 50% like each parent"
  Reality: You inherit 50% of your DNA from each parent, but WHICH 50% is random. Siblings (except identical twins) get different random halves, which is why they can look very different from each other.
  Strategy: Ask: If you flip two coins 10 times each, will you always get the same results?

• "Dominant means better or more common"
  Reality: Dominant only means the allele is expressed when one copy is present. It doesn't mean it's 'better' or more frequent. Polydactyly (extra fingers) is dominant but rare.
  Strategy: List dominant traits that aren't more common in the population.

FACILITATION TIPS:
• Step 1: Let students explore the interface. Don't over-explain.
  Let them discover. Circulate and support, don't lecture.
• Step 2: Ask "When this goes up, what happens to that?" to
  guide positive/negative relationship decisions. Let students debate.
• Step 3: Give time for students to "break" the model — turn
  things on/off and observe. This is where real insight happens.
• Step 4: Don't give answers. Ask questions. Let curiosity drive
  the research. Celebrate when students' additions don't work as
  expected — that's authentic science.

ANSWER KEY:
Big Question: Why do you look similar to your parents but not exactly like them?
Answer: You inherit a random combination of alleles from both parents. Because each parent has two alleles for every gene and you only get one from each, the combination creates a unique genetic blueprint that makes you similar to — but different from — both parents!

Simulation Answers:
• Carrier Cross (Bb × Bb): The model produces offspring in a ~3:1 phenotype ratio: approximately 75% show the dominant trait (BB or Bb genotype) and 25% show the recessive trait (bb genotype). The genotype ratio is 1 BB : 2 Bb : 1 bb.
• Mixed Cross (Bb × bb): The model produces offspring in a ~1:1 phenotype ratio: approximately 50% show the dominant trait (Bb) and 50% show the recessive trait (bb). Every offspring gets a recessive allele from the bb parent.

Reflection Exemplars:
• Q: How can two brown-eyed parents have a blue-eyed child?
  A: If both parents are carriers (Bb), each has one dominant brown allele and one recessive blue allele. There's a 25% chance they both pass on their recessive allele, creating a bb offspring with blue eyes. The recessive allele was hidden (masked by dominant) but still passed on.
• Q: Why is genetic variation important?
  A: Variation means a population has different traits. If the environment changes (new disease, climate shift), some individuals may have traits that help them survive. Without variation (like in asexual reproduction), every organism is identical and equally vulnerable. This connects to natural selection from L01.

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.3 (Develop and use a model) + DCI LS3.A.2 (Genes are located on chromosomes; each pair of chromosomes carries alleles) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A student builds a model with Parent 1 Alleles and Parent 2 Alleles as external components and Offspring Genotype and Trait Expression (Phenotype) as internal components. When both parents are set to Bb (heterozygous) for a single trait, the model produces offspring in the following ratio: approximately 25% BB, 50% Bb, and 25% bb. The student observes that 75% of offspring show the dominant phenotype and 25% show the recessive phenotype.

What does the 3:1 phenotype ratio mean in terms of trait expression?

A. Approximately 75% of offspring display the dominant trait (BB and Bb genotypes both express it) and 25% display the recessive trait (only bb expresses it), because the dominant allele masks the recessive allele in heterozygous individuals.
B. Three offspring survive and one does not, which is why only three phenotypes appear.
C. Three out of four offspring are heterozygous and one is homozygous for the trait.
D. The dominant trait is three times stronger than the recessive trait, which is why it appears more often.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: In a Bb x Bb cross, the Punnett square yields BB, Bb, Bb, and bb genotypes. The dominant allele (B) masks the recessive allele (b) in heterozygous individuals, so BB and Bb both display the dominant phenotype. Only bb, with no dominant allele to mask it, displays the recessive phenotype. Three of four possible genotype outcomes show dominant, one shows recessive. If you chose C, this describes genotype ratios, not phenotype ratios. The phenotype ratio is 3:1 (dominant:recessive), while the genotype ratio is 1:2:1 (BB:Bb:bb). If you chose D, dominant and recessive describe how alleles interact, not their strength. A recessive allele is not weaker. It simply requires two copies (bb) to be expressed because one dominant allele masks it. If you chose B, the 3:1 ratio has nothing to do with survival. All four offspring genotypes are viable. The ratio describes which trait is visibly expressed.

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 6.2 (Construct an explanation) + DCI LS3.A.2 (Each parent contributes one allele per gene) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

In the model, when one parent is set to Bb (heterozygous) and the other to bb (homozygous recessive), the offspring are 50% Bb and 50% bb. A student claims that the recessive parent "weakened" the dominant trait because fewer offspring show the dominant phenotype compared to a Bb x Bb cross.

Using the model, what is the correct scientific explanation for the 1:1 ratio?

A. Each parent randomly contributes one allele. The Bb parent passes B or b with equal probability (50/50). The bb parent can only pass b. This produces Bb (dominant phenotype) or bb (recessive phenotype) in equal proportions. No alleles are weakened.
B. The student is correct. Recessive alleles weaken dominant alleles when they interact in offspring.
C. The dominant allele became recessive after mixing with the recessive parent's genes during reproduction.
D. The outcome depends on which parent is taller, not on allele combinations.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: Alleles are discrete units of information that are inherited intact. They are not diluted, weakened, or changed by the other parent's alleles. The Bb parent has two alleles and randomly passes one (50% chance of B, 50% chance of b). The bb parent has only b alleles to contribute. The resulting offspring are either Bb (one dominant allele masks the recessive, showing dominant phenotype) or bb (two recessive alleles, showing recessive phenotype). If you chose B, alleles do not weaken each other. Dominance is about whether one allele masks the other's expression, not about alleles changing each other's function. If you chose C, alleles do not change between dominant and recessive forms. B remains B and b remains b through inheritance. The allele's function is determined by its DNA sequence, which does not change during reproduction. If you chose D, an organism's height does not determine which alleles are passed to offspring. Allele inheritance follows probability, not physical characteristics of the parents.

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 7.1 (Engage in argument from evidence) + DCI LS3.B.1 (Sexual reproduction produces genetic variation) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

The model demonstrates that sexual reproduction produces offspring with genetic variation (different combinations of alleles), while asexual reproduction produces genetically identical offspring. A scientist studying a species of lizard discovers that a population reproducing asexually has been thriving for decades, with all individuals genetically identical.

A student argues that genetic variation does not matter for survival because the asexual lizard population is doing fine. Using the model and principles of genetics, which argument best evaluates this claim?

A. Genetic variation only matters for mammals, not reptiles, so the student's observation is valid.
B. The population is stable under current conditions, but the model shows that genetic variation provides a survival advantage when environments change. If a new disease, predator, or climate shift occurs, every genetically identical lizard would be equally vulnerable, potentially causing total population collapse. A genetically varied population would likely include some individuals with traits suited to the new conditions.
C. The student is correct. Identical organisms are better adapted because they have proven their traits work.
D. The asexual lizards must actually have hidden genetic variation that scientists have not detected yet.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: The student's argument only holds under stable conditions. Genetic variation is like an insurance policy. When the environment is stable, identical well-adapted organisms may thrive. But environments inevitably change (disease, climate, predators). A genetically uniform population has no backup traits to draw from, making it vulnerable to extinction from any single change. Genetic diversity ensures that some individuals in a population are likely to survive environmental shifts. If you chose C, being well-adapted now does not guarantee survival when conditions change. The traits that work today may become liabilities tomorrow. This is precisely why genetic variation matters for long-term species survival. If you chose A, genetic variation is important for all sexually reproducing organisms, not just mammals. The principles of natural selection apply equally to reptiles, insects, plants, and all other organisms. If you chose D, the scenario specifies that all individuals are genetically identical. While some asexual organisms do accumulate mutations over time, the argument should be evaluated on the information provided.

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.3 (Use a model to predict) + DCI LS3.A.1 (Mutations may affect proteins and organisms) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

In the STEM challenge, students used genotype data to identify parents of an unknown animal cub. The cub expresses a recessive trait (bb genotype). The available parent candidates have these genotypes: Pair 1 (BB x BB), Pair 2 (Bb x Bb), Pair 3 (BB x bb), and Pair 4 (Bb x bb).

Which parent pair could NOT have produced the bb cub?

A. Pair 4 (Bb x bb), because having one recessive parent prevents the dominant allele from being passed on.
B. Pair 3 (BB x bb) only, because Pair 1 could produce bb offspring through a rare mutation.
C. Pair 1 (BB x BB) and Pair 3 (BB x bb), because neither cross can produce an offspring with two recessive alleles. Pair 1 can only produce BB offspring, and Pair 3 can only produce BB or Bb offspring.
D. Pair 2 (Bb x Bb), because heterozygous parents can only produce heterozygous offspring.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: A bb offspring must inherit one b allele from each parent. In Pair 1 (BB x BB), neither parent has a b allele to contribute, so all offspring are BB. In Pair 3 (BB x bb), the BB parent can only contribute B, so offspring are either Bb or Bb. Neither pair can produce a bb offspring. Pairs 2 and 4 can both produce bb offspring because each parent has at least one b allele. If you chose D, Bb x Bb can produce BB, Bb, and bb offspring. The 25% chance of bb means this pair could absolutely produce the recessive cub. If you chose A, Pair 4 (Bb x bb) can produce Bb and bb offspring. The Bb parent has a 50% chance of passing b, and the bb parent always passes b, giving a 50% chance of bb offspring. If you chose B, while mutations do occur, the question asks about normal inheritance patterns. The Punnett square for BB x BB produces only BB offspring under normal genetic inheritance.

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 6.2 (Construct an explanation) + DCI LS3.B.1 (Variation among individuals of a species provides advantages) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

Two siblings share the same biological parents but look noticeably different from each other. One has brown eyes and straight hair while the other has green eyes and curly hair. Both parents have brown eyes and wavy hair.

Using the model of genetic inheritance, which explanation best accounts for why siblings from the same parents can look so different?

A. The environment (food, sunlight, temperature) determined the siblings' appearance, and genes played no role in the differences.
B. The siblings look different because one inherited genes only from the mother and the other inherited genes only from the father.
C. One sibling must have different biological parents than the other, because children from the same parents should look identical.
D. Each parent carries two alleles for every gene and randomly passes one allele to each offspring. Since each parent independently sorts and randomly contributes alleles to each child, siblings receive different combinations, producing different genotypes and therefore different phenotypes.

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: Each human has approximately 20,000 genes, and for each gene, one of two alleles is randomly passed to each offspring. The number of possible combinations is astronomical (over 8 million unique combinations from each parent, multiplied together). This random assortment means each sibling receives a unique set of alleles, producing unique trait combinations. Both parents being Bb for eye color means green eyes (bb) can appear in some children but not others. If you chose C, siblings from the same parents are NOT expected to be identical (except for identical twins). The random nature of allele distribution during reproduction ensures each sibling receives a different combination. If you chose A, while environment can influence some traits, the differences described (eye color, hair texture) are primarily determined by genetics. These traits are controlled by specific genes and allele combinations inherited from parents. If you chose B, every child inherits genes from both parents. Each child receives 23 chromosomes from each parent, for 46 total. The difference is which specific alleles were passed from each parent, not whether one parent was excluded.
