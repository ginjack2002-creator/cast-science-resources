## Platform

### Title
**Potential to Kinetic: The Energy Transformation Chain — How Height, Mass, and Friction Determine Where Energy Goes**

### Overview
Students model how energy transforms from potential to kinetic to thermal as objects move through a system. By building a computational model of height, mass, and friction, students discover that energy is never created or destroyed, only transformed. The roller coaster serves as the real-world anchor for understanding conservation of energy.

### Cover Image
[Dramatic image of a roller coaster at the peak of its highest hill with a city skyline, golden hour lighting, emphasizing height and potential energy]

### Grade
8th

### NGSS Standard
**MS-PS3-1**: Construct and interpret graphical displays of data to describe the relationships of kinetic energy to the mass of an object and to the speed of an object.
**MS-PS3-5**: Construct, use, and present arguments to support the claim that when the kinetic energy of an object changes, energy is transferred to or from the object.

### Learning Objectives
- Explain how potential energy converts to kinetic energy as an object falls
- Model how mass and height determine the amount of potential energy stored
- Demonstrate that total energy is conserved even when friction generates heat
- Predict how friction changes the distribution of energy in a system

### Component List (Starting Model: 5 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Height | External | Starting elevation of the object above the ground |
| Mass | External | Weight of the object |
| Potential Energy | Internal | Energy stored due to position (PE = mgh) |
| Kinetic Energy | Internal | Energy of motion (KE = 1/2mv^2) |
| Thermal Energy (Friction) | Internal | Heat generated by friction between surfaces |

Right now, a roller coaster sitting at the top of its highest hill

  ○ "Height" — we decide how high the object starts
  ○ "Mass" — we decide how heavy the object is
• INTERNAL (energy forms that respond):
  ○ "Potential Energy" — stored energy from position
  ○ "Kinetic Energy" — energy of motion
  ○ "Thermal Energy (Friction)" — heat from surface contact

Task B: ADD TO YOUR MODEL — 5 components total

Task C: SORT YOUR COMPONENTS
• EXTERNAL = Height and Mass (set before the object moves)
• INTERNAL = PE, KE, and Thermal Energy (change during motion)

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Always. No exceptions. That's conservation of energy.

## Step 2: ESTABLISH — Connect the Relationships

### Text Editor

```
TIME TO WIRE THE ENERGY CHAIN

Energy flows in a predictable chain: stored energy becomes motion
energy, and friction converts some motion energy into heat. Let's
map it.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Task A: OPEN CONNECTION MODE

Task B: DRAW YOUR RELATIONSHIPS
• Height → Potential Energy
• Mass → Potential Energy
• Potential Energy → Kinetic Energy
• Kinetic Energy → Thermal Energy (Friction)
• Thermal Energy → Kinetic Energy

Task C: SET POSITIVE OR NEGATIVE
  ○ Height → PE = POSITIVE (+) — More height = more stored energy
  ○ Mass → PE = POSITIVE (+) — More mass = more stored energy
  ○ PE → KE = POSITIVE (+) — As object falls, PE converts to KE
  ○ KE → Thermal = POSITIVE (+) — Friction converts KE to heat
  ○ Thermal → KE = NEGATIVE (−) — Energy lost to heat reduces KE

Task D: THE CONSERVATION RULE
• At ANY time: PE + KE + Thermal = Starting PE
• Energy transforms between forms but the TOTAL never changes

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

KEY INSIGHT: Energy is the ultimate shape-shifter. It changes
form — potential to kinetic to thermal — but never changes amount.
```

### Video Script

```
"Your components are ready. Now let's wire the energy chain.

First: Height to Potential Energy. Greater height means more PE.
If you hold a ball at 10 meters, it has more stored energy than
at 5 meters. POSITIVE. PE equals mgh — mass times gravity times
height.

Next: Mass to Potential Energy. Heavier objects at the same height
have more PE. A bowling ball has more stored energy than a tennis
ball at the same height. POSITIVE.

Now: Potential Energy to Kinetic Energy. As the object falls,
height decreases, so PE drops. But speed increases, so KE rises.
PE is transforming INTO KE. POSITIVE.

Then: Kinetic Energy to Thermal Energy. Friction between the
moving object and surfaces converts some motion energy into heat.
POSITIVE — more KE means more energy available to become heat.

And the reverse: Thermal Energy to Kinetic Energy. Energy diverted
to heat REDUCES the kinetic energy available for motion. The
object slows down. NEGATIVE.

Now here's the proof. Add PE + KE + Thermal at any point during
the motion. What do you get? The starting PE. Every time. The
total never changes. That's conservation of energy — one of the
most fundamental laws of physics.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

5 questions aligned to MS-PS3-1 and MS-PS3-5.

---

### Question 1

A student sets up an experiment with a ball on a ramp. She controls the height and mass. Which correctly identifies external vs. internal components?

A. External: Height, KE — Internal: Mass, PE, Thermal
B. External: Height, Mass — Internal: PE, KE, Thermal Energy
C. External: PE, Mass — Internal: Height, KE, Thermal
D. External: Height, Mass, PE — Internal: KE, Thermal

Correct Answer: B

---

### Question 2

A ball rolls down a frictionless ramp. At the top, PE = 100 J and KE = 0 J. At the bottom, PE = 0 J. What is the KE at the bottom?

A. 0 J — the energy was used up going down
B. 50 J — half was lost to gravity
C. 100 J — all PE converted to KE
D. More than 100 J — gravity added energy

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: On a frictionless ramp, ALL potential energy converts to kinetic energy. PE at top (100J) = KE at bottom (100J). Energy is conserved, not created or consumed.

---

### Question 3

A 2 kg ball released from 10 meters has 196 J of PE. On a friction ramp, it arrives at the bottom with 150 J of KE. What happened to the other 46 J?

A. It was destroyed by friction.
B. It was converted to thermal energy (heat) in the ramp surface.
C. It was lost to gravity during the fall.
D. It stayed as PE because the ball didn't reach zero height.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Friction converts KE to thermal energy. The 46 J is now heat in the ramp surface and ball. Total energy is still 196 J (150 J KE + 46 J thermal = 196 J). Nothing was destroyed.

---

### Question 4

Two balls — one 5 kg and one 1 kg — are released from the same 8-meter height. Which statement is correct?

A. The heavier ball falls faster and has more KE at the bottom.
B. Both have the same KE at the bottom because they fall at the same speed.
C. The heavier ball has more PE at the top and more KE at the bottom, but both fall at the same rate (ignoring air resistance).
D. The lighter ball has more KE because it accelerates faster.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: PE = mgh, so the 5 kg ball has 5x more PE. At the bottom, it has 5x more KE. But in a vacuum, both fall at the same rate — mass affects energy quantity, not fall speed. The heavier ball is slower to accelerate but has more inertia.

---

### Question 5

An engineer designs two ramps: one smooth metal, one rough carpet. Same ball, same height. Which prediction is correct?

A. The metal ramp delivers more KE at the bottom because less energy is lost to thermal.
B. Both deliver the same KE because conservation of energy means the total is always the same.
C. The carpet ramp delivers more KE because friction pushes the ball faster.
D. Neither delivers any KE because all energy becomes thermal on both surfaces.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: Both start with the same PE. The carpet converts more KE to thermal energy (friction), leaving less KE at the bottom. The metal ramp has less friction, so more PE converts directly to KE. Total energy (KE + thermal) is equal in both cases, but the distribution differs.
