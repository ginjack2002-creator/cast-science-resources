## Platform

### Title
**Why Is Gold So Different from Iron? — How Atomic Structure Determines the Properties of Every Material**

### Overview
Gold and iron are so different because their electron configurations produce different reactivities and material properties. Iron has electrons that readily interact with oxygen and water, causing rust. Gold's electrons are held in a configuration that resists chemical reactions — its outer electron... Students build a cause-and-effect model exploring how atomic number and electron configuration interact with reactivity, conductivity, and material properties in this system.

### NGSS Standard
**MS-PS1-1, MS-PS1-3**: Develop models to describe the atomic composition of simple molecules and extended structures. Gather and make sense of information to describe that synthetic materials come from natural resources and impact society.

### Learning Objectives
- Explain how atomic number determines an element's identity and position on the periodic table
- Model how electron configuration affects an element's reactivity and bonding behavior
- Predict material properties like conductivity and reactivity based on atomic structure
- Compare how differences at the atomic level produce dramatically different macroscopic properties

|-----------|------|-------------------|

| Reactivity | Internal | How readily the element undergoes chemical reactions with other substances |

• "All metals behave the same way" → Metals vary enormously in reactivity, conductivity, hardness, and other properties based on their at

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format. Each item is three-dimensional, assessing a Science and Engineering Practice (SEP), Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and Crosscutting Concept (CCC) simultaneously, aligned to MS-PS1-1, MS-PS1-3.

---

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.1 (Determine components of a system) + DCI + CCC4 (Describe a system in terms of its components)

A scientist is studying why is gold so different from iron?. She identifies these components: Atomic Number, Electron Configuration, Reactivity, Conductivity, Material Properties. She sorts them into external (things we set or cannot control) and internal (things that change based on other factors).

Which list correctly sorts the components?

A. External: Reactivity, Conductivity — Internal: Atomic Number, Electron Configuration, Material Properties
B. External: Material Properties, Atomic Number — Internal: Electron Configuration, Reactivity, Conductivity
C. External: Atomic Number, Reactivity — Internal: Electron Configuration, Conductivity, Material Properties
D. External: Atomic Number, Electron Configuration — Internal: Reactivity, Conductivity, Material Properties

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: Atomic Number and Electron Configuration are external because they represent conditions that drive the system. Reactivity, Conductivity, and Material Properties are internal because they change based on other factors in the system.
---

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships among components) + DCI + CCC4 (Describe components and interactions)

Element X has 11 protons and 1 electron in its outermost shell. Element Y has 18 protons and a completely full outermost shell. Which element is more likely to react violently with water, and why?

A. Electron Configuration controls Atomic Number, not the other way around
B. Atomic Number has no effect on Electron Configuration
C. As Atomic Number increases, Electron Configuration increases
D. As Atomic Number decreases, Electron Configuration increases

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: More protons means more electrons in a neutral atom, filling additional energy levels and creating more complex configurations.
