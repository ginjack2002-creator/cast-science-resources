## Platform

### Title
**Trophic Cascades: When Predators Disappear — How Removing One Species Can Collapse an Entire Ecosystem**

### Overview
Students model what happens when apex predators are removed from an ecosystem. By building a computational model of predator-prey-vegetation-erosion dynamics, students discover how a single species at the top of the food web regulates everything below it. The Yellowstone wolf reintroduction serves as the real-world anchor for this lesson on ecological interconnection and top-down control.

### NGSS Standard
**MS-LS2-1**: Analyze and interpret data to provide evidence for the effects of resource availability on organisms and populations of organisms in an ecosystem.
**MS-LS2-4**: Construct an argument supported by empirical evidence that changes to physical or biological components of an ecosystem affect populations.

### Learning Objectives
- Explain how apex predators regulate ecosystem balance through top-down control
- Model the cascade of effects when a predator is removed from a food web
- Predict how vegetation loss leads to erosion and ecosystem collapse
- Evaluate real-world evidence from the Yellowstone wolf reintroduction

### Component List (Starting Model: 5 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Apex Predator Population | External | Number of top predators such as wolves or sharks in the ecosystem |
| Herbivore Population | Internal | Number of prey species like deer or elk |
| Vegetation Health | Internal | Plant coverage, growth rate, and root strength |
| Erosion Rate | Internal | Rate of soil loss from bare, unprotected ground |
| Ecosystem Stability | Internal | Overall health and balance of the entire ecosystem |

## Step 3: VISUALIZE & EVALUATE — Run Your Ecosystem Model

### Text Editor

```
TIME TO REMOVE THE WOLVES

You built it. You connected it. Now let's see what happens when
the top predator vanishes — and what happens when it comes back.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Task A: RUN SCENARIO 1 — BALANCED ECOSYSTEM
• Set Apex Predator Population to a moderate, natural level (50%)
• Click "Play" and let it run for 20 time steps
• What do you predict will happen to Vegetation Health over time?
• Observe: Everything stays relatively stable

Task B: RUN SCENARIO 2 — PREDATOR REMOVAL
• Lock Apex Predator Population to 0%
• Resume the simulation
• What do you predict will happen to Herbivore Population?
• Then what happens to Vegetation? To Erosion? To Stability?

Task C: RUN SCENARIO 3 — PREDATOR REINTRODUCTION
• Restore Apex Predator Population to 50% after observing the collapse
• Resume the simulation
• How long does it take for the cascade to reverse?

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

WHAT TO LOOK FOR:

Predator Removal: When Apex Predator Population drops to 0%,
Herbivore Population surges rapidly within 5-10 generations.
Vegetation Health crashes as overgrazing intensifies. Erosion Rate
spikes as bare soil is exposed. Ecosystem Stability plummets as
the cascade of destruction reaches every level. One removal
triggers a chain reaction through four connected components.

Predator Reintroduction: When Apex Predators are restored to 50%,
Herbivore Population begins declining. Vegetation Health starts
recovering, but slowly — plants take time to regrow. Erosion Rate
gradually decreases as roots re-establish. Ecosystem Stability
begins climbing but takes significantly longer to recover than it
took to collapse. This asymmetry shows why prevention is easier
than restoration.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

REFLECTION QUESTIONS:
• Why did removing ONE species affect FOUR other components?
• Why does recovery take longer than collapse?
• Does your model match the real Yellowstone wolf story?
```

## Step 4: REVISE & EXTEND — Play, Research, Expand

### Text Editor

```
SET THE CONDITIONS — REFINE YOUR MODEL

Your model shows a relationship between Vegetation Health and Ecosystem Stability,
but right now that relationship applies ALL the time. In the
real system, Vegetation Health only affects Ecosystem Stability when Erosion Rate is active.

This is called a CONDITIONAL RELATIONSHIP — the connection
between two components depends on a third component's state.

Task A: SET THE CONDITION
   • Click on the connection arrow from Vegetation Health to Ecosystem Stability
   • In the toolbar, click "Conditions"
   • In the conditions panel, set: IF Erosion Rate is ON
   • Click "Save Conditions"

Task B: VERIFY THE MODEL
   • Run the simulation with Erosion Rate OFF and Vegetation Health ON
   • Ecosystem Stability should NOT respond to Vegetation Health alone
   • Now turn Erosion Rate ON — Vegetation Health's effect on Ecosystem Stability activates
   • This matches how the real system behaves

Task C: SET ADDITIONAL CONDITION
   • Click on the arrow from Erosion Rate to Ecosystem Stability
   • Click "Conditions" and set: IF Vegetation Health is ON
   • Save — Erosion Rate now only affects Ecosystem Stability when
     Vegetation Health is active

Your model now captures the conditional logic of the real system.
Not all relationships are always active — some depend on conditions.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

NOW LET'S PLAY AND EXPLORE

YOUR MODEL WORKS — BUT ECOSYSTEMS ARE EVEN MORE CONNECTED

You've modeled the core cascade. It explains how wolves connect
to rivers. But real ecosystems have even more connections you
haven't mapped yet.

Time to play, explore, and make your model BETTER.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

PLAY TIME CHALLENGES:

1. TELL THE STORY

2. BREAK THE SYSTEM

   • Bird Diversity — bird species depend on habitat variety

🔍 QUESTIONS TO INVESTIGATE:

   • What is the "landscape of fear" and how does it affect elk behavior?
```

```
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
TEACHER GUIDE: Trophic Cascades: When Predators Disappear
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

LESSON PURPOSE:
This lesson serves as a HOOK for ecosystem dynamics and food web
inquiry. Students build a model demonstrating how changes at one
trophic level cascade through the system, using the Yellowstone
wolf story as a compelling real-world anchor.

NGSS ALIGNMENT:
MS-LS2-1: Analyze and interpret data to provide evidence for the
effects of resource availability on organisms and populations in
an ecosystem.
MS-LS2-4: Construct an argument supported by empirical evidence
that changes to physical or biological components of an ecosystem
affect populations.

THREE-DIMENSIONAL LEARNING:
• Science Practice: Developing and Using Models — Students build a
  computational model showing how changes to one trophic level
  cascade through the entire ecosystem.
• Disciplinary Core Idea: LS2.C Ecosystem Dynamics, Functioning,
  and Resilience — Ecosystems are dynamic; changes to physical or
  biological components can cascade through the entire system.
• Crosscutting Concept: Cause and Effect — Students trace the
  causal chain from predator removal through herbivore explosion,
  vegetation loss, erosion, and ecosystem instability.

LEVER FRAMEWORK:
• L (Locate): Students identify apex predator population, herbivore
  population, vegetation health, erosion rate, and ecosystem
  stability as system components.
• E (Establish): Students determine that predators negatively affect
  herbivores, herbivores negatively affect vegetation, vegetation
  negatively affects erosion, and both vegetation and erosion affect
  ecosystem stability.
• V (Visualize): Students build the trophic cascade model showing
  how predator removal triggers a chain reaction through the
  entire ecosystem.
• Ev (Evaluate): Students compare balanced, predator-removed, and
  predator-reintroduced scenarios to measure the cascade effect
  and recovery time.
• R (Revise): Students add stream health or bird diversity
  components to extend the cascade model further.

• Slide 4 — LEVER Framework: 2 min

Slide 7: Say: "Let's remove the wolves and watch. Then let's bring

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Predators are bad for ecosystems" → Apex predators are essential
   regulators. Without them, herbivore populations grow unchecked
   and damage the very habitat that supports all species. Show
   Yellowstone data: More wolves = healthier ecosystem.
• "Removing one species only affects that species" → Ecosystems
   are interconnected networks. Removing one species triggers
   cascading effects through multiple trophic levels. In the model,
   remove predators and count how many OTHER components change.
• "Ecosystems always recover naturally" → Degraded ecosystems can
   become trapped in unhealthy stable states. Without intervention,
   the system needs an external push to restart. Ask: If the model
   runs for 50 generations without predators, does vegetation
   recover on its own?

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
Trophic cascades are among the most dramatic demonstrations of
ecological interconnection. The removal or addition of a single
species at the top of a food web can trigger a chain of effects
that transforms entire landscapes.

What Is a Trophic Cascade? A trophic cascade occurs when changes
at one level of a food web ripple through other levels. Top-down
cascades happen when predator changes affect everything below.

The Yellowstone Wolf Story: Wolves were eliminated from Yellowstone
by 1926. Elk populations exploded and overgrazed willows, aspens,
and cottonwoods. When 41 wolves were reintroduced in 1995-96,
elk behavior changed immediately. Vegetation regrew, banks
stabilized, and rivers literally changed course.

The Ecology of Fear: Predators affect prey not just by killing
them but by changing their behavior. The 'landscape of fear'
describes how prey avoid areas where predators hunt, allowing
vegetation to recover.

Marine Trophic Cascades: When sea otters were hunted to near-
extinction, sea urchin populations exploded and devoured kelp
forests. Restoring otters allowed kelp forests to recover.

Challenge: Research the sea otter cascade. Add stream health and

ELL: Visual vocabulary cards. Pair with English-proficient partner.

• ELA: Write a persuasive essay arguing for or against predator

• Social Studies: Research the political and social conflicts
  surrounding wolf reintroduction in the American West

ASSESSMENT:
• Can student trace the cascade from predators to ecosystem stability?
• Does the model show correct positive/negative relationships?
• Did student compare removal vs. reintroduction scenarios?
• Did student add at least one researched component?
```

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format, aligned to MS-LS2-1 and MS-LS2-4.

---

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.1 (Determine components of a system) + DCI LS2.C (Ecosystem Dynamics) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

An ecologist is building a model of a forest ecosystem that includes wolves, elk, vegetation, soil erosion, and overall ecosystem health. She needs to sort these into external components (things that can be directly controlled) and internal components (things that respond to changes in the system).

Which list correctly sorts the components?

A. External: Wolves, Vegetation — Internal: Elk, Erosion, Ecosystem Health
B. External: Ecosystem Health — Internal: Wolves, Elk, Vegetation, Erosion
C. External: Wolves, Elk — Internal: Vegetation, Erosion, Ecosystem Health
D. External: Wolves — Internal: Elk, Vegetation, Erosion, Ecosystem Health

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: Wolves (apex predators) are external because humans can directly add or remove them through management programs. Elk, vegetation, erosion, and ecosystem health are all internal because they respond to wolf population levels and to each other. If you chose C, elk populations respond to predation and are internal. If you chose B, ecosystem health is an outcome, not an input. If you chose A, vegetation responds to herbivore grazing, making it internal.
---

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships) + DCI LS2.C (Ecosystem Dynamics) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

In a trophic cascade model, a student draws the following chain: Wolves → Elk → Vegetation → Erosion. The student labels all relationships as negative.

Which explanation best supports why the Vegetation → Erosion relationship is negative?

A. More vegetation means healthier soil that absorbs water, eliminating erosion entirely.
B. More vegetation means plant roots hold soil in place, reducing the amount of erosion from wind and water.
C. More vegetation creates shade that dries out the soil, making it less likely to erode.
D. More vegetation blocks rain from reaching the soil, so less water causes less erosion.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Plant roots physically bind soil particles together, and vegetation cover protects the surface from direct wind and rain impact. More vegetation means more root systems and more surface protection, directly reducing erosion. If you chose D, vegetation doesn't block significant rainfall. If you chose A, erosion is reduced but not eliminated. If you chose C, shade actually keeps soil moist, and the mechanism described is incorrect.
---

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships) + DCI LS2.C (Ecosystem Dynamics) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

Wolves are removed from a national park. A student uses a trophic cascade model to predict what will happen over the next 20 years. The model shows: Wolves (−) → Elk, Elk (−) → Vegetation, Vegetation (−) → Erosion.

Based on the model, what chain of events would most likely occur?

A. Elk increase → vegetation decreases → erosion increases → ecosystem degrades
B. Elk decrease → vegetation increases → erosion decreases → ecosystem improves
C. Elk stay the same → vegetation stays the same → erosion stays the same
D. Elk decrease → vegetation decreases → erosion decreases → ecosystem improves

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: When wolves are removed (predator population drops to zero), the negative relationship reverses: fewer predators means MORE elk. More elk means MORE grazing, so vegetation DECREASES. Less vegetation means MORE erosion. The cascade runs: removal of check → population explosion → overgrazing → erosion → ecosystem degradation. If you chose B, removing predators increases prey, not decreases. If you chose C, removing a key component always causes change. If you chose D, this incorrectly predicts elk declining without a predator.
---

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms) + DCI LS2.C (Ecosystem Dynamics) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

Wolves were removed from Yellowstone in 1926. Streambank erosion increased by 300% over the following decades. In 1995, wolves were reintroduced. By 2015, streambank vegetation had significantly recovered and erosion was declining.

Which observation from the data best supports the claim that wolves indirectly affect rivers?

A. The 1926 removal and 1995 reintroduction happened at different times, so they can't be compared.
B. Wolves eat elk near rivers, so their removal allowed elk to stand in the water and cause erosion.
C. Erosion would have increased anyway due to climate change, regardless of wolf presence.
D. Wolves were removed and erosion increased; wolves returned and erosion decreased — showing a correlation between wolf presence and stream conditions.

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: The temporal pattern — erosion increasing after wolf removal and decreasing after wolf return — provides strong evidence for an indirect connection. The mechanism runs through multiple trophic levels (wolves → elk behavior → vegetation → bank stability → erosion). If you chose B, wolves don't specifically target elk near rivers, and elk don't cause erosion by standing in water. If you chose A, the time difference doesn't invalidate the comparison when the same ecosystem is measured. If you chose C, the reversal after reintroduction contradicts a climate-only explanation.
---

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to predict) + DCI LS2.C (Ecosystem Dynamics) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A coastal ecosystem has experienced a decline in sea otters due to hunting. Kelp forests are disappearing, and fish populations are crashing. A marine biologist wants to restore the ecosystem.

Based on trophic cascade principles, which action would most likely restore the kelp forest ecosystem?

A. Wait for the ecosystem to naturally rebalance itself over time.
B. Remove all fishing boats from the area so fish populations recover first.
C. Protect and restore the sea otter population to control the animals eating the kelp.
D. Plant new kelp forests artificially to replace what was lost.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: In the sea otter cascade, otters eat sea urchins, which eat kelp. Without otters, urchin populations explode and devour kelp forests. Restoring the top predator (otters) would re-establish top-down control, reducing urchins and allowing kelp to recover naturally. If you chose D, planting kelp without controlling urchins means the new kelp would be eaten again. If you chose B, fish populations are crashing because of kelp loss, not fishing. If you chose A, the Yellowstone example shows degraded ecosystems often don't recover without intervention.
