## Platform

### Title
**Climate Feedback Loops: Why Small Changes Get Big — How CO2, Temperature, Ice, and Reflectivity Create a Runaway System**

### Overview
Students model why small increases in CO2 can trigger disproportionately large changes in global temperature. By building a computational model that includes the ice-albedo feedback loop, students discover how positive feedback mechanisms amplify initial changes and why climate scientists warn about tipping points. This is the first feedback loop model in the curriculum, introducing systems thinking at a new level.

### NGSS Standard
**MS-ESS2-6**: Develop and use a model to describe how unequal heating and rotation of the Earth cause patterns of atmospheric and oceanic circulation that determine regional climates.
**MS-ESS3-5**: Ask questions to clarify evidence of the factors that have caused the rise in global temperatures over the past century.

### Learning Objectives
- Explain how CO2 emissions drive the greenhouse effect and global warming
- Model the positive feedback loop between temperature, ice cover, and albedo
- Predict how feedback loops amplify small changes into large climate shifts
- Evaluate evidence for why climate scientists are concerned about tipping points

### Component List (Starting Model: 5 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| CO2 Emissions Rate | External | Amount of carbon dioxide released from human activities |
| Atmospheric CO2 Level | Internal | Concentration of greenhouse gases in the atmosphere |
| Global Temperature | Internal | Earth's average surface temperature |
| Ice Cover | Internal | Extent of ice at the Arctic, Antarctic, and glaciers |
| Albedo (Reflectivity) | Internal | How much incoming sunlight Earth's surface reflects back to space |

## Step 2: ESTABLISH — Connect the Feedback Loop

### Text Editor

```
TIME TO CLOSE THE LOOP

This is where it gets different. Previous models had chains —
A affects B affects C. This model has a LOOP — the last arrow
connects back to an earlier component. Watch for it.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Task A: OPEN CONNECTION MODE

Task B: DRAW YOUR RELATIONSHIPS
• CO2 Emissions Rate → Atmospheric CO2 Level
• Atmospheric CO2 Level → Global Temperature
• Global Temperature → Ice Cover
• Ice Cover → Albedo (Reflectivity)
• Albedo (Reflectivity) → Global Temperature ← THE LOOP CLOSES HERE

Task C: SET POSITIVE OR NEGATIVE
  ○ CO2 Emissions Rate → Atmospheric CO2 Level = POSITIVE (+)
    More burning releases more CO2 into the atmosphere

  ○ Atmospheric CO2 Level → Global Temperature = POSITIVE (+)
    Higher CO2 traps more heat through the greenhouse effect

  ○ Global Temperature → Ice Cover = NEGATIVE (−)
    Higher temperatures melt ice, reducing frozen surface area

  ○ Ice Cover → Albedo = POSITIVE (+)
    More ice means more reflective surface to bounce sunlight back

  ○ Albedo → Global Temperature = NEGATIVE (−)
    Higher albedo reflects more sunlight, cooling Earth
    Lower albedo absorbs more sunlight, warming Earth
    THIS IS WHERE THE LOOP CLOSES

Task D: THE FEEDBACK LOOP
• Follow the loop: Temperature rises → Ice melts → Albedo drops
  → More sunlight absorbed → Temperature rises MORE → More ice
  melts → Albedo drops MORE...
• This is a POSITIVE FEEDBACK LOOP — it amplifies the original change

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

KEY INSIGHT: The last arrow connects BACK to Global Temperature.
That's the loop. Small warming causes ice loss, which causes more
warming, which causes more ice loss. This is why small CO2 changes
lead to big temperature changes.
```

### Video Script

```
"Your pieces are on the board. Now we wire them — and this time,
the wiring is going to surprise you.

Click the 'Connect' icon.

First: CO2 Emissions Rate to Atmospheric CO2 Level. More burning,
more CO2 in the air. POSITIVE.

Next: Atmospheric CO2 Level to Global Temperature. More CO2 traps
more heat through the greenhouse effect. POSITIVE.

Then: Global Temperature to Ice Cover. When temperature goes UP,
does ice cover go UP or DOWN? Down. Ice melts. NEGATIVE.

Now: Ice Cover to Albedo. Ice is white and reflective. More ice
means more sunlight bounced back to space. POSITIVE.

And here's the moment — watch this last connection:
Albedo to Global Temperature. When albedo is HIGH, Earth reflects
more sunlight and stays COOLER. When albedo is LOW — when ice
has melted and revealed dark ocean — Earth ABSORBS more sunlight
and gets WARMER. NEGATIVE.

Now look at your model. Follow the arrows from Global Temperature.
Temperature up → ice down → albedo down → MORE sunlight absorbed
→ temperature up MORE → even more ice melts → albedo drops further.

See it? The loop CLOSES. The output feeds back into the input.
That's a positive feedback loop. Not positive as in 'good' —
positive as in 'amplifying.' It makes the original change BIGGER.

This is why climate scientists are alarmed. It's not just the CO2.
It's the feedback loop that multiplies CO2's effect. Small cause,
accelerating consequence.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

## Step 3: VISUALIZE & EVALUATE — Run Your Climate Model

### Text Editor

```
TIME TO WATCH THE FEEDBACK LOOP RUN

You've built the first feedback loop model in this curriculum.
Now let's see what happens at different emission levels — and
why scientists warn about tipping points.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Task A: SCENARIO 1 — PRE-INDUSTRIAL BASELINE
• Set CO2 Emissions Rate to 10% (natural background levels)
• Run for 50 time steps
• What happens to Ice Cover and Global Temperature?

Task B: SCENARIO 2 — CURRENT EMISSIONS
• Set CO2 Emissions Rate to 60% (approximate modern output)
• What happens to Albedo as ice melts?

Task C: SCENARIO 3 — RUNAWAY FEEDBACK
• Lock CO2 Emissions Rate to 90%
• Run for 50 years
• What happens when the feedback loop accelerates?

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

WHAT TO LOOK FOR:

Current Emissions: CO2 rises steadily. Temperature increases
gradually, causing Ice Cover to decline. As Ice Cover drops,
Albedo decreases, adding ADDITIONAL warming on top of the
greenhouse effect. The feedback loop is active but manageable.

Runaway Feedback: At 90% sustained emissions, the loop accelerates.
Temperature rises quickly → ice melts rapidly → albedo plummets →
absorbed sunlight adds massive warming → even more ice melts.
The graph shows exponential acceleration. This is the tipping
point scenario — the system 'runs away' from human control.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

REFLECTION QUESTIONS:
• Why does the temperature graph curve UPWARD instead of rising
  in a straight line?
• What's the difference between the greenhouse effect alone and
  the greenhouse effect PLUS the feedback loop?
• At what point does the system become hard to reverse?
```

### Video Script

```
"You've built the feedback loop. Now let's see it run.

Scenario one: Pre-industrial. Set emissions to 10%. This is Earth
before fossil fuels. Run it for 50 time steps.

Look at the graph. Temperature is stable. Ice Cover is stable.
Albedo is stable. The feedback loop exists, but it's barely active
at low emissions. The system is in balance.

Now let's enter the modern era. Set emissions to 60%. This is
approximately where we are today.

Watch. Atmospheric CO2 rises. Temperature follows. And here's
where the feedback loop kicks in — Ice Cover starts dropping.
As ice disappears, Albedo decreases. And that means MORE sunlight
is absorbed, adding EXTRA warming on top of the CO2 effect.

The temperature line isn't straight. It's starting to curve
upward. The feedback loop is amplifying the CO2 signal.

Now the scary scenario. Lock emissions to 90% for 50 years.

Watch the graph. Temperature doesn't just rise — it ACCELERATES.
The curve gets steeper over time. Ice Cover plummets. Albedo
crashes. Each degree of warming causes ice loss that causes more
warming that causes more ice loss.

This is exponential growth. This is why scientists use the word
'tipping point.' Once the feedback loop reaches a certain speed,
even cutting emissions may not stop it in time. The system has
momentum.

The pre-industrial graph was flat. The current graph curves. The
runaway graph goes exponential. Same planet. Same physics. The
only difference is how much CO2 we add — and the feedback loop
does the rest.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

## Step 4: REVISE & EXTEND — Play, Research, Expand

### Text Editor

```
SET THE CONDITIONS — REFINE YOUR MODEL

Your model shows a relationship between Atmospheric CO2 Level and Global Temperature,
but right now that relationship applies ALL the time. In the
real system, Atmospheric CO2 Level only affects Global Temperature when Albedo is active.

This is called a CONDITIONAL RELATIONSHIP — the connection
between two components depends on a third component's state.

Task A: SET THE CONDITION
   • Click on the connection arrow from Atmospheric CO2 Level to Global Temperature
   • In the toolbar, click "Conditions"
   • In the conditions panel, set: IF Albedo is ON
   • Click "Save Conditions"

Task B: VERIFY THE MODEL
   • Run the simulation with Albedo OFF and Atmospheric CO2 Level ON
   • Global Temperature should NOT respond to Atmospheric CO2 Level alone
   • Now turn Albedo ON — Atmospheric CO2 Level's effect on Global Temperature activates
   • This matches how the real system behaves

Task C: SET ADDITIONAL CONDITION
   • Click on the arrow from Albedo to Global Temperature
   • Click "Conditions" and set: IF Atmospheric CO2 Level is ON
   • Save — Albedo now only affects Global Temperature when
     Atmospheric CO2 Level is active

Your model now captures the conditional logic of the real system.
Not all relationships are always active — some depend on conditions.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

What story does your NEW model tell?

### Video Script

```
"Your climate feedback model works. CO2 drives warming, warming
melts ice, ice loss reduces albedo, and reduced albedo accelerates
warming. The loop amplifies small changes into big ones.

But the real climate system has ADDITIONAL loops.

Permafrost. Frozen ground in Siberia and Canada contains billions
of tons of methane — a greenhouse gas 80 times more potent than
CO2 over 20 years. When warming thaws permafrost, that methane
escapes, causing MORE warming, which thaws MORE permafrost.
That's a second feedback loop on top of the first.

Ocean heat absorption. Oceans absorb about 90% of excess heat,
which slows atmospheric warming but causes marine heat waves,
coral bleaching, and sea level rise. Where does that fit?

Your mission: Research ONE additional feedback mechanism. Add it
to your model. Does it create a second loop? Does it make the
system more or less stable?

This is how climate scientists work. They build models with
multiple feedback loops to predict what happens at different
emission levels. Your single-loop model is the foundation.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

5 multiple-choice questions aligned to MS-ESS2-6 and MS-ESS3-5.

---

### Question 1

A student builds a climate model with five components: CO2 Emissions Rate, Atmospheric CO2 Level, Global Temperature, Ice Cover, and Albedo. Which is the only external component?

A. Global Temperature
B. CO2 Emissions Rate
C. Albedo
D. Ice Cover

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: CO2 Emissions Rate is the only component directly controlled by human activity. All others respond to each other within the climate system's feedback loop.
---

### Question 2

In the ice-albedo feedback loop, ice melts and reveals dark ocean underneath. Why does this cause more warming?

A. Dark surfaces absorb more sunlight than white ice, converting solar energy to heat.
B. The ocean currents change when ice melts, bringing warm water to the surface.
C. Dark ocean water is warmer than ice, so it heats the air above it.
D. Melting ice releases trapped greenhouse gases that cause additional warming.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: Ice reflects 80-90% of sunlight (high albedo). Dark ocean absorbs 90-94% of sunlight (low albedo). When ice melts and reveals ocean, far more solar energy is absorbed and converted to heat, warming the area further and melting more ice.
---

### Question 3

Arctic ice cover has decreased by 40% since 1980. A student claims this creates a feedback loop causing more warming. Which chain of events supports this claim?

A. Less ice → less albedo → more sunlight absorbed → more warming → less ice
B. Less ice → more ocean exposed → more fish habitat → healthier ecosystem
C. Less ice → more evaporation → more clouds → more rain → more ice
D. Less ice → colder ocean → slower currents → less heat distribution → more ice

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: This describes the ice-albedo positive feedback loop. Less ice means lower reflectivity, which means more solar absorption, which causes more warming, which melts more ice. The loop amplifies the original change.
---

### Question 4

A student runs a climate model at 60% emissions for 50 years. The temperature graph curves upward instead of rising in a straight line. What explains the curve?

A. The model has a programming error that causes accelerating values.
B. CO2 emissions are increasing over time, adding more greenhouse gases each year.
C. The feedback loop amplifies the warming effect — each degree of warming triggers additional warming through ice-albedo changes.
D. The atmosphere can only hold a limited amount of CO2, so temperature rises faster as it approaches the limit.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: The curve is caused by the positive feedback loop. Initial warming melts ice, which reduces albedo, which absorbs more sunlight, which causes additional warming beyond the CO2 greenhouse effect alone. This amplification creates exponential-looking growth rather than a straight line.
---

### Question 5

A city wants to address climate change by intervening in the feedback loop. Based on a climate model, which strategy would most effectively slow the loop?

A. Plant trees near glaciers to shade the ice from sunlight.
B. Build sea walls to protect against rising seas from ice melt.
C. Paint all roads white to increase the city's albedo.
D. Reduce CO2 emissions to decrease the input that drives the entire feedback loop.

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: CO2 emissions are the external input that drives the entire feedback loop. Reducing emissions addresses the root cause. While albedo enhancement (C, D) targets one link in the loop, reducing the input that starts the chain is the most effective intervention. Sea walls (A) address a consequence but don't slow the feedback loop itself.
---
