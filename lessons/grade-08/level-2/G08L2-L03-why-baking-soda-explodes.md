## Platform

### Title
**Why Baking Soda Explodes: Reaction Rates and Energy — How Reactant Amount and Surface Area Control Chemical Reactions**

### Overview
Students model why baking soda and vinegar react with different intensities depending on conditions. By building a computational model of reactant amount, surface area, reaction rate, and gas production, students discover how chemical reactions work at the particle level and prove that mass is always conserved. This lesson serves as a hook for deeper inquiry into chemical reactions, conservation of mass, and atomic theory.

[Dramatic close-up of a baking soda and vinegar reaction erupting with foamy bubbles overflowing from a flask, laboratory setting, high-speed photography style]

### Grade
8th

### NGSS Standard
**MS-PS1-2**: Analyze and interpret data on the properties of substances before and after the substances interact to determine if a chemical reaction has occurred.
**MS-PS1-5**: Develop and use a model to describe how the total number of atoms does not change in a chemical reaction and thus mass is conserved.

### Learning Objectives
- Explain how reactant amount and surface area affect the rate of a chemical reaction
- Model the relationship between reaction rate, gas production, and mass conservation
- Demonstrate that total system mass is conserved even when gas is produced
- Predict how changing reaction conditions affects the speed and intensity of a reaction

### Component List (Starting Model: 5 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Reactant Amount | External | How much baking soda and vinegar are combined |
| Surface Area | External | Whether the baking soda is fine powder or large chunks |
| Reaction Rate | Internal | Speed at which the chemical reaction proceeds |
| Gas Production (CO2) | Internal | Amount of carbon dioxide bubbles produced |
| Total System Mass | Internal | Combined mass of all reactants and products — always conserved |

## Step 2: ESTABLISH — Connect the Relationships

### Text Editor

```
TIME TO WIRE THE REACTION

These five components follow the laws of chemistry. Let's map
exactly how they connect.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Task A: OPEN CONNECTION MODE
• Click the "Connect" icon in the TOP LEFT corner

Task B: DRAW YOUR RELATIONSHIPS
• Click on "Reactant Amount" → drag to "Reaction Rate"
• Click on "Surface Area" → drag to "Reaction Rate"
• Click on "Reaction Rate" → drag to "Gas Production (CO2)"
• Click on "Reaction Rate" → drag to "Reactant Amount"

Task C: SET POSITIVE OR NEGATIVE
  ○ Reactant Amount → Reaction Rate = POSITIVE (+)
    More reactant means more particles available to collide and react

  ○ Surface Area → Reaction Rate = POSITIVE (+)
    Greater surface area exposes more particles for contact,
    dramatically increasing collision frequency

  ○ Reaction Rate → Gas Production = POSITIVE (+)
    Faster reaction produces CO2 gas more quickly

  ○ Reaction Rate → Reactant Amount = NEGATIVE (−)
    As the reaction proceeds, reactants are consumed and converted
    into products, decreasing the amount remaining

Task D: SPECIAL NOTE — TOTAL SYSTEM MASS
• Total System Mass remains CONSTANT
• In a sealed system, the total mass of all reactants equals
  the total mass of all products
• Atoms are rearranged, not created or destroyed

Task E: CHECK YOUR MODEL
• You should have 4 arrows plus the constant mass note
• 3 positive relationships, 1 negative relationship

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

KEY INSIGHT: More surface area means more collisions between
reactant particles — that's why powder explodes and chunks
barely fizz. Same amount of stuff, wildly different speed.
```

### Video Script

```
"Your pieces are on the board. Time to wire the chemistry.

Click the 'Connect' icon in the top left corner.

First: Reactant Amount to Reaction Rate. More baking soda and
vinegar means more particles available to collide. More collisions
means faster reaction. POSITIVE.

Next: Surface Area to Reaction Rate. This is the big one. Think
of it like dodgeball — if everyone stands in a tight cluster,
few people get hit. If everyone spreads out, lots of hits. Powder
has MASSIVE surface area compared to chunks. Same mass, way more
contact points. POSITIVE.

Then: Reaction Rate to Gas Production. Faster reaction means CO2
bubbles out faster. More vigorous fizzing. POSITIVE.

Now the interesting one: Reaction Rate to Reactant Amount. As
the reaction runs, reactants get USED UP. They're converting into
products. So faster reaction means reactants decrease faster.
NEGATIVE.

And here's the punchline — Total System Mass. In a sealed system,
this line is FLAT. It never changes. The atoms from baking soda
and vinegar don't vanish — they rearrange into sodium acetate,
water, and CO2 gas. Same atoms. Same mass. Different arrangement.

That's conservation of mass. The most fundamental law in chemistry.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

## Step 3: VISUALIZE & EVALUATE — Run Your Reaction Model

### Text Editor

```
TIME TO MAKE IT EXPLODE (AND PROVE MASS IS CONSERVED)

You built it. You connected it. Now let's see if powder really
reacts differently from chunks — and whether mass truly stays
the same.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Task A: RUN SCENARIO 1 — STANDARD REACTION
• Set Reactant Amount to moderate (50%)
• Set Surface Area to medium (50%)
• Click "Play" and observe Gas Production over time
• What do you predict will happen to Total System Mass?

Task B: RUN SCENARIO 2 — MAXIMUM EXPLOSION
• Lock Reactant Amount to 100%
• Lock Surface Area to 100% (fine powder)
• Resume the simulation
• What do you predict will happen to Reaction Rate and Gas Production?

Task C: RUN SCENARIO 3 — SLOW FIZZ
• Set Reactant Amount to 30%
• Set Surface Area to 20% (large chunks)
• Resume the simulation
• How does this compare to the maximum explosion?

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

WHAT TO LOOK FOR:

Maximum Explosion: Reaction Rate spikes immediately. Gas Production
surges. But reactants are consumed quickly, so the reaction ends
sooner. Total System Mass stays perfectly flat.

Slow Fizz: Reaction Rate is much slower. Gas Production is gradual.
The reaction takes longer to complete because fewer particle surfaces
are exposed. Total System Mass is AGAIN perfectly constant.

THE PROOF: In both scenarios — explosive or gentle — Total System
Mass is identical. Conservation of mass holds regardless of speed.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

REFLECTION QUESTIONS:
• Why does powder react faster than chunks if the mass is the same?
• If mass is conserved, why does a fizzing open container weigh
  less afterward?
• What does the flat Total System Mass line PROVE about atoms?
```

```
Now let's crank it up. Lock Reactant Amount to 100%. Surface Area

Watch. Reaction Rate SPIKES. Gas Production surges — bubbles

rearranged, not created or destroyed. The gas has mass. The water
has mass. Everything is accounted for.

If the container is open and gas escapes, the SCALE shows less
mass — but the atoms are still out there, floating in the air.
Nothing was destroyed. It just left the container.
```

## Step 4: REVISE & EXTEND — Play, Research, Expand

### Text Editor

```
SET THE CONDITIONS — REFINE YOUR MODEL

Your model shows a relationship between Reactant Amount and Reaction Rate,
but right now that relationship applies ALL the time. In the
real system, Reactant Amount only affects Reaction Rate when Surface Area is active.

This is called a CONDITIONAL RELATIONSHIP — the connection
between two components depends on a third component's state.

Task A: SET THE CONDITION
   • Click on the connection arrow from Reactant Amount to Reaction Rate
   • In the toolbar, click "Conditions"
   • In the conditions panel, set: IF Surface Area is ON
   • Click "Save Conditions"

Task B: VERIFY THE MODEL
   • Run the simulation with Surface Area OFF and Reactant Amount ON
   • Reaction Rate should NOT respond to Reactant Amount alone
   • Now turn Surface Area ON — Reactant Amount's effect on Reaction Rate activates
   • This matches how the real system behaves

Task C: SET ADDITIONAL CONDITION
   • Click on the arrow from Surface Area to Reaction Rate
   • Click "Conditions" and set: IF Reactant Amount is ON
   • Save — Surface Area now only affects Reaction Rate when
     Reactant Amount is active

Your model now captures the conditional logic of the real system.
Not all relationships are always active — some depend on conditions.
```

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment and post-assessment. Aligned to MS-PS1-2 and MS-PS1-5.

---

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.1 + DCI PS1.B + CCC5 (Energy and Matter)

A chemistry student sets up an experiment with baking soda and vinegar. She identifies five factors: the amount of reactant, the surface area of the baking soda, the speed of the reaction, the amount of gas produced, and the total mass of the system. She needs to sort these into things she controls (external) and things that respond (internal).

Which sorting is correct?

A. External: Reactant Amount, Surface Area — Internal: Reaction Rate, Gas Production, Total Mass
B. External: Gas Production, Surface Area — Internal: Reactant Amount, Reaction Rate, Total Mass
C. External: Reactant Amount, Surface Area, Total Mass — Internal: Reaction Rate, Gas Production
D. External: Reactant Amount, Reaction Rate — Internal: Surface Area, Gas Production, Total Mass

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: The experimenter controls how much reactant to use and whether it's powder or chunks (surface area). Reaction rate, gas production, and total mass are outcomes determined by those inputs and the laws of chemistry.
---

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 + DCI PS1.B + CCC5

A student uses powdered baking soda in one trial and a single large chunk of baking soda in another. Both trials use the same mass of baking soda and the same amount of vinegar. The powdered trial reacts much faster.

Which explanation best describes why surface area affects reaction rate?

A. Powdered baking soda is a different chemical than chunk baking soda, so it reacts differently.
B. Powdered baking soda has more exposed surface where vinegar particles can make contact, increasing the number of simultaneous collisions.
C. Powdered baking soda is lighter than chunk baking soda, so it reacts faster.
D. The powder dissolves in the vinegar first, which makes a new chemical that reacts faster.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Grinding a solid into powder dramatically increases the number of surface particles available for contact with the liquid reactant. More contact points means more simultaneous chemical collisions, which means a faster overall reaction rate.
---

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 + DCI PS1.B + CCC5

A student seals baking soda and vinegar in a plastic bag, weighs it (150g), lets the reaction occur completely, and weighs it again. The scale reads 150g. The student then opens the bag and weighs it. The scale now reads 147g.

What happened to the 3 grams?

A. Measurement error caused the difference between sealed and open readings.
B. The reaction converted 3 grams of mass into energy.
C. The 3 grams of CO2 gas escaped when the bag was opened — the gas has mass.
D. The chemical reaction destroyed 3 grams of matter.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: The sealed bag proved mass is conserved (150g before = 150g after). When opened, CO2 gas escaped into the air, taking its 3 grams of mass with it. The atoms weren't destroyed — they left the container.
---

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 + DCI PS1.B + CCC5

Two students run the same baking soda and vinegar reaction. Student A uses fine powder and gets an intense 10-second eruption. Student B uses large chunks and gets a gentle fizz that lasts 2 minutes. Both use the same mass of baking soda and the same volume of vinegar.

Which prediction about total gas production is correct?

A. Student A produces more total gas because the reaction was more intense.
B. Neither can be predicted without knowing the temperature of the vinegar.
C. Student B produces more total gas because the reaction lasted longer.
D. Both produce the same total amount of gas because they started with the same amount of reactant.

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: Total product depends on the amount of reactant, not the speed. Same mass of baking soda produces the same total CO2 regardless of whether it reacts in 10 seconds or 2 minutes. Surface area changes RATE, not total YIELD.
---

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 + DCI PS1.B + CCC5

A chemical engineer needs to design a reaction process for a factory. The reaction produces a useful gas as a product. The engineer wants to collect all the gas and verify that no mass is lost during the process.

Which experimental setup would best demonstrate conservation of mass?

A. Run the reaction twice and average the mass measurements to reduce error.
B. Run the reaction and measure only the mass of the solid products remaining.
C. Run the reaction in a sealed container, weigh before and after, then open and weigh again to account for gas mass.
D. Run the reaction in an open flask and weigh it before and after.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: A sealed container captures all products including gas, proving mass is conserved (same mass before and after). Opening the container and reweighing shows exactly how much mass the gas carried away. This two-step approach proves both conservation AND accounts for the gas.
