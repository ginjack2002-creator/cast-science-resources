## Platform

### Title
**Survival of the Fittest: Can You Outrun Natural Selection? — How Environmental Pressure and Genetic Diversity Shape Who Survives**

### Overview
Students model why some populations survive environmental change while others collapse. By building a computational model of environmental pressure, population diversity, and trait frequency, students discover how natural selection acts on existing genetic variation to shift populations over generations. This lesson serves as a hook for deeper inquiry into evolution, genetics, and conservation biology.

### Cover Image
[Dramatic scene of diverse animals in a challenging desert landscape, some thriving and some struggling, golden hour lighting with dust in the air]

### Grade
8th

### NGSS Standard
**MS-LS4-4**: Construct an explanation based on evidence that describes how genetic variations of traits in a population increase some individuals' probability of surviving and reproducing in a specific environment.
**MS-LS4-6**: Use mathematical representations to support explanations of how natural selection may lead to increases and decreases of specific traits in populations over time.

### Learning Objectives
- Explain how environmental pressure selects for certain traits over others
- Model how population diversity affects a species' ability to survive change
- Use mathematical representations to track trait frequency changes across generations
- Predict outcomes for populations with high vs. low genetic diversity under stress

### Component List (Starting Model: 5 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Environmental Pressure | External | Intensity of drought, predators, or disease affecting the population |
| Population Diversity | External | Range of trait variations present in the population |
| Favorable Trait Frequency | Internal | Percentage of the population carrying advantageous traits |
| Survival Rate | Internal | Percentage of individuals that survive each generation |
| Reproduction Rate | Internal | Number of offspring produced per surviving individual |

## Step 1: LOCATE — Build Your Natural Selection System

### Text Editor

```
SURVIVAL OF THE FITTEST

If a deadly drought hit tomorrow, would every animal in the herd
survive? Not a chance. Some have traits that help them handle the

doesn't. You're about to model exactly how.

Task A: CHOOSE YOUR COMPONENTS
• Look at the component panel on the RIGHT side of your screen
• Find the EXTERNAL components (things set before the system runs):
  ○ Click "Environmental Pressure" — nature sets this, not the organisms
  ○ Click "Population Diversity" — this is the existing trait range in the gene pool
• Find the INTERNAL components (things that change within the system):
  ○ Click "Favorable Trait Frequency" — this shifts based on pressure and diversity
  ○ Click "Survival Rate" — this responds to which traits match the environment
  ○ Click "Reproduction Rate" — survivors pass on their traits

Task B: ADD TO YOUR MODEL
• Click the PLUS (+) button to add each component to your canvas
• You should now see 5 components on your canvas

Task C: SORT YOUR COMPONENTS
• Sort your components into EXTERNAL and INTERNAL
• EXTERNAL = conditions imposed on the population from outside
• INTERNAL = things that change as a direct result of those conditions
• Your teacher will show you how this works in the video

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

WHY THIS SORTING MATTERS:
Environmental Pressure and Population Diversity are external because
they represent conditions imposed on or brought into the population
from outside — the environment creates pressure, and diversity is a
pre-existing condition of the gene pool. Favorable Trait Frequency,
Survival Rate, and Reproduction Rate are internal because they change
as direct consequences of the interaction between pressure and diversity.

You now have the pieces of natural selection.
But pieces alone don't explain anything — next, we connect them.
```

## Step 2: ESTABLISH — Connect the Relationships

### Text Editor

```
TIME TO DRAW THE LINES OF SELECTION

Those five components don't just sit there — they DRIVE each other.
When one changes, others change too. These connections ARE natural
selection.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Task A: OPEN CONNECTION MODE
• Click the "Connect" icon in the TOP LEFT corner of your screen
• Your cursor is now ready to draw relationship arrows

Task B: DRAW YOUR RELATIONSHIPS
• Click on "Environmental Pressure" and drag an arrow to "Survival Rate"
• Click on "Population Diversity" and drag an arrow to "Favorable Trait Frequency"
• Click on "Favorable Trait Frequency" and drag an arrow to "Survival Rate"
• Click on "Survival Rate" and drag an arrow to "Reproduction Rate"
• Click on "Reproduction Rate" and drag an arrow to "Favorable Trait Frequency"

Task C: SET POSITIVE OR NEGATIVE
• Look at the +/- toggle in the TOP LEFT corner
• For each connection, ask: "When this goes UP, does the other go UP or DOWN?"

  ○ Environmental Pressure → Survival Rate = NEGATIVE (−)
    Harsher conditions reduce the percentage that survive

  ○ Population Diversity → Favorable Trait Frequency = POSITIVE (+)
    Greater diversity means a higher chance some carry the right traits

  ○ Favorable Trait Frequency → Survival Rate = POSITIVE (+)
    More individuals with advantageous traits = more survivors

  ○ Survival Rate → Reproduction Rate = POSITIVE (+)
    More survivors = more offspring in the next generation

  ○ Reproduction Rate → Favorable Trait Frequency = POSITIVE (+)
    Survivors with favorable traits reproduce, increasing those traits
    in the next generation — this IS natural selection in action

Task D: CHECK YOUR MODEL
• You should have 5 arrows total
• 1 negative relationship, 4 positive relationships
• Notice the FEEDBACK LOOP: Favorable Trait Frequency → Survival Rate
  → Reproduction Rate → back to Favorable Trait Frequency!

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

KEY INSIGHT: Environmental pressure doesn't CREATE favorable traits.
It REVEALS which existing traits matter. Diversity is the raw material.
Selection is the filter.
```

## Step 3: VISUALIZE & EVALUATE — Run Your Natural Selection Model

### Text Editor

```
TIME TO SEE SELECTION IN ACTION

You built it. You connected it. Now let's test whether natural

world.

Task A: RUN SCENARIO 1 — STABLE ENVIRONMENT
• Set Environmental Pressure to moderate (50%)
• Set Population Diversity to moderate (50%)
• Click "Play" and let it run for about 20 generations
• What do you predict will happen to Survival Rate over time?
• Observe how all the lines relate to each other

Task B: RUN SCENARIO 2 — LOW DIVERSITY CRISIS
• Lock Environmental Pressure to 90%
• Lock Population Diversity to 20%
• Resume the simulation
• What do you predict will happen to Favorable Trait Frequency?
• Watch what happens to Survival Rate and Reproduction Rate

Task C: RUN SCENARIO 3 — HIGH DIVERSITY ADVANTAGE
• Keep Environmental Pressure locked at 90%
• Change Population Diversity to 80%
• Resume the simulation
• How does this compare to the low diversity scenario?

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

WHAT TO LOOK FOR:

Scenario 2 (Low Diversity Crisis): When Environmental Pressure is
at 90% and Population Diversity is only 20%, Favorable Trait
Frequency starts very low — there simply aren't enough individuals
with the right traits. Survival Rate crashes within a few generations,
and Reproduction Rate follows. The population spirals toward
extinction because natural selection has too little variation to
work with.

Scenario 3 (High Diversity Advantage): When Environmental Pressure
is the same 90% but Population Diversity is 80%, Favorable Trait
Frequency starts much higher. Survival Rate dips initially but
stabilizes as favorable traits spread. Within 10-15 generations,
the population has adapted — Favorable Trait Frequency climbs toward
70-80%. This demonstrates why biodiversity is critical for
long-term survival.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

REFLECTION QUESTIONS:
• Which population survived the same pressure — the diverse one
  or the uniform one?
• What does this tell you about why genetic diversity matters?
• How does the feedback loop (trait → survival → reproduction →
  trait) drive adaptation over time?
```

## Step 4: REVISE & EXTEND — Play, Research, Expand

### Text Editor

```
SET THE CONDITIONS — REFINE YOUR MODEL

Your model shows a relationship between Environmental Pressure and Survival Rate,
but right now that relationship applies ALL the time. In the
real system, Environmental Pressure only affects Survival Rate when Favorable Trait Frequency is active.

This is called a CONDITIONAL RELATIONSHIP — the connection
between two components depends on a third component's state.

Task A: SET THE CONDITION
   • Click on the connection arrow from Environmental Pressure to Survival Rate
   • In the toolbar, click "Conditions"
   • In the conditions panel, set: IF Favorable Trait Frequency is ON
   • Click "Save Conditions"

Task B: VERIFY THE MODEL
   • Run the simulation with Favorable Trait Frequency OFF and Environmental Pressure ON
   • Survival Rate should NOT respond to Environmental Pressure alone
   • Now turn Favorable Trait Frequency ON — Environmental Pressure's effect on Survival Rate activates
   • This matches how the real system behaves

Task C: SET ADDITIONAL CONDITION
   • Click on the arrow from Population Diversity to Favorable Trait Frequency
   • Click "Conditions" and set: IF Reproduction Rate is ON
   • Save — Population Diversity now only affects Favorable Trait Frequency when
     Reproduction Rate is active

Your model now captures the conditional logic of the real system.
Not all relationships are always active — some depend on conditions.
```

```
TEACHER GUIDE: Survival of the Fittest
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

LESSON PURPOSE:
This lesson serves as a HOOK for natural selection and evolution
inquiry. Students build a computational model that demonstrates
how environmental pressure acts on existing genetic variation,
then identify gaps that drive further research into genetics,
adaptation, and conservation biology.

NGSS ALIGNMENT:
MS-LS4-4: Construct an explanation based on evidence that describes
how genetic variations of traits in a population increase some
individuals' probability of surviving and reproducing in a specific
environment.
MS-LS4-6: Use mathematical representations to support explanations
of how natural selection may lead to increases and decreases of
specific traits in populations over time.

THREE-DIMENSIONAL LEARNING:
• Science Practice: Developing and Using Models — Students develop
  a computational model to demonstrate how natural selection changes
  trait frequencies in a population over multiple generations.
• Disciplinary Core Idea: LS4.B Natural Selection — Natural selection
  leads to the predominance of certain traits in a population and
  the suppression of others based on environmental conditions.
• Crosscutting Concept: Cause and Effect — Students identify that
  environmental pressure CAUSES differential survival, which leads
  to the EFFECT of changed trait frequencies across generations.

LEVER FRAMEWORK:
• L (Locate): Students identify environmental pressure, population
  diversity, favorable trait frequency, survival rate, and
  reproduction rate as system components.
• E (Establish): Students determine that environmental pressure
  negatively affects survival rate, population diversity positively
  affects favorable trait frequency, and favorable traits positively
  affect survival, which drives reproduction.
• V (Visualize): Students build the natural selection model showing
  how environmental pressure and genetic diversity interact to drive
  population change.
• Ev (Evaluate): Students compare low-diversity vs. high-diversity

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Survival of the fittest means the strongest survive" → Fitness
   in biology means reproductive success in a specific environment,
   not physical strength. A small, camouflaged lizard may be 'fitter'
   than a large, conspicuous one if predators are the main pressure.
   Ask: Would the strongest animal survive a disease outbreak? What
   trait would actually help?
• "Animals choose to adapt or evolve on purpose" → Individual
   organisms cannot choose to evolve. Natural selection acts on
   variation that already exists in the population. Giraffes didn't
   'decide' to grow longer necks — individuals with slightly longer
   necks survived better and reproduced more. Run the model: Can the
   population 'choose' which traits to have? What determines which
   traits spread?
• "Evolution always makes species better" → Natural selection
   optimizes for the CURRENT environment. If conditions change again,
   previously favorable traits may become harmful. Evolution doesn't
   have a direction or goal — it responds to present conditions only.
   Change the environmental pressure in the model mid-simulation.
   Watch how previously favorable traits can become disadvantageous.

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
Natural selection is one of the key mechanisms of evolution, first
described by Charles Darwin and Alfred Russel Wallace. It explains
how populations change over time as environmental pressures favor
certain inherited traits over others. Understanding natural selection
is critical for conservation biology, agriculture, and medicine.

The Three Requirements for Natural Selection: Natural selection
requires three conditions: variation (individuals differ in their
traits), heritability (traits are passed from parents to offspring),
and differential survival (some traits provide better chances of
surviving and reproducing in the current environment). When all
three are present, populations evolve.

Genetic Diversity as a Survival Tool: A population's genetic
diversity is its toolkit for facing change. Large, diverse
populations contain many trait variations, increasing the odds
that some individuals will have traits suited to new conditions.
Small, inbred populations with low diversity are vulnerable because
they may lack the genetic 'raw material' for adaptation.

Mathematical Patterns of Selection: Trait frequency changes follow
predictable mathematical patterns. Under strong selection pressure,
favorable trait frequency increases rapidly in early generations,
then slows as it approaches fixation (100%). The Hardy-Weinberg
principle describes the mathematical conditions under which trait
frequencies remain stable.

Real-World Examples: The peppered moth in industrial England
evolved from mostly light-colored to mostly dark-colored in just
decades as pollution darkened tree bark. Darwin's finches on the
Galapagos Islands show beak size shifts in response to drought
years. Antibiotic-resistant bacteria demonstrate natural selection
happening in days rather than years.
```

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format. Each item is three-dimensional, assessing a Science and Engineering Practice (SEP), Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and Crosscutting Concept (CCC) simultaneously, aligned to MS-LS4-4 and MS-LS4-6.

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.1 (Determine components of a system) + DCI LS4.B (Natural Selection) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A biologist is studying why some lizard populations survive droughts while others die out. She identifies five key factors: environmental pressure, population diversity, favorable trait frequency, survival rate, and reproduction rate. She sorts them into two groups: things imposed on the population from outside (external) and things that change within the system (internal).

Which list correctly sorts the components of the natural selection system?

A. External: Population Diversity, Reproduction Rate — Internal: Environmental Pressure, Favorable Trait Frequency, Survival Rate
B. External: Favorable Trait Frequency, Environmental Pressure — Internal: Population Diversity, Survival Rate, Reproduction Rate
C. External: Environmental Pressure, Survival Rate — Internal: Population Diversity, Favorable Trait Frequency, Reproduction Rate
D. External: Environmental Pressure, Population Diversity — Internal: Favorable Trait Frequency, Survival Rate, Reproduction Rate

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: Environmental Pressure comes from outside the population (drought, predators, disease), and Population Diversity is a pre-existing condition of the gene pool. These are external inputs. Favorable Trait Frequency, Survival Rate, and Reproduction Rate all change as consequences of how pressure interacts with diversity, making them internal. If you chose C, survival rate is an outcome of the system, not an external input. If you chose B, favorable trait frequency changes within the system based on selection. If you chose A, reproduction rate is an internal response, not an external condition.

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships among components) + DCI LS4.B (Natural Selection) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

In a natural selection model, a student draws an arrow from Environmental Pressure to Survival Rate and labels it as a negative relationship. Another student disagrees and says it should be positive because "more pressure means the population works harder to survive."

Which explanation best supports why the relationship between Environmental Pressure and Survival Rate is negative?

A. More environmental pressure kills the weakest organisms, which helps the population get stronger over time.
B. More environmental pressure means harsher conditions that reduce the percentage of individuals surviving each generation.
C. Environmental pressure and survival rate always move in opposite directions because nature balances everything.
D. Less environmental pressure causes organisms to become lazy and stop adapting, lowering survival.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: A negative relationship means when one goes up, the other goes down. Higher environmental pressure (more drought, disease, or predators) directly reduces the percentage of individuals that survive each generation. If you chose A, this describes a long-term population effect but not the direct relationship between pressure and survival rate. If you chose D, this anthropomorphizes organisms and misrepresents how selection works. If you chose C, this oversimplifies the relationship without explaining the mechanism.

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships among components) + DCI LS4.B (Natural Selection) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A student builds a model of natural selection. The student tests two scenarios with the same 90% environmental pressure: one with 20% population diversity and one with 80% population diversity.

Based on the model, what would most likely happen in each scenario?

A. Both populations would survive equally because they face the same pressure.
B. Neither population would survive because 90% pressure is too strong for any population.
C. The high-diversity population would maintain higher survival rates because more individuals carry favorable traits.
D. The low-diversity population would adapt faster because it has fewer competing traits.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: Higher population diversity means a greater chance that some individuals carry traits suited to the environment. With 80% diversity, Favorable Trait Frequency starts higher, which positively affects Survival Rate even under heavy pressure. If you chose A, same pressure does NOT mean same outcome — diversity determines how many individuals have the right traits. If you chose D, fewer traits means fewer options for selection, not faster adaptation. If you chose B, the model shows that high-diversity populations can adapt even under extreme pressure through the feedback loop.

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to explain/predict events) + DCI LS4.B (Natural Selection) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A population of lizards lives in a region where temperatures are rising. Only 15% of the population carries a heat-tolerance gene. After 10 generations of intense heat, 80% of survivors carry the gene.

Which best explains this shift in trait frequency?

A. Heat-tolerant lizards survived and reproduced at higher rates, increasing the frequency of the heat-tolerance gene in each generation.
B. The lizards evolved the heat-tolerance gene because they needed it to survive.
C. The intense heat caused mutations that gave more lizards the heat-tolerance gene.
D. The non-heat-tolerant lizards migrated away, leaving only heat-tolerant ones behind.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: Natural selection acts on existing variation. Heat-tolerant lizards survived the intense heat at higher rates than non-tolerant ones, and they passed the gene to their offspring. Over 10 generations, the feedback loop (survival → reproduction → increased trait frequency) shifted the population. If you chose B, organisms don't evolve traits because they "need" them — selection acts on traits that already exist. If you chose D, migration is a different mechanism and the problem states only trait frequency changed. If you chose C, environmental pressure doesn't direct mutations toward useful traits — mutations are random.

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to predict a scientific event) + DCI LS4.B (Natural Selection) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A conservation biologist is studying two populations of the same fish species. Population A has high genetic diversity with many trait variations. Population B was bred from a small group and has very low genetic diversity. Both populations face a new water pollution challenge.

Based on a natural selection model, which prediction is best supported?

A. Population B will adapt faster because its limited traits are more focused.
B. Neither population will survive because pollution affects all individuals equally regardless of their traits.
C. Population A is more likely to contain individuals with traits that help them survive the new pollution, giving it a survival advantage.
D. Both populations will adapt at the same rate because they are the same species.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: Greater genetic diversity means more trait variations, which increases the probability that some individuals carry traits suited to the new challenge. Population A has more "options" for natural selection to work with. If you chose D, same species does NOT mean same diversity or same adaptive potential. If you chose A, limited traits means FEWER options, not better focus — the population may lack any pollution-resistance traits. If you chose B, pollution may affect individuals differently depending on their genetic traits, which is exactly what natural selection acts on.
