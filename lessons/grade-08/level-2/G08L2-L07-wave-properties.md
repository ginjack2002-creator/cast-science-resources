## Platform

### Title
**Wave Properties: Amplitude, Frequency, and Speed — How Energy and Medium Shape Every Wave in the Universe**

### Overview
Students model how wave source energy and medium type determine wave amplitude, frequency, wavelength, and speed. By building a computational model of wave properties, students discover the inverse relationship between frequency and wavelength, prove that the universal wave equation (v = f x lambda) holds in all media, and understand why sound changes when it enters water. This lesson applies to sound, light, ocean waves, and earthquakes.

### NGSS Standard
**MS-PS4-1**: Use mathematical representations to describe a simple model for waves that includes how the amplitude of a wave is related to the energy in a wave.
**MS-PS4-2**: Develop and use a model to describe that waves are reflected, absorbed, or transmitted through various materials.

### Learning Objectives
- Explain how wave source energy affects amplitude and frequency
- Model the relationship between frequency and wavelength (inverse relationship)
- Predict how different media affect wave speed while preserving frequency
- Demonstrate the universal wave equation: speed = frequency x wavelength

### Component List (Starting Model: 5 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Wave Source Energy | External | The amount of power or force creating the wave |
| Medium Type | External | The material the wave travels through (air, water, solid) |
| Amplitude | Internal | Wave height, representing energy carried |
| Frequency | Internal | How many wave cycles pass per second (Hz) |
| Wavelength | Internal | Distance between wave peaks |

## Step 2: ESTABLISH — Connect the Relationships

### Text Editor

```
TIME TO WIRE THE WAVE EQUATION

These connections reveal the universal math behind every wave.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Task A: DRAW YOUR RELATIONSHIPS
• Wave Source Energy → Amplitude
• Wave Source Energy → Frequency
• Medium Type → Wave Speed (note: this affects wavelength)
• Frequency → Wavelength
• Amplitude → Energy Carried (implicit)

Task B: SET POSITIVE OR NEGATIVE
  ○ Wave Source Energy → Amplitude = POSITIVE (+)
    More energy creates larger waves — louder, brighter, taller

  ○ Wave Source Energy → Frequency = POSITIVE (+)
    More energy can drive higher-frequency oscillations

  ○ Medium Type → Wave Speed = POSITIVE/NEGATIVE (+/−)
    Denser media generally increase speed for mechanical waves
    (sound in water: 1,480 m/s vs. air: 343 m/s)

  ○ Frequency → Wavelength = NEGATIVE (−)
    THIS IS THE KEY: Higher frequency = shorter wavelength
    The inverse relationship: if speed is constant, lambda = v/f

  ○ Amplitude → Energy Carried = POSITIVE (+)
    Larger amplitude means more energy (proportional to amplitude squared)

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

THE UNIVERSAL WAVE EQUATION:
Speed = Frequency x Wavelength (v = f x lambda)
This works for sound, light, ocean waves, seismic waves — everything.
If you know two of these three, you can calculate the third.

KEY INSIGHT: When a wave enters a new medium, speed changes but
FREQUENCY stays the same. Wavelength adjusts to match. The source
sets the frequency; the medium sets the speed; wavelength adjusts.
```

### Video Script

```
"Let's wire the wave math.

Wave Source Energy to Amplitude. More energy, bigger waves.
A shout has more amplitude than a whisper. A tsunami has more
amplitude than a gentle swell. POSITIVE.

Wave Source Energy to Frequency. More energy can drive faster
oscillations — higher pitch, higher frequency. POSITIVE.

Medium Type to Wave Speed. This is crucial. Sound travels at
343 m/s in air. In water? 1,480 m/s. In steel? 5,960 m/s.
The medium determines how fast vibrations propagate.

Now the most important connection in the entire model:
Frequency to Wavelength. NEGATIVE. Inverse relationship.

Higher frequency means shorter wavelength. Lower frequency
means longer wavelength. This is because speed equals frequency
times wavelength. If speed is constant, when frequency goes up,
wavelength MUST go down to keep the equation balanced.

v = f times lambda. Speed equals frequency times wavelength.

This works for EVERY wave in the universe. Sound. Light. Ocean
waves. Earthquakes. Radio signals. Everything.

And here's the critical insight: when a wave enters a new medium,
its speed changes. But frequency stays the SAME because the
source is still vibrating at the same rate. So wavelength adjusts.

Faster speed with same frequency means longer wavelength. The
wave stretches out.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

3.36 meters. Same frequency, faster speed, longer wavelength.

Air: 343 = 440 x 0.78. Check.
Water: 1,480 = 440 x 3.36. Check.

The universal wave equation works in every medium, for every
wave, every time. This single equation describes sound, light,
ocean waves, earthquakes, and radio signals.

Speed equals frequency times wavelength. The most powerful
equation in wave physics.

5 questions aligned to MS-PS4-1 and MS-PS4-2.

### Question 1

A student models wave properties with five components: Wave Source Energy, Medium Type, Amplitude, Frequency, and Wavelength. Which are external?

A. Wave Source Energy and Medium Type
B. Medium Type and Wavelength
C. Wave Source Energy and Amplitude
D. Amplitude and Frequency

Correct Answer: A

---

### Question 2

A student doubles the energy of a wave source. What happens to the wave?

A. Only frequency doubles — amplitude stays the same.
B. Amplitude increases, carrying more energy per wave.
C. Only wavelength doubles — amplitude stays the same.
D. The wave stops because too much energy breaks it.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: More source energy creates larger amplitude waves. Energy is proportional to the square of amplitude, so doubling energy increases amplitude by about 1.4x. A shout has more amplitude than a whisper.
---

### Question 3

A sound wave with a frequency of 440 Hz travels through air at 343 m/s. When the same sound enters water, its speed increases to 1,480 m/s. What happens?

A. Both frequency and wavelength increase proportionally.
B. Frequency stays at 440 Hz and wavelength decreases.
C. Frequency increases to 1,480 Hz and wavelength stays the same.
D. Frequency stays at 440 Hz and wavelength increases.

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: When a wave enters a new medium, frequency is preserved (the source still vibrates at 440 Hz). Speed increases. Since v = f x lambda, wavelength must increase to maintain the equation. In air: lambda = 343/440 = 0.78 m. In water: lambda = 1,480/440 = 3.36 m.

### Question 4

A student claims that louder sounds have higher frequency. Which evidence from the model disproves this?

A. Medium type determines both loudness and pitch simultaneously.
B. Increasing source energy increases both amplitude and frequency equally.
C. Increasing only amplitude while keeping frequency constant produces a louder sound at the same pitch.
D. Frequency and amplitude always increase together in the model.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: Loudness corresponds to amplitude, and pitch corresponds to frequency. These are independent properties. You can whisper a high note (low amplitude, high frequency) or shout a low note (high amplitude, low frequency). The model shows amplitude can change without frequency changing.

### Question 5

A marine team needs sonar to detect small objects on the ocean floor 2,000 meters below. Sound travels at 1,480 m/s in seawater. They send a pulse and receive an echo after 2.7 seconds. How far away is the object?

A. 1,480 meters — speed equals distance for sonar
B. 1,998 meters — speed times half the round-trip time
C. 4,000 meters — speed times total time
D. 2,700 meters — time determines distance directly

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Distance = (speed x round-trip time) / 2 = (1,480 x 2.7) / 2 = 3,996 / 2 = 1,998 meters. We divide by 2 because the sound traveled to the object AND back, so the round-trip time covers twice the distance.
