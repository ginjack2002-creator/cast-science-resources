### Title
**Can We Build a Fusion Reactor? — Modeling Plasma Confinement, Energy Balance, and the Path to Unlimited Clean Energy**

### Overview
Every star in the universe is a fusion reactor, converting hydrogen into helium and releasing staggering amounts of energy. Our Sun alone converts 600 million tons of hydrogen per second, a process that has been running continuously for 4.6 billion years and will continue for 5 billion more. Recreating this process on Earth would provide virtually unlimited clean energy — enough to power civilization for billions of years using fuel extracted from seawater. The physics has been understood since the 1920s. The engineering has defied humanity's best efforts since the 1950s. Students investigate the driving question: Why has the most abundant energy source in the universe — the same reaction that powers every star — remained just out of human reach for over 70 years? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

### Learning Objectives
- Model how plasma temperature, magnetic confinement, and fuel density must simultaneously satisfy the Lawson criterion for sustained fusion
- Analyze the engineering trade-offs between maximizing fusion energy output and minimizing neutron damage to reactor materials
- Evaluate why achieving net energy gain (Q greater than 1) has been the central challenge of fusion research for seven decades
- Predict the operating conditions under which a fusion reactor transitions from energy consumer to energy producer

### Component List (Starting Model: 7 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Plasma Temperature | External | The core temperature of the fusion fuel, which must exceed 150 million degrees Celsius for the deuterium-tritium reaction — at this temperature, nuclei have enough kinetic energy to overcome their electromagnetic repulsion and fuse |
| Magnetic Field Strength | External | The intensity of the superconducting magnets confining the plasma, measured in Tesla — stronger fields compress plasma to higher density and resist instabilities, but require enormous superconducting coils cooled to near absolute zero |
| Fuel Density | External | The concentration of deuterium and tritium nuclei in the plasma — higher density increases collision frequency and fusion reaction rate but also increases the power needed to sustain and confine the plasma |
| Energy Output | Internal | The total thermal energy produced by fusion reactions per second — determined by the product of plasma temperature, fuel density, and the fusion cross-section, which peaks at around 150 million degrees |
| Neutron Flux | Internal | The number of high-energy neutrons produced per unit area per second — each deuterium-tritium fusion reaction produces a 14.1 MeV neutron that carries 80% of the fusion energy and bombards reactor structural materials |
| Wall Material Degradation | Internal | The cumulative structural damage to the reactor first wall and blanket caused by decades of neutron bombardment — neutrons displace atoms from crystal lattices, create helium bubbles, and transmute structural elements into different atoms |
| Net Energy Gain | Internal | The Q factor representing whether the reactor produces net energy — determined by fusion energy output minus all energy inputs including plasma heating, magnet cooling, fuel injection, and facility operations |

• Click on "Energy Output" and drag an arrow to "Neutron Flux"
• Click on "Neutron Flux" and drag an arrow to "Wall Material Degradation"
• Click on "Magnetic Field Strength" and drag an arrow to "Energy Output"

• Look at the +/− toggle in the TOP LEFT corner
• For each connection, ask: "When this goes UP, does the other go UP or DOWN?"

  ○ Plasma Temperature → Energy Output = POSITIVE (+)
    Higher plasma temperature increases the kinetic energy of deuterium and tritium nuclei, dramatically increasing the probability that they overcome Coulomb repulsion and fuse. The fusion cross-section peaks at around 150 million degrees, so higher temperature means exponentially more fusion reactions and greater energy output.

  ○ Energy Output → Neutron Flux = POSITIVE (+)
    Higher energy output means more deuterium-tritium fusion reactions per second, and every single reaction produces exactly one 14.1 MeV neutron. The neutron flux is directly proportional to the fusion reaction rate — you cannot produce fusion energy without producing neutrons.

  ○ Neutron Flux → Wall Material Degradation = POSITIVE (+)
    Higher neutron flux means more high-energy neutrons bombarding the reactor first wall every second, displacing more atoms from their crystal lattice positions, creating more helium bubbles, and transmuting more structural atoms. Material degradation rate is directly proportional to neutron flux integrated over time.

  ○ Magnetic Field Strength → Energy Output = POSITIVE (+)
    Stronger magnetic fields confine plasma more tightly, increasing fuel density and extending confinement time — both of which increase the fusion reaction rate and energy output by helping satisfy the Lawson criterion.

STEP 4: CHECK YOUR MODEL
• You should have 4 arrows total
• 0 negative relationship(s), 4 positive relationship(s)
• This is your system model!

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

THINK ABOUT IT: The Lawson criterion demands extreme temperature AND extreme density AND long confinement time simultaneously. But hotter, denser plasma is harder to confine — it pushes harder against the magnetic bottle and generates more instabilities. How do engineers increase three variables when increasing any one makes the others harder to achieve?

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

WHAT DID YOU DISCOVER?
• The Lawson criterion creates a three-dimensional threshold problem — falling short on any single parameter (temperature, density, or confinement time) means zero net energy, even if the other two are perfect
• Every fusion reaction that produces useful energy also produces a high-energy neutron that damages the reactor, creating a fundamental trade-off between energy output and structural lifetime
• Plasma instabilities are the primary obstacle to sustained confinement — turbulence, edge-localized modes, and disruptions constantly try to breach the magnetic bottle, requiring real-time feedback control thousands of times per second
• The enormous energy cost of heating plasma, powering magnets, and running cooling systems means that the fusion reactions must produce not just some energy but enough to overcome all parasitic loads with margin

THE ANSWER: Fusion has eluded engineers for over 70 years because it requires simultaneously solving problems at the extremes of every relevant physical parameter. The plasma must be 150 million degrees — but no material can touch it, so it must float in a magnetic bottle. The magnets must be among the strongest ever built — but they require cooling to near absolute zero, meters from the hottest substance on Earth. Every fusion reaction produces energy — but also a 14.1 MeV neutron that erodes the reactor walls. And the total energy input for heating, confinement, cooling, and operations must be exceeded by the fusion output by a factor of 10 or more for commercial viability. We have achieved Q greater than 1 briefly. ITER aims for Q=10 by the early 2030s. Private companies are racing to get there sooner. The physics works — the Sun proves it every second. The engineering is what remains.

LESSON PURPOSE:
Every star in the universe is a fusion reactor, converting hydrogen into helium and releasing staggering amounts of energy. Our Sun alone converts 600 million tons of hydrogen per second, a process that has been running continuously for 4.6 billion years and will continue for 5 billion more. Recreating this process on Earth would provide virtually unlimited clean energy — enough to power civilization for billions of years using fuel extracted from seawater. The physics has been understood since the 1920s. The engineering has defied humanity's best efforts since the 1950s.

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• The Physics of Fusion:
  Nuclear fusion occurs when two light atomic nuclei overcome their electromagnetic repulsion and merge, releasing energy from the strong nuclear force. The most achievable reaction fuses deuterium (hydrogen with one extra neutron) with tritium (hydrogen with two extra neutrons) to produce a helium-4 nucleus, a 14.1 MeV neutron, and 17.6 MeV of total energy. This is the same process that powers the Sun, but the Sun cheats: its enormous gravitational pressure compresses hydrogen to extreme density, allowing fusion at a mere 15 million degrees. Without gravity's assistance, Earth-based reactors must reach 150 million degrees — ten times hotter than the Sun's core — to achieve useful fusion rates.

• The Confinement Challenge:
  At 150 million degrees, matter exists as plasma — a sea of charged particles that would vaporize any physical container on contact. Magnetic confinement uses powerful superconducting magnets to create a magnetic cage that suspends the plasma in vacuum. The most successful design is the tokamak, a donut-shaped chamber where spiraling magnetic field lines confine the plasma. But plasma is inherently unstable: magnetohydrodynamic instabilities, edge-localized modes, and turbulent transport constantly push plasma toward the walls. Maintaining stable confinement requires real-time feedback control systems adjusting magnetic fields thousands of times per second — the engineering equivalent of balancing an inverted pendulum on a moving platform.

• The Materials Crisis:
  Each deuterium-tritium fusion reaction produces a 14.1 MeV neutron that carries 80% of the reaction's energy. Unlike charged particles, neutrons are immune to magnetic confinement and slam directly into the reactor's structural materials. Over years of operation, these neutrons displace atoms from crystal lattices, create helium bubbles that swell and embrittle steel, and transmute structural elements into different atoms. No existing material can withstand the neutron flux of a commercial fusion reactor for its intended 30-year lifespan. Reduced-activation ferritic-martensitic steels, tungsten alloys, and silicon carbide composites are leading candidates, but all eventually succumb to neutron damage.

• The Race to Commercial Fusion:
  In December 2022, the National Ignition Facility achieved scientific breakeven (Q greater than 1) using inertial confinement. ITER, the 35-nation international tokamak in southern France, aims to achieve Q=10 by the early 2030s — producing ten times more energy than it consumes. Meanwhile, private fusion companies have raised over $6 billion: Commonwealth Fusion Systems is building SPARC, a compact tokamak using high-temperature superconducting magnets; Helion Energy is pursuing field-reversed configuration for direct electricity generation; and TAE Technologies uses beam-driven field-reversed configuration. The consensus that fusion is perpetually 30 years away is shifting — multiple credible pathways now aim for demonstration plants in the 2030s.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Fusion reactors can explode like nuclear bombs"
  Reality: A fusion reactor cannot produce a nuclear explosion. Nuclear weapons require precisely engineered implosion to compress fusion fuel to extreme density in nanoseconds — conditions that cannot occur accidentally. A fusion reactor contains only about one gram of fuel at any moment. If confinement fails, the plasma cools to room temperature in milliseconds and fusion stops. A reactor failure damages equipment — potentially costing billions to repair — but does not produce a nuclear explosion. The physics of weapons and reactors are fundamentally different despite both involving fusion.
  Strategy: Thought experiment: What happens if you open the lid of a pot of boiling water? The water stops boiling because it is no longer under pressure and at boiling temperature simultaneously. A fusion plasma does the same thing — remove confinement and the 'boiling' (fusion) stops instantly.

• "We already know how to build a fusion reactor, we just need more funding"
  Reality: While the basic physics of fusion is well understood, several critical engineering challenges remain unsolved. No material has been demonstrated to survive a commercial reactor's neutron flux for 30 years. Tritium self-sufficiency (breeding ratio greater than 1) has never been achieved. Steady-state plasma confinement for months or years (versus seconds in current experiments) has not been demonstrated. And the economics of fusion electricity versus increasingly cheap renewables is uncertain. More funding would accelerate progress, but money alone cannot solve problems that require fundamental materials science and engineering breakthroughs.
  Strategy: Model exercise: Point to Wall Material Degradation in the model. Even if every other parameter reaches its target, what happens if no material can survive the neutron environment for more than a few years? How does frequent replacement affect the economics?

• "Fusion produces no waste and is perfectly clean"
  Reality: Fusion is dramatically cleaner than fission or fossil fuels, but it is not waste-free. The 14.1 MeV neutrons from fusion activate structural materials, creating low-level radioactive waste that must be stored for 50-100 years before it decays to safe levels. Tritium is radioactive with a 12.3-year half-life and must be carefully contained. However, fusion produces no long-lived actinides (unlike fission's plutonium that remains dangerous for hundreds of thousands of years), no greenhouse gases, and no possibility of meltdown. The waste comparison: 50-100 years of storage versus 100,000+ years is an enormous improvement.
  Strategy: Compare: Draw a decay timeline. Fission waste (plutonium-239) has a half-life of 24,100 years — dangerous for over 100,000 years. Fusion-activated structural steel decays to safe levels in about 50-100 years. Neither is zero, but one is a problem for this century and the other is a problem for the next thousand centuries.

ANSWER KEY:
Big Question: Why has the most abundant energy source in the universe — the same reaction that powers every star — remained just out of human reach for over 70 years?
Answer: Fusion has eluded engineers for over 70 years because it requires simultaneously solving problems at the extremes of every relevant physical parameter. The plasma must be 150 million degrees — but no material can touch it, so it must float in a magnetic bottle. The magnets must be among the strongest ever built — but they require cooling to near absolute zero, meters from the hottest substance on Earth. Every fusion reaction produces energy — but also a 14.1 MeV neutron that erodes the reactor walls. And the total energy input for heating, confinement, cooling, and operations must be exceeded by the fusion output by a factor of 10 or more for commercial viability. We have achieved Q greater than 1 briefly. ITER aims for Q=10 by the early 2030s. Private companies are racing to get there sooner. The physics works — the Sun proves it every second. The engineering is what remains.

Simulation Answers:
• ITER Design Parameters Scenario: At 150 million degrees, 5.3 Tesla magnetic field, and design-level fuel density, the model predicts that the Lawson criterion is satisfied with margin. Energy Output reaches levels consistent with Q=10, meaning the reactor produces ten times more fusion energy than the total heating power input. Neutron Flux is substantial but within design limits for the planned 20-year operational lifetime when materials are periodically replaced. Net Energy Gain confirms that ITER's engineering parameters represent a carefully optimized sweet spot between fusion performance and structural survivability.
• Push to Ignition Scenario: Maximizing all three input parameters pushes toward ignition (Q approaching infinity), where the plasma self-heats from its own fusion reactions without external heating. Energy Output increases dramatically, but Neutron Flux increases proportionally, accelerating Wall Material Degradation to rates that would require first-wall replacement every few years rather than every decade. The model reveals why commercial reactors cannot simply maximize fusion output — materials lifetime and maintenance schedules become the limiting economic factors.

Reflection Exemplars:
• Q: Why is the Lawson criterion such a demanding requirement?
  A: The Lawson criterion requires that plasma temperature times density times confinement time exceeds a critical threshold. This is demanding because the three parameters work against each other: hotter plasma is harder to confine (higher pressure against the magnetic bottle), denser plasma has more instabilities, and longer confinement means maintaining extreme conditions for extended periods against continuous entropy increase. Achieving one extreme is straightforward — achieving all three simultaneously is the engineering equivalent of juggling on a tightrope while the tightrope is on fire. The model shows that falling even 20% short on any single parameter can drop net energy gain from Q=10 to Q less than 1.
• Q: Why does the energy-damage coupling represent an inherent design tension?
  A: The deuterium-tritium fusion reaction produces exactly one helium-4 nucleus (3.5 MeV) and one neutron (14.1 MeV) every time. The helium stays in the plasma and provides self-heating. The neutron escapes and carries 80% of the energy to the blanket for capture — but it also damages everything it hits. There is no way to separate the energy-carrying neutrons from the damage-causing neutrons because they are the same particles. The model shows this coupling perfectly: every increase in Energy Output produces an exactly proportional increase in Neutron Flux. Engineers must design materials that can withstand this bombardment for years or design reactors that are economical to rebuild when materials fail.

Key Concepts:
• Tokamak vs. Stellarator Design: The tokamak uses a combination of external magnets and an internal plasma current to create the confining magnetic field — simpler to build but prone to disruptions when the plasma current is lost. The stellarator uses only external magnets twisted in complex 3D shapes — more stable but much harder to engineer and build. The choice between them represents a fundamental design trade-off between simplicity and stability.
• High-Temperature Superconductors: Recent breakthroughs in high-temperature superconducting (HTS) magnets using REBCO tape allow much stronger magnetic fields in smaller reactors. Commonwealth Fusion Systems' SPARC reactor uses HTS magnets to achieve ITER-level performance in a device one-sixty-fifth the volume. Stronger magnets improve confinement, density, and stability simultaneously — potentially the most impactful single technology improvement.
• Advanced First-Wall Materials: The reactor first wall must survive decades of neutron bombardment at temperatures exceeding 500 degrees Celsius while maintaining structural integrity. Reduced-activation ferritic-martensitic steels (RAFM), tungsten alloys, and silicon carbide fiber composites are leading candidates. Liquid metal walls that continuously refresh their surface offer a radical alternative that could solve the solid-wall erosion problem entirely.

MISSION: Select the most critical engineering bottleneck preventing commercial fusion and design an innovative approach to overcome it, using evidence from your computational model to justify your strategy.

SCENARIO: The Department of Energy has launched a grand challenge for student teams to propose breakthrough approaches to commercial fusion energy. Your team must identify which variable in the fusion system represents the most critical bottleneck — plasma stability, neutron-resistant materials, tritium fuel supply, or energy balance optimization — and propose an engineering concept that could transform that variable, backed by quantitative evidence from your model.

GUIDING QUESTIONS:
• Which of the seven model components represents the single biggest barrier to commercial fusion power and why?
• How could advances in materials science, magnet technology, or AI-driven plasma control change your model's predictions?
• What would a complete fusion power plant look like beyond just the reactor — heat exchangers, turbines, electrical grid connections, tritium processing?

EVALUATION RUBRIC:
• Expert (4): Proposal identifies a specific bottleneck with quantitative model evidence, proposes an innovative approach grounded in real physics and engineering, addresses trade-offs with other system variables, includes a development timeline, and compares to existing approaches
• Proficient (3): Proposal identifies a bottleneck with model evidence, proposes a reasonable engineering approach, and considers trade-offs with other components
• Developing (2): Proposal identifies a challenge but lacks quantitative model evidence or proposes a solution without addressing engineering trade-offs
• Beginning (1): Proposal is vague, not grounded in model data, or does not address a real fusion engineering challenge
