## Platform

### Title
**Can We Capture Carbon from Thin Air? — Modeling Direct Air Capture Systems, Energy Requirements, and Scalability**

### Overview
Humanity has emitted over 1.5 trillion tonnes of CO2 since the Industrial Revolution, and we continue adding approximately 37 billion tonnes per year. Even if we stopped all emissions today, the CO2 already in the atmosphere would keep warming the planet for decades. This reality has driven interest in technologies that can actively remove CO2 from the atmosphere — essentially running the combustion reaction in reverse. Direct air capture is the most technologically mature approach, but it faces daunting challenges of thermodynamics, energy, cost, and scale. Students investigate the driving question: If the atmosphere contains too much CO2, why can't we just build machines to suck it out — and what would it actually take to make a difference? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

### Learning Objectives
- Model how direct air capture systems use chemical sorbents and thermal energy to extract CO2 from ambient air at 420 parts per million concentration
- Analyze the relationship between energy source, capture efficiency, and net carbon removal — recognizing that fossil-powered capture could emit more CO2 than it removes
- Evaluate the trade-offs between capture cost, energy requirements, land use, and the scale needed to meaningfully reduce atmospheric CO2 levels
- Predict how changes in energy source, sorbent chemistry, and deployment scale affect the viability of direct air capture as a climate solution

### Component List (Starting Model: 6 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Energy Source Carbon Intensity | External | The CO2 emissions per unit of energy used to power the DAC system — if the energy comes from fossil fuels, the capture process itself emits CO2, potentially negating the removal benefit |
| Sorbent Efficiency | Internal | The amount of CO2 captured per unit of sorbent material per cycle — affected by sorbent chemistry, air contact time, temperature, and humidity conditions |
| Thermal Energy Requirement | Internal | The heat energy needed to regenerate the sorbent by releasing captured CO2 — typically 80-120 degrees Celsius for solid sorbents, consuming 1,500-2,500 kWh per tonne of CO2 in current systems |
| Net Carbon Removal | Internal | The actual CO2 removed from the atmosphere after subtracting all emissions from energy use, construction, sorbent manufacturing, and transportation — must be strongly positive for the system to be useful |
| Capture Cost | Internal | The total economic cost per tonne of CO2 removed — currently $400-$1,000 per tonne for operational DAC plants, which must decrease dramatically for gigatonne-scale deployment |
| Deployment Scale | External | The number and size of DAC installations needed to achieve meaningful climate impact — removing just 1% of annual emissions would require thousands of large facilities |

• Look at the +/− toggle in the TOP LEFT corner
• For each connection, ask: "When this goes UP, does the other go UP or DOWN?"

  ○ Energy Source Carbon Intensity → Net Carbon Removal = NEGATIVE (−)
    Higher carbon intensity of the energy source means more CO2 is emitted to power the capture process, directly reducing and potentially eliminating or reversing the net carbon removal. At sufficiently high carbon intensity, the system adds more CO2 than it captures.

  ○ Sorbent Efficiency → Thermal Energy Requirement = NEGATIVE (−)
    More efficient sorbents capture more CO2 per cycle, meaning fewer cycles are needed to capture a given amount. Fewer cycles means less total thermal energy for sorbent regeneration. Higher efficiency sorbents also tend to release CO2 at lower temperatures, further reducing energy needs.

  ○ Deployment Scale → Capture Cost = NEGATIVE (−)
    Larger deployment scale enables manufacturing economies of scale, shared infrastructure, optimized supply chains, and learning-by-doing improvements that reduce the cost per tonne of CO2 captured — the same cost reduction pattern seen in solar panels and wind turbines over the past two decades.

  ○ Thermal Energy Requirement → Capture Cost = POSITIVE (+)
    Higher thermal energy requirements mean more energy must be purchased or generated per tonne of CO2 captured, directly increasing the operational cost. Energy is typically the largest single cost component in DAC operations.

Why is it so much harder and more expensive to capture from air?

WHAT DID YOU DISCOVER?
• The diluteness of atmospheric CO2 (420 ppm) is the fundamental challenge — extracting something present at 0.042% concentration requires processing enormous volumes of air and consumes far more energy than capturing from concentrated industrial sources
• Energy source is the make-or-break variable — fossil-powered DAC can actually INCREASE atmospheric CO2 if the energy emissions exceed the capture amount, making renewable or nuclear energy essential for net-negative carbon removal
• Current costs of $400-$1,000 per tonne must decrease by an order of magnitude for DAC to compete with other climate strategies — for reference, the social cost of carbon is estimated at $50-$200 per tonne
• Scaling DAC to remove even 10% of annual emissions would require energy equivalent to a significant fraction of current global electricity generation — raising questions about whether that energy would be better used replacing fossil fuels directly

THE ANSWER: Direct air capture works — the chemistry is proven and several commercial plants are operating today. The challenge is thermodynamics and scale. CO2 at 420 ppm is extraordinarily dilute, requiring vast amounts of air to be processed and significant energy to separate and concentrate the CO2. Current systems cost $400-$1,000 per tonne of CO2 removed. Scaling to climate-relevant levels (gigatonnes per year) would require energy equivalent to a significant fraction of global electricity production — energy that must come from zero-carbon sources or the system defeats its own purpose. DAC is a necessary tool in the climate toolkit, particularly for removing historical emissions and offsetting hard-to-decarbonize sectors like aviation. But it is not a substitute for rapidly reducing emissions, and claiming otherwise is dangerous because it creates a moral hazard — the false belief that future technology will clean up the mess, so we do not need to stop making it.

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• How Direct Air Capture Works:
  DAC systems draw ambient air through chemical sorbents that selectively bind CO2. In solid sorbent systems (like those used by Climeworks), air passes over porous materials coated with amine compounds that chemically bond with CO2 at ambient temperature. Once saturated, the sorbent is heated to 80-120 degrees Celsius, releasing concentrated CO2 for collection and storage. The sorbent cools and the cycle repeats. In liquid solvent systems (like Carbon Engineering's approach), air contacts a potassium hydroxide solution that absorbs CO2 to form potassium carbonate. The carbonate is processed through a calcium caustic recovery loop that regenerates the solvent at 900 degrees Celsius and produces pure CO2. Both approaches work but face the same fundamental challenge: CO2 is only 420 ppm in air — a haystack-to-needle ratio that demands enormous energy.

• The Thermodynamic Challenge:
  The minimum theoretical energy to separate CO2 from air at 420 ppm is approximately 250 kWh per tonne, dictated by the second law of thermodynamics. Real systems require 1,500-2,500 kWh per tonne — 6-10 times the theoretical minimum — because of heat losses, air movement energy, material inefficiencies, and process engineering constraints. For comparison, capturing CO2 from a coal plant's exhaust (at 120,000 ppm) requires only about 250-400 kWh per tonne total because the concentration is 300 times higher. This concentration difference is the fundamental reason DAC is far more expensive and energy-intensive than point-source capture — and it cannot be engineered away because it is a consequence of physics, not technology.

• The Scale Problem:
  Current global DAC capacity is approximately 10,000-50,000 tonnes of CO2 per year across all operational plants combined. To remove just 1% of annual emissions (370 million tonnes) would require scaling by a factor of 10,000 or more. The energy required would be equivalent to approximately 5-10% of current global electricity generation — energy that must come from zero-carbon sources or the system adds more CO2 than it removes. The physical infrastructure would include thousands of large facilities, millions of tonnes of sorbent materials, and a completely new pipeline and injection industry for CO2 storage. This is not technically impossible, but it represents an industrial buildout comparable to the entire global petroleum industry, constructed in decades rather than a century.

• The Cost and Policy Landscape:
  Current DAC costs range from $400-$1,000 per tonne of CO2, compared to the social cost of carbon estimated at $50-$200 per tonne and carbon offset prices of $5-$50 per tonne. For DAC to be economically viable at scale, costs must decrease by 5-10 times through improved sorbents, cheaper clean energy, and manufacturing scale effects. Government policies are beginning to support this: the US Inflation Reduction Act provides $180 per tonne tax credits for DAC with geological storage, and the Department of Energy has funded regional DAC hubs. Climeworks opened the world's largest DAC plant (Mammoth) in Iceland in 2024, capturing 36,000 tonnes per year using geothermal energy and storing CO2 in basalt formations where it mineralizes within 2 years. These are encouraging steps but represent less than 0.0001% of the needed scale.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Direct air capture can solve climate change so we do not need to reduce emissions"
  Reality: DAC is orders of magnitude too small, too expensive, and too energy-intensive to substitute for emission reductions. Current global DAC capacity removes less than 0.001% of annual emissions. Even optimistic projections for 2050 target 5-10 gigatonnes per year against 37+ gigatonnes of ongoing emissions. Furthermore, the energy required for gigatonne-scale DAC would consume a significant fraction of global clean energy production — energy better used to directly replace fossil fuels. The model clearly shows that DAC is a complement to emission reductions, not a substitute.
  Strategy: Scale exercise: Current DAC removes about 10,000 tonnes per year. Annual emissions are 37 billion tonnes. Calculate what factor of scaling is needed. Then calculate the energy required at 2,000 kWh/tonne. The numbers make the relationship between DAC and emission reductions vividly clear.

• "Planting trees is just as good as direct air capture"
  Reality: Trees are excellent carbon sinks but face limitations that DAC does not: they require land (competing with agriculture), take decades to reach full carbon absorption, are vulnerable to fire, drought, and disease that release stored carbon, and have a finite saturation point. A mature forest eventually reaches carbon equilibrium where absorption equals decomposition. DAC can operate on non-arable land, captures CO2 at a constant rate regardless of season, and stores carbon as permanent minerals rather than temporary biomass. However, trees cost $5-$50 per tonne versus $400-$1,000 for DAC — so where forests are viable, they are far more cost-effective.
  Strategy: Compare: Model both strategies side by side. Reforestation removes 2-5 tonnes per hectare per year at $5-$50/tonne but requires fertile land and takes 20-40 years to peak. DAC removes hundreds of tonnes per facility per year at $400-$1,000/tonne but works on any terrain with clean energy. Different tools for different situations.

• "Carbon capture technology is just greenwashing by fossil fuel companies"
  Reality: This is partially correct and partially wrong. Some fossil fuel companies do promote carbon capture as a way to justify continued fossil fuel production — this is a legitimate criticism. However, the technology itself is scientifically valid and may be genuinely necessary for addressing historical emissions and hard-to-decarbonize sectors. The critical distinction is between using DAC to enable continued fossil fuel extraction (greenwashing) versus using DAC to clean up residual emissions in a world that is rapidly decarbonizing (legitimate climate strategy). The model helps students distinguish between these uses by showing that DAC only achieves net removal when powered by clean energy.
  Strategy: Critical analysis: Show students corporate DAC pledges alongside their fossil fuel expansion plans. Does the math add up? If a company pledges to capture 1 million tonnes while its operations emit 100 million tonnes, is that a climate solution or a public relations strategy?

ANSWER KEY:
Big Question: If the atmosphere contains too much CO2, why can't we just build machines to suck it out — and what would it actually take to make a difference?
Answer: Direct air capture works — the chemistry is proven and several commercial plants are operating today. The challenge is thermodynamics and scale. CO2 at 420 ppm is extraordinarily dilute, requiring vast amounts of air to be processed and significant energy to separate and concentrate the CO2. Current systems cost $400-$1,000 per tonne of CO2 removed. Scaling to climate-relevant levels (gigatonnes per year) would require energy equivalent to a significant fraction of global electricity production — energy that must come from zero-carbon sources or the system defeats its own purpose. DAC is a necessary tool in the climate toolkit, particularly for removing historical emissions and offsetting hard-to-decarbonize sectors like aviation. But it is not a substitute for rapidly reducing emissions, and claiming otherwise is dangerous because it creates a moral hazard — the false belief that future technology will clean up the mess, so we do not need to stop making it.

Simulation Answers:
• Renewable-Powered DAC Scenario: With 100% renewable energy (zero carbon intensity), the system achieves maximum net carbon removal per tonne captured — essentially all CO2 extracted from air represents genuine atmospheric reduction. However, the cost remains high at $400-$600 per tonne with current sorbent technology, and the energy requirement of 1,500-2,500 kWh per tonne means that a facility removing 1 million tonnes per year would require 1.5-2.5 TWh of dedicated renewable energy — equivalent to a large wind farm or solar installation devoted entirely to DAC rather than replacing fossil electricity.
• Fossil-Powered DAC Scenario: When powered by natural gas (carbon intensity approximately 0.4 tonnes CO2 per MWh), the model reveals that the capture process itself emits 0.6-1.0 tonnes of CO2 for every tonne it captures (using 1.5-2.5 MWh of energy). Net carbon removal drops to 0-40% of gross capture, and in worst-case configurations, the system actually adds CO2 to the atmosphere. This scenario demonstrates quantitatively why energy source selection is non-negotiable for DAC — fossil-powered DAC is thermodynamically self-defeating.

Reflection Exemplars:
• Q: Why is the 420 ppm concentration the fundamental challenge?
  A: Atmospheric CO2 at 420 ppm means that only 0.042% of air molecules are CO2 — you must process enormous volumes of air to capture meaningful amounts. The thermodynamic minimum energy for separation increases as concentration decreases (following the entropy of mixing). This is why point-source capture from power plant exhaust at 120,000 ppm is 5-10 times cheaper than DAC — the CO2 is 300 times more concentrated. No engineering improvement can change this fundamental thermodynamic reality. The model shows that even a hypothetical perfect sorbent would still require the thermodynamic minimum of 250 kWh per tonne, and real systems will always be multiples above this floor.
• Q: Is DAC a climate solution or a moral hazard?
  A: The model supports both perspectives simultaneously. DAC is likely necessary for addressing historical emissions and hard-to-decarbonize sectors like aviation and cement production — these are real needs that only carbon removal can address. However, the model also shows that the energy required for gigatonne-scale DAC is enormous — energy that could alternatively replace fossil fuel electricity and eliminate emissions at the source. If DAC promises lead policymakers and corporations to delay emission reductions, the net climate impact could be negative because the delayed emissions accumulate faster than DAC can remove them. The responsible framing: DAC is a complement to, not a replacement for, aggressive emission reductions.

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.1 (Develop and use a model to represent relationships) + DCI ESS3.D (Sustainability and environmental solutions) + CCC4 (Systems and system models)

Engineers at a direct air capture (DAC) facility in the Mojave Desert operate fans that pull ambient air (containing 420 ppm CO2) through chemical sorbent filters. The sorbent bonds with CO2, which is later released by heating the sorbent to 900C. The facility captures 4,000 tons of CO2 per year but consumes 2,500 megawatt-hours of electricity annually to power fans, heating, and compression. Using current California grid electricity (40% fossil fuels), the facility's own energy consumption generates 900 tons of CO2, yielding a net capture of only 3,100 tons.

A student's DAC model shows that switching from natural gas power to 100% renewable energy increases net carbon removal from 600 to 1,000 tonnes per year (per facility) but increases capture cost from $400 to $550 per tonne. What is the most complete analysis of this trade-off?

A. The renewable system removes 67% more net CO2 per facility despite costing 38% more per tonne, and the fossil-powered system's lower cost is misleading because it includes 400 tonnes of self-generated emissions that the cost per tonne does not reflect
B. Both systems are equally effective because they use the same capture technology
C. Cost per tonne is the only metric that matters for evaluating DAC systems
D. The cost increase makes renewable-powered DAC economically nonviable
