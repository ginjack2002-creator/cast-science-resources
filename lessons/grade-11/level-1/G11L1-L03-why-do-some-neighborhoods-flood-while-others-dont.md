### Title
**Why Do Some Neighborhoods Flood While Others Don't? — Modeling Urban Water Systems and Environmental Justice**

### Overview
When Hurricane Harvey flooded Houston in 2017, the most severely impacted areas were disproportionately low-income neighborhoods and communities of color — the same neighborhoods that had been redlined and disinvested for decades. This pattern repeats in city after city: the communities with the least political power and fewest resources face the greatest flood risk. Understanding why requires modeling both the hydrology of urban stormwater AND the history of infrastructure inequality. Students investigate the driving question: When a storm hits a city, why do low-income neighborhoods and communities of color flood first — and is the infrastructure designed that way on purpose? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

### Learning Objectives
- Model how impervious surface coverage, storm drain capacity, elevation, and green infrastructure interact to determine neighborhood flood risk
- Explain the relationship between historical urban planning decisions and current disparities in flood vulnerability across neighborhoods
- Predict how different infrastructure investments affect flood outcomes in high-risk versus low-risk neighborhoods
- Analyze how environmental justice principles can be applied to redesign urban water management systems equitably

### Component List (Starting Model: 5 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Impervious Surface Coverage | External | The percentage of a neighborhood covered by pavement, buildings, and other surfaces that prevent water infiltration — controlled by zoning decisions, building codes, and development density |
| Storm Drain Capacity | External | The engineered capacity of underground pipes and drainage channels to move water out of the neighborhood during a storm — determined by historical and current infrastructure investment decisions |
| Green Infrastructure Area | External | The amount of parks, tree canopy, rain gardens, bioswales, and permeable surfaces that absorb rainwater — can be increased through deliberate investment but has been historically absent from disinvested neighborhoods |
| Runoff Volume | Internal | The total volume of water flowing across the surface during a storm, determined by rainfall intensity minus infiltration — higher impervious coverage means more runoff |
| Flood Depth | Internal | The depth of standing water in a neighborhood when runoff volume exceeds storm drain capacity — the direct measure of flood damage risk to homes, businesses, and residents |

WHAT DID YOU DISCOVER?
• Neighborhoods with high impervious surface coverage and outdated storm drain infrastructure experience dramatically higher flood depths during the same storm — flood risk is determined by infrastructure, not weather alone
• Historical patterns of disinvestment created the infrastructure disparities that cause unequal flood outcomes today — neighborhoods that were redlined in the 1930s-60s consistently have worse drainage systems and less green space
• Green infrastructure can significantly reduce flood risk in vulnerable neighborhoods, but it cannot fully compensate for undersized storm drains — a comprehensive approach requires both green infrastructure AND pipe upgrades
• The most cost-effective flood mitigation strategy combines green infrastructure (which slows and absorbs runoff at the surface) with targeted storm drain upgrades (which increase underground capacity for remaining runoff)

THE ANSWER: The reason some neighborhoods flood while others stay dry has almost nothing to do with weather and almost everything to do with infrastructure — which is really about money, power, and historical racism. Neighborhoods that were redlined in the 1930s-60s received minimal infrastructure investment for decades: older storm drains, less maintenance, more impervious surfaces, and almost no green space. When a storm hits, these neighborhoods have less infiltration, more runoff, and less capacity to move water away. The result is predictable and unjust: the people with the fewest resources suffer the most damage. The science shows this is not natural — it is engineered inequality. And it can be re-engineered through targeted green infrastructure investment, storm drain upgrades, and policy that prioritizes the most vulnerable communities first.

LESSON PURPOSE:
When Hurricane Harvey flooded Houston in 2017, the most severely impacted areas were disproportionately low-income neighborhoods and communities of color — the same neighborhoods that had been redlined and disinvested for decades. This pattern repeats in city after city: the communities with the least political power and fewest resources face the greatest flood risk. Understanding why requires modeling both the hydrology of urban stormwater AND the history of infrastructure inequality.

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• Urban Hydrology: How Cities Create Floods:
  In a natural landscape, approximately 50% of rainfall infiltrates into the ground, 40% evapotranspires through vegetation, and only 10% becomes surface runoff. In a dense urban area, these numbers are essentially reversed: 55% becomes surface runoff, 30% infiltrates (through lawns and parks), and only 15% evapotranspires. A single acre of parking lot generates 16 times more runoff than a forested acre during the same storm. Cities literally create flooding by replacing permeable earth with impervious surfaces.

• The Infrastructure Gap: Why Pipes Matter:
  Storm drains are designed to handle a specific rainfall intensity, typically a '10-year storm' — the statistical rainfall level expected once per decade. But infrastructure in disinvested neighborhoods is often 50-100 years old, designed for lower rainfall intensities and smaller populations. Meanwhile, climate change is increasing the frequency of extreme rainfall events. The result: storms that overwhelm aging pipes in poor neighborhoods while modern systems in affluent areas handle the same rain easily. Replacing aging storm drains costs $1-5 million per mile of pipe.

• Redlining to Flooding: The Environmental Justice Connection:
  In the 1930s, the Home Owners' Loan Corporation (HOLC) graded neighborhoods A through D based on perceived investment risk — with D ('hazardous') neighborhoods almost always being communities of color. Redlined neighborhoods received less mortgage lending, less commercial investment, and critically, less public infrastructure spending for decades. A 2020 study in 142 US cities found that formerly redlined neighborhoods have significantly less tree canopy, more impervious surfaces, higher temperatures, and worse drainage than neighborhoods that were graded A. The flood map of today is the redlining map of 1935.

• Green Infrastructure: Nature-Based Solutions:
  Green infrastructure uses vegetation, soil, and natural processes to manage stormwater at its source. A single mature tree intercepts 500-760 gallons of rainfall per year. A rain garden (a shallow planted depression) can absorb 30% more water than a conventional lawn. Bioswales (vegetated channels) slow and filter runoff while reducing volume by 40-60%. Green roofs absorb 50-80% of rainfall. When distributed across a neighborhood, green infrastructure can reduce stormwater runoff by 20-40%, taking pressure off aging pipe systems. Philadelphia's Green City Clean Waters program has invested $2.4 billion in green infrastructure since 2011, avoiding $8 billion in pipe upgrades.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Flooding is a natural disaster that affects everyone equally"
  Reality: While the rain itself is natural, urban flooding is largely determined by human infrastructure decisions. Neighborhoods with modern storm drains, green space, and lower impervious surface coverage experience significantly less flooding from the same storm. The model clearly shows that flood depth is a function of infrastructure quality, not just rainfall intensity. And infrastructure quality is a function of historical investment decisions that systematically favored white and wealthy communities.
  Strategy: Run the model with identical rainfall on two neighborhoods and show the dramatically different flood outcomes. Ask: If the rain is the same, what is causing the difference? Follow the evidence to infrastructure, then to the investment decisions that created the infrastructure gap.

• "Green infrastructure is just decorative landscaping"
  Reality: Green infrastructure is engineered to manage stormwater — rain gardens, bioswales, permeable pavement, and tree canopy are carefully designed systems that absorb specific volumes of water at specific rates. A properly designed bioswale can reduce runoff by 40-60% along a street. Philadelphia's $2.4 billion green infrastructure program has measurably reduced combined sewer overflows across the city. These are engineered systems with quantifiable performance metrics, not just landscaping.
  Strategy: Show engineering specifications for a bioswale or rain garden — absorption rates, capacity calculations, soil media composition. Compare to a conventional storm drain pipe. Ask: Which one handles water AND provides habitat, shade, and beauty?

• "Just building bigger pipes will solve urban flooding"
  Reality: Larger pipes can handle more water, but they are extremely expensive ($1-5 million per mile), disruptive to install, and don't address the root cause — too much water reaching the pipes in the first place. Green infrastructure reduces runoff at the source, decreasing the volume that pipes must handle. The most effective and cost-efficient approach combines green infrastructure (to reduce volume) with targeted pipe upgrades (to increase capacity for remaining runoff). Philadelphia found this combined approach saved $5.6 billion compared to a pipes-only strategy.
  Strategy: Compare costs: show that installing green infrastructure across a neighborhood costs a fraction of replacing all underground pipes while achieving similar flood reduction. Ask: If you had limited budget, which approach gives more flood protection per dollar?

ANSWER KEY:
Big Question: When a storm hits a city, why do low-income neighborhoods and communities of color flood first — and is the infrastructure designed that way on purpose?
Answer: The reason some neighborhoods flood while others stay dry has almost nothing to do with weather and almost everything to do with infrastructure — which is really about money, power, and historical racism. Neighborhoods that were redlined in the 1930s-60s received minimal infrastructure investment for decades: older storm drains, less maintenance, more impervious surfaces, and almost no green space. When a storm hits, these neighborhoods have less infiltration, more runoff, and less capacity to move water away. The result is predictable and unjust: the people with the fewest resources suffer the most damage. The science shows this is not natural — it is engineered inequality. And it can be re-engineered through targeted green infrastructure investment, storm drain upgrades, and policy that prioritizes the most vulnerable communities first.

Simulation Answers:
• Disinvested Neighborhood Scenario: With 90% Impervious Surface Coverage, low Storm Drain Capacity (1950s infrastructure), and near-zero Green Infrastructure, the model shows that a major storm produces extreme Runoff Volume — nearly all rainfall becomes surface flow with nowhere to go. Storm drains are overwhelmed within the first 30 minutes of heavy rain. Flood Depth reaches 12-18 inches across streets and low-lying areas, damaging homes, businesses, and vehicles. The model clearly shows that the neighborhood's infrastructure cannot handle the storm.
• Green Infrastructure Retrofit Scenario: Adding significant Green Infrastructure to the disinvested neighborhood — rain gardens at every intersection, bioswales along major streets, tree canopy in parks and medians, and permeable pavement in parking areas — reduces Runoff Volume by approximately 35%. This drops Flood Depth from 12-18 inches to 4-8 inches. However, parity with the affluent neighborhood is NOT achieved by green infrastructure alone — the model shows that storm drain upgrades are also necessary to handle the remaining runoff. The most effective solution combines both approaches.

Reflection Exemplars:
• Q: How does your model demonstrate that flooding is engineered rather than natural?
  A: My model proves that flooding is an engineering outcome by running the exact same storm through two different neighborhoods and getting completely different results. The Disinvested Neighborhood flooded to 12-18 inches while the Affluent Neighborhood stayed nearly dry — same rain, same city, completely different infrastructure. The only variables that changed were human decisions: how much pavement, what size pipes, how many trees. Nature delivered the same storm everywhere. Humans decided who gets protected and who doesn't. That's not weather — that's policy.
• Q: Why is it important to include community residents in flood mitigation planning?
  A: Communities that have been historically excluded from planning decisions often have the best understanding of how flooding actually affects their daily lives — which streets flood first, which homes are most vulnerable, which routes become impassable. Top-down solutions designed without community input may address the engineering problem but miss the human problem. For example, green infrastructure that displaces community gardens or gathering spaces may reduce flooding but remove assets the community values. Environmental justice requires that the people most affected have the most voice in the solution design.

Key Concepts:
• Low Impact Development: An urban planning approach that manages stormwater at its source using small-scale, distributed green infrastructure rather than relying solely on large pipes. LID includes rain gardens, bioswales, permeable pavement, and green roofs that collectively reduce runoff volume and peak flow rates across a watershed.
• Environmental Justice Screening: Tools like the EPA's EJScreen that identify communities facing disproportionate environmental burdens based on demographics, pollution exposure, health outcomes, and infrastructure quality. These tools help planners prioritize investments in the most vulnerable communities.
• Benefit-Cost Analysis for Equity: Traditional benefit-cost analysis often favors investments in wealthy areas because property values are higher, making flood damage appear more expensive. Equity-weighted analysis accounts for the disproportionate impact on vulnerable populations who have fewer resources to recover, ensuring investments prioritize need rather than property value.

• Developing (2): Plan proposes interventions but lacks equity analysis, model evidence, or integration of multiple strategies
• Beginning (1): Plan is incomplete, distributes resources without regard to vulnerability, or lacks connection to model data

### Question 1

An environmental engineering team conducts a flood risk analysis for three neighborhoods in a Southern California city. Neighborhood A (median income $125,000) has modern stormwater infrastructure, permeable surfaces covering 35% of the area, and maintained drainage systems. Neighborhood C (median income $67,000) sits at a lower elevation with moderate infrastructure quality.

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships among system components) + DCI ESS3.D (Sustainability and environmental solutions) + CCC2 (Cause and effect)

A computational model of urban flood dynamics shows that four variables interact to determine flood severity: rainfall intensity (external input), impervious surface coverage (infrastructure variable), drainage system capacity (infrastructure variable), and elevation (geographic variable). The model reveals that impervious surface coverage and drainage capacity have a multiplicative relationship: when both are unfavorable, flood risk increases exponentially rather than additively.

The model shows that impervious surface coverage has a nonlinear relationship with flood depth: increasing coverage from 60% to 70% adds 8 cm of flood depth, but increasing from 80% to 90% adds 22 cm. Which explanation best accounts for this nonlinear pattern?

A. At high impervious coverage, the remaining permeable surfaces are insufficient to absorb meaningful rainfall, so each additional percentage of pavement has a disproportionately large effect on runoff volume
B. The model contains a calculation error at higher percentages
C. Higher impervious coverage causes more rainfall to fall on the neighborhood
D. Storm drain capacity automatically increases at higher coverage levels, partially compensating

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: This is a threshold effect: when almost all ground is paved, the few remaining permeable surfaces provide the last buffer against flooding. Removing that final buffer has outsized consequences because the system has no remaining capacity to absorb water. At 60% coverage, 40% of land still absorbs rain. At 90%, only 10% absorbs rain. Losing half of that remaining 10% has a much bigger relative impact than losing the same amount at lower coverage. If you chose C, this answer overgeneralizes without considering the specific mechanisms and evidence presented. Think about what happens as you approach 100% pavement.
