## Platform

### Title
**Is Fast Fashion Destroying the Planet? — Modeling the Environmental Life Cycle of Clothing**

### Overview
The fashion industry has undergone a radical transformation over the past two decades. Where previous generations bought fewer, higher-quality garments and wore them for years, the rise of fast fashion has created a system where the average garment is worn just 7-10 times before being discarded. The industry now produces over 100 billion garments annually — more than 12 for every person on Earth — with devastating environmental consequences that are largely invisible to consumers. Students investigate the driving question: If a $5 t-shirt costs the planet 2,700 liters of water, 10 kilograms of CO2, and microplastic pollution that lasts centuries — who really pays the price? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

### Learning Objectives
- Model the complete environmental life cycle of a fast fashion garment from raw material extraction through disposal
- Explain the relationship between production volume, material choice, garment lifespan, and total environmental impact
- Predict how different consumer behaviors and industry practices affect the cumulative ecological footprint of clothing
- Analyze the hidden environmental costs embedded in cheap clothing and evaluate sustainable alternatives

### Component List (Starting Model: 6 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Production Volume | External | The number of garments produced annually by the fashion industry — currently over 100 billion pieces per year for a global population of 8 billion, meaning approximately 14 garments per person per year |
| Material Type | External | The fiber used to make the garment — polyester (60% of all clothing, made from petroleum), cotton (24%, requires enormous water and pesticides), or sustainable alternatives like organic cotton, hemp, or recycled fibers |
| Garment Lifespan | External | The number of times a garment is worn before being discarded — fast fashion averages 7-10 wears versus 100+ wears for quality clothing, making cost-per-wear dramatically different from purchase price |
| Water Consumption | Internal | The total water used across the garment's life cycle — growing cotton for one t-shirt requires 2,700 liters, dyeing uses 100-150 liters, and washing over the garment's life adds hundreds more |
| Carbon Emissions | Internal | The total CO2 released from fiber production, manufacturing, shipping, washing, and disposal — a single polyester shirt produces approximately 5.5 kg of CO2 over its lifetime |
| Textile Waste Generated | Internal | The volume of clothing that ends up in landfills or incinerators at end of life — the average American discards 37 kg of clothing per year, and only 15% is recycled or donated |

### Text Editor

```
TIME TO SEE YOUR SYSTEM IN ACTION

You built it. You connected it. Now let's see if it actually WORKS

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

STEP 2: OBSERVE THE BASELINE
• Let it run for about 30 time steps
• Notice how the lines relate to each other
• When Production Volume is HIGH, what happens to the internal components?

STEP 3: SCENARIO — FAST FASHION DEFAULT
• Production: Maximum | Material: Polyester | Lifespan: 7 wears
• PREDICT FIRST: Over one year for 1,000 consumers, what total water consumption, carbon emissions, and textile waste do you predict from the fast fashion model?
• Resume the simulation and observe what happens
• Was your prediction correct?

STEP 4: SCENARIO — SUSTAINABLE WARDROBE
• Production: -50% | Material: Organic cotton/recycled | Lifespan: 100 wears
• PREDICT FIRST: Do you predict that buying half as many garments but wearing them 10x longer will reduce environmental impact by half, or by more? Why?
• Resume the simulation and observe what happens
• Was your prediction correct?

STEP 5: SCENARIO — CIRCULAR FASHION SYSTEM
• Production: Moderate | Fiber recycling: 70% recovery
• PREDICT FIRST: If 70% of textile fiber is recycled into new clothing, do you predict this will eliminate the need for virgin materials entirely? What percentage remains?
• Resume the simulation and observe what happens
• Was your prediction correct?

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

WHAT DID YOU DISCOVER?
• The fast fashion industry's environmental impact is staggering: over 100 billion garments produced annually, 10% of global CO2 emissions, 20% of global water pollution from dyeing, and 92 million tons of textile waste — driven by a business model that incentivizes buying more and wearing less
• Garment lifespan is the single most impactful variable — doubling the number of times a garment is worn before disposal cuts its per-wear environmental footprint in half, regardless of material type
• Polyester produces less water pollution than cotton during production but generates persistent microplastic pollution during washing that cotton does not — every material involves trade-offs, and no option is perfectly clean
• A circular fashion system that recycles textile fiber dramatically reduces both virgin material demand and landfill waste, but current recycling technology can only handle simple, single-fiber garments — blended fabrics (most fast fashion) are nearly impossible to recycle

THE ANSWER: Fast fashion is indeed destroying the planet — but the destruction is hidden by geography and time. The water pollution from cotton farming and textile dyeing happens in developing countries far from the consumers who buy the clothes. The microplastic pollution from polyester washing is invisible and accumulates over decades. The CO2 emissions are diffused into the global atmosphere. And the textile waste is buried in landfills that will take centuries to decompose. The $5 price tag on a fast fashion garment does not include any of these environmental costs — economists call them 'externalities.' When the model accounts for the full life cycle, the true cost of a cheap shirt is many times its purchase price. The solutions are clear: buy fewer garments, wear them longer, choose sustainable materials, and demand industry accountability for the full environmental cost of production.
```

LESSON PURPOSE:
The fashion industry has undergone a radical transformation over the past two decades. Where previous generations bought fewer, higher-quality garments and wore them for years, the rise of fast fashion has created a system where the average garment is worn just 7-10 times before being discarded. The industry now produces over 100 billion garments annually — more than 12 for every person on Earth — with devastating environmental consequences that are largely invisible to consumers.

Evaluate a solution to a complex real-world problem based on prioritized criteria and trade-offs that account for a range of constraints, including cost, safety, reliability, and aesthetics, as well as possible social, cultural, and environmental impacts.

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• The Scale of Fast Fashion:
  The global fashion industry produces over 100 billion garments per year, generating $1.7 trillion in revenue. The average consumer buys 60% more clothing than 15 years ago but keeps each item half as long. Brands like Shein add 6,000 new styles per DAY to their website. This volume is driven by a business model that profits from disposability: cheap materials, minimal construction quality, and aggressive trend cycling that makes last month's styles feel obsolete. The result is a volume of production that no waste system can handle.

• Water: The Hidden Cost of Cotton and Dye:
  Cotton accounts for 24% of global fiber production and is extraordinarily water-intensive: growing enough cotton for a single t-shirt requires approximately 2,700 liters of water — equivalent to what one person drinks in 2.5 years. The Aral Sea in Central Asia has shrunk by 90% largely due to cotton irrigation. Textile dyeing is the second-largest water polluter globally, using over 1.5 trillion liters of water annually and discharging heavy metals, formaldehyde, and other toxins into rivers. In countries like Bangladesh, rivers near textile factories run the color of the season's trends.

• Carbon and Microplastics: The Polyester Problem:
  Polyester, made from petroleum, now accounts for 60% of all clothing fiber. Its production releases approximately 700 million tons of CO2 annually. But the carbon footprint is only part of the problem. When polyester garments are washed, they shed up to 700,000 microplastic fibers per load. These fibers pass through wastewater treatment plants, enter oceans and rivers, are consumed by marine life, and bioaccumulate up the food chain. Microplastics have been found in human blood, lung tissue, and placenta. Unlike organic fibers, synthetic microplastics persist in the environment for hundreds of years.

• The Waste Crisis: Where Clothes Go to Die:
  Of the 92 million tons of textile waste produced annually, 87% goes to landfills or is incinerated. Only 13% is recycled, and of that, most is 'downcycled' into lower-value products like insulation or rags rather than new clothing. The fundamental problem is that most fast fashion is made from fiber blends (polyester-cotton, nylon-elastane) that current recycling technology cannot separate. A polyester shirt in a landfill will take 200+ years to decompose, leaching chemicals throughout. The fashion industry's linear model — extract, produce, consume, discard — is fundamentally incompatible with environmental sustainability.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Recycling and donating clothes solves the waste problem"
  Reality: Only 13% of discarded clothing is recycled, and most of that is downcycled into insulation, rags, or carpet padding — not new clothing. Of clothing donated to thrift stores, only 10-20% is sold domestically; the rest is shipped to developing countries where it overwhelms local textile industries and often ends up in landfills anyway. True textile-to-textile recycling is limited by fiber blending — most fast fashion garments contain mixed fibers that cannot be separated for recycling. The model shows that reducing production and extending lifespan have far greater impact than end-of-life recycling.
  Strategy: Show the flow: 100 garments donated → 15 sold in thrift stores → 35 exported (many landfilled abroad) → 50 directly landfilled or incinerated. Ask: Is donating really the solution it feels like?

• "Natural fibers like cotton are always better for the environment than synthetics"
  Reality: While cotton avoids the microplastic problem of polyester, conventional cotton farming is enormously water-intensive (2,700 liters per t-shirt), requires heavy pesticide use (16% of global insecticide use for 2.4% of cropland), and degrades soil. Polyester requires less water and land but produces microplastic pollution and relies on petroleum. The model shows that no fiber is universally 'better' — the environmental profile depends on which impacts you prioritize. Organic cotton, recycled polyester, and hemp each represent different trade-off profiles.
  Strategy: Run the model with cotton versus polyester and compare across ALL metrics — water, carbon, microplastics, land use. Neither wins on every metric. Ask: If every impact matters, how do you choose?

• "Individual consumer choices can fix the fast fashion problem"
  Reality: While consumer choices matter — buying less, wearing longer, choosing better materials — the scale of the problem is fundamentally driven by industry business models. Fast fashion companies produce 100+ billion garments per year because the business model profits from disposability and trend cycling. Systemic change requires policy (extended producer responsibility, environmental labeling), technology (textile recycling at scale), and business model transformation (rental, resale, repair services) alongside consumer behavior change.
  Strategy: Compare the impact of one consumer switching to sustainable fashion versus one policy requiring all brands to fund textile recycling. Ask: Which creates more change? (The policy, by orders of magnitude.) Both are needed, but individual action alone cannot solve a systemic problem.

ANSWER KEY:
Big Question: If a $5 t-shirt costs the planet 2,700 liters of water, 10 kilograms of CO2, and microplastic pollution that lasts centuries — who really pays the price?
Answer: Fast fashion is indeed destroying the planet — but the destruction is hidden by geography and time. The water pollution from cotton farming and textile dyeing happens in developing countries far from the consumers who buy the clothes. The microplastic pollution from polyester washing is invisible and accumulates over decades. The CO2 emissions are diffused into the global atmosphere. And the textile waste is buried in landfills that will take centuries to decompose. The $5 price tag on a fast fashion garment does not include any of these environmental costs — economists call them 'externalities.' When the model accounts for the full life cycle, the true cost of a cheap shirt is many times its purchase price. The solutions are clear: buy fewer garments, wear them longer, choose sustainable materials, and demand industry accountability for the full environmental cost of production.

Simulation Answers:
• Fast Fashion Default Scenario: With maximum Production Volume, polyester Material Type, and 7-wear Garment Lifespan, the model shows devastating environmental impact for 1,000 consumers over one year: approximately 135 million liters of water consumed, 275,000 kg of CO2 emitted, and 37,000 kg of textile waste generated. Each consumer's clothing footprint is equivalent to driving a car 14,000 miles or filling a swimming pool with water. The waste volume would fill 1.5 garbage trucks per day.
• Sustainable Wardrobe Scenario: With 50% reduced Production Volume, organic cotton/recycled fiber, and 100-wear Garment Lifespan, the model shows dramatic reductions: water consumption drops 78% to approximately 30 million liters, carbon emissions drop 72% to approximately 77,000 kg, and textile waste drops 85% to approximately 5,500 kg. The garment lifespan increase is the single largest driver of improvement — even more impactful than the material switch — because each garment's environmental cost is amortized across many more uses.

Reflection Exemplars:
• Q: Why is garment lifespan a more powerful lever than material choice?
  A: My model shows that doubling garment lifespan from 7 to 14 wears reduces per-wear environmental impact by approximately 50% across ALL metrics — water, carbon, and waste — because the production impact is divided by more uses. Switching from polyester to organic cotton reduces water impact significantly but increases carbon in some production scenarios, and doesn't affect the rate at which clothing enters the waste stream. Lifespan is a universal multiplier that improves everything simultaneously. A well-made polyester shirt worn 100 times has a lower environmental footprint than an organic cotton shirt worn 10 times. This means quality and durability matter more than material alone.
• Q: What are the ethical implications of fast fashion's hidden costs?
  A: The model reveals that the $5 price tag on a fast fashion garment externalizes enormous environmental costs: water pollution in cotton-growing regions (often in developing countries), carbon emissions that drive global climate change, microplastic pollution that contaminates ecosystems and human bodies worldwide, and textile waste that fills landfills for centuries. These costs are real — they're just borne by different people and future generations rather than the consumer at the cash register. This is fundamentally an issue of environmental justice: the people who profit from and consume fast fashion are rarely the ones who suffer its environmental consequences.

Key Concepts:
• Extended Producer Responsibility: A policy framework where manufacturers are financially responsible for the end-of-life management of their products. In fashion, this could mean brands must fund textile recycling infrastructure, accept returns of worn garments, or pay disposal fees proportional to their production volume.
• Cost-Per-Wear Economics: A consumer metric that divides the purchase price by the number of times a garment is worn. A $5 fast fashion shirt worn 5 times costs $1/wear; a $30 quality shirt worn 100 times costs $0.30/wear. Communicating cost-per-wear can shift consumer behavior from cheap disposability to affordable durability.
• Textile-to-Textile Recycling: Advanced recycling processes that break down old garments into raw fibers or chemical feedstocks for new clothing production. Current technology works well for single-fiber garments (100% cotton, 100% polyester) but struggles with blended fabrics that dominate fast fashion.

Evaluation Rubric:
• Expert (4): Brand design specifies materials with LCA data, includes durability testing targets, proposes viable end-of-life strategy, demonstrates 70%+ reduction across all three metrics using model evidence, and addresses commercial viability for teen consumers
• Proficient (3): Brand design makes reasonable material and durability choices with some model evidence and considers end-of-life management
• Developing (2): Brand design identifies sustainable materials but lacks specific reduction targets, model evidence, or comprehensive end-of-life strategy
• Beginning (1): Brand design is vague or does not use model data to demonstrate environmental improvement over fast fashion

STEM CHALLENGE: Design a Sustainable Fashion Brand
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

MISSION: Design a clothing brand that produces fashionable garments while reducing environmental impact by at least 70% compared to fast fashion benchmarks across water, carbon, and waste metrics.

SCENARIO: A venture capital firm wants to invest in a sustainable fashion startup and has hired your environmental science team to design the brand's environmental strategy. The brand must produce affordable, stylish clothing while reducing per-garment water consumption by 70%, carbon emissions by 70%, and textile waste by 80% compared to fast fashion industry averages. Your design must specify material choices, production processes, garment durability targets, and end-of-life strategies.

GUIDING QUESTIONS:
• What material choices and production processes does your model show will achieve the 70% reduction targets for water and carbon?
• How will your brand ensure garments have a lifespan of at least 50 wears — and what model evidence shows the impact of increased lifespan?
• What end-of-life strategy does your model suggest for achieving 80% waste reduction — recycling, resale, composting, or a combination?

DESIGN THINKING:
• What specific fibers will you use and what are the environmental trade-offs of each compared to conventional polyester and cotton?

  _________________________________________________________

• How will you keep prices accessible to teenagers while absorbing the higher costs of sustainable materials and ethical production?

  _________________________________________________________

• What take-back or recycling program will you implement, and what percentage of sold garments does your model predict will actually be returned?

  _________________________________________________________

• How will you communicate the true cost-per-wear to consumers to shift purchasing behavior away from disposable fashion?

  _________________________________________________________


EVALUATION RUBRIC:
• Expert (4): Brand design specifies materials with LCA data, includes durability testing targets, proposes viable end-of-life strategy, demonstrates 70%+ reduction across all three metrics using model evidence, and addresses commercial viability for teen consumers
• Proficient (3): Brand design makes reasonable material and durability choices with some model evidence and considers end-of-life management
• Developing (2): Brand design identifies sustainable materials but lacks specific reduction targets, model evidence, or comprehensive end-of-life strategy
• Beginning (1): Brand design is vague or does not use model data to demonstrate environmental improvement over fast fashion
