### Title
**Why Do Bridges Collapse? — Modeling Structural Forces, Material Fatigue, and Engineering Failure**

### Overview
On August 1, 2007, the I-35W bridge in Minneapolis collapsed during rush hour, dropping 111 vehicles into the Mississippi River and killing 13 people. The bridge was not struck by a tornado or earthquake — it was carrying normal traffic on an ordinary Wednesday. The collapse was caused by decades of material fatigue, corrosion, and deferred maintenance that had invisibly reduced the bridge's structural capacity below its design safety threshold. Understanding how and why bridges fail requires modeling the invisible process of material fatigue. Students investigate the driving question: If engineers can calculate exactly how much force a bridge can handle, why do bridges still collapse — and what invisible process is weakening the structures we drive over every day? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

### Learning Objectives
- Model how tension, compression, and shear forces distribute through different bridge structural designs
- Explain the relationship between cyclic loading, material fatigue, and progressive structural weakening over time
- Predict the failure point of a bridge design based on load capacity, material fatigue rate, and maintenance interval
- Analyze how inspection frequency and maintenance investment affect bridge safety and prevent catastrophic failure

### Component List (Starting Model: 6 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Applied Load | External | The total force exerted on the bridge by vehicles, pedestrians, and the bridge's own weight (dead load) — a fully loaded semi-truck weighs approximately 36,000 kg, and rush hour traffic can place hundreds of trucks on a bridge simultaneously |
| Structural Design Type | External | The bridge's load-bearing configuration — beam, truss, arch, suspension, or cable-stayed — each distributes forces differently and has different strengths, weaknesses, and failure modes |
| Maintenance Interval | External | How frequently the bridge is inspected and repaired — regular maintenance catches fatigue cracks early; deferred maintenance allows cracks to grow toward critical failure length |
| Force Distribution | Internal | How the Applied Load is transferred through structural members via tension, compression, and shear — well-designed structures distribute forces evenly; poor designs create stress concentrations where failure initiates |
| Fatigue Damage | Internal | The cumulative microscopic crack growth caused by millions of repeated loading cycles — fatigue damage is invisible without specialized inspection and accumulates over years, reducing the bridge's load capacity |
| Structural Integrity | Internal | The overall ability of the bridge to safely carry its designed load — starts at 100% when new and decreases as fatigue damage accumulates, with catastrophic failure occurring when integrity drops below the critical threshold |

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

WHAT DID YOU DISCOVER?
• Material fatigue is invisible and cumulative — a bridge can lose 30% of its structural integrity over decades while appearing perfectly fine to visual inspection, because fatigue cracks grow inside the metal at the microscopic level
• Regular maintenance and inspection extend bridge life dramatically — not because they repair fatigue damage, but because they detect cracks early when they can be reinforced, before reaching critical length for sudden fracture
• Deferred maintenance creates a dangerous illusion of safety — the bridge looks the same on the outside, but internal fatigue damage is accumulating unchecked, and the margin between current load and failure capacity is shrinking invisibly
• Most bridge collapses are not caused by a single extraordinary event but by the combination of cumulative fatigue, deferred maintenance, and a load event that would have been survivable on a well-maintained structure

THE ANSWER: Bridges collapse not because engineers make calculation errors, but because of a silent, invisible process called material fatigue. Every vehicle that crosses a bridge creates tiny stress cycles in the steel and concrete. Over millions of cycles across decades, microscopic cracks form at stress concentration points and grow incrementally. A new bridge with a Factor of Safety of 2.0 can handle the load easily, but after 30-50 years of cyclic loading, those fatigue cracks have silently reduced its capacity. If inspections catch the cracks early, they can be repaired. If maintenance is deferred — as it is for 42% of American bridges — the cracks grow unchecked until a load event that the bridge could have easily handled when new becomes the event that triggers catastrophic failure. The I-35W bridge collapse in Minneapolis (2007) killed 13 people not because the traffic that day was unusual, but because decades of fatigue and deferred maintenance had invisibly reduced the bridge below its safety threshold.

Understanding how and why bridges fail requires modeling the invisible process of material fatigue.

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• Forces in Bridges: Tension, Compression, and Shear:
  Every bridge must transfer the weight of vehicles and its own structure safely to the ground through a series of structural members under different types of stress. Tension stretches members (like cables in a suspension bridge), compression squeezes them (like columns and piers), and shear acts perpendicular to the member's length (like the force on a beam at its supports). Different bridge designs distribute these forces differently: arches convert loads primarily to compression, trusses use a network of triangles to convert loads to axial forces, and suspension bridges hang the deck from cables in tension. Engineers analyze these forces using Newton's laws and statics to ensure every member can safely carry its share.

• Material Fatigue: The Silent Killer:
  When a material is subjected to a single large force, it either holds or breaks — this is ultimate strength. But material fatigue is different: it occurs when forces well below ultimate strength are applied repeatedly. Each load cycle creates microscopic damage at stress concentration points — holes, welds, sharp corners, and surface scratches. Over millions of cycles, a tiny crack initiates and grows incrementally with each cycle. The crack is invisible without specialized testing (ultrasonic, radiographic, magnetic particle) until it reaches a critical length, at which point the remaining material can no longer support the load and fracture occurs suddenly. Fatigue causes an estimated 90% of all metallic structural failures.

• The I-35W Bridge: Anatomy of a Collapse:
  The I-35W bridge was a steel deck truss bridge built in 1967. A 2001 study classified it as 'structurally deficient' due to fatigue cracking and corrosion. In 2005, it was rated '4 out of 9' for structural condition — meaning 'poor.' Despite these warnings, the bridge was kept in service. On the day of the collapse, construction work had placed additional steel and aggregate on the deck, adding approximately 262 tons of extra dead load. The NTSB investigation found that undersized gusset plates (connecting plates at truss joints) had corroded and fatigued over 40 years, and the additional construction load pushed these weakened plates past their reduced capacity. The bridge did not fail because of extraordinary circumstances — it failed because ordinary circumstances met a structure that had been silently weakening for decades.

• Infrastructure Crisis: The Scope of the Problem:
  The American Society of Civil Engineers (ASCE) gives US infrastructure a grade of C- overall, with bridges specifically rated C. As of 2023, approximately 42% of all US bridges are over 50 years old, and 7.5% (approximately 46,100 bridges) are classified as structurally deficient. The backlog of bridge maintenance and repair is estimated at $125 billion. State departments of transportation lack the funding to inspect and maintain all bridges at recommended intervals, forcing risk-based triage. The consequence is that millions of Americans cross bridges every day that are carrying invisible fatigue damage at unknown levels — a situation that transforms a manageable engineering problem into a public safety crisis.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Bridges collapse because of design or construction errors"
  Reality: While design and construction defects can cause failures, the majority of bridge collapses are caused by material fatigue accumulated over decades of normal service, compounded by insufficient maintenance and inspection. The bridge was designed correctly for its expected loads — but fatigue is a time-dependent process that was poorly understood when many current bridges were built, and maintenance has not kept pace with the aging infrastructure. The model shows that even a perfectly designed bridge will eventually fail if fatigue damage is not detected and repaired.
  Strategy: Show the model with a well-designed bridge (Factor of Safety 2.0) that still accumulates fatigue over 50 years. Ask: Was the design wrong? (No — the maintenance was wrong.) A good design with no maintenance is worse than a mediocre design with good maintenance.

• "If a bridge hasn't collapsed yet, it must be safe"
  Reality: This is survivorship bias applied to infrastructure. A bridge that has stood for 50 years may have accumulated significant invisible fatigue damage and may be much closer to failure than anyone realizes. The I-35W bridge stood for 40 years before collapsing during normal traffic. The model shows that Structural Integrity can decrease by 40% without any visible external signs. 'It hasn't failed yet' is not evidence of safety — it's evidence that the failure threshold hasn't been crossed YET.
  Strategy: Run the model showing a bridge declining from 100% to 55% Structural Integrity over 40 years with no visible change. Ask: At what point was this bridge 'unsafe'? When did the danger become visible? (Never — until collapse.)

• "We can see when a bridge is deteriorating — it will show signs of damage"
  Reality: Surface corrosion and visible cracks are obvious, but the most dangerous type of damage — internal fatigue cracking — is completely invisible without specialized testing equipment. Fatigue cracks initiate at microscopic defects inside the metal and grow internally for years before reaching the surface. By the time a fatigue crack is visible, it may already be at or near critical failure length. This is why non-destructive testing methods (ultrasonic, radiographic, magnetic particle) are essential — and why visual-only inspections are insufficient for detecting fatigue.
  Strategy: Show images of ultrasonic fatigue crack detection — the crack is invisible to the eye but clearly visible on the ultrasound screen. Ask: If you can't see it, smell it, or hear it, how do you know it's there? (You test for it — which requires time, equipment, and money.)

ANSWER KEY:
Big Question: If engineers can calculate exactly how much force a bridge can handle, why do bridges still collapse — and what invisible process is weakening the structures we drive over every day?
Answer: Bridges collapse not because engineers make calculation errors, but because of a silent, invisible process called material fatigue. Every vehicle that crosses a bridge creates tiny stress cycles in the steel and concrete. Over millions of cycles across decades, microscopic cracks form at stress concentration points and grow incrementally. A new bridge with a Factor of Safety of 2.0 can handle the load easily, but after 30-50 years of cyclic loading, those fatigue cracks have silently reduced its capacity. If inspections catch the cracks early, they can be repaired. If maintenance is deferred — as it is for 42% of American bridges — the cracks grow unchecked until a load event that the bridge could have easily handled when new becomes the event that triggers catastrophic failure. The I-35W bridge collapse in Minneapolis (2007) killed 13 people not because the traffic that day was unusual, but because decades of fatigue and deferred maintenance had invisibly reduced the bridge below its safety threshold.

Simulation Answers:
• Normal Service Life Scenario: Over 50 years with standard traffic and 2-year maintenance intervals, the model shows Fatigue Damage accumulating gradually in a predictable S-curve — slow at first, accelerating during middle life, then slowing as the most stressed areas are identified and repaired during inspections. Structural Integrity decreases from 100% to approximately 75% over the 50-year period. Each inspection catches growing cracks at 2-4 year intervals and reinforcement restores partial capacity. The bridge remains safely above the critical failure threshold throughout its service life.
• Deferred Maintenance Scenario: With the same traffic but inspections every 10 years, the model shows identical early-life fatigue accumulation but dramatically different outcomes after year 20. Without biennial inspections, cracks that would have been caught at 3-5mm length grow unchecked to 15-30mm over 10-year inspection gaps. By year 35, Structural Integrity has dropped to approximately 55% — dangerously close to the failure threshold. The overloading event at 70% integrity (150% load) causes catastrophic failure in the deferred-maintenance scenario while the well-maintained bridge at 85% integrity survives the same event.

Reflection Exemplars:
• Q: Why is material fatigue so dangerous compared to other types of damage?
  A: Material fatigue is uniquely dangerous because it is invisible, cumulative, and catastrophic. Unlike corrosion (which is visible on surfaces) or overload damage (which deforms the structure visibly), fatigue cracks initiate inside the metal at stress concentration points and grow microscopically with each load cycle. A bridge can have extensive internal fatigue damage while appearing perfectly intact to visual inspection. My model shows that a bridge at 60% Structural Integrity looks identical to one at 95% from the outside. Only specialized testing (ultrasonic, radiographic) can detect internal fatigue. And when a fatigue crack reaches its critical length, failure is sudden and total — there is no warning deformation or gradual sagging.
• Q: Why do governments defer bridge maintenance when they know the consequences?
  A: This is a classic case of visible costs versus invisible risks. Maintenance budgets are visible and compete with politically popular spending like schools, roads, and tax cuts. The risk of bridge collapse is invisible — you can't see fatigue damage, and no specific bridge is guaranteed to fail in any given year. Politicians face pressure to cut 'wasteful' maintenance spending because the consequences are probabilistic and delayed. My model shows that deferring maintenance saves money in years 1-10 but creates exponentially growing risk in years 20-40. The I-35W bridge collapse was the invisible cost becoming tragically visible. Infrastructure maintenance is the ultimate example of paying now versus paying much more later.

Key Concepts:
• Non-Destructive Testing: Inspection techniques that detect internal damage without harming the structure — ultrasonic testing sends sound waves through metal to find cracks, magnetic particle inspection reveals surface-breaking cracks, and radiographic testing uses X-rays to image internal defects. These methods detect fatigue damage years before it becomes visible.
• Risk-Based Inspection: A maintenance strategy that allocates limited inspection resources based on risk factors — age, traffic volume, structural type, maintenance history, and environmental exposure. High-risk bridges get more frequent inspections; low-risk bridges can be inspected less often, optimizing budget use.
• Structural Health Monitoring: Sensor systems permanently installed on bridges that continuously measure strain, vibration, deflection, and environmental conditions. Real-time data allows engineers to detect changes in structural behavior that indicate fatigue damage without physical inspection, enabling condition-based rather than schedule-based maintenance.

• Developing (2): Plan proposes maintenance activities but lacks risk-based prioritization, model evidence, or budget optimization
• Beginning (1): Plan is incomplete, spreads budget evenly without risk assessment, or does not connect maintenance to fatigue damage from model

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format. Each item is three-dimensional, assessing a Science and Engineering Practice (SEP), Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and Crosscutting Concept (CCC) simultaneously, aligned to HS-PS2-1, HS-ETS1-2.

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.1 (Develop and use a model to represent relationships) + DCI PS2.A (Newton's second law of motion) + CCC4 (Systems and system models)

A structural engineering team investigates why a pedestrian bridge in a California city developed visible cracks after only 15 years of service, well before its designed 75-year lifespan. Analysis reveals that the bridge was designed to handle a static load of 85 pounds per square foot and dynamic loads from pedestrian traffic, but the actual usage pattern includes heavy delivery vehicles using the bridge as a shortcut, imposing forces 4 times the design specification. The team models the relationship between applied force, material stress, and structural failure.

A student's 50-year simulation shows that Structural Integrity decreases linearly for the first 30 years but then drops exponentially in years 30-40 when maintenance is deferred. Which explanation best accounts for this nonlinear transition?

A. Without maintenance to repair small cracks, fatigue damage compounds: existing cracks accelerate the growth of new cracks through stress concentration, creating a self-reinforcing degradation cycle
B. The simulation model switches to a different equation at year 30
C. Traffic volume always increases exponentially after 30 years
D. The material suddenly becomes weaker at the 30-year mark due to age

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: Deferred maintenance allows fatigue cracks to grow past the point where they concentrate stress at their tips, accelerating further crack growth. Small cracks that could have been cheaply repaired become large cracks that create stress concentrations, which generate more cracks. The degradation becomes self-reinforcing. If you chose D, this response does not account for the key mechanism or relationship the evidence demonstrates. Consider what happens when small fatigue cracks are not repaired. A crack concentrates stress at its tip, making that location more likely to crack further. This self-reinforcing process means crack growth accelerates over time, especially when maintenance that would have interrupted this cycle is skipped. ### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships among system components) + DCI PS2.A (Newton's second law of motion) + CCC2 (Cause and effect)

A computational model of structural forces in a suspension bridge identifies four key variables: cable tension (supporting force), gravitational load (weight of the bridge and traffic), wind force (lateral stress), and material fatigue (cumulative damage from repeated stress cycles). The model shows that under normal conditions, the forces balance. However, when wind speed exceeds a critical threshold while the bridge carries maximum traffic load, the combined forces exceed the material's yield strength, causing progressive failure.

The model compares three bridge designs under identical loading: beam, truss, and arch. The truss bridge shows the most even force distribution across its members, while the beam bridge shows high stress concentration at midspan. What structural principle explains this difference?

A. Truss bridges weigh less than beam bridges, so they experience less force
B. Beam bridges receive more traffic than truss bridges
C. The truss geometry converts bending forces into axial tension and compression distributed across many triangular members, while the beam concentrates bending stress at a single cross-section
D. Truss bridges are always made of stronger materials than beam bridges

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: The fundamental advantage of a truss is geometric: triangular elements convert applied bending loads into axial forces (pure tension or compression) in individual members. This distributes stress across many members rather than concentrating it at the critical midspan section of a beam. A single beam must resist bending at its weakest point (midspan). A truss breaks the same load into many smaller tension and compression forces in individual members. The same total load is shared across the structure. If you chose B, this answer does not account for the key mechanism or relationship the evidence demonstrates. ### Question 3

Engineers analyze the 1940 Tacoma Narrows Bridge collapse using modern computational modeling. Their simulation demonstrates that wind at 42 mph created aerodynamic flutter, a positive feedback loop where wind-induced oscillations increased the bridge deck's surface area exposed to wind, which amplified the oscillations further. The model shows that the bridge entered resonance, where the frequency of wind-driven oscillations matched the bridge's natural frequency, exponentially increasing amplitude until structural failure occurred.

A student's model predicts that doubling the maintenance inspection frequency extends bridge service life by 40%, while doubling the factor of safety at design only extends it by 15%. Which analysis best explains why maintenance has a larger effect than initial overdesign?

A. Maintenance makes the bridge stronger than its original design
B. A higher factor of safety delays the onset of critical damage but does not stop fatigue accumulation, while regular inspections catch and repair damage before it enters the exponential growth phase, resetting the degradation clock
C. The factor of safety has no effect on bridge longevity
D. Maintenance is always cheaper than building a stronger bridge

Correct Answer: B

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to explain/predict events) + DCI PS2.A (Newton's second law of motion) + CCC7 (Stability and change)

A bridge inspection team uses sensor data to monitor stress levels in a 40-year-old highway overpass. Data from strain gauges shows that peak stress during rush hour has increased by 35% over the past decade due to heavier vehicles, while the steel's fatigue resistance has decreased by approximately 20% due to corrosion from road salt exposure. The team's model predicts that the bridge will reach its critical stress threshold within 3-7 years under current conditions, but this timeline could shorten dramatically if both trends continue simultaneously.

The simulation reveals that a bridge designed for 20,000 vehicles per day is now carrying 35,000 per day. The model predicts failure 18 years earlier than the original design life. A student argues this is solely the result of increased load exceeding the factor of safety. Which model-based critique identifies the missing factor?

A. Vehicle weight has not changed, so the total load is the same
B. The student's analysis is complete and correct
C. The bridge should be able to handle any amount of traffic because it was overdesigned
D. The student ignores the fatigue acceleration: 75% more load cycles per day means fatigue damage accumulates 75% faster, and fatigue failure can occur even while the instantaneous load remains within the factor of safety margin

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: The student focused only on peak load versus capacity, missing the critical fatigue component. Even if each individual truck is within the load limit, 75% more cycles per day means 75% more fatigue damage accumulation. Fatigue failure depends on cumulative cycles, not just peak force. If you chose B, this response does not account for the key mechanism or relationship the evidence demonstrates. The student is thinking about static load (can the bridge hold the weight right now?) but missing the dynamic effect. Material fatigue depends on the NUMBER of loading cycles, not just the magnitude of each cycle. More vehicles means more cycles, which means faster fatigue accumulation.
