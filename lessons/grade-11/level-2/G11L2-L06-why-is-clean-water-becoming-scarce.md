### Title
**Why Is Clean Water Becoming Scarce? — Modeling Global Freshwater Systems, Aquifer Depletion, and Water Stress**

### Overview
Water is the most essential and most overlooked resource crisis of the 21st century. While climate change dominates headlines, the quiet depletion of underground aquifers that took thousands to millions of years to fill threatens the food supply of billions. Understanding global water scarcity requires modeling the interaction between the water cycle, human demand, agricultural systems, and the geological constraints on groundwater replenishment. Students investigate the driving question: If Earth is 70% water, why are billions of people running out of clean water — and why can't we just make more? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

### Learning Objectives
- Model how the global water cycle distributes freshwater and how human extraction disrupts the recharge-withdrawal balance of aquifer systems
- Analyze the feedback loops connecting population growth, agricultural demand, aquifer depletion, and water stress that create scarcity spirals
- Predict the timelines for aquifer depletion in major agricultural regions and the cascading effects on food production
- Evaluate the trade-offs between different water management strategies including conservation, desalination, and groundwater governance

### Component List (Starting Model: 6 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Population and Agricultural Demand | External | The total human water withdrawal for drinking, sanitation, industry, and especially agriculture — irrigation accounts for 70% of global freshwater use and is increasing with population growth and changing diets |
| Groundwater Extraction Rate | External | The volume of water pumped from underground aquifers per year — currently exceeding natural recharge rates in most major agricultural aquifers worldwide, effectively mining an irreplaceable resource |
| Aquifer Water Level | Internal | The current volume of water stored in underground aquifer systems, measured by well depth and satellite gravity data — declining at alarming rates in major breadbasket aquifers globally |
| Natural Recharge Rate | Internal | The rate at which precipitation and surface water replenish aquifer systems through soil infiltration — constrained by geology, climate, and land cover, and declining in many regions due to urbanization and climate change |
| Surface Water Availability | Internal | The amount of freshwater available in rivers, lakes, and reservoirs, which depends on precipitation, snowpack, evaporation, and upstream withdrawals — increasingly unreliable due to climate change |
| Regional Food Production Capacity | Internal | The agricultural output of a region, critically dependent on reliable water supply — regions facing aquifer depletion will experience irreversible declines in food production capacity as groundwater disappears |

• Click on "Natural Recharge Rate" and drag an arrow to "Aquifer Water Level"

Task C: SET POSITIVE OR NEGATIVE
• Look at the +/− toggle in the TOP LEFT corner
• For each connection, ask: "When this goes UP, does the other go UP or DOWN?"

  ○ Population and Agricultural Demand → Groundwater Extraction Rate = POSITIVE (+)
    Higher population and agricultural demand drives increased Groundwater Extraction Rate as farmers pump more water to irrigate expanding cropland. Agriculture accounts for 70% of freshwater use, and demand grows with both population and per-capita consumption.

  ○ Groundwater Extraction Rate → Aquifer Water Level = NEGATIVE (−)
    Higher extraction directly depletes Aquifer Water Level by removing water faster than natural processes can replenish it. The relationship is arithmetic: if extraction exceeds recharge, the aquifer declines at a rate equal to the difference.

  ○ Aquifer Water Level → Regional Food Production Capacity = POSITIVE (+)
    Higher Aquifer Water Level means reliable irrigation supply, supporting full agricultural production. As the aquifer declines, wells must go deeper (increasing cost), eventually reaching depths where pumping is no longer economically viable, and finally running dry entirely.

  ○ Natural Recharge Rate → Aquifer Water Level = POSITIVE (+)
    Higher Natural Recharge Rate slows or reverses aquifer depletion by adding water back to the system. However, in fossil aquifer systems, recharge is negligible compared to extraction — the natural input cannot compensate for the human output.

Task D: CHECK YOUR MODEL
• You should have 4 arrows total
• 1 negative relationship(s), 3 positive relationship(s)
• This is your system model!

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

WHAT DID YOU DISCOVER?
• Only 0.5% of Earth's total water is accessible freshwater — the rest is saltwater (97.5%) or frozen in ice caps and glaciers (2%) — making freshwater one of the most limited and unevenly distributed resources on the planet
• Major agricultural aquifers including the Ogallala (USA), North China Plain, Upper Ganges (India), and Arabian aquifer systems are being depleted 3-10 times faster than natural recharge, effectively mining fossil water that accumulated over millennia
• Agriculture consumes 70% of all freshwater withdrawals globally, with inefficient flood irrigation wasting up to 60% of applied water — the food system is by far the largest driver of water scarcity
• Water scarcity creates cascading system failures: depleted aquifers reduce food production, food shortages drive migration, migration concentrates populations in cities that face their own water stress — a compounding crisis

THE ANSWER: Earth has vast quantities of water, but only 0.5% is accessible freshwater, and its distribution is profoundly unequal. The real crisis is groundwater depletion: humanity is pumping aquifers 3-10 times faster than rain can refill them, effectively mining fossil water accumulated over thousands to millions of years. Agriculture consumes 70% of global freshwater, and as population grows and diets shift toward more water-intensive foods, demand is surging. The model reveals that aquifer depletion is functionally irreversible — once an aquifer is drained, the geological timescale for refilling (thousands of years) means it is gone for all practical purposes. We cannot simply 'make more' freshwater: desalination is energy-intensive and expensive, water recycling has limits, and efficiency improvements alone cannot close the growing gap between supply and demand. The solution requires a fundamental transformation of agricultural water use, groundwater governance, and food system design.

Do: Have students read objectives. Pre-teach 'aquifer' using a sponge demonstration — saturate a sponge (aquifer full), squeeze slowly (extraction), drip water on top (recharge). Show that squeezing is much faster than dripping.
Time: 3 min

SLIDE 3: BIG QUESTION
Visual: Why is clean water becoming scarce?
Say: "70% of Earth is water. But if Earth's water fit in a gallon jug, the freshwater you could actually use would be about one tablespoon. That's it. For 8 billion people."
Do: Physical demonstration: bring a gallon jug of water. Pour out a tablespoon. Hold it up: 'This is all the freshwater we have.' Let it sink in. Quick-write: Who decides who gets that tablespoon?
Time: 3 min

SLIDE 4: LEVER FRAMEWORK
Visual: LEVER steps overview
Say: "We're modeling a resource that took millions of years to accumulate and decades to drain. A stock-and-flow system where the drain is 100 times bigger than the faucet."

LESSON PURPOSE:
Water is the most essential and most overlooked resource crisis of the 21st century. While climate change dominates headlines, the quiet depletion of underground aquifers that took thousands to millions of years to fill threatens the food supply of billions. Understanding global water scarcity requires modeling the interaction between the water cycle, human demand, agricultural systems, and the geological constraints on groundwater replenishment.

LEVER FRAMEWORK:
• Locate: Students identify six components of the freshwater system: Population and Agricultural Demand, Groundwater Extraction Rate, Aquifer Water Level, Natural Recharge Rate, Surface Water Availability, and Regional Food Production Capacity — distinguishing human-controlled withdrawal decisions from natural system responses.
• Establish: Students determine that Population Demand drives Groundwater Extraction Rate, which depletes Aquifer Water Level faster than Natural Recharge can replenish it. As Aquifer Water Level drops, extraction costs increase and food production capacity declines. Surface Water Availability is insufficient to replace groundwater, creating a structural deficit.
• Visualize: Students build a computational model showing the stock-and-flow dynamics of aquifer systems — the slow trickle of recharge versus the high-volume pump of extraction — visualizing the depletion timeline.
• Evaluate: Students run sustainable, current overdraft, and climate-change-compounded scenarios to quantify depletion timelines and identify the withdrawal rate that matches recharge for long-term sustainability.
• Revise: Students add Desalination Capacity, Climate Change Impact on Precipitation, or Water Pricing Policy to explore technological, climatic, and economic dimensions of water management.

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• Earth's Water Budget:
  Of Earth's total water, 97.5% is saltwater. Of the remaining 2.5% freshwater, 69% is locked in ice caps and glaciers, 30% is groundwater, and only 1% (0.025% of total water) is accessible surface freshwater in rivers, lakes, and soil moisture. This tiny fraction supports all terrestrial life, agriculture, and human civilization. The water cycle continuously recycles this water through evaporation, precipitation, and runoff — but the rate of cycling is fixed by solar energy and cannot be accelerated by human technology.

• Groundwater: The Hidden Resource:
  Aquifers — underground layers of permeable rock saturated with water — store approximately 10.6 million cubic kilometers of freshwater, about 100 times more than all surface freshwater combined. Some aquifers (like those in limestone karst regions) recharge relatively quickly from rainfall. But many major agricultural aquifers are 'fossil' water — accumulated during wetter climate periods thousands to millions of years ago, with negligible current recharge. The Ogallala Aquifer beneath the U.S. Great Plains, the North China Plain aquifer, and the Upper Ganges aquifer all fall into this category. Pumping fossil water is mining a non-renewable resource.

• The Agriculture Water Crisis:
  Agriculture consumes 70% of all freshwater withdrawals globally — and much of this is profoundly inefficient. Flood irrigation, still used on the majority of irrigated cropland, loses up to 60% of applied water to evaporation and runoff. Shifting to drip irrigation can reduce water use by 30-70% but requires significant capital investment. The global trend toward more water-intensive diets (more meat, more dairy) is increasing per-capita water demand even as population grows. Producing one kilogram of beef requires approximately 15,000 liters of water, compared to 1,800 liters for a kilogram of wheat.

• Cascading Consequences of Depletion:
  When aquifers are depleted, the consequences cascade through multiple systems. First, food production collapses in regions that depend on groundwater irrigation — the Ogallala Aquifer supports $20 billion in annual agricultural production. Second, land subsidence occurs as emptied aquifer spaces compact, permanently reducing storage capacity — Mexico City has sunk over 10 meters in some areas. Third, water scarcity drives migration, creating 'water refugees' who concentrate in cities that face their own water stress. Fourth, competition for remaining water resources creates political instability — the World Bank has warned that water scarcity will be a primary driver of conflict by 2050.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "There's plenty of water — it just needs to be distributed better"
  Reality: While water distribution is unequal, the fundamental problem in many regions is absolute depletion of groundwater stocks that cannot be replaced on human timescales. Redistribution cannot solve the crisis when total available water is declining. Furthermore, transporting water over long distances is enormously expensive and energy-intensive.
  Strategy: Show the global water stress map. Point out that some of the most water-stressed regions are also major food producers. Ask: If we redistributed water from wet regions, what would happen to their ecosystems and food production?

• "Desalination can solve water scarcity"
  Reality: Desalination technology is improving but faces fundamental constraints. It requires 3-10 kWh of energy per cubic meter of water, costs $0.50-2.00 per cubic meter (far more than groundwater at $0.05-0.20), produces toxic brine waste, and is practical only for coastal cities. Agriculture — which consumes 70% of freshwater — cannot economically operate on desalinated water. Desalination is part of the solution for drinking water but cannot replace aquifer depletion at scale.
  Strategy: Calculate: At $1/cubic meter for desalination vs. $0.10/cubic meter for groundwater, how much would food prices increase if agriculture switched to desalinated water? A wheat farmer using 5,000 cubic meters per hectare would see water costs rise from $500 to $5,000 per hectare.

• "Water is a renewable resource"
  Reality: Surface water flowing through the water cycle is renewable, but groundwater in fossil aquifers is not. Many of the world's most productive agricultural aquifers were filled during past geological periods and receive negligible recharge today. Pumping this water is extracting a non-renewable resource, no different in principle from mining coal or oil. The model makes this distinction critical: renewable surface water cannot replace non-renewable groundwater at the volumes agriculture demands.
  Strategy: Analogy: rainfall is like your salary (renewable income). An ancient aquifer is like an inheritance your family saved over 10,000 years. You're spending the inheritance 100 times faster than you earn your salary. When the inheritance runs out, you're left with just the salary — which covers a fraction of your lifestyle.

ANSWER KEY:
Big Question: If Earth is 70% water, why are billions of people running out of clean water — and why can't we just make more?
Answer: Earth has vast quantities of water, but only 0.5% is accessible freshwater, and its distribution is profoundly unequal. The real crisis is groundwater depletion: humanity is pumping aquifers 3-10 times faster than rain can refill them, effectively mining fossil water accumulated over thousands to millions of years. Agriculture consumes 70% of global freshwater, and as population grows and diets shift toward more water-intensive foods, demand is surging. The model reveals that aquifer depletion is functionally irreversible — once an aquifer is drained, the geological timescale for refilling (thousands of years) means it is gone for all practical purposes. We cannot simply 'make more' freshwater: desalination is energy-intensive and expensive, water recycling has limits, and efficiency improvements alone cannot close the growing gap between supply and demand. The solution requires a fundamental transformation of agricultural water use, groundwater governance, and food system design.

Simulation Answers:
• Sustainable Withdrawal: When Groundwater Extraction Rate matches Natural Recharge Rate, Aquifer Water Level stabilizes at a constant level. Regional Food Production Capacity is maintained indefinitely but at a lower level than current production because sustainable withdrawal is typically 3-10 times less than current pumping rates. The trade-off is clear: sustainability requires producing less food per region.
• Climate + Population: With Population Demand increasing 50% and Natural Recharge Rate decreasing 20% due to climate change, the depletion rate nearly doubles compared to current overdraft. Aquifer Water Level collapses decades earlier. Regional Food Production Capacity declines steeply as both the supply of water shrinks and demand for it grows. The model reveals that this compounding effect creates a converging crisis — supply and demand move in opposite directions simultaneously.

Reflection Exemplars:
• Q: Why is aquifer depletion irreversible?
  A: The model reveals that 'irreversible' in this context means irreversible on human timescales. The Ogallala Aquifer accumulated water over 2-6 million years during wetter climate periods. It has been depleted by 30% in just 60 years of pumping. Natural recharge replaces less than 1% of annual withdrawals. Simple math shows that refilling the aquifer at natural recharge rates would take thousands of years. Furthermore, as aquifers empty, the rock structure compacts (subsidence), permanently reducing storage capacity — even if water were available, the aquifer physically cannot hold as much as it once did.
• Q: How does virtual water change the picture?
  A: Virtual water reveals that water scarcity is a global, not just local, problem. When a country imports beef (15,000 liters of water per kg), it is effectively importing the water used to produce it. Wealthy nations can 'import' water by buying water-intensive products from regions that are draining their aquifers. This means global trade patterns drive local depletion — farmers in India pump their aquifer dry to export rice, while the water deficit is externalized. The model shows that solving water scarcity requires thinking about the entire food system, not just local water management.

Key Concepts:
• Stock and Flow Dynamics: Aquifer management requires understanding stock-flow relationships: the aquifer is a stock (accumulated water), recharge is an inflow (slow), and extraction is an outflow (fast). Sustainable management requires outflow to not exceed inflow — but for fossil aquifers with negligible inflow, any extraction is unsustainable by definition.
• Water Use Efficiency: Irrigation efficiency varies enormously: flood irrigation delivers 40-60% of water to crops (the rest evaporates or runs off), sprinkler systems deliver 65-80%, and drip irrigation delivers 85-95%. Converting to drip irrigation can reduce water use by 30-70% while maintaining or increasing crop yields.
• Managed Aquifer Recharge: Engineered systems that capture stormwater, treated wastewater, or diverted river flows and channel them into aquifer recharge zones through infiltration basins or injection wells. This can increase recharge rates 10-100 times above natural levels but requires suitable geology, clean water sources, and significant infrastructure investment.

Evaluation Rubric:
• Expert (4): Plan includes phased irrigation efficiency upgrades, crop portfolio optimization, managed aquifer recharge feasibility, governance framework for extraction limits, economic transition support, and 30-year timeline with milestones — all justified with model data
• Proficient (3): Plan addresses efficiency improvements and crop shifts with reasonable timeline and uses model evidence to justify the transition pace
• Developing (2): Plan mentions relevant strategies but lacks phased implementation, governance framework, or model-based justification for withdrawal targets
• Beginning (1): Plan is incomplete or relies on a single strategy without considering the systemic nature of the water-food-economy nexus

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format. Each item is three-dimensional, assessing a Science and Engineering Practice (SEP), Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and Crosscutting Concept (CCC) simultaneously, aligned to HS-ESS2-5, HS-ESS3-1.

---

### Pre-Assessment Questions

### Question 1

Earth is approximately 70% water. What percentage of Earth's total water is accessible freshwater?

A. About 10%
B. About 2.5%
C. About 0.5%
D. About 25%

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: Correct. Only about 0.5% of Earth's water is accessible freshwater. The rest is saltwater (97.5%) or frozen in ice caps and glaciers. Although Earth has vast water, 97.5% is saltwater and about 2% is frozen in ice caps and glaciers. Only about 0.5% is accessible freshwater in rivers, lakes, and reachable groundwater.

---

### Question 2

What is an aquifer?

A. A man-made reservoir for storing treated water
B. An underground layer of rock or sediment that holds and transmits groundwater
C. A type of water treatment plant
D. A dam built across a river to store water

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. An aquifer is a geological formation (rock, gravel, sand) that stores groundwater in pore spaces and can transmit it to wells and springs. An aquifer is a natural underground formation of permeable rock, sand, or gravel that holds groundwater. Wells are drilled into aquifers to access this water for drinking, irrigation, and industry.

---

### Question 3

Which sector consumes the largest share of global freshwater withdrawals?

A. Residential and municipal use (drinking, bathing, toilets)
B. Industrial manufacturing
C. Agriculture (irrigation)
D. Energy production (power plant cooling)

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: Correct. Agriculture accounts for approximately 70% of all global freshwater withdrawals, primarily for irrigation. Agriculture consumes about 70% of all freshwater withdrawn globally. Irrigation is by far the largest use, with much of it using inefficient flood methods that waste 40-60% of applied water.

---

### Question 4

What does 'water stress' mean for a region?

A. The region receives too much rainfall, causing flooding
B. Water demand exceeds available supply, or poor water quality restricts use
C. Water infrastructure requires expensive repairs
D. The region has more surface water than groundwater

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. A region is water-stressed when demand approaches or exceeds the available supply, or when water quality is too poor for intended uses. Water stress occurs when the demand for freshwater (from population, agriculture, industry) approaches or exceeds the available supply. Regions where more than 40% of available water is withdrawn are classified as severely stressed.

---

### Question 5

Why might depleting an aquifer be considered 'functionally irreversible'?

A. Empty aquifers immediately fill with saltwater that cannot be removed
B. Many major aquifers accumulated water over thousands to millions of years, and natural recharge replaces less than 1% of withdrawals annually
C. Government regulations permanently prevent refilling depleted aquifers
D. Aquifer rock collapses when water is removed, destroying the formation

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. Major agricultural aquifers like the Ogallala formed over millions of years. Current extraction rates far exceed natural recharge, making depletion effectively permanent on human timescales. Many large aquifers are 'fossil water' systems that accumulated over geological timescales. When extraction vastly exceeds the tiny natural recharge rate, the aquifer is being mined like a non-renewable resource. Refilling would take thousands of years.

### Post-Assessment Questions

### Question 1

The model shows that the Ogallala Aquifer has lost 30% of its volume in 60 years while natural recharge replaces less than 1% of annual withdrawals. A farmer proposes 'just pumping less.' At what extraction rate does the model show the aquifer stabilizes?

A. Extraction must be reduced to 50% of current levels
B. Extraction must equal the natural recharge rate, which would require reducing current withdrawals by approximately 99%
C. Any reduction in pumping will stabilize the aquifer within 10 years
D. The aquifer cannot stabilize because climate change is increasing recharge

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. For the aquifer to stabilize, extraction cannot exceed recharge. Since recharge is less than 1% of current withdrawal, extraction would need to drop by roughly 99%, which is practically impossible without completely transforming regional agriculture. The model shows sustainability requires withdrawal equals recharge. Since natural recharge is less than 1% of current extraction, stabilization requires a near-complete halt to pumping. This reveals the magnitude of the problem: current agriculture depends on mining a non-renewable resource.

---

### Question 2

The model demonstrates a cascading failure: aquifer depletion reduces food production, food shortages drive migration, and migration concentrates populations in cities facing their own water stress. What systems concept does this cascade illustrate?

A. Negative feedback that stabilizes the system through migration
B. A positive feedback loop where each system failure worsens conditions for the connected systems, compounding the crisis
C. An isolated local problem with no broader systemic effects
D. A self-correcting market response to resource scarcity

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. This cascade is a positive feedback loop across coupled systems: water depletion drives food loss, which drives migration, which intensifies urban water stress, worsening conditions across all connected systems. The cascade demonstrates how interconnected systems amplify failures: aquifer depletion is not just a water problem but triggers food insecurity, migration, and urban stress in a compounding positive feedback loop that affects multiple sectors simultaneously.

---

### Question 3

A student proposes desalination as the solution to global water scarcity. Based on the model, which limitation most significantly constrains this approach?

A. Desalination technology does not yet exist at commercial scale
B. Desalination is energy-intensive and expensive ($0.50-$2.00/m3), currently supplies less than 1% of global freshwater, and generates concentrated brine waste that harms marine ecosystems
C. Desalinated water is unsafe for irrigation
D. Only coastal cities can benefit, and most water stress occurs inland

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. Desalination faces scalability challenges: high energy costs, limited current contribution (<1% of global supply), and environmental impacts from brine disposal make it a supplementary solution, not a replacement for sustainable groundwater management. While desalination works technically, it currently provides less than 1% of global freshwater and faces major scalability constraints: high energy requirements, significant cost per unit volume, and environmental damage from concentrated brine waste discharged into marine environments.

### Question 4

The model's 'virtual water' concept reveals that a kilogram of beef requires approximately 15,000 liters of water. How does this inform a systems-level approach to water scarcity?

A. Virtual water is theoretical and has no practical policy implications
B. Shifting dietary patterns and agricultural choices can dramatically reduce water demand because food production accounts for 70% of withdrawals, and water intensity varies enormously between crop and livestock types
C. Consumers should only eat locally grown food to reduce virtual water
D. Beef production should be banned in all water-stressed regions

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. Virtual water analysis reveals that demand-side changes (dietary shifts, crop choice, agricultural efficiency) can achieve far greater water savings than supply-side engineering because agriculture dominates consumption. Understanding virtual water shows that water scarcity is partly a demand problem. Since agriculture uses 70% of freshwater and water intensity varies widely (15,000 L/kg beef vs. 1,800 L/kg grain), dietary and crop shifts can yield massive water savings.

### Question 5

The model shows that increasing Population and Agricultural Demand by 50% while decreasing Natural Recharge Rate by 20% (due to climate change) causes Aquifer Water Level to crash decades sooner than either factor alone. What does this reveal about compounding stressors in water systems?

A. Population growth and climate change affect water systems independently
B. The interaction between rising demand and declining supply creates a nonlinear acceleration of depletion because both forces simultaneously widen the gap between extraction and recharge
C. Climate change is the only significant factor in water scarcity
D. Population growth can always be offset by more efficient irrigation technology

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. When demand rises while supply falls simultaneously, the depletion gap widens from both sides, creating a multiplicative effect that accelerates collapse far beyond what either stressor would cause independently. Compounding stressors are worse than additive: rising demand and declining recharge simultaneously widen the extraction-recharge gap, causing nonlinear acceleration. The aquifer depletes much faster under compound stress than under either factor alone.
