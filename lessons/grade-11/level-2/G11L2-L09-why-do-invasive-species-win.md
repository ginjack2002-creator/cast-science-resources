### Title
**Why Do Invasive Species Win? — Modeling Ecological Competition, Niche Displacement, and Biodiversity Collapse**

### Overview
Invasive species represent one of the most powerful and underappreciated drivers of global biodiversity loss. Understanding biological invasions requires modeling the intersection of population ecology, competition theory, evolutionary biology, and human-mediated transport to explain why organisms that evolved on one continent can devastate ecosystems on another — and why the scale of the problem is accelerating with globalization. Students investigate the driving question: What gives invasive species their competitive edge — and why do native ecosystems, shaped by millions of years of evolution, crumble so quickly when invaders arrive? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

### Learning Objectives
- Model how invasive species exploit competitive advantages — rapid reproduction, generalist diets, and freedom from natural predators — to displace native species from their ecological niches
- Analyze the feedback loops that accelerate invasion once an invasive population crosses a critical establishment threshold
- Predict the tipping points at which native biodiversity loss becomes self-reinforcing and ecosystem function degrades irreversibly
- Evaluate the effectiveness and ecological trade-offs of different management strategies — from prevention to eradication to biocontrol

### Component List (Starting Model: 6 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Invasive Species Introduction Rate | External | The rate at which non-native organisms enter an ecosystem through human activities — international shipping, pet trade, ornamental planting, aquaculture, and accidental transport — global trade has increased introduction rates by 40% since 1970 |
| Invasive Population Growth Rate | Internal | The reproductive rate of the invasive species in its new environment, driven by the combination of high fecundity, short generation times, and absence of natural predators and diseases — many invaders can double their population in weeks to months |
| Native Species Competitive Fitness | Internal | The ability of native organisms to acquire food, territory, and mates in the presence of an invasive competitor — typically declining as invaders monopolize shared resources, introduce novel diseases, or physically modify the habitat in ways that disadvantage native-adapted traits |
| Resource Availability | Internal | The quantity and accessibility of critical resources — food, nesting sites, water, light, soil nutrients — that both native and invasive species require, which shifts progressively toward the invader as its population grows and it consumes, degrades, or monopolizes shared resources |
| Native Biodiversity Index | Internal | The species richness, population sizes, and functional diversity of the native ecological community — which declines as invasive species displace native organisms through competition, predation, and habitat alteration, reducing the ecosystem's resilience and adaptive capacity |
| Ecosystem Function Integrity | Internal | The overall health and stability of ecosystem services — nutrient cycling, pollination, water filtration, soil formation, and food web connectivity — which degrades as biodiversity loss removes species that performed irreplaceable ecological roles |

    More frequent introductions increase the probability that at least one founding population will survive initial environmental filtering and reach the establishment threshold. Multiple introduction events also increase genetic diversity in the invasive population, improving its adaptive potential.

  ○ Invasive Population Growth Rate → Resource Availability = NEGATIVE (−)
    As the invasive population grows, it consumes, degrades, or monopolizes resources that native species also depend on. Invasive plants shade out native seedlings, invasive predators deplete native prey, and invasive filter-feeders strip nutrients from the water column.

  ○ Resource Availability → Native Species Competitive Fitness = POSITIVE (+)
    When resources are abundant, native species can maintain healthy population sizes and reproductive success even in the presence of competitors. As resources decline due to invasive consumption, native species face food scarcity, habitat loss, and increased competition stress.

  ○ Native Species Competitive Fitness → Native Biodiversity Index = POSITIVE (+)
    When native species maintain competitive fitness, populations remain viable and species richness is preserved. As fitness declines, populations drop below minimum viable sizes, local extinctions occur, and biodiversity decreases — with specialist species disappearing first and generalists persisting longest.

  ○ Native Biodiversity Index → Ecosystem Function Integrity = POSITIVE (+)
    Higher biodiversity supports more complete ecosystem function — pollination networks, decomposition cycles, nutrient transport, predator-prey balance, and disease regulation all depend on having sufficient species to fill functional roles. As biodiversity declines, ecosystem services degrade in a nonlinear pattern where the loss of keystone species triggers disproportionate functional collapse.

WHAT DID YOU DISCOVER?
• Invasive species are the second leading cause of species extinction globally (after habitat destruction) and have contributed to 60% of all documented plant and animal extinctions — costing the global economy an estimated $423 billion annually in damages and control efforts
• The 'invasion curve' demonstrates that management cost-effectiveness declines exponentially with invasion stage — spending $1 on prevention or early detection avoids $100-1,000 in later eradication or damage control costs
• Invasive species create positive feedback loops: as they modify habitat (e.g., invasive grasses increasing fire frequency), they make the environment more favorable for themselves and less favorable for natives, accelerating displacement in a self-reinforcing cycle
• Island ecosystems and isolated habitats (lakes, mountaintops) are disproportionately vulnerable because their native species evolved without exposure to aggressive competitors or predators — 75% of all documented extinctions have occurred on islands

THE ANSWER: Invasive species win because of a fundamental asymmetry: they arrive in a new ecosystem free from the predators, parasites, and diseases that kept them in check at home (enemy release), while native species have no evolutionary experience with the invader's competitive strategies. This asymmetry drives exponential population growth that overwhelms native defenses. The model reveals that invasion is not a gradual process — it has critical thresholds. Below the establishment threshold, removal can succeed. Above it, the invasion becomes self-reinforcing through positive feedback loops: the invader modifies habitat in its own favor, consumes resources that natives depend on, and disrupts ecological interactions (pollination, seed dispersal, predator-prey relationships) that maintained native biodiversity. Once these feedback loops engage, native biodiversity loss cascades through the food web. The critical insight is that prevention and early detection are orders of magnitude more effective than late-stage management — the invasion curve shows that waiting costs increase exponentially with time.

LESSON PURPOSE:
Invasive species represent one of the most powerful and underappreciated drivers of global biodiversity loss. Understanding biological invasions requires modeling the intersection of population ecology, competition theory, evolutionary biology, and human-mediated transport to explain why organisms that evolved on one continent can devastate ecosystems on another — and why the scale of the problem is accelerating with globalization.

NGSS ALIGNMENT:
HS-LS2-2, HS-LS2-6, HS-LS4-5: Use mathematical representations to support and revise explanations based on evidence about factors affecting biodiversity and populations in ecosystems of different scales. Evaluate claims, evidence, and reasoning that the complex interactions in ecosystems maintain relatively consistent numbers and types of organisms in stable conditions, but changing conditions may result in a new ecosystem. Evaluate the evidence supporting claims that changes in environmental conditions may result in increases in the number of individuals of some species, the emergence of new species over time, and the extinction of other species.

THREE-DIMENSIONAL LEARNING:
• Science Practice: Using Mathematics and Computational Thinking
  Students use mathematical representations of population dynamics, competitive exclusion, and resource partitioning to model how invasive species displace native communities and to predict thresholds at which management interventions succeed or fail.
• Disciplinary Core Idea: LS2.A Interdependent Relationships in Ecosystems / LS2.C Ecosystem Dynamics / LS4.D Biodiversity and Humans
  Students model how species interactions maintain ecosystem stability, how the introduction of invasive competitors disrupts these relationships, and how biodiversity loss from invasion degrades ecosystem services that humans depend on.
• Crosscutting Concept: Cause and Effect / Stability and Change
  Students analyze the cause-and-effect chains through which invasive species destabilize ecosystems, identifying the feedback loops and tipping points that determine whether ecosystems can absorb the disturbance or collapse to a degraded state.

LEVER FRAMEWORK:
• Locate: Students identify six components of the invasion system: Invasive Species Introduction Rate, Invasive Population Growth Rate, Native Species Competitive Fitness, Resource Availability, Native Biodiversity Index, and Ecosystem Function Integrity — distinguishing the human-mediated introduction from the ecological processes that determine invasion success or failure.
• Establish: Students determine that Invasive Species Introduction Rate initiates the invasion, but Invasive Population Growth Rate (driven by enemy release and reproductive advantage) determines whether the population establishes. As the invasive population grows, it reduces Resource Availability for native species, which declines Native Species Competitive Fitness. As natives decline, Native Biodiversity Index drops, degrading Ecosystem Function Integrity.
• Visualize: Students build a computational model showing the competitive dynamics between invasive and native populations — visualizing the establishment threshold, the exponential growth phase, and the cascading collapse of native biodiversity.
• Evaluate: Students run early detection, established invasion, and managed ecosystem scenarios to identify the critical population threshold that determines whether intervention succeeds and quantify the cascade of biodiversity loss at each invasion stage.
• Revise: Students add Disease and Parasite Introduction, Habitat Modification Feedback, or Genetic Hybridization to explore how invasive pathogens, ecosystem engineering by invaders, and genetic swamping create additional and often more devastating invasion pathways.

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• The Scale of the Crisis:
  Invasive species are the second leading cause of species extinction worldwide, surpassed only by habitat destruction. They have contributed to 60% of all documented plant and animal extinctions since 1500. The rate of new introductions has increased by 40% since 1970, driven by expanding international trade, travel, and shipping. The global economic cost of invasive species is estimated at $423 billion annually — including agricultural losses, infrastructure damage, control efforts, and ecosystem service degradation. In the United States alone, invasive species cause an estimated $120 billion in annual damages. The problem is accelerating: climate change is expanding the geographic range in which warm-adapted invasive species can establish, while simultaneously stressing native species and reducing their competitive ability.

• Why Invaders Win: The Competitive Asymmetry:
  Invasive species succeed through a combination of traits and circumstances that create a fundamental competitive asymmetry. The enemy release hypothesis explains the most important factor: in their native range, species are controlled by co-evolved predators, parasites, herbivores, and diseases that limit population growth. When transported to a new environment, invaders leave these natural enemies behind while native species have not evolved defenses against the newcomer. This asymmetry is compounded by the traits common to successful invaders: rapid reproduction, short generation times, broad environmental tolerances, generalist diets, high dispersal ability, and phenotypic plasticity. Burmese pythons in the Everglades, for example, face no predators, produce 50-100 eggs per clutch, tolerate a wide range of habitats, and eat virtually anything — they have reduced mammal populations by 90-99% in some areas.

• Invasion Dynamics and Tipping Points:
  Biological invasions follow a characteristic pattern with critical thresholds. The introduction phase is the initial arrival of a small founding population. Most introductions fail — perhaps 10% of introduced species establish self-sustaining populations (the 'tens rule'). But those that survive the establishment phase enter a lag phase where the population grows slowly and remains localized, often for years to decades. If conditions allow, the population eventually crosses the establishment threshold — a critical density above which growth becomes exponential and self-sustaining. The subsequent spread phase is characterized by rapid range expansion, resource monopolization, and native species displacement. Critically, many invasive species create positive feedback loops that accelerate their own spread: invasive grasses increase fire frequency (which kills native trees and creates more grassland), invasive mussels filter-feed plankton (reducing food for native species while increasing water clarity that favors invasive aquatic plants), and invasive predators reduce native prey (releasing invasive plants from herbivory).

• Management Strategies and the Invasion Curve:
  The invasion curve illustrates that management cost-effectiveness decreases exponentially with invasion stage. Prevention — biosecurity screening at borders, trade regulation, pathway risk analysis — costs pennies per species prevented and is 100-1,000 times more cost-effective than any later intervention. Early detection and rapid response — systematic monitoring, eDNA sampling, citizen science networks — can eradicate incipient invasions when populations are small and localized. Once invasion passes the establishment threshold, eradication becomes progressively more difficult and eventually impossible for widespread species. Long-term management — biocontrol, mechanical removal, habitat restoration — can reduce invasive species impacts but rarely eliminates them. Biocontrol introduces a natural enemy from the invader's native range; when carefully researched (10-15 years of host-specificity testing), it can provide self-sustaining population control. However, poorly researched biocontrol has itself created invasive species disasters — the cane toad introduction in Australia being the most infamous example.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "All non-native species are invasive"
  Reality: Only a small fraction of introduced non-native species become invasive — the 'tens rule' estimates that roughly 10% of introduced species establish self-sustaining populations, and roughly 10% of those become invasive (causing ecological or economic harm). Many non-native species coexist with native communities without significant impact. The distinction between non-native (present but benign) and invasive (present and harmful) is critical for directing limited management resources toward genuine threats.
  Strategy: Give examples: wheat, chickens, and honeybees are non-native to North America but are not considered invasive because they do not spread uncontrollably or displace native species in natural ecosystems. Contrast with kudzu, Burmese pythons, and zebra mussels — which do. Ask: What makes the difference?

• "Nature will eventually reach a new balance with invasive species"
  Reality: While ecosystems can eventually incorporate non-native species into new community structures, this 'new balance' typically involves dramatic biodiversity loss, reduced ecosystem function, and the permanent extinction of species that cannot adapt or relocate. The new equilibrium may be stable but drastically impoverished — like the Guam forest that functions without birds or the Great Lakes fishery restructured around invasive species. Evolution can produce co-adaptation, but it operates on timescales of thousands to millions of years — far longer than the decades over which invasions cause extinction.
  Strategy: Show the Guam example: before brown tree snakes — diverse forest with 12 native bird species. After — silent forest with zero native forest birds. Is that 'balance'? Technically yes — it's stable. But 10 species went extinct permanently. The new balance is impoverished and irreversible.

• "We can always eradicate invasive species if we try hard enough"
  Reality: Eradication is only feasible for small, localized populations detected early — typically below the establishment threshold. Once an invasive species is widespread, eradication is virtually impossible for all but the smallest and most isolated ecosystems (small islands). The history of invasive species management is littered with failed eradication attempts that consumed billions of dollars. This is not a failure of effort — it is a mathematical reality: organisms that reproduce exponentially, disperse widely, and have no natural enemies cannot be removed faster than they reproduce once populations are large. The model makes this clear: prevention and early detection are not 'better' strategies — they are the only strategies that work.
  Strategy: Calculate: a species that doubles every month reaches 1 million from 1 individual in 20 months. If you can remove 10,000 per month, when does removal exceed reproduction? (Never — because the population keeps doubling). Now recalculate if you catch it at 100 individuals. The math is the argument for early detection.

ANSWER KEY:
Big Question: What gives invasive species their competitive edge — and why do native ecosystems, shaped by millions of years of evolution, crumble so quickly when invaders arrive?
Answer: Invasive species win because of a fundamental asymmetry: they arrive in a new ecosystem free from the predators, parasites, and diseases that kept them in check at home (enemy release), while native species have no evolutionary experience with the invader's competitive strategies. This asymmetry drives exponential population growth that overwhelms native defenses. The model reveals that invasion is not a gradual process — it has critical thresholds. Below the establishment threshold, removal can succeed. Above it, the invasion becomes self-reinforcing through positive feedback loops: the invader modifies habitat in its own favor, consumes resources that natives depend on, and disrupts ecological interactions (pollination, seed dispersal, predator-prey relationships) that maintained native biodiversity. Once these feedback loops engage, native biodiversity loss cascades through the food web. The critical insight is that prevention and early detection are orders of magnitude more effective than late-stage management — the invasion curve shows that waiting costs increase exponentially with time.

Simulation Answers:
• Early Detection: When the invasive population is detected at 100 individuals — well below the establishment threshold — targeted removal can eliminate the population before exponential growth begins. Resource Availability remains stable, Native Species Competitive Fitness is unaffected, and Native Biodiversity Index is preserved. The model reveals that early intervention requires minimal effort (removing 100 individuals) compared to the massive effort required once populations reach thousands or millions. This is the mathematical basis for prioritizing early detection over late-stage management.
• Established Invasion: Once the invasive population crosses the establishment threshold, growth becomes exponential and self-sustaining. The invader monopolizes resources, creating a cascading decline: Resource Availability drops 30-60%, Native Species Competitive Fitness collapses, and Native Biodiversity Index declines rapidly as the least competitive native species go locally extinct first. Ecosystem Function Integrity degrades as functional roles go unfilled. The model shows that above the establishment threshold, even aggressive management (removing 50% of the invasive population annually) cannot reverse the invasion — only slow its spread — because the remaining population reproduces faster than removal can keep pace.
• Managed Ecosystem: Combining biocontrol agents with active removal reduces Invasive Population Growth Rate significantly but does not return the ecosystem to its pre-invasion state. Biocontrol reduces the invader's competitive advantage (partially restoring the enemy pressure it escaped), and active removal further suppresses population density. Native Biodiversity Index partially recovers as resource competition eases, but species that went locally extinct during the invasion do not spontaneously return — active reintroduction is required. The model reveals that management can shift the ecosystem toward a 'new normal' that is better than unmanaged invasion but still fundamentally altered from the original community.

Reflection Exemplars:
• Q: Why is the establishment threshold the most critical point in an invasion?
  A: The establishment threshold represents the population size above which invasive growth becomes self-sustaining — where reproduction and survival rates exceed mortality and removal rates regardless of management effort. Below this threshold, the population is vulnerable: a small, localized group can be found and removed at relatively low cost. Above it, exponential growth kicks in, the population disperses across a larger area, and the feedback loops that favor the invader engage. The model shows that crossing this threshold changes the fundamental dynamics: below it, eradication is feasible; above it, only containment and management are possible. This is why the invasion curve economics are so stark — the threshold is the dividing line between affordable success and enormously expensive partial failure.
• Q: How do positive feedback loops make invasions self-reinforcing?
  A: The model reveals multiple self-reinforcing feedback loops. First, the resource monopolization loop: as the invader grows, it consumes resources that natives need, weakening native populations, which reduces competition for the invader, which allows faster growth, which consumes more resources. Second, the habitat modification loop: many invaders alter their environment — invasive grasses increase fire frequency, which kills fire-intolerant native trees, which creates more grassland habitat for the grass, which increases fire frequency further. Third, the mutualist disruption loop: when invasive species eliminate native pollinators or seed dispersers, native plants that depend on those mutualists cannot reproduce, reducing native vegetation, which further favors the invader. Each loop accelerates the invasion independently, and when multiple loops operate simultaneously, the cascade becomes virtually impossible to reverse.

Key Concepts:
• Environmental DNA (eDNA) Monitoring: eDNA sampling detects species presence by analyzing DNA fragments shed into water and soil through skin cells, feces, and mucus. A single water sample can reveal the presence of invasive fish, amphibians, or invertebrates days to weeks after they arrive — far earlier than visual surveys. eDNA monitoring can detect species at densities as low as 1 individual per hectare in aquatic systems, providing the earliest possible warning of new introductions.
• Invasion Risk Assessment: Not all pathways and species pose equal risk. Risk assessment frameworks score potential invaders based on their invasion history elsewhere, reproductive traits, climate match with the target ecosystem, and the volume and frequency of the introduction pathway. Prioritizing monitoring on high-risk pathways (e.g., ports, nursery trade, aquaculture releases) concentrates limited resources where detection matters most.
• Rapid Response Protocols: Effective rapid response requires pre-authorized action plans, trained response teams, pre-positioned equipment, and clear decision trees. The Incident Command System (ICS) used for wildfire and disaster response has been adapted for invasive species — providing a scalable framework for mobilizing resources within hours of a confirmed detection. Response speed is critical: a one-month delay can allow an invasive population to increase 10-100x.

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format. Each item is three-dimensional, assessing a Science and Engineering Practice (SEP), Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and Crosscutting Concept (CCC) simultaneously, aligned to HS-LS2-2, HS-LS2-6, HS-LS4-5.

### Pre-Assessment Questions

### Question 1

What makes a non-native species 'invasive'?

A. Any species found outside its native range is considered invasive
B. A non-native species that establishes self-sustaining populations and causes ecological or economic harm
C. A native species that becomes unusually abundant due to human activity
D. Any species that migrates seasonally between different regions

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. Not all non-native species are invasive. The 'invasive' designation requires that the species establishes, spreads, and causes measurable ecological or economic harm. A species is invasive only if it is (1) non-native, (2) establishes self-sustaining populations, and (3) causes harm to the ecosystem, economy, or human health. Many non-native species exist harmlessly.

---

### Question 2

The 'enemy release hypothesis' suggests that invasive species thrive because:

A. They evolve new defensive adaptations in their new environment
B. They are freed from the predators, parasites, and diseases that controlled them in their native range
C. Native species actively cooperate with invaders to share resources
D. Human activity eliminates all predators in every ecosystem

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. Invasive species leave behind the specialized natural enemies (predators, parasites, pathogens) that kept their populations in check, allowing unconstrained growth in new environments. The enemy release hypothesis explains that invasive species succeed because they arrive without the specialized predators, parasites, and diseases that evolved alongside them to control their populations. Without these natural checks, their populations can grow exponentially.

---

### Question 3

What is 'competitive exclusion'?

A. The principle that two species competing for the same limited resource cannot coexist indefinitely
B. A conservation strategy that physically separates native and invasive species
C. The process by which invasive species are excluded from protected areas
D. A genetic mechanism that prevents hybridization between species

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: Correct. Competitive exclusion states that if two species compete for exactly the same limited resource, the one with even a slight advantage will eventually drive the other to local extinction. The competitive exclusion principle states that two species occupying the same ecological niche cannot coexist indefinitely. The species with even a small competitive advantage will eventually outcompete and locally eliminate the other.

### Question 4

Invasive species are the second leading cause of species extinction globally. What is the first?

A. Climate change
B. Pollution
C. Habitat destruction
D. Overhunting

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: Correct. Habitat destruction is the leading cause of extinction, followed by invasive species, which have contributed to approximately 60% of all documented extinctions. Habitat destruction (deforestation, land conversion, urbanization) is the leading cause of species extinction globally. Invasive species rank second, having contributed to about 60% of all documented plant and animal extinctions.

### Question 5

Why are island ecosystems disproportionately vulnerable to invasive species?

A. Islands have more resources available for invasive species to exploit
B. Island species evolved in isolation without exposure to aggressive competitors or predators, leaving them with no defensive adaptations
C. Islands are closer to continental sources of invasive species
D. Island climates are more favorable for non-native species than mainland climates

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. Island species evolved in isolation and never developed defenses against the types of predators and competitors found on continents. This evolutionary naivety makes them extremely vulnerable. Island species evolved in isolation, often without exposure to mammalian predators, aggressive competitors, or continental diseases. This evolutionary inexperience leaves them defenseless against invaders. About 75% of all documented extinctions have occurred on islands.

### Post-Assessment Questions

### Question 1

The model shows that when Invasive Population is at 100 individuals, removal efforts successfully prevent establishment. At 10,000 individuals, the same removal rate fails. What does this threshold behavior demonstrate?

A. Removal technology becomes less effective over time regardless of population size
B. Below the establishment threshold, removal can outpace reproduction, but above it, the invasive population's growth rate exceeds removal capacity, and positive feedback loops (habitat modification, resource monopolization) make eradication increasingly difficult
C. Invasive species develop resistance to removal methods after reaching 10,000 individuals
D. At 10,000 individuals, invasive species begin migrating to new areas beyond the removal zone

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. Below the threshold, removal can suppress growth. Above it, exponential reproduction overwhelms removal capacity, and positive feedbacks (habitat modification, resource depletion) create conditions that further favor the invader. The establishment threshold marks a critical transition: below it, removal can outpace reproduction. Above it, exponential growth exceeds management capacity, and the invader begins modifying habitat in its own favor, creating positive feedback loops that make control progressively harder.

---

### Question 2

The model demonstrates that invasive grasses increase fire frequency, which kills fire-intolerant native trees, creating more grassland for the invader. What type of system dynamic does this represent?

A. Negative feedback that restores the native tree population
B. A positive feedback loop where the invasive species modifies the environment to favor its own spread, making each increment of invasion accelerate the next
C. A neutral interaction with no effect on invasion dynamics
D. Random environmental variation unrelated to the invasion

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. This is a textbook invasion-fire feedback loop: invasive grass increases fire frequency, fire eliminates native trees, more open space favors more grass, more grass means more fire. Each cycle accelerates invasion. The invasion-fire feedback is a positive (self-reinforcing) loop: the invader modifies its environment (more flammable grass) to create conditions (frequent fire) that eliminate competitors (fire-intolerant trees) and expand its own habitat, accelerating further invasion.

### Question 3

The 'invasion curve' shows that spending $1 on prevention or early detection avoids $100-$1,000 in later control costs. A budget committee asks why prevention funding should be prioritized over more visible eradication programs. Which model-based argument is strongest?

A. Eradication programs are always unsuccessful
B. Prevention catches invasions when populations are small and feedback loops have not engaged, making control feasible. Once populations cross the establishment threshold, positive feedbacks make eradication exponentially more expensive and less likely to succeed
C. Prevention is cheaper because it requires fewer staff
D. Early detection technology is more advanced than eradication technology

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. The cost-effectiveness case is rooted in systems dynamics: below the establishment threshold, small populations are manageable. Above it, positive feedbacks create exponentially growing costs and diminishing returns. The invasion curve demonstrates that cost-effectiveness declines exponentially with invasion stage. Prevention and early detection target populations before positive feedback loops (habitat modification, resource monopolization) engage, making control feasible and affordable.

### Question 4

In the biocontrol scenario, introducing a natural predator from the invader's native range reduces invasive population growth. However, the model shows that Native Biodiversity Index does not return to pre-invasion levels. What best explains this incomplete recovery?

A. Biocontrol agents always harm native species more than invasive species
B. The invasion caused irreversible changes: native species driven to local extinction cannot return, habitat modifications persist, and ecological interactions (pollination networks, predator-prey relationships) were permanently disrupted
C. Native species refuse to recolonize areas previously occupied by invasive species
D. Biocontrol agents become invasive themselves 100% of the time

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. Even after reducing the invader, the ecosystem has undergone lasting changes: species extinctions, altered habitat structure, and disrupted ecological networks create a 'new normal' that differs from the pre-invasion state. The ecosystem has undergone a regime shift: some native species are locally extinct and cannot recolonize, habitat has been physically altered, and ecological interaction networks (pollination, seed dispersal, predation) have been rewired. The ecosystem stabilizes at a new, less diverse state.

### Question 5

A student examines the model and notes that Resource Availability declines as Invasive Population grows, while Native Species Competitive Fitness drops simultaneously. How do these two variables interact to create the 'biodiversity collapse' seen in the model?

A. They are independent variables that coincidentally decline at the same time
B. As invasive species monopolize resources, native species face both resource scarcity AND reduced competitive ability (due to smaller populations, fragmented habitat, and disrupted ecological partnerships), creating a compounding decline where each factor worsens the other
C. Resource availability declines because native species consume too much
D. Native competitive fitness increases as resources decline because scarcity drives adaptation

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. The interaction is synergistic: invasive resource monopolization weakens native populations, which reduces their competitive fitness, which allows the invader to capture even more resources, compounding the decline in a reinforcing spiral. These variables interact synergistically: the invader takes resources, weakening natives. Weakened natives compete less effectively, losing more resources. Smaller native populations lose ecological partnerships (pollinators, seed dispersers). Each factor amplifies the other in a downward spiral.
