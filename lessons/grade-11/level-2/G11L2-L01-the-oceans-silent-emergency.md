## Platform

### Title
**The Ocean's Silent Emergency — Modeling Ocean Acidification, pH Tipping Points, and Marine Ecosystem Collapse**

### Overview
Ocean acidification is often called climate change's 'equally evil twin' — a global chemical transformation happening beneath the waves that threatens marine ecosystems, fisheries worth $150 billion annually, and the ocean's fundamental ability to regulate Earth's climate. Understanding this crisis requires connecting atmospheric chemistry, carbonate geochemistry, marine biology, and complex systems dynamics. Students investigate the driving question: What happens to the ocean — and everything living in it — when seawater absorbs too much carbon dioxide? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

### Learning Objectives
- Model how rising atmospheric CO2 drives ocean acidification through carbonic acid formation
- Analyze the feedback loops connecting pH decline, carbonate availability, and shell-building organism survival
- Predict the pH tipping points at which marine food webs begin to collapse
- Evaluate the cascading effects of ocean acidification on global fisheries and coastal economies

### Component List (Starting Model: 6 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Atmospheric CO2 Concentration | External | The level of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere, primarily driven by fossil fuel combustion and deforestation — currently over 420 ppm and rising approximately 2.5 ppm per year |
| Ocean CO2 Absorption Rate | Internal | The rate at which ocean surface waters absorb atmospheric CO2 through gas exchange at the air-sea interface, influenced by temperature, wind, and existing dissolved CO2 levels |
| Seawater pH | Internal | The measure of hydrogen ion concentration in ocean water — currently averaging 8.1, down from pre-industrial 8.2, with projections of 7.7-7.8 by 2100 under high-emission scenarios |
| Carbonate Ion Availability | Internal | The concentration of carbonate ions (CO3²⁻) dissolved in seawater, which decreases as pH drops because hydrogen ions bind with carbonate to form bicarbonate |
| Shell-Building Organism Survival | Internal | The population health and reproductive success of calcifying organisms including corals, pteropods, oysters, and sea urchins that depend on carbonate ions to build protective structures |
| Marine Food Web Stability | Internal | The overall resilience and interconnection of ocean food chains, from phytoplankton and zooplankton through fish and marine mammals, which depends on healthy populations at every trophic level |

• Click on "Carbonate Ion Availability" and drag an arrow to "Shell-Building Organism Survival"
• Click on "Shell-Building Organism Survival" and drag an arrow to "Marine Food Web Stability"

Task C: SET POSITIVE OR NEGATIVE
• Look at the +/− toggle in the TOP LEFT corner
• For each connection, ask: "When this goes UP, does the other go UP or DOWN?"

  ○ Atmospheric CO2 → Ocean CO2 Absorption Rate = POSITIVE (+)
    Higher Atmospheric CO2 increases the concentration gradient at the air-sea interface, driving more CO2 into ocean surface waters through gas exchange. The ocean absorbs approximately 25-30% of anthropogenic CO2 emissions.

  ○ Ocean CO2 Absorption Rate → Seawater pH = NEGATIVE (−)
    Higher CO2 absorption produces more carbonic acid, which dissociates into hydrogen ions and lowers pH. Each additional unit of CO2 absorbed drives pH further down on a logarithmic scale.

  ○ Seawater pH → Carbonate Ion Availability = POSITIVE (+)
    Lower pH means more hydrogen ions, which bind with carbonate ions to form bicarbonate. As pH drops, carbonate ion concentration decreases — at pH 7.8, carbonate levels drop below the threshold needed for aragonite shell formation.

  ○ Carbonate Ion Availability → Shell-Building Organism Survival = POSITIVE (+)
    Shell-building organisms require sufficient carbonate ions to construct and maintain calcium carbonate shells. When carbonate falls below saturation thresholds, shells dissolve faster than organisms can build them, leading to population collapse.

  ○ Shell-Building Organism Survival → Marine Food Web Stability = POSITIVE (+)
    Calcifying organisms form critical links in marine food webs — pteropods feed salmon, coral reefs shelter thousands of species, oyster reefs filter water. Their decline cascades through every trophic level above them.

Task D: CHECK YOUR MODEL
• You should have 5 arrows total
• 1 negative relationship(s), 4 positive relationship(s)
• This is your system model!

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

THINK ABOUT IT: When Atmospheric CO2 rises, the ocean absorbs more CO2, forming carbonic acid that lowers pH. As pH drops, carbonate ions become scarce. If shell-building organisms cannot build shells, what happens to the species that eat them — and the species that eat those species?

WHAT DID YOU DISCOVER?
• The ocean has absorbed roughly 30% of human CO2 emissions since the Industrial Revolution, making it approximately 30% more acidic — a rate of change unprecedented in at least 300 million years
• Carbonate ion availability drops exponentially as pH decreases, creating a tipping point where shell dissolution exceeds shell formation for most calcifying organisms
• Pteropods — tiny sea snails that form the base of many polar food webs — show shell dissolution at pH levels projected for 2050, threatening entire Arctic and Antarctic food chains
• Ocean acidification interacts synergistically with warming and deoxygenation, meaning the combined stress is far greater than any single stressor alone

THE ANSWER: When seawater absorbs excess CO2, it forms carbonic acid that lowers pH and strips the water of carbonate ions that shell-building organisms need to survive. The ocean has already become 30% more acidic since pre-industrial times. As pH continues to drop, pteropods dissolve, coral reefs crumble, oyster larvae fail to develop, and the cascading collapse ripples up through every level of the marine food web. The critical insight is that ocean acidification has tipping points — thresholds beyond which damage becomes self-reinforcing as dying organisms release stored carbon and weakened ecosystems lose their ability to buffer pH changes. Without aggressive CO2 reduction, models project that by 2100 most ocean waters will be corrosive to the shells and skeletons that marine life depends on.

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• The Chemistry of Ocean Acidification:
  When CO2 dissolves in seawater, it reacts with water molecules to form carbonic acid (H2CO3), which quickly dissociates into hydrogen ions (H+) and bicarbonate ions (HCO3-). The excess hydrogen ions then react with carbonate ions (CO3²⁻) to form more bicarbonate. This double reaction simultaneously lowers pH and depletes the carbonate ions that marine organisms need for calcification. The ocean has absorbed approximately 525 billion tons of CO2 since 1800, lowering average surface pH from 8.2 to 8.1 — a 30% increase in acidity on the logarithmic pH scale. Current absorption rate is approximately 22 million tons of CO2 per day.

• Biological Impacts Across Trophic Levels:
  Calcifying organisms are the most directly threatened: corals, mollusks, echinoderms, and pteropods all build structures from calcium carbonate that dissolves in acidified water. Pteropods — rice-grain-sized sea snails — are particularly critical because they form the base of polar food webs eaten by salmon, herring, and whales. Laboratory studies show pteropod shells dissolving within 45 days at pH levels projected for 2050. Coral reef calcification rates have declined 14% since 2000. Non-calcifying organisms are also affected: fish experience impaired sensory systems, altered behavior, and reduced reproductive success in acidified water.

• Tipping Points and Irreversibility:
  Ocean acidification exhibits critical tipping points. Below a carbonate saturation state (Omega) of 1.0, seawater becomes corrosive to aragonite — the mineral form used by corals and pteropods. Models project that 70% of cold-water corals will be in undersaturated water by 2050. Once calcifying organisms decline past a critical population threshold, their role in the carbon cycle is lost — healthy shellfish beds actually help buffer pH by incorporating carbon into shells. The loss of this biological buffering capacity creates a positive feedback loop that accelerates acidification.

• Economic and Food Security Dimensions:
  Global fisheries and aquaculture contribute $150 billion annually to the world economy and provide primary protein for over 3 billion people. The U.S. shellfish industry alone is worth $1.5 billion per year. Oregon and Washington oyster hatcheries have already experienced larval die-offs linked to acidified upwelling water, costing millions in losses. Coral reef ecosystems support tourism, coastal protection, and fisheries worth $375 billion per year globally. Communities in Southeast Asia, the Pacific Islands, and West Africa that depend on reef fisheries for food security face disproportionate risk from acidification.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "The ocean is becoming actual acid"
  Reality: Despite the term 'acidification,' the ocean is not becoming acidic — it is becoming less alkaline. Ocean pH has dropped from 8.2 to 8.1, still on the basic side of the pH scale. The danger is the rate and direction of change, not the absolute pH value. Even small shifts in pH have enormous consequences for organisms adapted to narrow chemical ranges.
  Strategy: Clarify the pH scale: 7 is neutral, above 7 is basic, below 7 is acidic. The ocean is still basic but moving toward neutral. Use the analogy of body temperature — a 2-degree fever doesn't make you 'hot' but it still makes you very sick.

• "Ocean acidification only affects shellfish"
  Reality: While calcifying organisms face the most direct chemical threat, acidification affects virtually all marine life. Fish exhibit impaired olfactory systems and altered behavior in acidified water, making them more vulnerable to predators. Phytoplankton communities shift composition, altering the base of the food web. Even organisms without shells are affected through food web disruption when their prey species decline.
  Strategy: Show the food web diagram and trace the connections. Ask: If pteropods disappear, what happens to salmon? What happens to communities that depend on salmon fisheries?

• "The ocean can just keep absorbing CO2 without consequences"
  Reality: The ocean's capacity to absorb CO2 is real but limited and comes with severe chemical side effects. As the ocean absorbs more CO2, its buffering capacity decreases — meaning each additional ton of CO2 causes a larger pH drop than the last. Furthermore, warming waters absorb less CO2, potentially reducing the ocean's role as a carbon sink precisely when we need it most.
  Strategy: Demonstrate with a buffer solution: add acid drop by drop and show how pH drops slowly at first, then rapidly as buffer capacity is overwhelmed. Connect to the model's nonlinear behavior.

ANSWER KEY:
Big Question: What happens to the ocean — and everything living in it — when seawater absorbs too much carbon dioxide?
Answer: When seawater absorbs excess CO2, it forms carbonic acid that lowers pH and strips the water of carbonate ions that shell-building organisms need to survive. The ocean has already become 30% more acidic since pre-industrial times. As pH continues to drop, pteropods dissolve, coral reefs crumble, oyster larvae fail to develop, and the cascading collapse ripples up through every level of the marine food web. The critical insight is that ocean acidification has tipping points — thresholds beyond which damage becomes self-reinforcing as dying organisms release stored carbon and weakened ecosystems lose their ability to buffer pH changes. Without aggressive CO2 reduction, models project that by 2100 most ocean waters will be corrosive to the shells and skeletons that marine life depends on.

Simulation Answers:
• Pre-Industrial Baseline: At 280 ppm CO2, Seawater pH remains at 8.2, Carbonate Ion Availability is well above saturation thresholds, Shell-Building Organism Survival is at maximum, and Marine Food Web Stability is robust. The system is in a self-maintaining equilibrium where biological processes actually help buffer pH through shell formation and carbon cycling.
• Current Trajectory (RCP 8.5): As CO2 rises from 420 to 900+ ppm, Seawater pH drops to 7.7 by 2100. Carbonate Ion Availability crosses the aragonite undersaturation threshold around 2040-2060 in polar waters and by 2080-2100 in tropical waters. Shell-Building Organism Survival collapses in a nonlinear crash once carbonate falls below critical levels. Marine Food Web Stability degrades progressively, with cascading extinctions accelerating after the pteropod and coral collapse points.

Reflection Exemplars:
• Q: Why is the current rate of acidification more dangerous than past natural changes?
  A: Although the ocean has experienced lower pH in its deep geological past, the current rate of change is 10-100 times faster than anything in the last 300 million years. Marine organisms could adapt to slow pH changes over millions of years, but the current pace — driven by anthropogenic CO2 — gives species no time to evolve tolerance. The model shows that it's the speed of change, not the absolute pH level, that creates the crisis. Rapid acidification overwhelms biological adaptation and chemical buffering capacity simultaneously.
• Q: How does ocean acidification create positive feedback loops?
  A: The model reveals several self-reinforcing loops. First, healthy shellfish beds incorporate carbon into shells, effectively removing it from the water column and buffering pH. When shell-building organisms die, this biological buffering is lost, accelerating pH decline. Second, acidified water dissolves calcium carbonate sediments on the seafloor, but this geological buffering operates on thousand-year timescales — too slow to counteract the rate of human CO2 emissions. Third, stressed marine ecosystems release stored organic carbon as organisms die and decompose, adding more CO2 to the water. These feedback loops mean that crossing a tipping point accelerates further acidification.
