## Platform

### Title
**Can Soil Save the Climate? — Modeling Soil Carbon Sequestration, Regenerative Agriculture, and Feedback Loops**

### Overview
Beneath our feet lies the largest terrestrial carbon reservoir on Earth — soil holds more carbon than the atmosphere and all plant life combined. Understanding soil carbon dynamics requires modeling the intersection of plant biology, microbial ecology, agricultural science, and climate physics to evaluate whether changing how we manage soil could meaningfully slow climate change, or whether warming itself could turn this vast carbon store into an accelerant. Students investigate the driving question: Could changing how we farm pull enough carbon out of the atmosphere to slow climate change — or is soil carbon sequestration too good to be true? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

### Learning Objectives
- Model how soil organic carbon accumulates through plant photosynthesis, root exudation, and microbial processing in healthy soils
- Analyze the feedback loops between soil carbon storage, microbial respiration, temperature, and moisture that determine whether soil is a net carbon sink or source
- Predict the carbon sequestration potential of regenerative agriculture practices compared to conventional tillage across different climate zones
- Evaluate the realistic limits, co-benefits, and timescales of soil carbon sequestration as a climate mitigation strategy

### Component List (Starting Model: 6 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Agricultural Management Practice | External | The farming methods applied to cropland — ranging from conventional tillage with synthetic inputs (which depletes soil carbon) to regenerative practices like no-till, cover cropping, and composting (which rebuild soil carbon) — the single most controllable variable in soil carbon dynamics |
| Plant Carbon Fixation Rate | Internal | The rate at which crops and cover crops convert atmospheric CO2 into organic carbon through photosynthesis and allocate it belowground through root growth and exudation — cover crops can fix an additional 1-3 tons of carbon per hectare per year compared to bare fallow |
| Soil Microbial Activity | Internal | The metabolic activity of billions of bacteria, fungi, and other soil organisms that decompose organic matter, cycle nutrients, and determine whether plant-derived carbon is mineralized back to CO2 or stabilized into long-lasting soil organic matter |
| Soil Organic Carbon Stock | Internal | The total mass of organic carbon stored in the soil profile, typically measured in the top 30 cm — global agricultural soils have lost 50-70% of their original carbon stock through centuries of tillage, representing both the scale of the problem and the potential for restoration |
| Soil Respiration Rate | Internal | The rate at which soil microbes and roots convert stored organic carbon back to CO2, which increases with temperature and moisture — if warming accelerates respiration faster than plants can add carbon, soils flip from carbon sinks to carbon sources |
| Net Soil Carbon Balance | Internal | The difference between carbon inputs (plant residues, root exudates, compost) and carbon outputs (microbial respiration, erosion, leaching) — positive balance means soil is sequestering carbon; negative balance means soil is emitting carbon to the atmosphere |

## Step 2: ESTABLISH — Connect the Relationships

### Text Editor

```
TIME TO DRAW THE INVISIBLE LINES

Those 6 components don't just sit there — they AFFECT each other.
When one changes, others change too. Let's map those connections.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Task A: OPEN CONNECTION MODE
• Click the "Connect" icon in the TOP LEFT corner of your screen
• Your cursor is now ready to draw relationship arrows

Task B: DRAW YOUR RELATIONSHIPS
• Click on "Agricultural Management Practice" and drag an arrow to "Plant Carbon Fixation Rate"
• Click on "Plant Carbon Fixation Rate" and drag an arrow to "Soil Microbial Activity"
• Click on "Soil Microbial Activity" and drag an arrow to "Soil Organic Carbon Stock"
• Click on "Temperature" and drag an arrow to "Soil Respiration Rate"
• Click on "Soil Respiration Rate" and drag an arrow to "Net Soil Carbon Balance"

Task C: SET POSITIVE OR NEGATIVE
• Look at the +/− toggle in the TOP LEFT corner
• For each connection, ask: "When this goes UP, does the other go UP or DOWN?"

  ○ Agricultural Management Practice → Plant Carbon Fixation Rate = POSITIVE (+)
    Regenerative practices like cover cropping and diverse rotations increase the total amount of plant photosynthesis occurring on farmland by maintaining living roots year-round rather than leaving soil bare between cash crops. No-till also preserves root systems that pump carbon underground.

  ○ Plant Carbon Fixation Rate → Soil Microbial Activity = POSITIVE (+)
    Higher plant carbon inputs — through root exudates, dead root tissue, and leaf litter — provide more food for soil microorganisms. Mycorrhizal fungi receive up to 30% of plant-fixed carbon directly through symbiotic root connections, feeding the soil food web.

  ○ Soil Microbial Activity → Soil Organic Carbon Stock = POSITIVE (+)
    Microbial activity has a dual role: microbes stabilize some carbon into long-lasting organic compounds (humus formation, mineral association) while simultaneously respiring other carbon back to CO2. The net effect depends on microbial community composition, soil mineralogy, and environmental conditions.

  ○ Temperature → Soil Respiration Rate = POSITIVE (+)
    Soil microbial respiration approximately doubles for every 10°C increase in temperature (Q10 ≈ 2). As global temperatures rise, microbes decompose soil organic matter faster, releasing more CO2. This is the mechanism that drives the dangerous soil-climate positive feedback loop.

  ○ Soil Respiration Rate → Net Soil Carbon Balance = NEGATIVE (−)
    Higher respiration removes carbon from the soil and returns it to the atmosphere as CO2. When Soil Respiration Rate exceeds Plant Carbon Fixation Rate, Net Soil Carbon Balance turns negative — the soil becomes a net carbon source rather than a sink.

Task D: CHECK YOUR MODEL
• You should have 5 arrows total
• 1 negative relationship(s), 3 positive relationship(s)
• This is your system model!

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

THINK ABOUT IT: When Agricultural Management shifts to regenerative practices, Plant Carbon Fixation increases through cover crops and diverse rotations. This feeds Soil Microbial Activity, which can stabilize carbon into long-lasting organic matter. But warming temperatures also accelerate Soil Respiration, releasing stored carbon. If the planet warms enough that respiration exceeds fixation, what happens to the 2,500 gigatons of carbon stored in the world's soils — three times more than the entire atmosphere contains?
```

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

WHAT DID YOU DISCOVER?
• The world's soils contain approximately 2,500 gigatons of organic carbon — more than three times the carbon in the atmosphere (880 Gt) and four times the carbon in all living vegetation (450 Gt) combined
• Agricultural soils have lost 50-70% of their original organic carbon through centuries of tillage, which exposes buried carbon to oxygen and accelerates microbial decomposition — this represents a massive potential reservoir for re-sequestration
• Regenerative agriculture can sequester 0.5-3.0 tons of carbon per hectare per year, but gains slow as soils approach a new equilibrium — most sequestration occurs in the first 20-30 years after practice change, then plateaus
• The soil carbon-climate feedback is one of the most dangerous positive feedback loops in the climate system: warming accelerates microbial respiration, releasing stored carbon, which causes more warming, which accelerates more respiration — potentially releasing hundreds of gigatons of carbon from thawing permafrost alone

THE ANSWER: Soil can absolutely help slow climate change — but it cannot save it alone. Regenerative agriculture rebuilds soil organic carbon by increasing plant inputs and reducing disturbance, potentially sequestering 0.5-3 tons of carbon per hectare per year. With 1.5 billion hectares of cropland globally, the theoretical maximum is significant — perhaps 2-5 gigatons of CO2 per year, offsetting 5-10% of global emissions. However, the model reveals critical limits: sequestration rates slow as soils approach a new equilibrium (typically within 20-30 years), gains are reversible if practices revert to conventional tillage, and climate warming itself accelerates soil carbon loss through increased microbial respiration. The most alarming finding is the soil-climate feedback loop: global soils hold three times more carbon than the atmosphere, and if warming pushes soils from net sinks to net sources, it would create a self-reinforcing cycle that dwarfs any agricultural sequestration effort. Soil carbon sequestration is a valuable climate tool, but it buys time rather than solving the problem — fossil fuel emissions must still be eliminated.

LESSON PURPOSE:
Beneath our feet lies the largest terrestrial carbon reservoir on Earth — soil holds more carbon than the atmosphere and all plant life combined. Understanding soil carbon dynamics requires modeling the intersection of plant biology, microbial ecology, agricultural science, and climate physics to evaluate whether changing how we manage soil could meaningfully slow climate change, or whether warming itself could turn this vast carbon store into an accelerant.

NGSS ALIGNMENT:
HS-LS2-4, HS-ESS3-3: Use mathematical representations to support claims for the cycling of matter and flow of energy among organisms in an ecosystem. Create a computational simulation to illustrate the relationships among management of natural resources, the sustainability of human populations, and biodiversity.

• Science Practice: Using Mathematics and Computational Thinking
  Students use mathematical representations to model carbon flux rates between atmosphere, plants, and soil, calculating net sequestration under different management scenarios and evaluating whether soil carbon gains can meaningfully offset fossil fuel emissions.
• Disciplinary Core Idea: LS2.B Cycles of Matter and Energy Transfer / ESS3.C Human Impacts on Earth Systems
  Students model how matter cycles between living and nonliving components of the soil ecosystem, and how agricultural management decisions alter these cycles with consequences for atmospheric carbon concentration and climate stability.
• Crosscutting Concept: Stability and Change / Energy and Matter
  Students analyze how soil carbon exists in a dynamic equilibrium between inputs and outputs, and how human practices and climate change can shift this equilibrium, transforming soils from carbon sinks to carbon sources.

LEVER FRAMEWORK:
• Locate: Students identify six components of the soil carbon system: Agricultural Management Practice, Plant Carbon Fixation Rate, Soil Microbial Activity, Soil Organic Carbon Stock, Soil Respiration Rate, and Net Soil Carbon Balance — distinguishing the human-controlled management decisions from the biological and chemical processes that govern carbon cycling.
• Establish: Students determine that Agricultural Management Practice controls Plant Carbon Fixation Rate through crop selection and soil disturbance levels. Plant inputs feed Soil Microbial Activity, which processes carbon into stable organic matter (increasing Soil Organic Carbon Stock) or respires it back to CO2 (Soil Respiration Rate). The balance between these processes determines Net Soil Carbon Balance.
• Visualize: Students build a computational model showing the stock-and-flow dynamics of soil carbon — the slow accumulation through plant inputs and microbial stabilization versus the rapid loss through tillage disturbance and temperature-driven respiration.
• Evaluate: Students run conventional baseline, regenerative transition, and climate warming feedback scenarios to quantify sequestration potential, identify saturation timescales, and test whether warming can flip soils from sinks to sources.
• Revise: Students add Permafrost Carbon Pool, Soil Biodiversity Index, or Water Holding Capacity to explore how Arctic thaw amplifies global feedback, how biological diversity affects carbon processing efficiency, and how carbon sequestration creates water management co-benefits.

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• The Soil Carbon Reservoir:
  Global soils contain approximately 2,500 gigatons of organic carbon in the top 2 meters — more than three times the 880 gigatons in the atmosphere and four times the 450 gigatons in all living vegetation. This carbon accumulated over thousands of years as plant material was incorporated into soil and partially decomposed by microorganisms. The resulting soil organic matter (humus) is a complex mixture of compounds ranging from fresh plant residues to highly stable molecular aggregates that can persist for centuries. Soil organic carbon is not static — it exists in a dynamic equilibrium between inputs (plant growth, root exudation, leaf litter) and outputs (microbial respiration, erosion, leaching). The balance between these fluxes determines whether soil accumulates or loses carbon.

• How Agriculture Destroyed Soil Carbon:
  When humans first plowed native grasslands and forests for agriculture, they initiated a massive transfer of carbon from soil to atmosphere. Tillage physically disrupts soil aggregates that protect organic matter from decomposition, exposes buried carbon to oxygen, and dramatically accelerates microbial breakdown. Globally, agricultural soils have lost an estimated 50-70% of their original organic carbon — approximately 133 gigatons of carbon — since cultivation began. The U.S. Midwest prairie soils, which originally contained 6-8% organic matter, now average 1-3%. This loss represents both an environmental catastrophe and an opportunity: the depleted carbon represents reservoir capacity that could theoretically be refilled.

• Regenerative Agriculture and Carbon Rebuilding:
  Regenerative agriculture reverses the practices that deplete soil carbon. No-till farming eliminates the physical disruption that exposes organic matter to rapid decomposition. Cover crops — planted between cash crop seasons — maintain living roots in the soil year-round, continuously pumping carbon below ground through root growth and exudation. Diverse rotations support broader microbial communities that process carbon more efficiently. Compost and manure additions provide direct organic matter inputs. Research shows these practices can sequester 0.5-3.0 tons of carbon per hectare per year, depending on soil type, climate, and specific practices. However, gains are not permanent: they slow as soils approach a new equilibrium (typically 20-30 years), and they reverse rapidly if practices revert to conventional tillage.

• The Dangerous Feedback Loop:
  The soil-climate feedback is among the most concerning positive feedback mechanisms in the climate system. Soil microbial respiration — the process by which microbes decompose organic matter and release CO2 — is highly temperature-sensitive. For every 10°C increase in temperature, respiration rates roughly double (the Q10 relationship). As global temperatures rise, soil microbes decompose organic matter faster, releasing more CO2, which causes more warming, which accelerates more decomposition. Arctic permafrost — containing approximately 1,500 gigatons of frozen carbon — is particularly vulnerable. As permafrost thaws, previously frozen organic matter becomes available to microbes for the first time in thousands of years. Models suggest that permafrost carbon release alone could add 0.3-1.0°C of additional warming by 2100 — a feedback that is essentially irreversible on human timescales.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Soil can absorb unlimited carbon and solve climate change"
  Reality: Soil carbon sequestration has a physical saturation point determined by soil mineralogy, clay content, and climate. Once soil reaches its carbon carrying capacity — typically after 20-30 years of regenerative management — it can no longer accumulate additional carbon regardless of management intensity. Furthermore, even at maximum global sequestration rates, soil can offset only 5-10% of annual fossil fuel emissions. Soil carbon is a valuable tool in the climate toolkit, not a silver bullet.
  Strategy: Run the model: show the sequestration curve flattening after 20-30 years. Calculate total global potential (2-5 Gt CO2/year) versus total global emissions (36 Gt CO2/year). Ask: Is 10% meaningful? Absolutely. Is it sufficient alone? Absolutely not.

• "No-till farming automatically increases soil carbon"
  Reality: No-till reduces carbon loss from tillage disturbance but does not guarantee net carbon gain. Without additional practices — cover crops, diverse rotations, organic matter additions — no-till may simply redistribute carbon within the soil profile rather than increasing total stocks. Some studies show no-till increases carbon in the top 0-10 cm but decreases it at 10-30 cm depth. The full regenerative package is needed for reliable sequestration.
  Strategy: Show data comparing no-till alone versus no-till plus cover crops plus diverse rotations. The combined approach sequesters 2-5 times more carbon than no-till alone. Ask: Why does just stopping tillage not automatically rebuild carbon?

• "Soil carbon, once stored, stays stored permanently"
  Reality: Soil carbon is inherently impermanent — it exists in a dynamic equilibrium that can shift rapidly. A single pass with a moldboard plow can expose decades of accumulated organic matter to rapid decomposition, releasing stored carbon within a few years. Climate warming independently accelerates decomposition rates. Drought can kill plants and reduce inputs. This permanence problem is the central challenge of soil carbon as a climate strategy — carbon credits based on soil storage must account for the risk that the carbon will be re-released.
  Strategy: Analogy: storing carbon in soil is like saving money in an account with no lock. It stays as long as you keep making deposits and avoid withdrawals — but the account can be emptied in a fraction of the time it took to fill. What safeguards would you design to ensure permanence?

ANSWER KEY:
Big Question: Could changing how we farm pull enough carbon out of the atmosphere to slow climate change — or is soil carbon sequestration too good to be true?
Answer: Soil can absolutely help slow climate change — but it cannot save it alone. Regenerative agriculture rebuilds soil organic carbon by increasing plant inputs and reducing disturbance, potentially sequestering 0.5-3 tons of carbon per hectare per year. With 1.5 billion hectares of cropland globally, the theoretical maximum is significant — perhaps 2-5 gigatons of CO2 per year, offsetting 5-10% of global emissions. However, the model reveals critical limits: sequestration rates slow as soils approach a new equilibrium (typically within 20-30 years), gains are reversible if practices revert to conventional tillage, and climate warming itself accelerates soil carbon loss through increased microbial respiration. The most alarming finding is the soil-climate feedback loop: global soils hold three times more carbon than the atmosphere, and if warming pushes soils from net sinks to net sources, it would create a self-reinforcing cycle that dwarfs any agricultural sequestration effort. Soil carbon sequestration is a valuable climate tool, but it buys time rather than solving the problem — fossil fuel emissions must still be eliminated.

Simulation Answers:
• Conventional Baseline: Under conventional tillage, Soil Organic Carbon Stock declines steadily as tillage-accelerated decomposition exceeds plant carbon inputs. Net Soil Carbon Balance is negative, meaning the soil emits more CO2 than it sequesters. Over 50 years, the soil may lose an additional 20-40% of its remaining organic carbon. The model reveals that conventional agriculture is not carbon-neutral — it is actively mining soil carbon and transferring it to the atmosphere.
• Climate Warming Feedback: Under the combined regenerative + warming scenario, the model reveals a critical race: regenerative practices increase carbon inputs by 1-3 tons C/hectare/year, but warming-accelerated respiration can increase carbon losses by 0.5-2 tons C/hectare/year depending on the degree of warming. At 2°C warming, regenerative gains still exceed warming losses in most soil types. At 3°C+, respiration begins to overwhelm fixation, and soils in warm regions may flip to net sources regardless of management. The tipping point is not fixed — it depends on soil type, initial carbon stock, and management intensity.

Reflection Exemplars:
• Q: Why does soil carbon sequestration have a saturation point?
  A: The model reveals that soil carbon accumulation follows a diminishing returns curve. When degraded soil first receives increased plant inputs through regenerative practices, there is abundant 'space' in the soil matrix — mineral surfaces to bind organic molecules, aggregate structures to protect them, and depleted microbial communities to rebuild. Carbon accumulates rapidly at 1-3 tons per hectare per year. But as the soil approaches its carrying capacity for organic carbon (determined by clay content, mineralogy, and climate), fewer binding sites remain available, microbial respiration catches up with inputs, and a new equilibrium is reached where inputs equal outputs. This typically occurs after 20-30 years of regenerative management. The saturation point means soil carbon sequestration is a one-time benefit — not a permanent annual offset — and its total contribution is bounded by the soil's physical and biological capacity.
• Q: How does the soil-climate feedback create a tipping point?
  A: The feedback loop operates through temperature-sensitive microbial respiration. Soil microbes decompose organic matter faster as temperatures rise — approximately doubling their CO2 output for every 10°C increase. When warming accelerates respiration beyond the rate of new carbon inputs from plants, the soil flips from a net sink to a net source. This releases more CO2, which causes more warming, which accelerates more respiration — a self-reinforcing cycle with no natural brake. The model shows that this feedback is particularly dangerous in permafrost regions, where 1,500 gigatons of frozen carbon has been locked away from microbial access for thousands of years. As permafrost thaws, this carbon becomes available for decomposition, potentially adding 0.3-1.0°C of additional warming that is essentially irreversible because refreezing would require sustained cooling over centuries.

Key Concepts:
• Soil Carbon Measurement: Accurate soil carbon quantification requires systematic sampling at multiple depths (0-15 cm, 15-30 cm, 30-60 cm), bulk density measurements, and laboratory analysis. Changes of 0.1-0.5% organic matter per year must be detected against high spatial variability, requiring statistical design with 20-30 sample points per field. Remote sensing and spectroscopic methods are emerging but not yet sufficiently accurate for carbon credit verification.
• Practice-Specific Sequestration Rates: Different regenerative practices have different carbon sequestration potentials: no-till alone sequesters 0.3-0.8 tons C/ha/year, adding cover crops increases this to 0.5-1.5 tons C/ha/year, and integrating compost application and diverse rotations can reach 1.0-3.0 tons C/ha/year. However, all rates vary significantly by soil type, climate, and baseline carbon level — sandy soils in dry climates sequester less than clay soils in humid climates.
• Carbon Credit Economics: Soil carbon credits on voluntary markets trade at $15-50 per ton of CO2 equivalent. A farmer sequestering 2 tons C/ha/year generates approximately 7.3 tons CO2-equivalent in credits worth $110-365/ha/year. This can offset the economic risk of transition but requires robust measurement, reporting, and verification (MRV) to maintain market credibility. Permanence guarantees (buffer pools, insurance) address the risk of reversal if practices change.

Evaluation Rubric:
• Expert (4): Program includes soil-type-specific practice recommendations, rigorous measurement and verification protocols, phased economic incentive structure, farmer support and education plan, 25-year sequestration projections with uncertainty ranges, and permanence risk mitigation — all justified with model data

• Developing (2): Program mentions relevant practices and measurement needs but lacks soil-type specificity, economic analysis, or model-based justification for sequestration targets
