## Platform

### Title
**Chain Reaction: When Energy Moves Object to Object — Why Each Collision Loses a Little Energy Along the Way**

### Overview
Students model how energy transfers from one object to the next during collisions and discover why each collision in a chain reaction has slightly less energy than the one before it. By building a cause-and-effect model of initial push force, object mass, kinetic energy transfer, energy lost to sound, and final object speed, students learn that every collision leaks energy as sound and heat, explaining why dominoes slow down, Newton's cradles eventually stop, and Rube Goldberg machines need strong starting pushes.

### Grade

### NGSS Standard
**4-PS3-3**: Ask questions and predict outcomes about the changes in energy that occur when objects collide.
**4-PS3-4**: Apply scientific ideas to design, test, and refine a device that converts energy from one form to another.

### Learning Objectives
- Explain how kinetic energy transfers from one object to the next during collisions
- Model how initial push force, object mass, kinetic energy transfer, energy lost to sound, and final object speed interact
- Describe how energy is lost to sound and heat at each collision, reducing the energy available for the next object
- Use evidence from a model to explain why chain reactions lose energy over successive collisions

### Component List (Starting Model: 5 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Initial Push Force | External | How hard the first object in the chain is pushed — a stronger push gives the first object more kinetic energy to transfer |
| Object Mass | External | How heavy the objects in the chain are — heavier objects are harder to get moving and require more energy to push |
| Kinetic Energy Transfer | Internal | The amount of motion energy that passes from one object to the next during each collision |
| Energy Lost to Sound | Internal | The kinetic energy that transforms into sound energy during each collision — you can hear each impact |
| Final Object Speed | Internal | How fast the last object in the chain moves after receiving energy through all the previous collisions |

## Step 2: ESTABLISH — Connect the Relationships

### Text Editor

```
TIME TO TRACE THE ENERGY LEAK

These five components reveal something surprising: Kinetic Energy
Transfer feeds TWO things at once — and they are COMPETING for
the same energy.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Task A: OPEN CONNECTION MODE
• Click the "Connect" icon in the TOP LEFT corner

Task B: DRAW YOUR RELATIONSHIPS
• Initial Push Force → Kinetic Energy Transfer
• Object Mass → Final Object Speed
• Kinetic Energy Transfer → Final Object Speed
• Kinetic Energy Transfer → Energy Lost to Sound
• Energy Lost to Sound → Final Object Speed

Task C: SET POSITIVE OR NEGATIVE
  ○ Initial Push Force → Kinetic Energy Transfer = POSITIVE (+)
    Harder push = MORE energy entering the chain!

  ○ Object Mass → Final Object Speed = NEGATIVE (−)
    Heavier objects = LESS final speed!
    Heavy things are harder to get moving.

  ○ Kinetic Energy Transfer → Final Object Speed = POSITIVE (+)
    More energy transferring = FASTER final object!

  ○ Kinetic Energy Transfer → Energy Lost to Sound = POSITIVE (+)
    More energy flowing = MORE sound at each collision!
    (Bigger collisions are louder!)

  ○ Energy Lost to Sound → Final Object Speed = NEGATIVE (−)
    More sound loss = LESS speed at the end!
    Energy that becomes sound cannot push the last object.

Task D: CHECK YOUR MODEL
• You should have 5 arrows total
• 3 positive, 2 negative
• Notice: KE Transfer feeds BOTH Final Speed AND Sound Loss!
  They are competing for the same energy!

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

The two arrows coming OUT of Kinetic Energy Transfer tell the
whole story. Some energy goes to the final object (good). Some
energy escapes as sound (lost). Every collision is a split.
```

### Video Script

```
"Time to trace where the energy goes. This model has a
competition at its heart.

First: 'Initial Push Force' to 'Kinetic Energy Transfer.' Harder
push means more energy enters the chain. POSITIVE. More in, more
to work with.

Second: 'Object Mass' to 'Final Object Speed.' Heavier objects
are harder to get moving. They absorb more energy from each
collision just to start moving, leaving less for the final object.
More mass, LESS final speed. NEGATIVE.

Third: 'Kinetic Energy Transfer' to 'Final Object Speed.' More
energy flowing through the chain means more reaches the end.
POSITIVE.

Fourth — and here is where it gets interesting: 'Kinetic Energy
Transfer' to 'Energy Lost to Sound.' When more energy flows through
a collision, the collision is more energetic. More energetic
collisions are LOUDER. The sound you hear is energy LEAVING the
motion system. POSITIVE — more transfer means more sound.

Fifth: 'Energy Lost to Sound' to 'Final Object Speed.' Every
bit of energy that becomes sound is energy that is NOT available
for the final object. More sound loss means less final speed.
NEGATIVE.

Look at Kinetic Energy Transfer. It has TWO positive arrows going
out — one to Final Object Speed and one to Energy Lost to Sound.
They are COMPETING for the same energy pool.

At every collision, energy splits. Some goes forward to the next
object. Some escapes as sound. By the end of a long chain, all
those little leaks add up to a big difference.

That is why the last domino always falls slower than the first
one was pushed.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

• Compare Object Mass at 20% vs. 50% vs. 80%

```

### Video Script

```
"Let us run these chains and see what comes out the other end.

Scenario 1: Strong Push, Light Objects. Push at 80, Mass at 20.

Watch the graph. Lots of kinetic energy enters the system.
Light objects transfer it efficiently — they do not absorb much
just getting started. Energy Lost to Sound is noticeable at each
collision (you would hear the clicks) but small compared to the
total. Final Object Speed is high. This is the ideal chain.

Scenario 2: Weak Push, Heavy Objects. Push at 20, Mass at 80.

Very little energy enters the system, and the heavy objects
absorb most of it just trying to get moving. Energy Lost to Sound
still happens at each collision. By the time energy reaches the
final object, there is almost nothing left. The last object barely
moves. This chain barely works.

Scenario 3: Strong push on heavy objects. Push at 80, Mass at 80.
More energy enters, which helps. But heavy objects still absorb
a lot at each step. Final speed is moderate — better than Scenario
2 but much worse than Scenario 1.

Scenario 4: Same push, three different masses. Watch Final Object
Speed change as mass increases. More mass, less speed. Every time.

The lesson is clear: light objects and strong pushes maximize
what reaches the end. Heavy objects and weak pushes waste energy.
And at EVERY collision, sound leaks away.

Every click, every bang, every tap you hear is energy escaping
from the motion system forever.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

### Video Script

```
"You understand the energy leak. Every collision loses a little
to sound and heat. Now let us push further.

collisions, and the more opportunities for energy to leak.

energy. After 20 collisions? About 64 percent lost.

balls. Wood blocks absorb energy differently than plastic.

efficiently.

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format aligned to 4-PS3-3 and 4-PS3-4.

---

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.1 (Determine components of a system) + DCI PS3.C (Relationship Between Energy and Forces) + CCC5 (Energy and Matter)

A student is building a model of energy transfer during collisions. She sorts five components into choices we make (external) and outcomes physics determines (internal). Which list is correct?

A. External: Initial Push Force, Object Mass — Internal: Kinetic Energy Transfer, Energy Lost to Sound, Final Object Speed
B. External: Object Mass, Energy Lost to Sound — Internal: Initial Push Force, Kinetic Energy Transfer, Final Object Speed
C. External: Initial Push Force, Final Object Speed — Internal: Object Mass, Kinetic Energy Transfer, Energy Lost to Sound
D. External: Initial Push Force, Kinetic Energy Transfer — Internal: Object Mass, Energy Lost to Sound, Final Object Speed

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: Initial Push Force (how hard we push) and Object Mass (which objects we choose) are external because we decide them. Kinetic Energy Transfer, Energy Lost to Sound, and Final Object Speed are internal because they are determined by the physics of each collision. If you chose D, KE Transfer is a physics result, not our choice. If you chose C, Final Object Speed is the result we are measuring. If you chose B, Energy Lost to Sound happens automatically during collisions.
---

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships among components) + DCI PS3.C (Relationship Between Energy and Forces) + CCC5 (Energy and Matter)

In the collision model, Kinetic Energy Transfer has a POSITIVE relationship with both Final Object Speed AND Energy Lost to Sound. A student asks why one component feeds two different things.

Which explanation best describes what is happening?

A. Final Object Speed and Energy Lost to Sound are the same thing measured differently.
B. More energy transferring through a collision means more energy splits — some goes forward to the next object (speed) and some escapes as sound (loss). They are competing for the same energy.
C. This is an error in the model — one component cannot feed two others.
D. Kinetic Energy Transfer creates new energy that goes to both places at once.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: At each collision, the total kinetic energy splits. Some transfers forward to the next object (contributing to final speed) and some transforms into sound waves (the click or bang you hear). They compete for the same pool of energy. More total energy means more going to both, but every bit that becomes sound is energy that cannot contribute to speed.
---

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships among components) + DCI PS3.C (Relationship Between Energy and Forces) + CCC5 (Energy and Matter)

A student runs two scenarios. In Scenario 1, Initial Push Force is 80% and Object Mass is 20% (strong push, light objects). In Scenario 2, Initial Push Force is 20% and Object Mass is 80% (weak push, heavy objects).

Based on the model, how would Final Object Speed compare?

A. Both scenarios would have similar final speed because the total components are the same (80+20 vs. 20+80).
B. Scenario 2 would have higher final speed because heavy objects have more momentum.
C. Scenario 1 would have much higher final speed because strong push gives more energy and light objects transfer it efficiently.
D. Final speed would be zero in both cases because energy is always lost.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: In Scenario 1, a strong push puts lots of kinetic energy into the system, and light objects transfer it efficiently (they do not absorb much just getting moving). In Scenario 2, little energy enters the system, and heavy objects absorb a large share at each collision. The combination of more input energy and more efficient transfer in Scenario 1 produces dramatically higher final speed.
---

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to explain/predict events) + DCI PS3.C (Relationship Between Energy and Forces) + CCC5 (Energy and Matter)

A student pushes a marble into a line of 10 identical marbles. The last marble rolls out the other end but moves slower than the first marble was pushed.

Which explanation from the model best describes why the last marble is slower?

A. The last marble is heavier because it absorbed material from the other marbles.
B. At each of the 10 collisions, a small amount of kinetic energy transformed into sound and heat, and these losses accumulated so less energy reached the last marble.
C. The marbles in the middle stole energy and kept it for themselves.
D. The first marble was pushed harder than the force that reached the last marble because pushing gets weaker over distance.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: At each collision in the chain, a small percentage of kinetic energy transforms into sound (the click) and heat (tiny friction at contact). After 10 collisions, these small losses add up significantly. If each collision loses 5% as sound and heat, after 10 collisions only about 60% of the original energy remains. The last marble receives less energy, so it moves slower. The energy was not stolen — it was transformed into forms (sound, heat) that cannot push the marble.
---

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to predict a scientific event) + DCI PS3.D (Energy in Everyday Life) + CCC5 (Energy and Matter)

A student wants to build a Rube Goldberg machine with 20 collisions that ends by ringing a bell. Based on the model, which design choice would best ensure enough energy reaches the bell?

A. Use the heaviest objects possible so they carry more energy.
B. Make the chain as short as possible by spacing objects far apart.
C. Use soft objects like cotton balls so collisions are quiet and less energy is lost to sound.
D. Use a very strong initial push and light objects so maximum energy enters the system and transfers efficiently through all 20 collisions.

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: The model shows that a strong initial push maximizes energy input, and light objects maximize transfer efficiency. With 20 collisions, energy losses at each step accumulate significantly. Starting with maximum energy and minimizing loss at each step gives the best chance of enough energy surviving to ring the bell at the end. If you chose A, heavy objects absorb more energy at each collision, leaving less for the next step. If you chose B, spacing does not reduce the number of collisions. If you chose C, while quieter collisions lose less to sound, cotton balls transfer very little kinetic energy forward because they deform and absorb energy.
