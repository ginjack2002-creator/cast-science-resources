## Platform

### Title
**Building for the Big One: Earthquake Engineering Lab — How Engineers Design Buildings That Bend Instead of Break**

### Overview
Students model why some buildings collapse during earthquakes while others right next to them survive with barely a crack. By building a cause-and-effect model of earthquake magnitude, building flexibility, shaking force, structural damage, and occupant safety, students discover that the secret to earthquake survival is flexibility, not strength. This lesson connects natural hazards to engineering design and empowers students to think like seismic engineers.

### Cover Image
[Dramatic split image showing two buildings during an earthquake — one crumbling and collapsing while the other flexes and stays standing, with visible seismic waves in the ground beneath]

### Grade
4th

### NGSS Standard
**4-ESS3-2**: Generate and compare multiple solutions to reduce the impacts of natural Earth processes on humans.
**4-ETS1-2**: Generate and compare multiple solutions to a problem based on how well they meet the criteria and constraints of the design problem.

### Learning Objectives
- Explain how earthquake magnitude determines the shaking force experienced by buildings
- Model how earthquake magnitude, building flexibility, shaking force, structural damage, and occupant safety interact
- Describe why flexible buildings survive earthquakes better than rigid buildings
- Use evidence from a model to design buildings that reduce earthquake damage and protect occupants

### Component List (Starting Model: 5 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Earthquake Magnitude | External | The strength of the earthquake as measured on the Richter scale — stronger earthquakes release more energy and shake the ground harder |
| Building Flexibility | External | How well the building can absorb and distribute shaking forces by bending, swaying, or flexing without breaking apart |
| Shaking Force | Internal | The actual force transmitted from the moving ground into the building's structure — stronger earthquakes create stronger shaking forces |
| Structural Damage | Internal | The amount of physical damage to the building — cracks in walls, broken windows, collapsed floors, and foundation failures |
| Occupant Safety | Internal | How safe the people inside the building are during and after the earthquake — depends on whether the structure protects them from falling debris |

```
is active. Without Shaking Force, Building Flexibility alone can't change Structural Damage.
Let's teach your model this rule.
```

### Video Script

```
"You proved that flexibility saves lives. Now let us explore the
actual engineering solutions that protect buildings around the
world.

First — explain to someone why a swaying building is SAFER than
a still building. This is counterintuitive but critical.

Second — test the limits. Can maximum flexibility protect against
the absolute strongest earthquake? What does the model say?

Third — explore real engineering solutions.

Base Isolation. Imagine putting your building on giant rubber pads.
When the ground shakes, the rubber pads absorb the motion. The
ground moves but the building barely moves. Like putting a book
on a wobbly table — the table shakes but the book slides smoothly.

Tuned Mass Dampers. Taipei 101 has a 730-ton golden ball hanging
near the top. During an earthquake or typhoon, when the building
sways left, the ball swings right — counteracting the motion. It
is a 730-ton pendulum that fights the shaking.

Cross-Bracing. Diagonal steel beams in X patterns that add strength
while maintaining flexibility. Triangles are the strongest shape
in engineering.

Add ONE to your model. Test it. See if it improves Occupant Safety.

Engineers cannot stop earthquakes. But they can design buildings
where everyone walks out alive.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format aligned to 4-ESS3-2 and 4-ETS1-2.

---

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.1 (Determine components of a system) + DCI ESS3.B (Natural Hazards) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A student building an earthquake model sorts five components. Which correctly sorts them?

A. External: Earthquake Magnitude, Building Flexibility — Internal: Shaking Force, Structural Damage, Occupant Safety
B. External: Earthquake Magnitude, Shaking Force — Internal: Building Flexibility, Structural Damage, Occupant Safety
C. External: Earthquake Magnitude, Occupant Safety — Internal: Building Flexibility, Shaking Force, Structural Damage
D. External: Shaking Force, Building Flexibility — Internal: Earthquake Magnitude, Structural Damage, Occupant Safety

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: Earthquake Magnitude is external (nature controls it). Building Flexibility is external (engineers choose it before the earthquake). Shaking Force, Structural Damage, and Occupant Safety are internal outcomes. If you chose B, Shaking Force is a result, not an input. If you chose C, Occupant Safety is the final result. If you chose D, Shaking Force is a result of the earthquake.
---

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships among components) + DCI ESS3.B (Natural Hazards) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

Building Flexibility has a NEGATIVE relationship with Structural Damage. What does this mean?

A. Flexible buildings are weaker, so they get more damage.
B. More flexibility means LESS damage because the building absorbs shaking by bending instead of breaking.
C. Flexible buildings take longer to collapse, but they still collapse eventually.
D. Flexibility does not affect damage because earthquakes destroy all buildings equally.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: A flexible building moves WITH the earthquake, absorbing and distributing shaking energy through controlled swaying. This prevents the catastrophic cracking and collapsing that happens in rigid buildings. Like a living tree bending in a storm versus a dead tree snapping.
---

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships among components) + DCI ESS3.B (Natural Hazards) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

Two identical buildings are next to each other. Building A has 20% flexibility (rigid). Building B has 80% flexibility. A magnitude 7.0 earthquake strikes both.

Based on the model, which prediction is correct?

A. Building A will have more structural damage and lower occupant safety than Building B.
B. Building B will have more damage because flexible buildings are weaker.
C. Neither building will be damaged because magnitude 7.0 is not strong enough.
D. Both buildings will have identical damage because they experienced the same earthquake.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: The same shaking force hits both buildings, but flexible Building B absorbs the energy by swaying while rigid Building A fights it and cracks. The model shows flexibility has a negative relationship with damage — more flexibility means less damage, which means better occupant safety.
---

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to explain/predict events) + DCI ESS3.B (Natural Hazards) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

Scientists compare earthquake deaths in two countries:

| Event | Magnitude | Deaths | Building Type |
|-------|-----------|--------|--------------|
| Haiti 2010 | 7.0 | 200,000+ | Rigid, unreinforced concrete |
| Japan 2011 | 9.0 (900x stronger) | ~20,000 (mostly tsunami) | Flexible, engineered steel frames |

Which conclusion from the model best explains this data?

A. The Richter scale does not accurately measure earthquake danger.
B. Japan had fewer people, so fewer died.
C. Haiti's earthquake was more dangerous because it was closer to cities.
D. Japan's flexible buildings dramatically reduced structural damage and saved lives despite a much stronger earthquake, proving that engineering design is more important than earthquake strength.

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: Japan experienced an earthquake over 900 times more powerful than Haiti's, yet far fewer people died from building collapse. The model explains this: flexible buildings reduce structural damage (negative relationship), which improves occupant safety. Japan invested in earthquake engineering; Haiti had mostly rigid, unreinforced buildings. The engineering made the difference.
---

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to predict a scientific event) + DCI ETS1.B (Developing Possible Solutions) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A city in an earthquake zone is building a new hospital. Based on the model, which design choice would MOST improve occupant safety during a major earthquake?

A. Build the hospital far from any fault lines so earthquakes cannot reach it.
B. Build the hospital underground to avoid the surface shaking.
C. Build the hospital with a flexible steel frame and base isolation system that allows controlled movement during shaking.
D. Build the hospital with the thickest, most rigid concrete walls possible to resist the shaking.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: The model shows that flexibility reduces structural damage (negative relationship) and improves occupant safety (positive relationship). A flexible steel frame allows controlled swaying, and base isolation lets the building move independently of the shaking ground. Rigid walls (A) would fight the earthquake and fail catastrophically. Underground buildings (C) still experience shaking. Moving far from faults (D) is not always possible and earthquakes can affect large areas.
