## Platform

### Title
**The Mountain That Used to Be an Ocean: How Seashells End Up on Mountaintops Millions of Years Later**

### Overview
Students model one of geology's greatest mysteries: how fossils of ocean creatures end up on top of mountains thousands of feet above sea level. By building a cause-and-effect model of plate movement speed, sediment deposit rate, compression force, rock uplift, and fossil elevation, students discover how tectonic forces operating over millions of years can push the ocean floor into the sky, carrying ancient marine fossils with it.

### NGSS Standard
**4-ESS1-1**: Identify evidence from patterns in rock formations and fossils in rock layers to support an explanation for changes in a landscape over time.
**4-ESS2-1**: Make observations and measurements to identify materials based on their properties.

### Learning Objectives
- Explain how tectonic plate movement creates the forces that push ocean floors upward into mountains
- Model how plate movement speed, sediment deposit rate, compression force, rock uplift, and fossil elevation interact
- Describe how fossils trapped in ocean sediment layers can end up at high elevations over millions of years
- Use evidence from a model to explain why the Himalayas contain fossils of sea creatures

### Component List (Starting Model: 5 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Plate Movement Speed | External | How fast the tectonic plates are pushing toward each other — faster movement creates more force |
| Sediment Deposit Rate | External | How fast layers of sediment (sand, mud, shells) accumulate on the ocean floor — more sediment means thicker layers with more fossils |
| Compression Force | Internal | The pressure created when two plates collide — more speed means more compression, which squeezes and folds rock layers |
| Rock Uplift | Internal | How quickly the compressed rock layers are pushed upward — stronger compression creates faster uplift |
| Fossil Elevation | Internal | The height above sea level where ocean fossils end up — determined by both how thick the sediment layers are and how much uplift has occurred |

```

Second: 'Compression Force' to 'Rock Uplift.' When the compression

the entire column of rock higher, carrying embedded fossils to
greater heights. This is the direct mechanism — the elevator that
takes sea creatures to the sky. POSITIVE.

Look at your model. Something special about this one — ALL four
relationships are POSITIVE. Everything amplifies. Faster plates
mean stronger compression, stronger compression means more uplift,
more uplift means higher fossils.

of years. And the result? Mountains.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

## Step 3: VISUALIZE & EVALUATE — Run Your Tectonic Model

### Text Editor

```
TIME TO BUILD MOUNTAINS

You built the system. Now let us see if your model can turn an
ocean floor into a mountain range.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Task A: SCENARIO 1 — FAST COLLISION, THICK SEDIMENT
• Set Plate Movement Speed to 80% and Sediment Deposit Rate to 80%
• This is like the formation of the Himalayas
• What do you predict will happen to Fossil Elevation?

Task B: SCENARIO 2 — SLOW COLLISION, THIN SEDIMENT
• Set Plate Movement Speed to 20% and Sediment Deposit Rate to 20%
• Will mountains still form?

Task C: SCENARIO 3 — NO MOVEMENT
• Set Plate Movement Speed to 0%
• What happens when plates stop moving entirely?

OBSERVE:
• What happened to Compression Force?
• What happened to Rock Uplift?
• What happened to Fossil Elevation?

Task D: SCENARIO 4 — FAST COLLISION, THIN SEDIMENT
• Set Plate Movement Speed to 80% but Sediment Deposit Rate to 20%
• Strong forces but few fossil layers — what happens?

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

REFLECTION QUESTIONS:
• Which scenario produced the highest Fossil Elevation?
• What happened when plates stopped moving?
• Do you need BOTH plate movement and sediment for fossils on mountains?
```

### Video Script

```
"Time to build some mountains. Let us see how different conditions
create different results.

Scenario 1: Fast Collision, Thick Sediment. This is the Himalayan
scenario. Plate Movement at 80 percent. Sediment at 80 percent.

Watch the graph. Compression Force climbs. Rock Uplift follows.
And Fossil Elevation just keeps going up and up. This is how
Mount Everest formed — the Indian Plate slamming into Asia at high
speed, pushing thick layers of fossil-rich ocean floor five miles
into the sky. In real life, this took about fifty million years.

Scenario 2: Slow Collision, Thin Sediment. Everything at 20 percent.
Mountains still form, but they are lower and have fewer fossils.
These might be ancient, worn-down mountains like the Appalachians.

Now Scenario 3: No Movement. Set Plate Movement Speed to zero.

Watch everything. Compression Force drops to zero. Rock Uplift
stops. Fossil Elevation freezes. Without plate movement, nothing
happens. Mountains stop growing.

And in fact, without uplift pushing back against erosion, existing
mountains would slowly wear away over millions of years until the
landscape is flat.

Scenario 4: Fast collision but thin sediment. Strong forces push
rock up, but there are not as many fossil layers to be elevated.
You get tall mountains but fewer fossils at the top.

The mystery is solved. The mountain DID NOT rise up from the land.
The OCEAN FLOOR was pushed up to become the mountain.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

```
worn them down from Himalayan heights to gentle hills.

of rock determines what the mountains look like.
```

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format aligned to 4-ESS1-1.

---

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.1 (Determine components of a system) + DCI ESS1.C (History of Planet Earth) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A student is building a model of how mountains form. She has five components: Plate Movement Speed, Sediment Deposit Rate, Compression Force, Rock Uplift, and Fossil Elevation. She sorts them into geological inputs (external) and results (internal).

Which list correctly sorts the components?

A. External: Rock Uplift, Plate Movement Speed — Internal: Sediment Deposit Rate, Compression Force, Fossil Elevation
B. External: Plate Movement Speed, Fossil Elevation — Internal: Sediment Deposit Rate, Compression Force, Rock Uplift
C. External: Plate Movement Speed, Compression Force — Internal: Sediment Deposit Rate, Rock Uplift, Fossil Elevation
D. External: Plate Movement Speed, Sediment Deposit Rate — Internal: Compression Force, Rock Uplift, Fossil Elevation

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: Plate Movement Speed and Sediment Deposit Rate are external because they are geological processes driven by Earth's internal heat and ocean chemistry that happen independently. Compression Force, Rock Uplift, and Fossil Elevation are internal because they are direct consequences of plate movement and sedimentation interacting over time. If you chose C, Compression Force is a result of plate movement, not an independent input. If you chose A, Rock Uplift is a result of compression. If you chose B, Fossil Elevation is the final output of the system.
---

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships among components) + DCI ESS1.C (History of Planet Earth) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

In a mountain-building model, all four relationships are positive. A student asks why there are no negative relationships in this model.

Which explanation best describes why all relationships in this model are positive?

A. All models should have only positive relationships because positive means correct.
B. The student made an error and should change some to negative.
C. In this system, each step amplifies the next — faster plates create more compression, which creates more uplift, which carries fossils higher.
D. Negative relationships only exist in models about living things, not geological models.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: In this particular system, each component feeds forward into the next in an amplifying chain. Faster plate movement creates stronger compression. Stronger compression creates more uplift. More uplift pushes fossils higher. More sediment provides more fossil material to be lifted. Each step builds on the previous one without any opposing forces (in this simplified model). This is what makes mountain building a powerful, cumulative process over millions of years. If you chose A, positive does not mean correct — some models have negative relationships. If you chose D, negative relationships exist in all types of models. If you chose B, the all-positive pattern accurately represents this system.
---

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships among components) + DCI ESS1.C (History of Planet Earth) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A student runs the mountain-building model with Plate Movement Speed set to 0%. All other settings remain the same.

Based on the model, what would most likely happen?

A. Compression Force, Rock Uplift, and Fossil Elevation would all drop to their current levels and stop changing.
B. The model would reverse and mountains would shrink back into the ocean.
C. Mountains would continue growing because sediment is still accumulating.
D. Only Fossil Elevation would stop, but Compression Force and Rock Uplift would continue.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: Plate Movement Speed drives the entire system. Without plate movement, there is no collision, so Compression Force drops to zero. Without compression, Rock Uplift stops. Fossil Elevation remains wherever it currently is but stops increasing. The system freezes. Sediment may continue accumulating on ocean floors, but without compression and uplift, it stays at the bottom. If you chose C, sediment alone cannot create mountains without compression from plate collision. If you chose B, the model does not reverse, but without uplift, erosion would slowly wear mountains down over additional millions of years. If you chose D, all three internal components depend on plate movement.

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to explain/predict events) + DCI ESS1.C (History of Planet Earth) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

Scientists find the following fossils in the rock layers of a mountain:

| Rock Layer | Fossils Found | Original Environment |
|------------|--------------|---------------------|
| Top layer | Ancient seashells | Shallow ocean |
| Middle layer | Deep-sea fish bones | Deep ocean |
| Bottom layer | Coral reef fragments | Warm shallow sea |

Which conclusion best explains this pattern based on the mountain-building model?

A. These rock layers formed at the bottom of the ocean over different time periods, then tectonic plate collision pushed all the layers upward together.
B. Animals migrated from the ocean to the mountain and died at different elevations.
C. Earthquakes shook the fossils loose from the ocean floor and scattered them onto the mountain.
D. The ocean level rose and fell many times, leaving different fossils at different heights.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: The layered pattern of different marine fossils shows that the rock formed underwater over millions of years, with different ocean creatures living at different times. The bottom layers formed first (oldest), then the middle layers, then the top layers (youngest). When tectonic plates collided, the entire stack of ocean-floor rock was pushed upward through compression and uplift, carrying all the fossil layers with it. If you chose D, while ocean levels do change, the fossils are embedded IN the rock layers, not deposited on top. If you chose B, ocean creatures did not migrate to mountaintops. If you chose C, earthquakes do not move fossils from ocean to mountain.

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to predict a scientific event) + DCI ESS1.C (History of Planet Earth) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

The Himalayan mountains are still growing about 4 mm taller per year because the Indian Plate is still pushing into the Asian Plate. The Appalachian Mountains in the eastern United States stopped growing millions of years ago because the plates that formed them are no longer colliding.

Based on the model, what would you predict about the Appalachian Mountains over the next 100 million years?

A. They will collapse suddenly into the ocean.
B. They will slowly get shorter because erosion wears them down and there is no uplift to replace the lost material.
C. They will continue to grow taller because mountains always grow.
D. They will stay exactly the same height forever.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: The model shows that without plate movement, there is no compression and no uplift. Without uplift pushing rock upward, there is nothing to counteract erosion from wind and rain, which slowly wears mountains down. The Appalachians were once as tall as the Himalayas but have been eroding for hundreds of millions of years without any new uplift, which is why they are now gentle, rounded mountains. Given another 100 million years without plate collision, they would continue to shrink. If you chose C, mountains only grow when plates are actively colliding. If you chose D, erosion is always at work. If you chose A, mountains erode gradually, not suddenly.
