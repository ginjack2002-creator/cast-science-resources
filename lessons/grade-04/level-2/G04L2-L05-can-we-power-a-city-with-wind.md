## Platform

### Title
**Can We Power a City with Wind? The Science and Challenge of Renewable Energy for Everyone**

### Overview
Students model the strengths and limitations of wind energy by building a cause-and-effect model of wind speed, number of turbines, electricity generated, city energy demand, and grid reliability. Students discover that while wind energy is free, clean, and renewable, its intermittent nature creates a fundamental challenge: cities need power 24/7, but the wind does not blow 24/7. This lesson connects energy science to real-world engineering and policy decisions about our energy future.

### NGSS Standard
**4-ESS3-1**: Obtain and combine information to describe that energy and fuels are derived from natural resources and their uses affect the environment.
**4-PS3-1**: Use evidence to construct an explanation relating the speed of an object to the energy of that object.

### Learning Objectives
- Explain how wind turbines convert the kinetic energy of moving air into electrical energy
- Model how wind speed, number of turbines, electricity generated, city energy demand, and grid reliability interact
- Describe the challenge of intermittent energy sources and why grid reliability depends on supply matching demand
- Use evidence from a model to evaluate the strengths and limitations of wind energy

### Component List (Starting Model: 5 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Wind Speed | External | How fast the wind is blowing — faster wind spins turbine blades faster and generates more electricity |
| Number of Turbines | External | How many wind turbines are installed in the wind farm — more turbines can capture more wind energy |
| Electricity Generated | Internal | The total amount of electrical power produced by all the turbines combined |
| City Energy Demand | Internal | How much electricity the city needs to power all homes, businesses, schools, and hospitals |
| Grid Reliability | Internal | Whether the electrical supply consistently meets the city's demand — high reliability means no blackouts |

## Step 2: ESTABLISH — Connect the Relationships

### Text Editor

```
TIME TO WIRE THE ENERGY GRID

Those five components form the story of whether wind can keep
a city running. Some connections help. One critical connection
works AGAINST reliability.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Task A: OPEN CONNECTION MODE
• Click the "Connect" icon in the TOP LEFT corner

Task B: DRAW YOUR RELATIONSHIPS
• Click on "Wind Speed" and drag an arrow to "Electricity Generated"
• Click on "Number of Turbines" and drag an arrow to "Electricity Generated"
• Click on "City Energy Demand" and drag an arrow to "Grid Reliability"
• Click on "Electricity Generated" and drag an arrow to "Grid Reliability"

Task C: SET POSITIVE OR NEGATIVE
  ○ Wind Speed → Electricity Generated = POSITIVE (+)
    Faster wind = MORE electricity!

  ○ Number of Turbines → Electricity Generated = POSITIVE (+)
    More turbines = MORE electricity!

  ○ City Energy Demand → Grid Reliability = NEGATIVE (−)
    More demand WITHOUT more supply = LESS reliability (blackouts)!

  ○ Electricity Generated → Grid Reliability = POSITIVE (+)
    More electricity supply = MORE reliability!

Task D: CHECK YOUR MODEL
• You should have 4 arrows total
• 3 positive relationships, 1 negative relationship
• The negative arrow is the KEY — demand works against reliability

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Notice: Grid Reliability has TWO arrows pointing at it — one
positive (supply) and one negative (demand). Reliability depends
on supply BEATING demand. If demand wins, blackout.
```

### Video Script

```
"Time to wire the grid. This model has a battle at the center.

First: 'Wind Speed' to 'Electricity Generated.' Faster wind spins
blades faster and generates more power. POSITIVE.

Second: 'Number of Turbines' to 'Electricity Generated.' More
turbines capture more wind. More turbines, more power. POSITIVE.

Third — and this is the critical one: 'City Energy Demand' to
'Grid Reliability.' When the city needs MORE power but the supply
stays the same, the grid gets strained. More demand, LESS

supply stabilizes the grid. When you generate enough to meet
demand, the lights stay on. POSITIVE.

Look at Grid Reliability. It has TWO arrows pointing at it.
One positive from supply. One negative from demand. This is a
BATTLE. If electricity generated is higher than city demand,
reliability is high and everyone has power. If demand exceeds
supply? Blackout.

And here is the problem with wind: Wind Speed changes. It is not
constant. The wind does not blow the same speed every minute of
every day. Sometimes it is strong. Sometimes it is calm. Sometimes
it stops entirely.

But the city needs power ALL the time. Hospitals cannot lose
electricity. Schools need lights. Your phone needs charging.

Can wind win the battle? Let us find out.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

## Step 3: VISUALIZE & EVALUATE — Run Your Wind Model

### Text Editor

```
TIME TO TEST WIND POWER

Can wind really power a city? Let us test four scenarios and
find out the truth.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Task A: SCENARIO 1 — WINDY DAY, PLENTY OF TURBINES
• Set Wind Speed to 80% and Number of Turbines to 80%
• What do you predict will happen to Grid Reliability?

Task B: SCENARIO 2 — CALM DAY, PLENTY OF TURBINES
• Set Wind Speed to 15% but keep Turbines at 80%
• What happens when the wind dies down?

OBSERVE:
• Even with LOTS of turbines, what happened to reliability?
• Why did all those turbines not help?

Task C: SCENARIO 3 — WINDY DAY, FEW TURBINES
• Set Wind Speed to 80% but Number of Turbines to 20%
• Lots of wind but not enough turbines — what happens?

Task D: SCENARIO 4 — BUILDING UP THE FARM
• Start Turbines at 20%, gradually increase to 100%
• Keep Wind Speed at 50%
• Does adding more turbines solve the reliability problem?

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

REFLECTION QUESTIONS:
• What is the BIGGEST challenge with wind energy?
• Why can we not just build more turbines to solve the problem?
• What would you ADD to this system to make it more reliable?
```

### Video Script

```
"Let us test wind power. Can it keep a city running?

Scenario 1: Windy Day, Plenty of Turbines. Wind at 80, Turbines
at 80. Click Play.

Look at that. Electricity Generated is very high. Grid Reliability
is strong. On a day like this, wind energy works perfectly. More
than enough clean power for everyone.

Now Scenario 2. This is the big test. Keep all those turbines but
drop Wind Speed to 15 percent. A calm day.

Watch Electricity Generated. It crashed. Even with 80 percent of
your turbines built, they are producing almost nothing because
there is not enough wind to spin the blades. And look at Grid
Reliability — it is plummeting. The city still needs power but
the turbines have almost nothing to give.

THIS is the problem with wind energy. Not windy days — calm days.

Scenario 3: Windy day but only 20 percent turbines. The wind is
blowing strong but there are not enough turbines to capture it.
You need BOTH wind AND turbines.

Scenario 4: Start with few turbines and build up. Even at 100
percent turbines, if the wind drops tomorrow, reliability drops
with it.

The word you need to know is INTERMITTENT. It means not constant.
Wind is intermittent. It blows sometimes and not others. Cities
need power ALL the time. That mismatch is the core challenge.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

## Step 4: REVISE & EXTEND — Play, Research, Expand

### Text Editor

```
SET THE CONDITIONS — MAKE YOUR MODEL SMARTER

Your model works, but something isn't quite right. When Wind Speed
is at 100%, Electricity Generated shoots up — even when it shouldn't!

In the real world, Wind Speed only affects Electricity Generated when Number of Turbines
is active. Without Number of Turbines, Wind Speed alone can't change Electricity Generated.
Let's teach your model this rule.

Task A: FIND THE PROBLEM
   • Run your simulation and watch Electricity Generated
   • Notice how Wind Speed pushes Electricity Generated up even when
     Number of Turbines is OFF — that's not right!

Task B: ADD THE RULE
   • Click on the arrow from Wind Speed to Electricity Generated
   • In the toolbar that pops up, click "Conditions"
   • A panel opens. Set: IF Number of Turbines is ON
   • Click "Save Conditions"
   • This tells the model: "Wind Speed only affects Electricity Generated
     when Number of Turbines is active"

Task C: TEST YOUR SMARTER MODEL
   • Re-run the simulation
   • Try different combinations of Wind Speed and Number of Turbines
   • Does the model match the real world now?

Task D: ADD ANOTHER RULE
   • Click on the arrow from Number of Turbines to Electricity Generated
   • Click "Conditions"
   • Set: IF Wind Speed is ON
   • Click "Save Conditions"
   • Number of Turbines only affects Electricity Generated when
     Wind Speed is active

Task E: ADD ANOTHER RULE
   • Click on the arrow from City Energy Demand to Grid Reliability
   • Click "Conditions"
   • Set: IF Electricity Generated is ON
   • Click "Save Conditions"
   • City Energy Demand only affects Grid Reliability when
     Electricity Generated is active

Your model is SMARTER now — it knows the rules!

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

NOW LET'S PLAY AND EXPLORE

YOUR MODEL WORKS — NOW SOLVE THE PROBLEM

You discovered wind's biggest challenge: intermittency. The wind
does not blow constantly. But the city needs power constantly.
So how do we solve it?

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

   • Can 100% turbines overcome 0% wind?
   • What wind speed is the minimum for reliable power?
   • At what point does adding more turbines stop helping?

3. WHAT IS MISSING? (Solutions!)
   • Battery Storage — save extra energy from windy days
   • Solar Panels — sunny when not windy, windy when not sunny
   • Fossil Fuel Backup — reliable but produces emissions
   • Smart Grid — computers that switch sources automatically

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

RESEARCH DIRECTIONS:

QUESTIONS TO INVESTIGATE:
   • How does Denmark get over 50% of its electricity from wind?
   • What are the largest wind farms in the world?
   • How do battery storage systems work?
   • Why are offshore wind farms more reliable than onshore ones?

RELIABLE SOURCES:
   • Department of Energy: energy.gov/eere/wind
   • EIA: eia.gov/energyexplained/wind

ADD TO YOUR MODEL:
   • Pick ONE solution component (batteries, solar, backup)
   • Add it and connect it — how does it improve reliability?
   • Run the simulation with your solution on a calm day
   • Does it fix the problem?
```

### Video Script

```
"You found the problem. Wind is intermittent. Now let us talk
about SOLUTIONS. Because real engineers are not giving up on wind
— they are solving the intermittency problem.

First — tell the story. Explain to someone why a city cannot rely
on wind alone. Use the word intermittent.

Second — break the system. Set turbines to maximum but wind to
zero. What happens? That proves the fundamental problem.

Third — solve the problem. This is where it gets exciting.

Battery Storage. On windy days, turbines produce MORE electricity
than the city needs. What if you could SAVE that extra energy in
giant batteries? Then on calm days, you use the batteries. It is
like a piggy bank for energy.

Solar Panels. Here is something interesting: often when it is NOT
windy, it IS sunny. And when it IS windy, it might be cloudy.
Wind and solar complement each other.

Smart Grid. Computers that automatically switch between energy
sources — wind when it is blowing, solar when the sun is out,
batteries when neither is available, and a small gas backup for
emergencies.

Add ONE of these solutions to your model. Connect it. Test it.
Does it improve Grid Reliability on a calm day?

This is exactly what energy engineers around the world are working
on right now. The future of energy is not one source — it is a
SMART MIX.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format aligned to 4-ESS3-1 and 4-PS3-1.

---

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.1 (Determine components of a system) + DCI ESS3.A (Natural Resources) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A student is building a model of wind energy for a city. She sorts five components into inputs and results. Which list correctly sorts the components?

A. External: Wind Speed, Number of Turbines — Internal: Electricity Generated, City Energy Demand, Grid Reliability
B. External: Wind Speed, Grid Reliability — Internal: Number of Turbines, Electricity Generated, City Energy Demand
C. External: Wind Speed, Electricity Generated — Internal: Number of Turbines, City Energy Demand, Grid Reliability
D. External: Number of Turbines, City Energy Demand — Internal: Wind Speed, Electricity Generated, Grid Reliability

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: Wind Speed is external because weather controls it. Number of Turbines is external because humans choose how many to build. Electricity Generated, City Energy Demand, and Grid Reliability are internal outcomes of the system.
---

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships among components) + DCI ESS3.A (Natural Resources) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

In the wind energy model, City Energy Demand has a NEGATIVE relationship with Grid Reliability. A student is confused because they think more demand should make the grid work harder and become more reliable.

Which explanation best supports why this relationship is negative?

A. Demand and reliability are not actually connected in this model.
B. High demand means the wind blows less, so turbines produce less.
C. The city is using too much electricity, which breaks the turbines.
D. When demand increases without an increase in supply, the gap between what is needed and what is available grows, making blackouts more likely.

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: Grid reliability depends on supply MATCHING demand. When demand increases but supply stays the same, the grid becomes strained because there is not enough electricity for everyone. This is why demand has a negative relationship with reliability — more demand without more supply equals less reliability.
---

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships among components) + DCI PS3.A (Definitions of Energy) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A city has 100 wind turbines. On Monday, wind speed is 80% and grid reliability is high. On Tuesday, wind speed drops to 15% but the city still has the same 100 turbines.

Based on the model, what would most likely happen to grid reliability on Tuesday?

A. Reliability would drop dramatically because the turbines produce very little electricity without wind, but the city still needs the same amount of power.
B. Reliability would not change because turbines store energy from windy days.
C. Reliability would stay the same because the same number of turbines are still there.
D. Reliability would increase because the turbines are not working as hard.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: Wind turbines only generate electricity when the wind blows. On a calm day, even 100 turbines produce almost nothing. But the city's demand does not change — hospitals, homes, and schools still need power. When supply drops far below demand, grid reliability crashes. This is the intermittency problem.
---

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to explain/predict events) + DCI ESS3.A (Natural Resources) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A wind farm operator records data for one week:

| Day | Wind Speed | Electricity Generated | Grid Status |
|-----|-----------|----------------------|-------------|
| Mon | Strong | Very High | Reliable |
| Tue | Moderate | Moderate | Reliable |
| Wed | Calm | Very Low | Blackouts |
| Thu | Calm | Very Low | Blackouts |
| Fri | Strong | Very High | Reliable |

Which conclusion from the model best explains this data?

A. The city used too much electricity on Wednesday and Thursday.
B. Wind farms only work three days per week on average.
C. Wind energy is intermittent — on calm days, turbines produce almost no electricity, and when supply drops below demand, blackouts occur.
D. The turbines broke on Wednesday and Thursday and were fixed by Friday.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: The data shows a clear pattern: when wind is strong, generation is high and the grid is reliable. When wind is calm, generation drops and blackouts occur. The city's demand did not change — the wind did. This is the intermittency problem that the model predicts.
---

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to predict a scientific event) + DCI ESS3.A (Natural Resources) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A city wants to solve the intermittency problem with its wind farm. Based on the model, which solution would MOST improve grid reliability on calm days?

A. Build more wind turbines so there are 200 instead of 100.
B. Add large battery storage systems that save excess electricity from windy days to use on calm days.
C. Move the city to a windier location.
D. Ask residents to use less electricity on calm days.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: The model shows that more turbines help on windy days but produce nothing on calm days — so A does not solve calm-day problems. Battery storage directly addresses intermittency by saving windy-day surplus for calm-day shortfalls, keeping supply above demand at all times.
