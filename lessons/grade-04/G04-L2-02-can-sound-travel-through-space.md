## Platform

### Title
**Can Sound Travel Through Space? Why Astronauts Need Radios and Explosions Are Silent in a Vacuum**

### Overview
Students model why sound cannot travel through the vacuum of space, debunking every space movie they have ever seen. By building a cause-and-effect model of vibration strength, medium density, wave speed, sound loudness, and wave absorption, students discover that sound waves require molecules to travel and that denser materials transmit sound faster and farther. This lesson connects wave science to real-world engineering challenges like submarine communication and astronaut radios.

4th

### NGSS Standard
**4-PS4-1**: Develop a model of waves to describe patterns in terms of amplitude and wavelength.
**4-PS4-3**: Generate and compare multiple solutions that use light or sound to transfer information.

### Learning Objectives
- Explain how sound waves require a medium (air, water, or solids) to travel
- Model how vibration strength, medium density, wave speed, sound loudness, and wave absorption interact
- Describe why sound travels at different speeds through different materials
- Use evidence from a model to explain why sound cannot travel through the vacuum of space

### Component List (Starting Model: 5 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Vibration Strength | External | How hard the sound source vibrates — like the difference between tapping a drum gently vs. hitting it hard |
| Medium Density | External | How tightly packed the molecules are in the material sound is traveling through — air is thin, water is dense, steel is very dense |
| Wave Speed | Internal | How fast the sound wave moves through the medium — denser materials with tightly packed molecules pass vibrations faster |
| Sound Loudness | Internal | How loud the sound is when it reaches the listener — depends on both the source strength and how much energy is absorbed along the way |
| Wave Absorption | Internal | How much sound energy gets soaked up by the medium as the wave passes through — some materials absorb more than others |

• Your cursor is now ready to draw relationship arrows

Task B: DRAW YOUR RELATIONSHIPS
• Click on "Vibration Strength" and drag an arrow to "Sound Loudness"
• Click on "Medium Density" and drag an arrow to "Wave Speed"
• Click on "Medium Density" and drag an arrow to "Wave Absorption"
• Click on "Wave Absorption" and drag an arrow to "Sound Loudness"
• Click on "Vibration Strength" and drag an arrow to "Wave Speed"

Task C: SET POSITIVE OR NEGATIVE
• For each connection, ask: "When this goes UP, does the other go
  UP or DOWN?"

  ○ Vibration Strength → Sound Loudness = POSITIVE (+)
    Stronger vibration = LOUDER sound!

  ○ Medium Density → Wave Speed = POSITIVE (+)
    Denser material = FASTER sound travel!

  ○ Medium Density → Wave Absorption = NEGATIVE (−)
    Denser material = LESS absorption (sound stays stronger)!

  ○ Wave Absorption → Sound Loudness = NEGATIVE (−)
    More absorption = QUIETER sound (energy soaked up)!

  ○ Vibration Strength → Wave Speed = POSITIVE (+)
    Stronger vibrations push through slightly faster!

Task D: CHECK YOUR MODEL
• You should have 5 arrows total
• 3 positive relationships, 2 negative relationships
• Two different paths affect Sound Loudness!

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Notice the two NEGATIVE arrows? Wave Absorption is the key.
Materials that absorb sound make it quieter. But what happens
when there IS no material at all?
```

### Video Script

```
"Your pieces are on the board, but they are not talking to each
other yet. Time to draw the connections — the physics that makes
sound work... or not work.

Click the 'Connect' icon in the top left corner.

First connection: 'Vibration Strength' to 'Sound Loudness.'
Stronger vibration means louder sound. That is POSITIVE. Simple.

Second: 'Medium Density' to 'Wave Speed.' Here is something cool.
Sound travels FASTER in denser materials. In air, sound moves at
about 343 meters per second. In water? Over 1,400 meters per second.
In steel? Almost 6,000! More density, more speed. POSITIVE.

Third: 'Medium Density' to 'Wave Absorption.' This one is tricky.
Denser materials actually absorb LESS sound. In air, molecules are
spread out, and sound energy scatters and gets soaked up. In water
and solids, molecules are packed tight and pass energy along
efficiently. More density, LESS absorption. That is NEGATIVE.

Fourth: 'Wave Absorption' to 'Sound Loudness.' When the material
soaks up sound energy, less reaches your ears. More absorption
means QUIETER sound. Another NEGATIVE.

Fifth: 'Vibration Strength' to 'Wave Speed.' Stronger vibrations
push through a bit faster. POSITIVE but a smaller effect.

Look at your model. Sound Loudness has TWO paths feeding into it.
One direct from vibration strength. One indirect through absorption.
The medium determines which path wins.

But here is the real question: what happens when Medium Density is
ZERO? When there are no molecules at all? That is what space is.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"

in a vacuum.

Space movies have been lying to you. In space, no one can hear
you scream. No one can hear explosions. No one can hear anything.

That is why astronauts use RADIOS. Radio waves are electromagnetic
waves, not sound waves. They do NOT need molecules. They travel
through vacuum just fine.

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format. Each item is three-dimensional, assessing a Science and Engineering Practice (SEP), Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and Crosscutting Concept (CCC) simultaneously, aligned to 4-PS4-1 and 4-PS4-3.

---

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.1 (Determine components of a system) + DCI PS4.A (Wave Properties) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A student is building a model of how sound travels. She has five components: Vibration Strength, Medium Density, Wave Speed, Sound Loudness, and Wave Absorption. She sorts them into things we control (external) and things determined by physics (internal).

Which list correctly sorts the components?

A. External: Vibration Strength, Wave Speed — Internal: Medium Density, Sound Loudness, Wave Absorption
B. External: Vibration Strength, Medium Density — Internal: Wave Speed, Sound Loudness, Wave Absorption
C. External: Sound Loudness, Medium Density — Internal: Vibration Strength, Wave Speed, Wave Absorption
D. External: Vibration Strength, Wave Absorption — Internal: Medium Density, Wave Speed, Sound Loudness

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Vibration Strength is external because the sound source (a person, instrument, or machine) controls how hard it vibrates. Medium Density is external because the environment determines what material sound travels through. Wave Speed, Sound Loudness, and Wave Absorption are internal because they are determined by the physics of how the wave interacts with the medium. If you chose A, Wave Speed is determined by physics, not by our choice. If you chose C, Sound Loudness is a result, not something we directly control. If you chose D, Wave Absorption depends on the medium, not on our direct control.

---

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships among components) + DCI PS4.A (Wave Properties) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

In a sound model, a student claims that when Medium Density increases, Wave Absorption decreases. Another student says that does not make sense because denser materials should absorb more.

Which explanation best supports why the relationship between Medium Density and Wave Absorption is negative?

A. Denser materials have more molecules, so they absorb more sound energy as the wave passes through.
B. Denser materials have tightly packed molecules that pass energy along efficiently, absorbing LESS of it along the way.
C. All materials absorb the same amount of sound regardless of density.
D. Denser materials are always louder because they vibrate more.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: In denser materials, molecules are packed close together and efficiently pass vibrations from one to the next without absorbing much energy. In air (low density), molecules are spread out and scatter sound energy, absorbing more of it. This is why whale songs travel hundreds of miles through dense water but a shout barely carries across a field in air. If you chose A, this seems logical but is actually backward. If you chose C, absorption varies dramatically by material. If you chose D, density does not make materials vibrate more.

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships among components) + DCI PS4.A (Wave Properties) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A student builds a sound model and sets Medium Density to 0% to represent the vacuum of space. Vibration Strength is set to 90% — the loudest possible source.

Based on the model, what would most likely happen?

A. Sound Loudness would be very high because the vibration is so strong.
B. Wave Speed would be moderate because the strong vibration pushes through empty space.
C. All internal components (Wave Speed, Sound Loudness, Wave Absorption) would drop to zero because there are no molecules to vibrate.
D. Sound would travel faster in vacuum because there are no molecules blocking the way.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: Sound is a vibration that passes from molecule to molecule. In a vacuum, there are no molecules at all. No matter how strongly the source vibrates, the vibration has nothing to push on and cannot travel. All internal components drop to zero — Wave Speed is zero, Sound Loudness is zero. The source vibrates but the sound goes nowhere. If you chose A, vibration strength does not matter without a medium. If you chose B, without molecules, there is nothing to push. If you chose D, molecules do not "block" sound — they ARE how sound travels.

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to explain/predict events) + DCI PS4.A (Wave Properties) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

Scientists measured how fast sound travels through three different materials:

| Material | Speed of Sound |
|----------|---------------|
| Air | 343 meters per second |
| Water | 1,480 meters per second |
| Steel | 5,960 meters per second |

Which observation from the model best explains this data?

A. Steel is the strongest material, so sound pushes through it the hardest.
B. Steel has the most tightly packed molecules, so vibrations pass from molecule to molecule the fastest.
C. Air has the most molecules, so sound has more things to bump into and slows down.
D. Water is between air and steel in every way, so its speed is in the middle.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Sound speed depends on how close together the molecules are. In steel, molecules are packed extremely tightly — each molecule barely has to move before bumping into the next one, so the vibration chain reaction happens very quickly. In air, molecules are far apart, so each one must travel farther before hitting the next. More density equals faster molecular bumping equals faster sound. If you chose A, strength is not the relevant property — density of molecular packing is. If you chose C, air has the FEWEST molecules per volume, not the most. If you chose D, this restates the data without explaining it.

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to predict a scientific event) + DCI PS4.C (Information Transfer) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

Astronauts on the International Space Station need to communicate during spacewalks outside the station. They cannot use sound because space is a vacuum. Based on the model, which solution would best allow astronauts to communicate?

A. Shout louder so the vibrations are strong enough to travel through empty space.
B. Use radio waves, which are electromagnetic waves that do not need molecules to travel.
C. Connect a long tube between the astronauts so sound can travel through the air inside.
D. Wait until they are close enough that sound can jump the small gap between them.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: The model showed that sound cannot travel through a vacuum regardless of vibration strength, so shouting louder would not work. Radio waves are electromagnetic waves — a completely different type of wave that does NOT need molecules and can travel through vacuum at the speed of light. This is exactly what NASA uses. If you chose A, no amount of vibration strength can overcome zero medium density. If you chose C, while this would technically work if the tube were sealed with air inside, it is not practical for spacewalks. If you chose D, sound cannot jump any gap in a vacuum, no matter how small — there must be molecules present.
