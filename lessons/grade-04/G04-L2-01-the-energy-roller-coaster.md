## Platform

### Title
**The Energy Roller Coaster: Where Does Speed Come From?**

### Overview
Students model why a roller coaster eventually slows down and stops, even though energy is never destroyed. By building a cause-and-effect model of hill height, track friction, potential energy, kinetic energy, and heat loss, students discover how energy transforms from one form to another and how friction diverts motion energy into heat. This lesson serves as a foundation for understanding energy conservation and transformation in everyday systems.

### Grade
4th

### NGSS Standard
**4-PS3-1**: Use evidence to construct an explanation relating the speed of an object to the energy of that object.
**4-PS3-4**: Apply scientific ideas to design, test, and refine a device that converts energy from one form to another.

### Learning Objectives
- Explain how potential energy at the top of a hill converts to kinetic energy at the bottom
- Model how hill height, track friction, potential energy, kinetic energy, and heat loss interact in a system
- Describe how friction causes energy to leave the motion system as heat, reducing speed
- Use evidence from a model to explain why roller coasters slow down even though energy is conserved

### Component List (Starting Model: 5 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Hill Height | External | How tall the starting hill of the roller coaster is — taller hills store more potential energy at the top |
| Track Friction | External | How rough or smooth the track surface is — rougher tracks create more friction between the car and the rails |
| Potential Energy | Internal | The stored energy the coaster has at the top of the hill because of its height — waiting to be converted into motion |
| Kinetic Energy | Internal | The energy of the coaster's motion at the bottom of the hill — how fast and powerful the movement is |
| Heat Loss | Internal | Energy that gets transformed into heat because of friction between the coaster wheels and the track surface |

## Step 2: ESTABLISH — Connect the Relationships

### Text Editor

```
TIME TO DRAW THE ENERGY FLOW

Those five components do not just sit there — energy FLOWS between
them. When one changes, others change too. Let us map the connections.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Task A: OPEN CONNECTION MODE
• Click the "Connect" icon in the TOP LEFT corner of your screen
• Your cursor is now ready to draw relationship arrows

Task B: DRAW YOUR RELATIONSHIPS
• Click on "Hill Height" and drag an arrow to "Potential Energy"
• Click on "Potential Energy" and drag an arrow to "Kinetic Energy"
• Click on "Track Friction" and drag an arrow to "Heat Loss"
• Click on "Heat Loss" and drag an arrow to "Kinetic Energy"

Task C: SET POSITIVE OR NEGATIVE
• Look at the +/- toggle in the TOP LEFT corner
• For each connection, ask: "When this goes UP, does the other go UP or DOWN?"

  ○ Hill Height → Potential Energy = POSITIVE (+)
    Taller hill = MORE stored energy at the top!

  ○ Potential Energy → Kinetic Energy = POSITIVE (+)
    More stored energy = MORE speed at the bottom!

  ○ Track Friction → Heat Loss = POSITIVE (+)
    Rougher track = MORE energy lost as heat!

  ○ Heat Loss → Kinetic Energy = NEGATIVE (−)
    More heat loss = LESS energy left for speed!

Task D: CHECK YOUR MODEL
• You should have 4 arrows total
• 3 positive relationships, 1 negative relationship
• This is your energy system!

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Notice the NEGATIVE arrow? That is the key. Heat Loss STEALS energy
from Kinetic Energy. Friction is the energy thief!
```

### Video Script

```
"Your pieces are on the board, but they are not talking to each other
yet. Time to draw the arrows — the energy flow lines that make this
a SYSTEM.

Click the 'Connect' icon in the top left corner. Now you are in
connection mode.

First connection: Click on 'Hill Height' and drag an arrow to
'Potential Energy.' A taller hill means MORE potential energy stored
at the top. When one goes up, the other goes up. That is POSITIVE.

Second connection: 'Potential Energy' to 'Kinetic Energy.' As the
coaster rolls down, stored energy CONVERTS to motion energy. More
potential energy at the top means more kinetic energy at the bottom.
Another POSITIVE relationship.

Third: 'Track Friction' to 'Heat Loss.' A rougher track creates more
friction. More friction means more energy gets transformed into heat.
POSITIVE — both go up together.

Now here is the key one. Fourth: 'Heat Loss' to 'Kinetic Energy.'

When this goes up, does Kinetic Energy go up or down?

Think about it. If MORE energy is being lost as heat, there is LESS
energy available for motion. Heat Loss goes up, Kinetic Energy goes
DOWN. That is a NEGATIVE relationship.

Click the minus sign. This negative arrow is the whole answer to our
mystery. Friction transforms motion energy into heat, and that heat
cannot push the coaster forward anymore.

Look at your model now. Energy flows from height to potential energy
to kinetic energy. But friction creates a LEAK — energy escaping as
heat. That leak is why the coaster slows down.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

### Text Editor

```
  is tall and the track is smooth?

• How much Heat Loss is there?

• Keep Hill Height at 80% but increase Track Friction to 80%

  How will that affect Kinetic Energy?

OBSERVE:
• Compare the KE line to Scenario 1
• Where did all that energy go?

Task C: SCENARIO 3 — SMOOTH TRACK, SHORT HILL
• Set Hill Height to 20% and Track Friction to 10%
• Will a short smooth hill give the coaster enough energy to go fast?

• Set Hill Height to 30% and Track Friction to 90%
• Can the coaster even make it through the track?

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

• Which scenario was the worst? Why?
• What happens when you have a lot of starting energy but also a lot
  of friction?
```

### Video Script

```
"You have built the machine. You have wired the connections. Now let
us flip the switch and see if this model works like a real roller
coaster.

Scenario 1: Smooth Track, Tall Hill. Set Hill Height to 80 percent
and Track Friction to just 10 percent. Click Play.

Watch the graph. Potential Energy starts high because the hill is
tall. As the coaster rolls down, that energy converts to Kinetic
Energy. And because friction is low, Heat Loss stays small. The
coaster ZOOMS. Almost all the stored energy becomes speed.

Now Scenario 2: Same tall hill, but crank Track Friction up to 80
percent. Watch what happens.

See that? Heat Loss is climbing. And as Heat Loss rises... Kinetic
Energy drops. The coaster starts with the same amount of energy but
friction is STEALING it, turning motion into heat. The coaster is
noticeably slower even though it started from the same height.

Scenario 3: Short hill, smooth track. Hill Height at 20, Friction
at 10. Not much energy to start with, so even though friction is
low, the coaster does not have much speed.

Scenario 4: The worst case. Short hill AND rough track. Hill Height
at 30, Track Friction at 90. Almost all the small amount of starting
energy gets transformed into heat. The coaster barely moves.

Now you can see the answer to our puzzle. The coaster stops because
friction transforms kinetic energy into heat at every point along the
track. The energy is not destroyed — it is just changed into a form
that cannot push the coaster forward anymore.

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"
```

```
• "How is rubbing your hands together related to what happens between

• "If you could build a frictionless track, would the coaster ride
   forever?"
```

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format. Each item is three-dimensional, assessing a Science and Engineering Practice (SEP), Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and Crosscutting Concept (CCC) simultaneously, aligned to 4-PS3-1 and 4-PS3-4.

---

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.1 (Determine components of a system) + DCI PS3.A (Definitions of Energy) + CCC5 (Energy and Matter)

A student is building a model of a roller coaster energy system. She has five components: Hill Height, Track Friction, Potential Energy, Kinetic Energy, and Heat Loss. She needs to sort them into two groups: things that are design choices made before the ride (external) and things that happen automatically during the ride (internal).

Which list correctly sorts the components?

A. External: Hill Height, Potential Energy — Internal: Track Friction, Kinetic Energy, Heat Loss
B. External: Hill Height, Track Friction — Internal: Potential Energy, Kinetic Energy, Heat Loss
C. External: Kinetic Energy, Hill Height — Internal: Potential Energy, Track Friction, Heat Loss
D. External: Hill Height, Heat Loss — Internal: Track Friction, Potential Energy, Kinetic Energy

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Hill Height and Track Friction are external because they are design choices made before the coaster starts — engineers decide how tall to build the hill and what material to use for the track surface. Potential Energy, Kinetic Energy, and Heat Loss are internal because they are results that happen automatically inside the system in response to those design choices. If you chose A, Potential Energy is not a design choice — it results from the height of the hill. If you chose C, Kinetic Energy is a result of the ride, not a design choice. If you chose D, Heat Loss happens because of friction during the ride, not as a design decision.

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships among components) + DCI PS3.B (Conservation of Energy) + CCC5 (Energy and Matter)

In a roller coaster energy model, a student draws an arrow from Heat Loss to Kinetic Energy and labels it as a negative relationship. This means that when Heat Loss increases, Kinetic Energy decreases. Another student disagrees and says the arrow should be positive because both involve energy.

Which explanation best supports why the relationship between Heat Loss and Kinetic Energy is negative?

A. When more energy becomes heat, there is less energy available for motion, so Kinetic Energy decreases.
B. When Heat Loss increases, the coaster gets hotter and goes faster because heat is energy.
C. Heat Loss and Kinetic Energy are the same type of energy, so they always increase together.
D. When the track gets hotter from friction, the coaster slides faster on the warm surface.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: Energy is conserved — the total amount stays the same. When more of that energy is converted to heat through friction, less energy remains in the form of kinetic energy (motion). This is why Heat Loss and Kinetic Energy have a negative relationship — they are competing for the same total energy. If you chose B, while heat is indeed energy, heat energy escapes into the air and cannot push the coaster forward. If you chose C, they are different forms of energy and they move in opposite directions. If you chose D, a hotter track does not make the coaster faster — the heat represents energy that has already left the motion system.

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships among components) + DCI PS3.A (Definitions of Energy) + CCC5 (Energy and Matter)

A student builds a roller coaster model and runs two scenarios. In Scenario 1, Hill Height is at 80% and Track Friction is at 10%. In Scenario 2, Hill Height is still at 80% but Track Friction is increased to 80%. In both scenarios, the coaster starts from the same height.

Based on the model, what would most likely happen in Scenario 2 compared to Scenario 1?

A. Kinetic Energy would be higher because more friction creates more energy.
B. Kinetic Energy would be the same because the hill height did not change.
C. Kinetic Energy would be lower because more energy is lost as heat from the higher friction.
D. Potential Energy would decrease because friction affects how much energy the hill stores.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: Both scenarios start with the same Potential Energy because the hill height is the same. But in Scenario 2, the rougher track creates much more friction, which transforms more kinetic energy into heat. Since total energy is conserved, more Heat Loss means less Kinetic Energy — the coaster is noticeably slower. If you chose A, friction does not create energy — it transforms existing kinetic energy into heat. If you chose B, while the starting energy is the same, friction diverts more of it away from motion. If you chose D, friction does not affect Potential Energy — that depends only on height.

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to explain/predict events) + DCI PS3.B (Conservation of Energy) + CCC5 (Energy and Matter)

Engineers tested a roller coaster on four different track surfaces. They kept the hill height the same for all tests and measured the coaster's speed at the bottom. Here are the results:

| Track Surface | Coaster Speed at Bottom |
|---------------|------------------------|
| Polished Steel | Very Fast |
| Smooth Plastic | Fast |
| Wood | Medium |
| Sandpaper | Very Slow |

Which observation best supports the model's prediction that more friction leads to more heat loss and less kinetic energy?

A. The polished steel track had the fastest coaster because steel is a stronger material than wood.
B. The sandpaper track had the slowest coaster because sandpaper creates the most friction, converting the most energy to heat.
C. The wood track was medium because wood is a natural material and nature is balanced.
D. All four tracks showed different speeds because the coaster was pushed differently each time.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: The sandpaper surface creates the most friction, which means the most kinetic energy is transformed into heat as the coaster rolls. With more energy lost as heat, less energy remains as kinetic energy, so the coaster moves the slowest. This perfectly matches the model's negative relationship between Heat Loss and Kinetic Energy. If you chose A, the speed difference is about friction, not material strength. If you chose C, the medium speed is because wood has moderate friction, not because of "balance." If you chose D, the problem states the hill height was the same, meaning the starting push (potential energy) was identical — only friction changed.

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to predict a scientific event) + DCI PS3.B (Conservation of Energy) + CCC5 (Energy and Matter)

A theme park wants their roller coaster to go faster at the bottom of the first hill. The model shows that Hill Height positively affects Potential Energy, Potential Energy positively affects Kinetic Energy, Track Friction positively affects Heat Loss, and Heat Loss negatively affects Kinetic Energy. The engineers cannot change the hill height because of space limits.

Based on the model, which action would most likely increase the coaster's speed at the bottom?

A. Add more cars to the roller coaster to make it heavier.
B. Reduce Track Friction by using smoother rails and lubricated wheels.
C. Add a loop after the first hill to give the coaster more speed.
D. Paint the track a darker color so it absorbs more sunlight.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Since the engineers cannot increase Hill Height (and therefore cannot increase Potential Energy), the only way to increase Kinetic Energy is to reduce Heat Loss. The model shows Track Friction positively affects Heat Loss, and Heat Loss negatively affects Kinetic Energy. By reducing Track Friction (smoother rails, lubricated wheels), less energy is lost as heat, and more energy stays as motion — the coaster goes faster. If you chose A, adding weight is not in this model and would actually increase friction. If you chose C, a loop does not add energy — it uses existing kinetic energy and the coaster must slow down to go through it. If you chose D, track color does not affect friction or energy in this system.
