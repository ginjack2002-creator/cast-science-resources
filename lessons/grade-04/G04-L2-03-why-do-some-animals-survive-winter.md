## Platform

### Title
**Why Do Some Animals Survive Winter? The Hidden Science of Insulation, Metabolism, and Staying Alive**

### Overview
Students model how arctic animals survive freezing temperatures that would be dangerous for humans in minutes. By building a cause-and-effect model of temperature, food availability, body insulation, metabolic rate, and survival chance, students discover the energy balance that determines whether an animal thrives, migrates, hibernates, or perishes in winter. This lesson connects animal structures to survival functions and explores how evolution has produced remarkable solutions to the cold challenge.

### NGSS Standard
**4-LS1-1**: Construct an argument that plants and animals have internal and external structures that function to support survival, growth, behavior, and reproduction.
**4-LS1-2**: Use a model to describe that animals receive different types of information through their senses, process the information in different ways, and respond to the information in different ways.

### Learning Objectives
- Explain how body insulation (fur, fat, feathers) helps animals survive cold temperatures
- Model how temperature, food availability, body insulation, metabolic rate, and survival chance interact
- Describe how metabolic rate increases in cold temperatures and why that can be dangerous without enough food
- Use evidence from a model to explain why some animals thrive in winter while others migrate or hibernate

### Component List (Starting Model: 5 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Temperature | External | The environmental temperature — how cold or warm it is outside, which the animal cannot control |
| Food Availability | External | How much food is accessible in the environment — less food is available in winter for most animals |
| Body Insulation | Internal | How well the animal's body prevents heat loss — thick fur, blubber, dense feathers all provide insulation |
| Metabolic Rate | Internal | How fast the animal's body burns energy to maintain body temperature — colder conditions require faster energy burning |
| Survival Chance | Internal | The overall probability that the animal will survive the season — depends on the balance between energy needs and energy supply |

• Click on "Body Insulation" and drag an arrow to "Metabolic Rate"
• Click on "Metabolic Rate" and drag an arrow to "Survival Chance"
• Click on "Food Availability" and drag an arrow to "Body Insulation"

Task C: SET POSITIVE OR NEGATIVE
  ○ Temperature → Metabolic Rate = NEGATIVE (−)
    When temperature DROPS, metabolic rate goes UP!
    The body works harder to generate heat in the cold.

  ○ Food Availability → Survival Chance = POSITIVE (+)
    More food = BETTER survival! Energy to keep going.

  ○ Body Insulation → Metabolic Rate = NEGATIVE (−)
    Better insulation = LOWER metabolic rate!
    Less heat escaping means the body does not work as hard.

  ○ Metabolic Rate → Survival Chance = NEGATIVE (−)
    HIGHER metabolic rate = LOWER survival!
    Burning energy too fast without food is fatal.

  ○ Food Availability → Body Insulation = POSITIVE (+)
    More food = better insulation! Animals build fat stores.

• You should have 5 arrows total

```
"Your pieces are on the board. Now let us wire the survival system.
This one has more negative arrows than any model we have built so

energy, more survival. POSITIVE. Simple.

Third: 'Body Insulation' to 'Metabolic Rate.' This is the key.

the body does not have to work as hard. So better insulation LOWERS
metabolic rate. NEGATIVE.

HIGH metabolic rate is actually DANGEROUS. Why? Because burning
energy fast means you need lots of food. If food is scarce, you
burn through your reserves and starve. High metabolic rate LOWERS
survival. NEGATIVE.

Fifth: 'Food Availability' to 'Body Insulation.' More food means
the animal can build fat stores — both energy reserves and physical
insulation. POSITIVE.

Look at your model. It is a battle of forces. Cold pushes metabolic
rate up. Insulation pushes it down. Food determines whether the
energy supply can keep up with the demand.

This is the survival equation that every arctic animal has evolved
to solve.

• Watch Metabolic Rate spike as the temperature drops
• Watch Survival Chance drop — why?

• Set Temperature to 10% but Food Availability to 80%
• Can abundant food make up for extreme cold?

• Run the harsh winter scenario
• What happens when Body Insulation is high vs. low?

### Video Script

```
"Your survival model works. You know that insulation reduces
metabolic rate, food supplies energy, and the balance between
energy demand and energy supply determines survival. But real
animal survival involves way more factors.

First — tell the story. Explain to someone next to you why
arctic foxes survive blizzards. Use the words insulation,
metabolic rate, and energy balance.

Second — break the system. Try to figure out whether insulation
alone can save an animal with zero food. What does your model say?

Third — what is MISSING?

Body Size. Arctic animals tend to be LARGE. Why? Larger animals
have less surface area compared to their volume, which means they
lose heat slower. A mouse loses heat much faster than a polar bear.

Group Behavior. Emperor penguins huddle together in groups of
thousands. The penguins in the middle are protected by the ones
on the outside. They rotate positions so no one freezes.

Hibernation. What if you could just... turn your metabolism almost
OFF? Ground squirrels drop their heart rate from 200 beats per
minute to about 5. Their body temperature drops to just above
freezing. They survive months without eating.

Research ONE new factor. Add it to your model. Test it.

Does your expanded model explain even more about animal survival?

Now it's your turn to ModelIt!"

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format aligned to 4-LS1-1 and 4-LS1-2.

---

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.1 (Determine components of a system) + DCI LS1.A (Structure and Function) + CCC4 (Systems and System Models)

A student is building a model of how animals survive winter. She has five components: Temperature, Food Availability, Body Insulation, Metabolic Rate, and Survival Chance. She needs to sort them into environmental conditions (external) and body responses (internal).

Which list correctly sorts the components?

A. External: Temperature, Body Insulation — Internal: Food Availability, Metabolic Rate, Survival Chance
B. External: Temperature, Food Availability — Internal: Body Insulation, Metabolic Rate, Survival Chance
C. External: Temperature, Metabolic Rate — Internal: Food Availability, Body Insulation, Survival Chance
D. External: Survival Chance, Temperature — Internal: Food Availability, Body Insulation, Metabolic Rate

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Temperature and Food Availability are external because they are environmental conditions that exist independently of the animal. Body Insulation, Metabolic Rate, and Survival Chance are internal because they are properties of or responses within the animal's body. If you chose A, Body Insulation is part of the animal's body, not an environmental condition. If you chose C, Metabolic Rate is how fast the animal's body burns energy, an internal response. If you chose D, Survival Chance is the result of the system, not an environmental input.

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships among components) + DCI LS1.A (Structure and Function) + CCC4 (Systems and System Models)

In an animal survival model, a student says that when Temperature drops (gets colder), Metabolic Rate increases. Another student says that does not make sense because cold slows everything down.

Which explanation best supports why the relationship between Temperature and Metabolic Rate is negative (they move in opposite directions)?

A. Cold temperatures slow down the animal's body, so metabolic rate decreases.
B. When it gets colder, the animal's body must work HARDER to generate heat, so it burns energy faster.
C. Temperature and Metabolic Rate are not connected because they are in different parts of the model.
D. Cold animals stop eating, which causes their metabolic rate to increase.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Warm-blooded animals must maintain a constant internal body temperature. When external temperature drops, the difference between body temperature and air temperature increases, and the body must burn more energy to generate enough heat to compensate. This is why metabolic rate goes UP when temperature goes DOWN — opposite directions make this a negative relationship. If you chose A, cold does not slow metabolic rate in warm-blooded animals — it increases it. If you chose C, they are directly connected. If you chose D, while cold may reduce food finding, the metabolic increase is caused by the body's need for more heat.

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.2 (Determine relationships among components) + DCI LS1.A (Structure and Function) + CCC4 (Systems and System Models)

A student runs two scenarios in the winter survival model. In Scenario 1, Temperature is 10% (very cold) and Food Availability is 20% (very scarce). In Scenario 2, Temperature is 10% (same cold) and Food Availability is 80% (abundant).

Based on the model, how would Survival Chance compare between the two scenarios?

A. Survival Chance would be the same because the temperature is identical in both scenarios.
B. Survival Chance would be higher in Scenario 2 because more food provides more energy to fuel the high metabolic rate.
C. Survival Chance would be lower in Scenario 2 because more food makes the animal heavier and slower.
D. Survival Chance would be higher in Scenario 1 because the animal would be forced to find creative solutions.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: In both scenarios, the cold temperature causes metabolic rate to spike — the body needs lots of energy to stay warm. In Scenario 1, with scarce food, the animal cannot refuel fast enough and burns through its reserves. In Scenario 2, abundant food provides enough energy to sustain the high metabolic rate, so survival improves dramatically. The model shows that food availability is critical when metabolic demands are high. If you chose A, temperature is only part of the equation — food matters enormously. If you chose C, this is not how the model works. If you chose D, desperation does not improve survival in this system.

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to explain/predict events) + DCI LS1.A (Structure and Function) + CCC4 (Systems and System Models)

An arctic fox has very thick fur while a desert fox has thin fur. Both are foxes, but they live in very different environments.

Which explanation from the model best describes why the arctic fox's thick fur helps it survive?

A. Thick fur makes the arctic fox look bigger to scare away predators.
B. Thick fur provides better insulation, which reduces metabolic rate so the fox burns less energy to stay warm.
C. Thick fur traps food close to the body so the fox always has something to eat.
D. Thick fur creates heat that warms the fox from the outside in.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: The model shows that Body Insulation has a negative relationship with Metabolic Rate — better insulation reduces how fast the body needs to burn energy. Thick fur traps air close to the body, slowing heat loss. With less heat escaping, the body does not need to generate as much heat, so metabolic rate stays lower. Lower metabolic rate means the fox needs less food to survive, which is critical when winter food is scarce. If you chose A, while fur may affect appearance, the survival benefit is about heat retention. If you chose C, fur does not trap food. If you chose D, fur does not create heat — it only prevents the fox's own body heat from escaping.

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.1.4 (Represent mechanisms to predict a scientific event) + DCI LS1.A (Structure and Function) + CCC4 (Systems and System Models)

A wildlife biologist is studying why a population of rabbits in a northern forest is declining during winters that are getting longer and colder due to climate change. The rabbits have moderate insulation and depend on grasses and shrubs that are buried under deep snow.

Based on the model, which combination of factors best explains why the rabbit population is declining?

A. Longer winters mean more time with low temperatures (increasing metabolic rate) AND deep snow reduces food availability — the energy equation becomes unsustainable.
B. The rabbits are declining because they do not like cold weather and choose to leave.
C. Longer winters give predators more time to hunt the rabbits.
D. The rabbits are declining because their fur changes color in winter and predators can see them better.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: The model shows that lower temperatures increase metabolic rate (the body burns energy faster) and that food availability directly affects survival. When winters are longer, rabbits spend more time with high metabolic rates while simultaneously having less access to food buried under deep snow. The energy supply (food) cannot keep up with the energy demand (high metabolic rate), and survival drops. If you chose B, rabbits do not choose to leave based on preference. If you chose C, while predation matters, the model specifically addresses the energy balance. If you chose D, while camouflage matters, the declining population is best explained by the metabolic-food imbalance.
