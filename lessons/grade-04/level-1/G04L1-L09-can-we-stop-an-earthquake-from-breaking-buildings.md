## Platform

### Title
**Can We Stop an Earthquake from Breaking Buildings? — Engineering Solutions to Protect People from Natural Hazards**

### Overview
Earthquake engineering is one of the most important applications of science to human safety. Engineers cannot prevent earthquakes, but they have developed remarkable technologies that allow buildings to survive even the most powerful shaking. Students investigate the driving question: How do engineers design buildings that can survive an earthquake without falling down? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

LEVER FRAMEWORK:
• Locate: Students identify earthquake strength, building flexibility, and damage level as the key components of the earthquake engineering system.
• Establish: Students determine that earthquake strength positively affects damage level (stronger quake = more damage), while building flexibility negatively affects damage level (more flexibility = less damage).
• Visualize: Students build a model showing how earthquake intensity interacts with building design to determine how much damage occurs.
• Evaluate: Students run scenarios comparing stiff vs flexible buildings in the same earthquake to see the dramatic difference in damage levels.
• Revise: Students add foundation depth or ground type to explore more complex factors in earthquake engineering.

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• How Earthquakes Damage Buildings:
  Earthquakes damage buildings through ground shaking, which causes structures to vibrate. If the shaking frequency matches the building's natural frequency (resonance), the vibrations amplify dramatically. Stiff, rigid buildings tend to crack and crumble because they resist the movement. The most dangerous situation is when heavy concrete buildings on soft soil experience a strong earthquake — the soft soil amplifies the shaking.

• Flexible Design Philosophy:
  Modern earthquake engineering follows the principle that buildings should bend, not break. Steel-frame buildings with cross-bracing can sway several feet during an earthquake and return to their original position. This flexibility absorbs the earthquake's energy gradually rather than resisting it rigidly. Think of it like a palm tree in a hurricane — it bends with the wind and survives, while a stiff oak might snap.

• Base Isolation Technology:
  Base isolation is one of the most effective earthquake protection technologies. The building sits on special rubber and steel pads (isolators) that allow the foundation to move with the ground while the building above stays relatively still. During an earthquake, the ground may move several inches, but the building barely moves. This technology is used in hospitals, museums, and critical infrastructure worldwide.

• Real-World Success Stories:
  Japan's Yokohama Landmark Tower (296m tall) uses a combination of base isolation and active dampers to survive earthquakes. During the 2011 magnitude 9.0 earthquake, buildings designed with modern seismic technology performed remarkably well while older buildings suffered severe damage. Chile's building codes, updated after the 1960 magnitude 9.5 earthquake (the largest ever recorded), have saved countless lives in subsequent earthquakes.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Earthquakes destroy all buildings equally"
  Reality: Building design is the biggest factor in whether a structure survives an earthquake. In the same earthquake, a poorly designed building might collapse while a well-designed one next door suffers no damage. Modern earthquake engineering can make buildings that survive even the strongest earthquakes. Japan, Chile, and California have proven this with decades of building code improvements.
  Strategy: Show photos from the same earthquake where modern buildings survived and older buildings collapsed side by side. Design matters more than earthquake strength.

• "The strongest building is the stiffest building"
  Reality: This is the opposite of what earthquake engineering teaches. Stiff buildings are actually MORE dangerous in earthquakes because they resist the shaking until they break catastrophically. Flexible buildings that sway with the earthquake absorb the energy gradually and survive. Think of a palm tree vs an oak in a hurricane — the palm bends and survives, the oak can snap.
  Strategy: Demo with two pencils: try to shake a wooden pencil (it stays rigid until it snaps) vs a flexible straw (it bends in all directions but never breaks). Which one would you want your building to be like?

• "We cannot do anything about earthquakes"
  Reality: While we cannot prevent earthquakes, we have enormous power to reduce their impact. Better building codes, earthquake-resistant design, early warning systems, emergency preparedness plans, and community education have saved millions of lives. Countries with strong building codes (Japan, Chile, New Zealand) experience far fewer earthquake deaths than countries without them, even from stronger earthquakes.
  Strategy: Compare: The 2010 Haiti earthquake (magnitude 7.0) killed over 200,000 people. The 2010 Chile earthquake (magnitude 8.8 — nearly 500 times more powerful) killed about 500 people. Why? Chile had modern building codes. Engineering saves lives.

ANSWER KEY:
Big Question: How do engineers design buildings that can survive an earthquake without falling down?
Answer: Engineers cannot stop earthquakes, but they CAN stop buildings from falling down! They design flexible buildings that sway with the shaking instead of fighting it. Special features like base isolators (rubber pads under the building), cross-bracing (X-shaped supports), and deep foundations help buildings absorb earthquake energy. A well-designed building can survive even a powerful earthquake!

Simulation Answers:
• Major Quake, Stiff Building Scenario: When Earthquake Strength is high and Building Flexibility is low, Damage Level skyrockets. The stiff building fights against the shaking and cracks under the stress. The model shows that rigidity is dangerous in an earthquake — the stiffer the building, the more likely it is to break because it cannot absorb the energy.
• Major Quake, Flexible Building Scenario: When the same high Earthquake Strength hits a building with high flexibility, Damage Level is dramatically lower. The flexible building sways with the earthquake, absorbing and dissipating the energy gradually. Comparing the two scenarios side by side shows that building design is MORE important than earthquake strength in determining survival.

Reflection Exemplars:
• Q: Why is a flexible building safer than a stiff building?
  A: A flexible building is safer because it moves WITH the earthquake instead of fighting against it. Imagine holding a stiff stick and shaking it hard — it snaps. Now imagine holding a rubber band and shaking it — it stretches and bounces but does not break. A flexible building works like the rubber band. It bends, sways, and absorbs the earthquake's energy gradually, then returns to its original shape when the shaking stops. A stiff building is like the stick — it resists the shaking until the stress becomes too much, then it cracks and crumbles.
• Q: What other natural hazards could engineers design solutions for?
  A: Engineers design solutions for many natural hazards! For hurricanes, they build houses with reinforced roofs and impact-resistant windows. For floods, they build levees, seawalls, and elevated homes. For tornadoes, they design underground storm shelters and reinforced safe rooms. For wildfires, they use fire-resistant building materials and create defensible space around homes. For tsunamis, they build seawalls and design buildings that can withstand wave forces. In every case, the key idea is the same as with earthquakes — you cannot stop the natural hazard, but you CAN design structures that survive it.

Key Concepts:
• Cross-Bracing: X-shaped supports in the walls of a building that help it resist sideways forces. Cross-bracing allows the building to flex without collapsing, like how a spider web bends in wind but does not break.
• Base Isolation: Special rubber and steel pads placed between the building and its foundation. During an earthquake, the ground moves but the building stays still because the isolators absorb the motion — like a hockey puck sliding on ice.
• Dampers: Large shock absorbers built into tall buildings that slow down the swaying motion. They work just like the shock absorbers in a car that smooth out bumps in the road, but they are much bigger and designed for earthquake forces.

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.3 (Developing and Using Models) + DCI ESS3.B.1 (Natural hazards and solutions to reduce impacts) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A student is building a model of how earthquakes affect buildings. She places three components on the screen: Earthquake Strength, Building Flexibility, and Damage Level. Earthquake Strength is labeled as an external component.

Which of the following best explains why Earthquake Strength is classified as an external component in this model?

A. Earthquake Strength changes whenever Damage Level changes.
B. Earthquake Strength is determined by natural forces deep underground before the shaking reaches buildings, so it is an input that comes from outside the system.
C. Earthquake Strength is internal because buildings control how strong the earthquake is.
D. Earthquake Strength is the same thing as Damage Level.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: If you chose A, that reverses the relationship. Earthquake Strength does not change because of Damage Level. Damage Level changes because of Earthquake Strength. If you chose B, you are correct. External components are inputs from outside the system that are set before the simulation runs. Earthquakes are caused by tectonic plate movement deep underground, and their strength is determined before the shaking reaches any buildings. If you chose D, Earthquake Strength and Damage Level are two different components. Earthquake Strength is the force of shaking, while Damage Level is how much destruction results. If you chose C, external means the component is NOT controlled by other parts inside the system. Buildings have no effect on how strong an earthquake is.

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.3 (Developing and Using Models) + DCI ESS3.B.1 (Generate and compare solutions to reduce impacts) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A student built a model showing two relationships. When Earthquake Strength increases, Damage Level increases (positive arrow). When Building Flexibility increases, Damage Level decreases (negative arrow).

What does the NEGATIVE relationship between Building Flexibility and Damage Level mean in this model?

D. When Building Flexibility goes up, Damage Level also goes up because flexible buildings break more easily.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: If you chose D, that would be a positive relationship, where both go up together. The model shows a negative relationship here, meaning they move in opposite directions. If you chose C, you are correct. A negative relationship means the two components move in opposite directions. When Building Flexibility increases, Damage Level decreases because flexible buildings can sway with the earthquake energy instead of resisting it and cracking. If you chose B, a negative relationship does not mean the values are equal. It means when one goes up, the other goes down. If you chose A, the negative sign in the model refers to the direction of the relationship between two components, not whether something is good or bad. In this case, the negative relationship is actually a good thing because it means flexibility reduces damage.

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 4.1 (Analyzing and Interpreting Data) + DCI ESS3.B.1 (Generate and compare multiple solutions) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A group of students ran four simulation trials. In Trial 1, Earthquake Strength was strong and Building Flexibility was low, and Damage Level was very high. In Trial 2, Earthquake Strength was strong and Building Flexibility was high, and Damage Level was medium. In Trial 3, Earthquake Strength was weak and Building Flexibility was low, and Damage Level was medium. In Trial 4, Earthquake Strength was weak and Building Flexibility was high, and Damage Level was very low.

Which claim is best supported by this simulation data?

A. The most damage occurs when Earthquake Strength is high AND Building Flexibility is low, while the least damage occurs when Earthquake Strength is low AND Building Flexibility is high. Both factors work together to determine Damage Level.
B. Earthquake Strength is the only factor that matters for damage.
C. Building Flexibility does not affect damage because Trials 2 and 3 both had medium damage.
D. The simulation is broken because the results are inconsistent.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: If you chose C, comparing Trial 1 (very high damage) with Trial 2 (medium damage) shows that increasing flexibility reduced damage even when the earthquake was the same strength. Building Flexibility clearly matters. If you chose B, comparing Trials 1 and 2 shows that with the SAME earthquake strength, increasing flexibility reduced damage from very high to medium. Both factors matter, not just earthquake strength. If you chose A, you are correct. The data shows that both factors work together. The worst case (Trial 1) combines a strong earthquake with a rigid building. The best case (Trial 4) combines a weak earthquake with a flexible building. Engineers can reduce damage by increasing building flexibility even though they cannot control earthquake strength. If you chose D, the results are consistent with the model. The positive relationship (earthquake strength increases damage) and negative relationship (flexibility decreases damage) work together to produce these results.

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.3 (Developing and Using Models) + DCI ESS3.B.1 (Solutions to reduce impacts of natural Earth processes) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

Feedback: If you chose D, the model showed that rigid buildings suffer MORE damage because they resist the shaking and crack. Cross-bracing, flexible steel frames, and base isolation pads all increase the building's ability to absorb earthquake energy. If you chose B, underground buildings still experience earthquake shaking because the energy travels through the ground.
