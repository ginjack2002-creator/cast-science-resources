## Platform

### Title
**How Does Your Phone Hear Your Voice? — The Invisible Science of Sound Waves**

### Overview
Sound is all around us every moment of every day, yet we cannot see it. Understanding sound waves helps us explain how we communicate, how music works, and how modern technology like phones and speakers turn invisible vibrations into the sounds we love. Students investigate the driving question: When you talk to your phone, how does it turn the invisible vibrations in the air into something it can understand? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

### NGSS Standard
**4-PS4-1**: Develop a model of waves to describe patterns in terms of amplitude and wavelength and that waves can cause objects to move.

### Learning Objectives
- Explain how sound waves are created by vibrations and travel through the air
- Model how sound wave strength, vibration size, and volume level are connected
- Describe how changing the strength of a sound changes its amplitude and volume

| Volume Level | Internal | How loud the sound is when it reaches your ear or a microphone |

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• What Makes Sound?:
  Every sound begins with a vibration. When you speak, your vocal cords vibrate. When you clap, your hands vibrate. When a drum is hit, the drum skin vibrates. These vibrations push air molecules back and forth, creating a chain reaction of moving air particles that spreads outward in all directions — these traveling vibrations are sound waves.

• How Sound Waves Travel:
  Sound waves are longitudinal waves — the air molecules compress together (compression) and spread apart (rarefaction) in the same direction the wave is traveling. Sound needs a medium (material) to travel through. It moves through air at about 343 meters per second, faster through water (1,480 m/s), and even faster through solids like steel (5,960 m/s). Sound cannot travel through a vacuum (empty space).

• Amplitude and Volume:
  Amplitude is the height of a wave — how far the air molecules move from their resting position. Larger amplitude means the molecules move farther back and forth, carrying more energy, which our ears detect as louder sound. When you whisper, the amplitude is tiny. When you shout, the amplitude is large. Volume is measured in decibels (dB). A whisper is about 30 dB, normal talking is 60 dB, and a rock concert is 110 dB.

• How Microphones Work:
  A microphone is a device that converts sound waves into electrical signals. Inside a basic microphone is a thin membrane called a diaphragm. When sound waves hit the diaphragm, it vibrates back and forth just like the original source. These vibrations are converted into electrical signals that match the pattern of the original sound wave. This is how phones, computers, and recording studios capture sound.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Sound can travel through empty space"
  Reality: Sound MUST have a medium (air, water, or a solid) to travel through. Unlike light, sound cannot travel through a vacuum because there are no molecules to vibrate. This is why there is no sound in outer space — even if a star explodes!
  Strategy: Put a ringing phone inside a sealed jar and show that it gets quieter as you pump out the air (or show a video of this experiment).

• "Louder sounds travel faster"
  Reality: Loud and quiet sounds travel at the SAME speed through the same material. A whisper and a shout both travel through air at about 343 meters per second. Volume only changes the SIZE of the vibrations (amplitude), not the speed of the wave.
  Strategy: Clap loudly and softly from the same distance — both sounds reach the listener at the same time.

• "Sound only travels in one direction"
  Reality: Sound waves spread out in ALL directions from the source, like ripples from a stone dropped in water. That is why you can hear someone talking even if you are behind them or around a corner. Sound waves expand as a sphere from the source.
  Strategy: Have one student talk while others listen from different positions around the room — everyone can hear, proving sound goes in all directions.

ANSWER KEY:
Big Question: When you talk to your phone, how does it turn the invisible vibrations in the air into something it can understand?
Answer: Your phone hears you because sound waves are real, physical vibrations in the air! When you speak, your vocal cords vibrate and push air molecules back and forth. Those vibrations travel through the air as sound waves. Inside your phone, a tiny microphone has a piece called a diaphragm that vibrates when sound waves hit it — turning your voice into electrical signals the phone can process!

Simulation Answers:
• Whisper Scenario: When Sound Wave Strength is set to low, Vibration Size is very small and Volume Level is quiet. The air molecules barely move back and forth, carrying only a tiny amount of energy. The sound can only be heard very close to the source.
• Shout Scenario: When Sound Wave Strength is set to maximum, Vibration Size is very large and Volume Level is very loud. The air molecules move far back and forth, carrying lots of energy. The model clearly shows that stronger sounds create bigger waves with more volume.

Reflection Exemplars:
• Q: Why can't you hear sounds in outer space?
  A: Sound waves are vibrations that travel by pushing air molecules back and forth. In outer space, there are almost no molecules at all — it is a vacuum. With nothing to vibrate and push, the sound wave cannot travel. It is like trying to do the wave at a stadium with no people — the wave needs something to move through!
• Q: How is a shout different from a whisper in terms of what the air is doing?
  A: When you whisper, you push a tiny amount of air out gently. The air molecules barely move back and forth — the vibrations are small, so the amplitude is low and the sound is quiet. When you shout, you push a LOT of air out forcefully. The air molecules move much farther back and forth — big vibrations, big amplitude, loud sound. Same type of wave, just bigger!

• Resonance: Some materials vibrate more easily than others when sound waves hit them. Choosing the right material for your sound catcher is key to making it work well.

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format. Each item is three-dimensional, assessing a Science and Engineering Practice (SEP), Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and Crosscutting Concept (CCC) simultaneously, aligned to 4-PS4-1.

---

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.3 (Developing and Using Models) + DCI PS4.A.1 (Waves are regular patterns of motion) + CCC1 (Patterns)

A student is building a model of how sound travels from a speaker to a person's ear. She places three components on the screen: Sound Wave Strength, Vibration Size, and Volume Level. Sound Wave Strength is labeled as an external component.

Which of the following best explains why Sound Wave Strength is classified as an external component in this model?

A. Sound Wave Strength changes whenever Volume Level changes.
B. Sound Wave Strength is the same thing as Volume Level.
C. Sound Wave Strength is set by the speaker before the sound enters the system, so it is an input that comes from outside.
D. Sound Wave Strength is internal because the air controls how strong the wave is.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: If you chose A, that reverses the relationship. Sound Wave Strength does not change because of Volume Level. Volume Level changes because of Sound Wave Strength. If you chose C, you are correct. External components are inputs from outside the system that are set before the simulation runs. The speaker determines how strong the sound wave is. If you chose B, Sound Wave Strength and Volume Level are two different components. Sound Wave Strength is the input energy, while Volume Level is what we hear as a result. If you chose D, external means the component is NOT controlled by other parts inside the system. The speaker sets the strength before the sound wave enters the model.

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.3 (Developing and Using Models) + DCI PS4.A.1 (Waves have amplitude and wavelength) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A student built a model showing that when Sound Wave Strength increases, Vibration Size also increases. The arrow between them was set to positive.

What does the positive relationship between Sound Wave Strength and Vibration Size mean in this model?

A. When Sound Wave Strength goes up, Vibration Size goes down.
B. When Sound Wave Strength goes up, Vibration Size also goes up because stronger sound waves push air molecules back and forth with greater force.
C. Sound Wave Strength and Vibration Size always stay at the same exact number.
D. The positive sign means the sound is pleasant rather than unpleasant.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: If you chose A, that would be a negative relationship, where one goes up and the other goes down. The model shows a positive relationship here. If you chose B, you are correct. A positive relationship means both components move in the same direction. Stronger sound waves carry more energy, which pushes air molecules farther, creating larger vibrations. If you chose C, a positive relationship does not mean the values are equal. It means they increase or decrease together. If you chose D, the positive sign in the model refers to the direction of the relationship between two components, not whether the sound is pleasant.

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.3 (Developing and Using Models) + DCI PS4.A.1 (Waves can cause objects to move) + CCC4 (Systems and System Models)

A student ran a simulation where Sound Wave Strength was set to high. The model showed that Vibration Size increased, and then Volume Level also increased.

Trace the cause-and-effect chain through this model and explain what would happen if Sound Wave Strength were set to low instead.

A. Vibration Size would increase and Volume Level would increase because the air is still there.
B. Vibration Size would decrease but Volume Level would increase because sound energy never changes.
C. Vibration Size would decrease and Volume Level would also decrease because weaker sound waves create smaller vibrations, and smaller vibrations produce a quieter sound.
D. Nothing would change because sound always travels at the same volume.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: If you chose A, setting Sound Wave Strength to low means less energy entering the system, so vibrations cannot be as large. If you chose B, Volume Level depends on Vibration Size in this model. If Vibration Size decreases, Volume Level also decreases through the positive relationship. If you chose C, you are correct. The chain works like this: lower Sound Wave Strength means less energy, which means smaller Vibration Size, which means lower Volume Level. Both positive arrows mean all three decrease together. If you chose D, the simulation shows that changing Sound Wave Strength does change Vibration Size and Volume Level. That is the whole point of the model.

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 4.1 (Analyzing and Interpreting Data) + DCI PS4.A.1 (Amplitude determines loudness) + CCC2 (Cause and Effect)

A group of students ran three simulation trials. In Trial 1, Sound Wave Strength was set to low, and Vibration Size was small and Volume Level was quiet. In Trial 2, Sound Wave Strength was set to medium, and Vibration Size was medium and Volume Level was moderate. In Trial 3, Sound Wave Strength was set to high, and Vibration Size was large and Volume Level was loud.

Which claim is best supported by this simulation data?

A. As Sound Wave Strength increases, both Vibration Size and Volume Level increase, showing that the amplitude of a wave determines how loud a sound is.
B. Higher Sound Wave Strength causes smaller vibrations because the air gets tired.
C. Volume Level stays the same no matter what Sound Wave Strength is set to.
D. The simulation is broken because all three values should be different from each other.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: If you chose C, the data clearly shows Volume Level changing from quiet to moderate to loud across the three trials. It does not stay the same. If you chose B, the data shows the opposite pattern. Higher Sound Wave Strength produced larger vibrations in every trial. If you chose A, you are correct. The data from all three trials shows a consistent pattern: increasing Sound Wave Strength leads to increasing Vibration Size, which leads to increasing Volume Level. This directly supports the idea that amplitude determines loudness. If you chose D, the three components are connected by positive relationships, so it makes sense that they would all increase together. The simulation is working correctly.

### Question 5

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.3 (Developing and Using Models) + DCI PS4.A.1 (Waves can cause objects to move) + CCC5 (Energy and Matter)

A student taps a drum lightly and places small grains of rice on the drum surface. The rice barely moves. Then the student hits the drum hard, and the rice grains bounce high into the air.

Using what you learned from the sound wave model, which explanation best applies the model to this new situation?

A. The rice bounces because the drum is made of a special bouncing material.
B. The rice bounces because hitting the drum harder makes the sound travel faster.
C. The rice would bounce the same amount no matter how hard you hit the drum because rice is always light.
D. Hitting the drum harder creates stronger sound waves with larger vibrations. The larger vibrations push the rice grains with more force, causing them to bounce higher, just like higher Sound Wave Strength leads to larger Vibration Size in the model.

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: If you chose A, the drum material matters, but the key factor is how hard you hit it. A light tap on the same drum barely moves the rice. If you chose D, you are correct. Hitting harder creates a stronger vibration (larger amplitude), which pushes the rice with more force. This matches the model: more Sound Wave Strength leads to more Vibration Size, which causes more movement. If you chose B, hitting harder does not change the speed of sound in air. It changes the amplitude of the vibration. Bigger vibrations mean more energy pushing the rice. If you chose C, the model showed clearly that strength affects vibration size. A harder hit makes larger vibrations that push the rice higher.
