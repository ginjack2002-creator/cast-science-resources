## Platform

### Title
**Why Do Animals Have Superpowers? — The Science of Body Structures and Survival**

### Overview
Every animal on Earth is equipped with an amazing set of body structures — both inside and outside — that help it survive, find food, avoid danger, and raise its young. These structures are like built-in tools that have been perfected over millions of years of evolution. Students investigate the driving question: How do animals survive in dangerous environments using just the body parts they were born with? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

### NGSS Standard
**4-LS1-1**: Construct an argument that plants and animals have internal and external structures that function to support survival, growth, behavior, and reproduction.

### Learning Objectives
- Explain how different body structures help animals survive in their environments
- Model how body structure type, environment danger level, and survival rate are connected
- Describe how animals with better-matched structures survive at higher rates in dangerous environments

LESSON PURPOSE:
Every animal on Earth is equipped with an amazing set of body structures — both inside and outside — that help it survive, find food, avoid danger, and raise its young. These structures are like built-in tools that have been perfected over millions of years of evolution.

NGSS ALIGNMENT:
4-LS1-1: Construct an argument that plants and animals have internal and external structures that function to support survival, growth, behavior, and reproduction.

THREE-DIMENSIONAL LEARNING:
• Science Practice: Developing and Using Models
  Students develop a computational model showing how body structure type and environment danger level interact to determine survival rate.
• Disciplinary Core Idea: LS1.A Structure and Function
  Plants and animals have both internal and external structures that serve various functions in growth, survival, behavior, and reproduction.
• Crosscutting Concept: Structure and Function
  Students explore how the structure (shape, form, material) of an animal's body parts directly relates to their function (what job they do for survival).

PACING GUIDE:
• Step 1 (Locate): 8-10 minutes
• Step 2 (Establish): 8-10 minutes
• Step 3 (Visualize & Evaluate): 10-12 minutes
• Step 4 (Revise & Extend): 10-15 minutes
• Total: 35-45 minutes (or split across 2 class periods)

PRE-LESSON PREP:
□ Test ModelIt access on student devices
□ Review vocabulary: Structure, Function, External Structure, Internal Structure
□ Prepare How do animals survive in dangerous environments using just the body parts they were born with discussion hook
□ Have images or video ready for phenomenon introduction

LEVER FRAMEWORK:
• Locate: Students identify body structure type and environment danger level as external components and survival rate as an internal component that responds to how well structures match dangers.
• Establish: Students determine that better structure-environment matches positively affect survival rate, and that mismatches reduce survival rate.
• Visualize: Students build a model showing how different structure types perform differently depending on the environment's danger profile.
• Evaluate: Students run perfect match, mismatch, and high danger scenarios to observe how the relationship between structure and environment determines survival.
• Revise: Students add food availability or predator speed to explore more complex survival challenges.

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• External Structures:
  External structures are body parts on the outside that you can see. Examples include a bird's beak (shaped for the type of food it eats), a turtle's shell (armor against predators), a polar bear's thick fur (insulation against cold), a chameleon's color-changing skin (camouflage from predators), and a cheetah's long legs (speed for catching prey). Each structure's shape and design matches the specific job it needs to do.

• Internal Structures:
  Internal structures are body parts inside the animal that you cannot see. A bird's hollow bones make it light enough to fly. A whale's enormous lungs let it hold its breath for over an hour while diving. A snake's flexible spine lets it squeeze through tight spaces. An eagle's sharp-focused eyes (with internal structures for magnification) let it spot a mouse from a mile away.

• Structure Matches Environment:
  The key idea is that an animal's structures match the challenges of its environment. Desert animals have structures for conserving water and staying cool. Arctic animals have structures for staying warm and walking on ice. Forest animals have structures for climbing and hiding among trees. When the environment changes faster than animals can adapt, species can become endangered or extinct.

• Biomimicry — Copying Nature's Designs:
  Engineers often study animal structures to inspire human inventions. Velcro was inspired by burrs that stick to animal fur. Bullet trains were redesigned after the kingfisher's beak to reduce noise. Shark skin patterns inspired swimsuits that reduce drag. This field is called biomimicry — 'bio' means life and 'mimicry' means copying. Animals have been solving engineering problems for millions of years!

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Animals choose to grow the structures they need"
  Reality: Animals cannot choose their body structures — they are born with them based on their genes from their parents. Over millions of years, animals with better-matched structures survived more often and had more babies, passing those useful features to the next generation. This is called natural selection.
  Strategy: Ask: Can you choose to grow claws or wings? No! You were born with the structures your parents' genes gave you. Animals work the same way.

• "All animals in the same environment look the same"
  Reality: Animals in the same environment can have VERY different structures that solve the same problem in different ways. In the Arctic, polar bears have thick fur for warmth while walruses have thick blubber. Both stay warm, but the structure is totally different.
  Strategy: Show images of multiple animals from the same environment and compare their different strategies for solving the same survival challenge.

• "Body structures are only on the outside"
  Reality: Many of the most important survival structures are INSIDE the animal where you cannot see them. A bird's hollow bones let it fly. A whale's giant lungs let it dive for hours. A snake's flexible spine lets it squeeze through tight spaces. Both internal AND external structures are essential for survival.
  Strategy: Have students list structures they can see (fur, claws, shells) AND structures they cannot see (bones, lungs, muscles) to show that both types matter equally.

FACILITATION TIPS:
• Step 1: Let students explore the interface. Don't over-explain.
  Let them discover. Circulate and support, don't lecture.
• Step 2: Ask "When this goes up, what happens to that?" to
  guide positive/negative relationship decisions. Let students debate.
• Step 3: Give time for students to "break" the model — turn
  things on/off and observe. This is where real insight happens.
• Step 4: Don't give answers. Ask questions. Let curiosity drive
  the research. Celebrate when students' additions don't work as
  expected — that's authentic science.

ANSWER KEY:
Big Question: How do animals survive in dangerous environments using just the body parts they were born with?
Answer: Animals have 'superpowers' because their body structures are perfectly designed for the dangers in their environment! A polar bear's thick fur is a superpower in the Arctic but would be terrible in a desert. An armadillo's armor is perfect for protection against predators. These structures evolved over millions of years — animals with the best body parts for their environment survived and passed those features to their babies!

Simulation Answers:
• Perfect Match Scenario: When Body Structure Type is set to thick fur and Environment Danger Level is set to arctic cold, Survival Rate is very high. The thick fur provides excellent insulation, keeping the animal warm despite freezing temperatures. The structure perfectly matches the environmental challenge.
• Mismatch Scenario: When Body Structure Type is set to thick fur but Environment Danger Level is desert heat, Survival Rate drops dramatically. The thick fur that was a superpower in the cold becomes a deadly problem in the heat — the animal cannot cool down. This proves that the SAME structure can help or hurt depending on the environment.

Reflection Exemplars:
• Q: Why would thick fur be a problem in the desert?
  A: Thick fur is designed to trap heat close to the body — it is like wearing a winter coat. In the Arctic, trapping heat is exactly what you need to survive the freezing cold. But in the desert, the problem is the opposite — it is TOO hot. Thick fur would trap body heat and prevent the animal from cooling down, causing it to overheat. The same structure that saves a life in one place could end a life in another!
• Q: What body structures do YOU have that help you survive?
  A: Humans have many survival structures! Our skin protects us from germs and holds water in. Our bones support our body and protect organs like the brain (skull) and heart (rib cage). Our eyes help us see danger. Our fingers can grip tools. Our sweat glands cool us down when we are hot. We even have tiny hairs in our noses that filter out dust and dirt from the air we breathe!

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.3 (Developing and Using Models) + DCI LS1.A.1 (Plants and animals have structures that support survival) + CCC6 (Structure and Function)

A student is building a model of how animal body structures affect survival. She places three components on the screen: Body Structure Type, Environment Danger Level, and Survival Rate. Body Structure Type and Environment Danger Level are labeled as external components.

Which of the following best explains why Body Structure Type is classified as an external component in this model?

A. Body Structure Type is a characteristic of the animal that is set before the simulation runs, so it is an input that comes from outside the system.
B. Body Structure Type is the same thing as Survival Rate.
C. Body Structure Type changes whenever Survival Rate changes.
D. Body Structure Type is internal because the animal can change its own structures whenever it wants.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: If you chose C, that reverses the relationship. Body Structure Type does not change because of Survival Rate. Survival Rate changes because of Body Structure Type and the environment. If you chose A, you are correct. External components are inputs from outside the system that are set before the simulation runs. The animal already has its structures before the model begins. If you chose B, Body Structure Type and Survival Rate are two different components. Body Structure Type refers to the kind of structure the animal has, while Survival Rate refers to how well the animal survives. If you chose D, external means the component is NOT controlled by other parts inside the system. Animals cannot change their basic body structures during the simulation.

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 7.1 (Engaging in Argument from Evidence) + DCI LS1.A.1 (Internal and external structures function to support survival) + CCC6 (Structure and Function)

A student built a model showing that when an animal's body structures match its environment, Survival Rate increases. When the structures do not match, Survival Rate decreases.

A polar bear has thick white fur and a layer of blubber under its skin. It lives in the Arctic where temperatures drop below freezing and the ground is covered in snow.

Which of the following best explains why these structures increase the polar bear's survival rate?

A. Thick fur and blubber make the bear look bigger, which scares away all predators.
B. Thick fur makes the bear heavier, which helps it run faster to catch prey.
C. The thick fur keeps the bear warm in freezing temperatures and provides camouflage in the snow, while the blubber stores energy and adds insulation. Both structures match the environment's dangers of extreme cold and predator-prey visibility.
D. The bear's structures would work equally well in any environment because fur and blubber are always helpful.

Correct Answer: C

Feedback: If you chose B, thick fur does not make a bear run faster. The fur's function is insulation and camouflage, not speed. If you chose C, you are correct. Each structure has a specific function that matches the Arctic environment. Fur provides warmth and camouflage in snow. Blubber adds insulation and energy storage. These are a strong match for the dangers of extreme cold. If you chose A, while size can deter some predators, the main survival functions of fur and blubber are warmth, camouflage, and energy storage. If you chose D, the model showed that structures must match the environment. Thick fur and blubber would cause overheating in a desert. The match between structure and environment is what matters.

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.3 (Developing and Using Models) + DCI LS1.A.1 (Structures function to support survival, growth, behavior) + CCC4 (Systems and System Models)

A student ran a simulation where Body Structure Type was set to "thick fur and blubber" and Environment Danger Level was set to "extreme heat desert." The model showed that Survival Rate dropped to very low.

What does this result tell us about the relationship between body structures and survival?

A. Thick fur is always a bad body structure because it made survival drop in the first trial.
B. Survival Rate does not depend on the environment because the body structure was the same in both trials.
C. The simulation is broken because the same body structure should always give the same survival rate.

If you chose D, you are correct. The model demonstrates that survival depends on the match between structure and environment. ### Question 4
