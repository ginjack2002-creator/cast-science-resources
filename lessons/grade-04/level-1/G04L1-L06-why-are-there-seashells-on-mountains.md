## Platform

### Title
**Why Are There Seashells on Mountains? — The Mystery of Fossils Found in Surprising Places**

### Overview
The discovery of marine fossils on mountaintops was one of the great mysteries that early geologists had to solve. It is now understood as powerful evidence for the dynamic nature of Earth's surface. Students investigate the driving question: How did seashells and ocean fossils end up on top of a mountain thousands of feet above the sea? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

### Learning Objectives
- Explain how sediment layers build up over time and trap living things as fossils
- Model how sediment buildup and Earth movement work together to expose fossils on mountains
- Use fossil evidence to describe how a landscape has changed over millions of years

LESSON PURPOSE:
The discovery of marine fossils on mountaintops was one of the great mysteries that early geologists had to solve. It is now understood as powerful evidence for the dynamic nature of Earth's surface.

NGSS ALIGNMENT:
4-ESS1-1: Identify evidence from patterns in rock formations and fossils in rock layers to support an explanation for changes in a landscape over time.

THREE-DIMENSIONAL LEARNING:
• Science Practice: Constructing Explanations and Designing Solutions
  Students use fossil evidence and rock layer patterns to construct an explanation for how landscapes change over time.
• Disciplinary Core Idea: ESS1.C The History of Planet Earth
  Local, regional, and global patterns of rock formations reveal changes over time due to Earth forces such as earthquakes and volcanic eruptions.
• Crosscutting Concept: Patterns
  Students identify patterns in rock layers and fossil locations to explain how landscapes have changed over millions of years.

LEVER FRAMEWORK:
• Locate: Students identify sediment buildup, Earth movement speed, and fossil exposure as the key components of the fossil formation and discovery system.
• Establish: Students determine that sediment buildup positively affects fossil creation (more sediment = more burial = more fossils), and Earth movement speed positively affects fossil exposure (more uplift = more surface exposure).
• Visualize: Students build a model showing how sediment layers create fossils underwater, and how Earth movement lifts those layers to where erosion can reveal them.
• Evaluate: Students run ocean floor and mountain building scenarios to observe how the same fossil can go from deep underwater to a mountaintop.
• Revise: Students add erosion rate or volcanic activity to explore more complex interactions in the fossil exposure system.

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• How Fossils Form:
  Fossils form when an organism dies and is quickly buried by sediment like mud, sand, or volcanic ash. Over thousands to millions of years, the sediment hardens into rock, and minerals replace the original biological material. The most common fossils are from hard parts like shells, bones, and teeth, but soft-bodied organisms can be preserved under exceptional conditions.

• Rock Layers Tell a Story:
  The principle of superposition states that in undisturbed rock layers, the oldest layers are on the bottom and the youngest are on top. Each layer represents a different period in Earth's history. By examining the fossils in each layer, scientists can reconstruct what the environment was like at that time — ocean, swamp, desert, or forest.

• How Mountains Rise:
  Mountains form through tectonic forces — when two plates collide, the crust crumples and folds upward. The Himalayas, for example, contain marine limestone fossils from an ancient ocean floor (the Tethys Sea) that was pushed upward when the Indian plate collided with the Eurasian plate about 50 million years ago. Mount Everest's summit contains marine fossils.

• Famous Fossil Locations:
  The Grand Canyon reveals nearly 2 billion years of rock layers. The Burgess Shale in Canada preserves 500-million-year-old soft-bodied marine creatures. The La Brea Tar Pits in Los Angeles preserve Ice Age mammals. Each location tells a different chapter of Earth's long history through its rock layers and fossils.

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Fossils are just old rocks shaped like animals"
  Reality: Fossils are the actual preserved remains of once-living organisms, not random rock shapes. The organism died, was buried in sediment, and over millions of years the original material was replaced by minerals, turning it to stone while keeping its exact shape. Scientists can identify species, diet, and even diseases from fossils.
  Strategy: Show a real fossil (or detailed photo) next to the living organism it came from. The detail preserved in fossils — teeth, leaf veins, shell ridges — proves these were once alive.

• "Mountains have always been mountains"
  Reality: Many mountains were once ocean floors, plains, or even swamps. The Himalayas were a shallow sea 50 million years ago. The Appalachian Mountains were once taller than the Himalayas but have been worn down by hundreds of millions of years of erosion. Earth's surface is constantly changing — just very slowly.
  Strategy: Show before-and-after diagrams of the same location millions of years apart. Ask: If mountains were always there, how do we explain ocean fossils at the summit?

• "Fossils form quickly"
  Reality: Fossil formation takes thousands to millions of years. An organism must be buried quickly in sediment before it decomposes, then the sediment must harden into rock over vast time periods. The minerals slowly replace the original material molecule by molecule. This is why fossils are rare — most organisms decompose before they can be preserved.
  Strategy: Ask: Why do we not find fossils of every animal that has ever lived? Because fossilization requires very specific conditions and enormous amounts of time.

FACILITATION TIPS:
• Step 1: Let students explore the interface. Don't over-explain.
  Let them discover. Circulate and support, don't lecture.
• Step 2: Ask "When this goes up, what happens to that?" to
  guide positive/negative relationship decisions. Let students debate.
• Step 3: Give time for students to "break" the model — turn
  things on/off and observe. This is where real insight happens.
• Step 4: Don't give answers. Ask questions. Let curiosity drive
  the research. Celebrate when students' additions don't work as
  expected — that's authentic science.

ANSWER KEY:
Big Question: How did seashells and ocean fossils end up on top of a mountain thousands of feet above the sea?
Answer: Seashells ended up on mountains because those mountains used to be ocean floor! Over millions of years, sediment buried sea creatures and turned them into fossils. Then enormous forces inside the Earth slowly pushed those rock layers upward, creating mountains. Wind and rain then wore away the surface, revealing the ancient ocean fossils at the top!

Simulation Answers:
• Ocean Floor Scenario: When Sediment Buildup is high but Earth Movement is zero, many fossils form in the layers but none are exposed at the surface. They stay buried deep underwater. The model shows that fossils need both burial AND uplift to become discoverable.
• Mountain Building Scenario: When Earth Movement Speed is high, rock layers that were once on the ocean floor get pushed upward. As they rise, erosion wears away the surface layers, revealing the ancient fossils inside. The model shows that Earth movement is the key to bringing deep fossils to the surface.

Reflection Exemplars:
• Q: If a mountain has ocean fossils, what was that land like long ago?
  A: If there are ocean fossils on a mountain, that land must have been under the ocean millions of years ago. Sea creatures lived there, died, and got buried in sediment on the ocean floor. Over a very long time, enormous forces inside the Earth pushed that ocean floor upward until it became a mountain. The fossils are like a message from the past telling us the land's secret history.
• Q: Why are the oldest fossils in the deepest layers?
  A: The oldest fossils are deepest because rock layers form from the bottom up. The first layer of sediment settles on the ground and gets buried by the next layer, and the next, and the next. Each new layer is younger than the one below it. So if you dig down, you are going back in time — the deeper you go, the older the fossils you find. It is like a stack of books where the bottom book was placed first.

Key Concepts:
• Superposition: In undisturbed rock layers, the oldest layer is always on the bottom and the youngest is on top. This principle helps scientists figure out the age of fossils without fancy equipment.
• Index Fossils: Certain fossils are found only in specific time periods. Finding one of these index fossils tells scientists exactly how old that rock layer is, like a timestamp in the rock.
• Preservation: Not all organisms become fossils. Hard parts like shells and bones preserve best. Soft-bodied creatures rarely fossilize unless conditions are perfect, like being buried in fine mud or volcanic ash very quickly.

### Question 1

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.3 (Developing and Using Models) + DCI ESS1.C.1 (Evidence from rock formations and fossils supports changes in landscape) + CCC4 (Systems and System Models)

A student is building a model of how fossils end up on mountains. She places three components on the screen: Sediment Buildup, Earth Movement Speed, and Fossil Exposure. Sediment Buildup and Earth Movement Speed are labeled as external components.

Which of the following best explains why Sediment Buildup is classified as an external component in this model?

A. Sediment Buildup is a natural process that happens over time and is set as an input before the simulation runs, so it comes from outside the system.
B. Sediment Buildup is internal because the fossils control how much sediment builds up.
C. Sediment Buildup changes whenever Fossil Exposure changes.
D. Sediment Buildup is the same thing as Fossil Exposure.

Correct Answer: A

Feedback: If you chose C, that reverses the relationship. Sediment Buildup does not change because of Fossil Exposure. Fossil Exposure changes because of Sediment Buildup and Earth Movement Speed. If you chose A, you are correct. External components are inputs from outside the system that are set before the simulation runs. Sediment Buildup is a geological process that the model treats as an input to control. If you chose D, Sediment Buildup and Fossil Exposure are two different components. Sediment Buildup refers to how much material buries organisms, while Fossil Exposure refers to whether fossils become visible at the surface. If you chose B, external means the component is NOT controlled by other parts inside the system. Fossils do not control how much sediment builds up.

### Question 2

CAST Alignment: SEP 7.1 (Engaging in Argument from Evidence) + DCI ESS1.C.1 (Patterns in rock formations support explanations for landscape changes) + CCC1 (Patterns)

A scientist hikes to the top of a mountain in California. At the summit, she discovers fossils of seashells, clams, and fish embedded in the rock layers. She knows that these organisms only live in the ocean.

Which explanation best uses this fossil evidence to support a claim about the mountain's history?

A. The fossils formed on the mountain because the mountain was very wet from rain.
B. Hikers carried the fossils up the mountain and left them there.
C. The ocean creatures crawled up the mountain millions of years ago.
D. The rock layers at the top of the mountain were once at the bottom of an ocean. Over millions of years, forces inside Earth pushed the ocean floor rock upward, creating the mountain with the ocean fossils still embedded inside.

Correct Answer: D

Feedback: If you chose C, ocean creatures like clams and fish cannot crawl up mountains. They lived and died in the ocean, and their remains were preserved in the ocean floor rock. If you chose D, you are correct. Finding ocean fossils on a mountaintop is strong evidence that the rock was once ocean floor. Earth's internal forces slowly pushed the rock layers upward over millions of years, bringing the fossils with them. If you chose B, these fossils are embedded inside the rock, not sitting on top of it. They were preserved millions of years ago when the rock formed underwater. If you chose A, fossils of ocean creatures form in the ocean, not from rain on a mountain. The presence of ocean fossils means the rock itself was once underwater.

### Question 3

CAST Alignment: SEP 2.3 (Developing and Using Models) + DCI ESS1.C.1 (Rock layers and fossils show landscape changes over time) + CCC4 (Systems and System Models)

A student ran a simulation where Sediment Buildup was set to high and Earth Movement Speed was set to high. The model showed that Fossil Exposure increased to high because many organisms were buried and then pushed to the surface quickly.

The student then changed Earth Movement Speed to low while keeping Sediment Buildup at high. What does the model predict will happen to Fossil Exposure?

A. Fossil Exposure will increase even more because slow movement is better for fossils.
B. Fossil Exposure will stay the same because Sediment Buildup did not change.
C. Fossil Exposure will decrease because even though many fossils are being created by sediment, the slow Earth movement means rock layers are not being pushed to the surface as quickly for erosion to reveal them.

### Question 4

CAST Alignment: SEP 4.1 (Analyzing and Interpreting Data) + DCI ESS1.C.1 (Evidence from rock layers supports landscape change explanations) + CCC1 (Patterns)

A group of students examined a cliff face with four visible rock layers. The bottom layer contained fossils of ocean fish. The next layer up contained fossils of shallow-water plants. The third layer contained fossils of swamp ferns. The top layer contained fossils of land animals.

Which claim is best supported by this pattern of fossils across the rock layers?

A. The bottom layer is the newest because it is the deepest underground.
B. The land slowly changed from deep ocean to shallow water to swamp to dry land over millions of years, and each rock layer recorded the organisms that lived during that time period.
C. All four types of organisms lived at the same time in the same place.
D. The fossils were placed in the wrong order by the people who discovered them.

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: If you chose C, the four types of organisms represent different environments (ocean, shallow water, swamp, land) that existed at different times. They did not all live together. If you chose B, you are correct. The pattern of fossils from bottom to top tells the story of how the landscape changed over time. The oldest layer (bottom) shows a deep ocean environment, and each newer layer shows the land gradually changing until it became dry land. If you chose D, fossils are embedded in the rock where they formed. They cannot be rearranged. The pattern reflects the actual history of the environment. If you chose A, the bottom layer is the OLDEST, not the newest. Rock layers are deposited from bottom to top, so the bottom layer formed first (law of superposition).
