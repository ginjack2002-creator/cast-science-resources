## Platform

### Title
**Climate-Ecosystem Coupled Models — Modeling Feedback Loops Between Climate Change, Ecosystems, and Human Society**

### Overview
This lesson introduces students to coupled climate-ecosystem modeling — the integration of atmospheric physics, ocean chemistry, terrestrial ecology, and human systems into a single interconnected model. Biotech skill focus: Multi-system feedback analysis and tipping point dynamics. Understanding the Earth as a coupled system is essential for making evidence-based decisions about climate mitigation and adaptation. Students investigate the driving question: Climate change doesn't just heat the planet — it triggers cascading feedback loops through every ecosystem on Earth. When permafrost melts and releases methane, which accelerates warming, which melts more permafrost, which releases more methane — how do you model a system where the output becomes the input? Using the LEVER framework, students identify key components, establish cause-and-effect relationships, run simulations to test scenarios, and extend their understanding through research and engineering challenges.

### Learning Objectives
- Build a coupled climate-ecosystem model that traces feedback loops between atmospheric CO2, temperature, ocean absorption, permafrost methane release, and biological carbon sequestration
- Analyze how positive and negative feedback loops create tipping points, runaway effects, and nonlinear system behavior in the Earth's climate-ecosystem system
- Predict the cascading consequences of climate change across ecological, agricultural, and societal systems using multi-variable simulation
- Evaluate the effectiveness and trade-offs of different adaptation and mitigation strategies using model-derived evidence

### Component List (Starting Model: 10 Components)

| Component | Type | What It Represents |
|-----------|------|-------------------|
| Atmospheric CO2 | External | The concentration of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere, measured in parts per million (ppm) — currently ~425 ppm and rising approximately 2.5 ppm/year due to fossil fuel combustion, deforestation, and industrial processes, driving the greenhouse effect that warms the planet |
| Global Temperature | Internal | The average surface temperature of the Earth relative to pre-industrial levels — currently approximately 1.2 degrees C above pre-industrial baseline, with projections of 1.5-4.5 degrees C increase by 2100 depending on emission trajectories |
| Ocean Absorption | Internal | The rate at which the ocean absorbs CO2 from the atmosphere and heat from the climate system — the ocean has absorbed approximately 30% of anthropogenic CO2 and over 90% of excess heat, but absorption capacity decreases as the ocean warms and acidifies |
| Permafrost Methane Release | Internal | The rate at which thawing Arctic permafrost releases trapped methane (CH4) and CO2 — permafrost contains approximately 1,500 gigatons of organic carbon (twice the current atmospheric carbon), and methane is 80x more potent than CO2 as a greenhouse gas over 20 years |
| Forest Carbon Sequestration | Internal | The net rate at which global forests absorb CO2 from the atmosphere through photosynthesis minus the rate they release it through respiration, decomposition, and fire — currently forests absorb approximately 2.6 gigatons of CO2 per year, but this sink is declining as forests face heat stress, drought, fire, and deforestation |
| Species Migration Rate | Internal | The speed at which plant and animal species shift their geographic ranges in response to changing climate conditions — many species must move poleward or to higher elevations to maintain suitable habitat, but migration speed is limited by dispersal ability, habitat fragmentation, and physical barriers |
| Crop Yield Change | Internal | The percentage change in global agricultural productivity resulting from temperature increases, altered precipitation patterns, increased CO2 fertilization, and changing pest and disease pressure — moderate warming may benefit some crops temporarily while severe warming reduces yields for most staple crops |
| Sea Level Rise | Internal | The increase in global mean sea level caused by thermal expansion of ocean water and melting of land-based ice (glaciers, Greenland, and Antarctic ice sheets) — currently rising approximately 3.6 mm/year and accelerating, with 0.5-2.0 meters projected by 2100 |
| Extreme Weather Frequency | Internal | The rate of occurrence of extreme weather events (hurricanes, heatwaves, droughts, floods, wildfires) relative to historical baselines — warmer atmosphere holds 7% more water vapor per degree C of warming, intensifying the hydrological cycle and amplifying weather extremes |
| Societal Adaptation Capacity | External | The ability of human societies to adjust to climate change impacts through infrastructure, technology, policy, economic resources, and institutional capacity — wealthy nations can build sea walls and develop drought-resistant crops while vulnerable nations face disproportionate climate risk with fewer resources to adapt |

```
TIME TO DRAW THE INVISIBLE LINES

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Task A: OPEN CONNECTION MODE
• Click the "Connect" icon in the TOP LEFT corner of your screen
• Your cursor is now ready to draw relationship arrows

Task B: DRAW YOUR RELATIONSHIPS
• Click on "Atmospheric CO2" and drag an arrow to "Global Temperature"
• Click on "Global Temperature" and drag an arrow to "Ocean Absorption"
• Click on "Global Temperature" and drag an arrow to "Permafrost Methane Release"
• Click on "Forest Carbon Sequestration" and drag an arrow to "Atmospheric CO2"
• Click on "Global Temperature" and drag an arrow to "Extreme Weather Frequency"
• Click on "Extreme Weather Frequency" and drag an arrow to "Societal Adaptation Capacity"

Task C: SET POSITIVE OR NEGATIVE
• Look at the +/− toggle in the TOP LEFT corner
• For each connection, ask: "When this goes UP, does the other go UP or DOWN?"

  ○ Atmospheric CO2 → Global Temperature = POSITIVE (+)
    Higher atmospheric CO2 traps more outgoing infrared radiation through the greenhouse effect, increasing the planet's energy imbalance and raising Global Temperature — each doubling of CO2 produces approximately 3 degrees C of equilibrium warming (climate sensitivity).

  ○ Global Temperature → Ocean Absorption = NEGATIVE (−)
    Higher temperatures reduce the ocean's capacity to absorb CO2 because warm water holds less dissolved gas (Henry's Law) and because warming stratifies the ocean, reducing mixing between surface and deep layers that sequester carbon long-term.

  ○ Global Temperature → Permafrost Methane Release = POSITIVE (+)
    Higher temperatures thaw Arctic permafrost, exposing frozen organic matter to microbial decomposition that releases CO2 and methane — this is a POSITIVE FEEDBACK because the released greenhouse gases cause additional warming that thaws more permafrost.

  ○ Forest Carbon Sequestration → Atmospheric CO2 = NEGATIVE (−)
    Greater forest growth and photosynthesis remove more CO2 from the atmosphere, reducing the atmospheric concentration — this is a NEGATIVE FEEDBACK that partially counteracts emissions, but it can reverse at high temperatures when forests die and release stored carbon.

  ○ Global Temperature → Extreme Weather Frequency = POSITIVE (+)
    Higher temperatures intensify the hydrological cycle (warmer air holds 7% more water vapor per degree C), increase ocean heat content that fuels tropical storms, and create more extreme temperature events — all increasing the frequency and intensity of weather extremes.

  ○ Extreme Weather Frequency → Societal Adaptation Capacity = NEGATIVE (−)
    More frequent extreme weather events damage infrastructure, displace populations, destroy crops, and consume disaster response resources, progressively degrading a society's capacity to adapt to ongoing and future climate impacts.

Task D: CHECK YOUR MODEL
• You should have 6 arrows total
• 3 negative relationship(s), 3 positive relationship(s)
• This is your system model!

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

THINK ABOUT IT: This model is fundamentally different from the others because it contains MULTIPLE feedback loops — and some of them are positive (self-amplifying). Atmospheric CO2 increases Global Temperature through the greenhouse effect. Higher temperature reduces Ocean Absorption capacity (warm water holds less CO2). Higher temperature thaws Permafrost, which releases more greenhouse gases, which increases Global Temperature further — a classic positive feedback loop. But higher CO2 also increases photosynthesis (CO2 fertilization), which increases Forest Carbon Sequestration — a negative feedback that partially counteracts the warming. Meanwhile, higher temperatures cause Species Migration, Crop Yield Change, Sea Level Rise, and Extreme Weather Frequency — all of which affect Societal Adaptation Capacity, which determines whether human societies can respond effectively. The question isn't just 'how much will it warm?' — it's 'when do we cross tipping points where the feedbacks become self-sustaining regardless of what we do?'

Societal Adaptation Capacity determines whether communities survive these impacts, but adaptation has limits — no amount of adaptation can compensate for a fundamentally destabilized climate system. The model demonstrates that early, aggressive emission reduction is not just environmentally preferable but mathematically necessary to avoid crossing irreversible tipping points.

BACKGROUND CONTENT:
• The Carbon Cycle and Climate System:
  The Earth's climate is regulated by the balance between incoming solar radiation and outgoing infrared radiation. Greenhouse gases — primarily CO2, methane (CH4), nitrous oxide (N2O), and water vapor — trap outgoing radiation, warming the surface. The carbon cycle moves carbon between four major reservoirs: the atmosphere (approximately 880 GtC), the ocean (approximately 38,000 GtC), terrestrial biomass and soil (approximately 4,000 GtC), and fossil fuels (approximately 10,000 GtC). Human activity has transferred approximately 440 GtC from the fossil fuel reservoir to the atmosphere since 1750, increasing atmospheric CO2 from 280 to 425 ppm. The ocean and terrestrial biosphere have absorbed approximately half of these emissions — but their absorption capacity is declining as the system warms.

• Positive Feedback Loops and Tipping Points:
  Several positive feedback loops in the climate system can amplify warming beyond what greenhouse gas emissions alone would produce. The ice-albedo feedback: warming melts reflective ice, exposing dark ocean or land that absorbs more solar radiation, causing more warming. The permafrost carbon feedback: warming thaws Arctic permafrost containing 1,500 GtC of frozen organic matter, releasing CO2 and methane as it decomposes, causing more warming. The water vapor feedback: warming evaporates more water, and water vapor is a greenhouse gas, causing more warming (this is the single largest amplifying feedback). The forest die-off feedback: extreme warming causes drought, heat stress, and wildfire that kill forests, releasing stored carbon and eliminating future sequestration capacity. Each feedback has a potential tipping point — a temperature threshold beyond which it becomes self-sustaining regardless of future emission reductions.

• Ecosystem Responses to Climate Change:
  Ecosystems respond to climate change through three primary mechanisms. Migration: species shift their ranges poleward (average 17 km per decade for terrestrial species) or to higher elevations (11 m per decade) to track suitable climate conditions. Adaptation: populations with genetic variation may evolve tolerance to new conditions over generations — but the rate of current climate change far exceeds the rate at which most species can adapt genetically. Collapse: when species cannot migrate fast enough or adapt, populations decline and ecosystems reorganize — coral reef bleaching (affecting 75% of global reefs), boreal forest die-off, and savanna desertification are already underway. Agricultural systems face the same pressures: moderate warming (1-2 degrees C) may increase yields for some temperate crops through longer growing seasons and CO2 fertilization, but warming beyond 2-3 degrees C reduces yields for most staple crops (wheat: -6% per degree C, corn: -7.4% per degree C globally).

• Climate Justice and Adaptation Inequality:
  Climate change impacts are profoundly unequal. The nations that have contributed the most cumulative CO2 emissions (US, EU, China) are generally wealthier and better equipped to adapt — with resources for sea walls, disaster response, crop insurance, and technological innovation. The nations that have contributed the least (sub-Saharan Africa, South Pacific islands, Southeast Asia) face the most severe impacts — sea level rise threatening low-lying nations, extreme heat reducing agricultural productivity, and intensified tropical storms — with the fewest resources to adapt. This asymmetry between responsibility and vulnerability is the central challenge of climate justice. The model's Societal Adaptation Capacity component makes this inequality visible: a 0.5-meter sea level rise is manageable for the Netherlands (which has centuries of flood engineering expertise and trillions of dollars of GDP) but existential for Tuvalu (population 12,000, maximum elevation 4.6 meters, GDP $63 million).

COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS:
• "Climate change is a gradual, linear process that we can slowly adjust to"
  Reality: The model reveals that climate change contains TIPPING POINTS — critical thresholds beyond which the system shifts abruptly to a new state. These transitions are nonlinear, meaning the relationship between cause (CO2 emissions) and effect (climate impacts) is not proportional. A small additional temperature increase near a tipping point can trigger dramatically larger consequences than the same increase at lower temperatures. The permafrost methane feedback, ice sheet collapse, and forest die-off all have tipping point characteristics. This means that 'wait and see' approaches to climate policy risk crossing irreversible thresholds.
  Strategy: Analogy: Imagine heating a pot of water. Temperature rises linearly until 100 degrees C — then the system abruptly transitions from liquid to gas. Climate tipping points are similar — gradual change until a threshold, then abrupt system shift. What's different: you can cool the water back down, but you can't easily refreeze 1,500 gigatons of permafrost.

• "Technology will solve climate change so we don't need to reduce emissions"
  Reality: While technology is essential — renewable energy, carbon capture, energy storage — no technology can reverse the effects of positive feedback loops that have already been triggered. The model shows that once Permafrost Methane Release becomes self-sustaining, no amount of direct air carbon capture (currently removing approximately 0.01 Mt CO2/year) can compensate for the approximately 1-2 Gt CO2-equivalent/year of additional emissions. Technology COMPLEMENTS emission reduction — it cannot REPLACE it. The math is simple: we emit approximately 40 Gt CO2/year; the most optimistic carbon capture projections remove approximately 10 Gt CO2/year by 2050; we need to reduce emissions AND deploy capture simultaneously.
  Strategy: Calculate: Current global emissions are approximately 40 GtCO2/year. The largest direct air capture plant removes 36,000 tons/year. How many of these plants would you need to offset current emissions? (Answer: over 1 million.) How much would this cost at $600/ton? (Answer: $24 trillion/year — roughly 25% of global GDP.)

• "Climate change only affects polar bears and distant future generations"
  Reality: The model's downstream components (Crop Yield Change, Extreme Weather Frequency, Sea Level Rise) demonstrate that climate change is affecting human systems NOW — 2023 was the hottest year in recorded history, extreme weather events have increased 5x since the 1970s, and agricultural disruptions are already reducing food security in vulnerable regions. Sea Level Rise is displacing communities in the Marshall Islands, Bangladesh, and Louisiana TODAY. The model shows that the impacts accelerate over time, meaning every year of delay in emission reduction commits future generations to worse outcomes. This is not a distant, abstract problem — it is a present, concrete reality with measurable human costs.
  Strategy: Evidence: 2023 saw record global temperatures, Canadian wildfires that blanketed US cities in smoke, Mediterranean heat waves exceeding 48 degrees C, and flooding in Libya that killed 11,000 people. These are model predictions becoming measured reality.

Reflection Exemplars:
• Q: What makes positive feedback loops so dangerous in the climate system?
  A: The model reveals that positive feedback loops amplify change — small perturbations grow larger over time instead of being dampened. In the climate system, this means that a moderate amount of warming from CO2 emissions can trigger self-reinforcing processes (permafrost methane release, ice-albedo feedback, forest die-off) that produce ADDITIONAL warming beyond what the emissions alone would cause. The critical danger is tipping points: if a positive feedback loop becomes self-sustaining, it will continue to drive change even if the original perturbation (CO2 emissions) is removed. This is analogous to pushing a boulder uphill — you can stop pushing and it stays put (negative feedback), or you can push it past the hilltop and it rolls down the other side on its own (positive feedback past tipping point).
• Q: Why is Societal Adaptation Capacity so unequally distributed?

This means that climate change transfers risk from those who caused it to those who didn't — a fundamental violation of fairness. Addressing this inequality through climate finance, technology transfer, and adaptation support is as essential as emission reductions.

## CAST-Aligned Pre/Post Assessment

### Administration Instructions

These 5 multiple-choice questions are administered identically as both a pre-assessment (before Activity 1) and a post-assessment (after Activity 4). Score each out of 5. Learning growth = post-score minus pre-score.

Questions follow the California Science Test (CAST) stimulus-response format. Each item is three-dimensional, assessing a Science and Engineering Practice (SEP), Disciplinary Core Idea (DCI), and Crosscutting Concept (CCC) simultaneously, aligned to HS-ESS3-5, HS-LS2-6.

### Pre-Assessment Questions

### Question 1

The greenhouse effect is a natural process that keeps Earth's surface warm enough to support life. Which statement BEST describes how human activities have altered this process?

A. Humans have created the greenhouse effect, which did not exist before industrialization
B. Burning fossil fuels has increased atmospheric CO2 concentration, which enhances the natural greenhouse effect and causes additional warming beyond what would occur naturally
C. The greenhouse effect only affects greenhouse buildings, not the entire planet
D. Human activities have weakened the greenhouse effect, causing cooling

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. The greenhouse effect existed naturally for billions of years. Human activities (primarily fossil fuel combustion and deforestation) have increased atmospheric CO2 from ~280 ppm (pre-industrial) to ~425 ppm, enhancing the effect and driving additional warming. Distinguish between the natural greenhouse effect (which has always existed and made Earth habitable) and the enhanced greenhouse effect caused by human activity. What specific change have humans made to the atmosphere?

---

### Question 2

A positive feedback loop in climate science refers to a process where warming causes changes that produce MORE warming. Which of the following is an example of a climate positive feedback loop?

A. Warmer temperatures increase plant growth, which absorbs more CO2 and reduces warming
B. Warming melts arctic ice, which exposes dark ocean water that absorbs more sunlight, which causes more warming
C. Increased cloud cover reflects sunlight back to space
D. Ocean currents distribute heat evenly across the planet

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. This is the ice-albedo feedback. White ice reflects sunlight (high albedo), but when ice melts, it exposes dark ocean water that absorbs sunlight (low albedo). This absorption warms the water further, melting more ice, creating a self-amplifying cycle. A positive feedback amplifies the original change. Which process creates a cycle where warming leads to changes that cause even more warming, rather than counteracting it?

---

### Question 3

Scientists warn about 'tipping points' in the climate system. What does a climate tipping point represent?

A. The point at which climate change begins to occur
B. A critical threshold beyond which a large, potentially irreversible change in the climate system is triggered, even if the original cause is removed
C. The exact temperature at which all ice on Earth melts simultaneously
D. A point that can only be identified after it has been passed, making it scientifically useless

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. Tipping points represent thresholds where self-reinforcing feedbacks take over. For example, if permafrost warming triggers methane release that causes more warming that releases more methane, the process may become self-sustaining even if human emissions stop. The system transitions to a new state. Think about what makes a threshold 'irreversible.' If you push a ball over the edge of a hill, it continues rolling down even if you stop pushing. What climate processes behave similarly once a critical temperature is reached?

---

### Question 4

Forests absorb CO2 from the atmosphere through photosynthesis. A student claims that planting enough trees could solve climate change entirely. Which scientific limitation is MOST important for evaluating this claim?

A. Trees do not actually absorb CO2
B. While forests sequester significant carbon, there is not enough land area to plant sufficient trees to offset current annual emissions of ~40 billion tons of CO2, and forests under heat stress can flip from carbon sinks to carbon sources
C. Trees produce more CO2 than they absorb
D. Planting trees would block too much sunlight from reaching the ground

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. Global forests currently absorb ~2.6 billion tons of CO2/year, but annual emissions are ~40 billion tons. Even massive reforestation cannot offset total emissions. Additionally, forests under climate stress (drought, heat, wildfire) can die and release their stored carbon, converting from sinks to sources. Compare the scale of the solution to the scale of the problem. How much CO2 do forests absorb versus how much humans emit? And what happens to forest carbon storage under climate stress?

### Question 5

Climate change impacts are often described as 'disproportionate,' meaning some communities are affected much more severely than others. Which factor MOST directly determines a community's vulnerability to climate impacts?

A. The community's latitude (distance from the equator)
B. The community's economic resources, infrastructure, and institutional capacity to adapt to changing conditions
C. The community's historical CO2 emissions
D. The community's total population size

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. Adaptation capacity depends on wealth, infrastructure, governance, and institutional capacity. Wealthy nations can build sea walls, develop drought-resistant crops, and relocate populations. Vulnerable communities with fewer resources face the same or worse physical impacts with far less ability to respond. Consider two coastal cities facing identical sea level rise: one has billions of dollars for flood infrastructure and the other has minimal resources. Which will be more severely impacted, and why?

### Post-Assessment Questions

### Question 1

A student's coupled climate-ecosystem model shows that Forest Carbon Sequestration increases initially as Atmospheric CO2 rises (due to CO2 fertilization), but then sharply decreases after Global Temperature exceeds 2.5 degrees C above pre-industrial levels. Which phenomenon BEST explains this reversal?

A. CO2 fertilization saturates at a fixed concentration level
B. Above 2.5 degrees C, widespread forest die-off from heat stress, drought, and wildfire converts forests from carbon sinks to carbon sources, flipping a negative feedback into a positive feedback that accelerates warming
C. Forest growth always decreases linearly with temperature
D. CO2 fertilization does not exist and the initial increase was a model error

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. This is a critical tipping point in the carbon cycle. Below 2.5 degrees C, forests partially counteract warming by absorbing CO2 (negative feedback). Above this threshold, forests die back and release stored carbon (positive feedback). This flip from sink to source accelerates the very warming that caused it, exemplifying nonlinear system behavior. Consider what happens to a forest when temperatures and drought exceed what trees can tolerate. What becomes of the carbon stored in dead trees? Does the forest continue to absorb CO2, or does it release it?

### Question 2

The model reveals that Permafrost Methane Release becomes self-sustaining above a specific Global Temperature threshold. A student identifies this as a tipping point. Which characteristic of the permafrost system MOST directly makes this self-sustaining?

A. Methane is heavier than CO2 and sinks to ground level
B. Released methane is a powerful greenhouse gas (80x CO2 over 20 years) that causes additional warming, which thaws more permafrost, releasing more methane, creating a positive feedback loop that continues regardless of human emission reductions
C. Permafrost methane is released all at once in a single event
D. Methane in permafrost is replenished by underground geological processes

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. The permafrost-methane feedback is a classic positive feedback tipping point. Once warming triggers sufficient methane release, the warming from that methane thaws additional permafrost, releasing more methane. Beyond the tipping point, this cycle generates enough warming to sustain itself regardless of whether human emissions continue. Think about the cycle: warming causes methane release, which causes more warming, which causes more methane release. At what point does this cycle generate enough warming on its own that it no longer needs human emissions as a driver?

### Question 3

A student compares the Paris Agreement scenario (1.5-2.0 degrees C warming) with the Business-As-Usual scenario (3-4 degrees C warming) in the model. Sea Level Rise projections differ by a factor of 4 between the scenarios even though temperature differs by only a factor of 2. Which property of the climate system explains this disproportionate response?

A. Sea level rise is directly proportional to temperature change
B. Nonlinear ice sheet dynamics: above critical temperature thresholds, ice sheet collapse accelerates dramatically through feedback mechanisms (marine ice cliff instability, albedo loss) that do not operate below those thresholds
C. The ocean in the higher scenario contains four times more water
D. Measurement uncertainty accounts for the entire difference

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. Ice sheet loss is not linear. Below certain thresholds, ice sheets lose mass gradually from surface melting. Above those thresholds, catastrophic processes activate: marine ice cliffs become unstable and collapse, exposed dark surfaces absorb more heat, and warm water undercuts ice shelves. These nonlinear processes produce disproportionately large sea level rise at higher temperatures. If the response were linear, a 2x temperature increase would produce a 2x sea level increase. The fact that the relationship is 4x suggests nonlinear processes are operating. What changes about ice sheet dynamics above certain temperature thresholds?

### Question 4

The model shows that Societal Adaptation Capacity is the most unevenly distributed component globally. A student uses this finding to argue that climate change is fundamentally a justice issue, not just an environmental one. Which evidence from the model MOST STRONGLY supports this argument?

A. All countries experience identical climate impacts
B. Nations that have contributed the least to Atmospheric CO2 (e.g., sub-Saharan Africa, Pacific island nations) face the most severe physical impacts (extreme heat, sea level rise, crop failure) while having the fewest economic and institutional resources to adapt
C. Wealthy nations experience worse climate impacts than poor nations
D. Societal Adaptation Capacity is unrelated to climate outcomes

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. The model demonstrates a fundamental asymmetry: the nations that industrialized and generated most historical emissions have the wealth and infrastructure to adapt, while nations that contributed minimally to the problem face disproportionate impacts with minimal resources. This mismatch between contribution and consequence is the core of climate justice. Climate justice asks: who caused the problem, who suffers the consequences, and who has the resources to adapt? Use the model to trace the relationship between historical emissions, current vulnerability, and adaptation capacity across different regions.

### Question 5

A student proposes two climate intervention strategies and uses the model to evaluate them: Strategy A reduces Atmospheric CO2 emissions by 50% immediately; Strategy B invests in maximizing Societal Adaptation Capacity while maintaining current emission levels. The model shows that Strategy A produces better long-term outcomes. Which systems-level principle EXPLAINS why emission reduction outperforms adaptation alone?

A. Adaptation is always inferior to mitigation
B. Adaptation addresses symptoms while emission reduction addresses the root cause; once positive feedback tipping points are crossed, adaptation capacity cannot compensate for self-reinforcing warming regardless of investment, making early emission reduction mathematically necessary to stay below irreversible thresholds
C. Emission reduction is cheaper than adaptation
D. The model is biased toward emission reduction strategies

Correct Answer: B

Feedback: Correct. This is the fundamental asymmetry between mitigation and adaptation. Adaptation has limits: no amount of adaptation can sustain agriculture, coastal cities, or ecosystems under 4+ degrees of warming with self-reinforcing feedbacks. Emission reduction operates upstream, preventing the tipping point crossings that would overwhelm any adaptation strategy. Both are necessary, but emission reduction is the only intervention that prevents irreversible system state changes. Think about where each strategy operates in the causal chain. Emission reduction prevents warming from crossing tipping points. Adaptation manages the consequences of warming that has already occurred. What happens when adaptation faces consequences that exceed its capacity because tipping points were crossed?
