NGSS Standards & CAST Alignment
ModelIt!
Performance Expectation: 5-ESS2-1
Develop a model using an example to describe ways the geosphere, biosphere, hydrosphere, and/or atmosphere interact.

Three-Dimensional Learning

Science & Engineering Practice Developing and Using Models — Students develop and use models to describe phenomena. In this lesson, students build a computational model showing how components interact in California's fire system.
Disciplinary Core Idea ESS2.A: Earth Materials and Systems — Earth's major systems are the geosphere, hydrosphere, atmosphere, and biosphere. These systems interact in multiple ways to affect Earth's surface materials and processes.
Crosscutting Concept Systems and System Models — A system can be described in terms of its components and their interactions. Students identify components and model the relationships between them.

CAST Testing Alignment

Sample Multiple Choice
A student builds a model showing that when rainfall decreases, dry vegetation increases. What type of relationship does this represent?
A. Positive   B. Negative   C. No relationship   D. Random
Sample Constructed Response
Using evidence from the simulation, explain how at least two Earth systems interact to create wildfire conditions in California. Include the role of at least one external and one internal component.

California Wildfire Science

California's Mediterranean climate creates a predictable cycle: wet winters grow vegetation, followed by long dry summers that turn that vegetation into fuel. When hot, dry Santa Ana winds arrive in fall, the conditions are set for catastrophic fire events. This is why California burns “at the same time every year.”

Earth Systems Connections

Earth System Role in Wildfires Components in Model
Hydrosphere Rainfall patterns determine vegetation moisture levels. Drought = dry fuel. Rainfall (External)
Biosphere Living plants become fuel when dried out. Density and type of vegetation matter. Dry Vegetation (Internal)
Atmosphere Wind carries fire embers, feeds oxygen to flames, and affects humidity. Wind (External)
Geosphere Terrain and slopes affect fire behavior — fire travels uphill faster. Extension component
Key Misconception Alert
Students often think wildfires are caused only by human carelessness (campfires, arson). While human activity is a factor, the underlying conditions — dry vegetation, wind, low humidity — are what make large fires possible. Help students see that Earth systems create the conditions, even if humans sometimes provide the spark.

The LEVER Framework
ModelIt!
About the LEVER Framework
LEVER is ModelIt!'s systematic approach to building computational models. Each phase scaffolds student thinking from identifying components to revising models based on evidence.
L
Locate the System Identify the components (parts) of the system. Determine which are EXTERNAL (inputs from outside) and INTERNAL (processes within the system). In this lesson: Rainfall and Wind are external; Dry Vegetation and Fire Spread are internal.
E
Establish Relationships Determine how components affect each other using (+) positive and (-) negative relationships. Example: Rainfall has a NEGATIVE effect on Dry Vegetation (more rain = less dry vegetation).
V
Visualize & Model Build the model in ModelIt! Set up components, draw relationship arrows, and configure the simulation. Students see their conceptual model come to life computationally.
E
Evaluate Outcomes Run simulations with different scenarios (drought, wind, fire season). Analyze graphs showing how components change over time. Compare predictions to actual results.
R
Revise & Extend Add complexity to the model (new components like temperature or humidity). Improve the model based on new evidence. Connect to real-world applications.

Slide-by-Slide Facilitation
ModelIt!
Slide 1: Cover & Introduction 3–5 min
Say: “Today we're going to investigate a real mystery: Why does California burn at the same time every year? We're going to build a scientific model to figure this out — and by the end, you'll be able to explain it like a scientist.”
Watch For
Gauge prior knowledge. Some students may have personal experience with wildfires. Be sensitive to students who may have been affected by fires.
Slide 2: Learning Objectives & Vocabulary 3–4 min
Say: “Let's preview the key words we'll use today. A COMPONENT is a part of a system. A MODEL is a simplified version of something complex. A SIMULATION lets us test 'what if' scenarios.”
Tip
Have students turn and talk: “What models have you seen in science before?” Accept all answers — globes, diagrams, dioramas are all models.
Slide 3: The Big Question 2–3 min
Say: “Here's our big question: Why does California burn at the same time every year? By the end of today's lesson, you'll be able to answer this using evidence from your model.”
Slide 5: Activity 1 — Sort Components 5–8 min
Say: “Now you'll sort these four components into EXTERNAL and INTERNAL. External means it comes from outside the system — we can't control it from within. Internal means it's part of the system's inner workings.”
Common Mistake
Students often think “fire” is external because it seems to “come from outside.” Help them see that Fire Spread is an internal process that results from the interaction of other components.

Answer Key & Expected Responses
ModelIt!

Component Sorting (Section 3)

  • EXTERNAL: Rainfall, Wind — these come from outside the wildfire system
  • INTERNAL: Dry Vegetation, Fire Spread — these are processes within the system

Relationships (Section 4)

  • Rainfall → Dry Vegetation: NEGATIVE (-) — More rain = less dry vegetation
  • Dry Vegetation → Fire Spread: POSITIVE (+) — More dry vegetation = more fire spread
  • Wind → Fire Spread: POSITIVE (+) — More wind = more fire spread

Simulation Observations (Section 5)

  • Scenario 1 (Drought): Without rainfall, dry vegetation increases steadily. Fire spread increases as fuel accumulates.
  • Scenario 2 (Windy Day): Wind causes fire to spread faster and farther. Even with normal vegetation, wind accelerates fire.
  • Scenario 3 (Fire Season): Combined conditions create the worst-case scenario. Both dry vegetation AND wind amplify fire spread dramatically.

Reflection: The Big Answer

California burns at the same time every year because of its Mediterranean climate cycle. Wet winters grow vegetation, long dry summers turn it into fuel (hydrosphere + biosphere), and fall Santa Ana winds provide the atmospheric push (atmosphere). When these Earth systems align, fire conditions peak. The model shows that removing rainfall and adding wind creates maximum fire spread — exactly what happens during California's fall fire season.

Differentiation & Extensions
ModelIt!
Below Grade Level
  • Pre-sort components as a whole class before independent work
  • Provide sentence frames: “When _____ increases, _____ decreases because...”
  • Use visual vocabulary cards with pictures
  • Pair with a strong reader for the activity pack
Advanced Learners
  • Add Santa Ana Winds and Terrain as new components
  • Research historical fire data from CAL FIRE
  • Create a multi-step causal chain explanation
  • Compare California to Australia's fire seasons
English Language Learners
  • Pre-teach vocabulary: component, model, simulation, relationship
  • Provide bilingual glossary if available
  • Use visual supports and diagrams alongside text
  • Allow verbal responses recorded on device
Gifted & Talented
  • Challenge: Create a feedback loop diagram
  • Research prescribed burns as fire prevention
  • Write a policy brief for fire prevention
  • Connect to climate change projections

Common Misconceptions
ModelIt!
Why This Matters
Addressing misconceptions proactively helps students build accurate scientific understanding. Use these as formative assessment checkpoints during the lesson.
Students May Think
“All wildfires are caused by people being careless.”
Scientific Understanding
While human activity can start fires, the conditions for large wildfires — dry vegetation, low humidity, wind — are created by natural Earth system interactions. Without these conditions, a spark won't become a wildfire.
Students May Think
“Fire just needs fuel to burn.”
Scientific Understanding
The fire triangle requires fuel, heat, AND oxygen. Our model focuses on fuel (dry vegetation) and wind (which provides oxygen and spreads heat), showing how multiple factors work together.
Students May Think
“Drought kills plants right away.”
Scientific Understanding
Drought is a gradual process. Plants lose moisture over weeks and months, becoming increasingly flammable. This is why California's fire risk peaks in late fall after months of dry conditions.
Students May Think
“All wildfires happen in summer.”
Scientific Understanding
In California, the worst fires often occur in October–November when Santa Ana winds arrive. The combination of months of drought-dried vegetation plus these hot, dry winds creates peak fire danger in fall, not summer.
