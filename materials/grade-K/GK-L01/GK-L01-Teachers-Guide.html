Background Content Knowledge
ModelIt!
For Your Confidence
This section provides the science background you need to facilitate this lesson with authority. You don’t need to teach all of this — it’s here so you can answer student questions accurately.

Gravity is one of the most important forces in the universe. It keeps our feet on the ground, makes rain fall from clouds, and holds the Moon in orbit around Earth.

What Is Gravity?

Gravity is a force — an invisible pull — that attracts objects toward each other. On Earth, gravity pulls everything toward the center of the planet. This is why when you jump, you come back down. When you drop a ball, it falls to the floor. Gravity is always pulling on us, even though we cannot see it.

Why Things Fall at Different Speeds

When an object is held up high and released, gravity pulls it downward. The higher the starting point, the longer gravity has to pull on the object, so it reaches a faster speed before hitting the ground. In a vacuum (with no air), all objects fall at the same rate regardless of weight. In air, lighter objects like feathers fall more slowly because air resistance pushes against them.

The LEVER Framework
ModelIt!
About LEVER
LEVER is how every ModelIt! lesson works. Students move through five phases to build, test, and refine a computational model. Each lesson is self-contained — students complete all five phases in one session.
L
Locate the SystemStudents identify Drop Height as the external component (we choose how high to hold it) and Fall Speed as the internal component (it changes on its own based on height).
E
Establish RelationshipsStudents discover that higher Drop Height leads to faster Fall Speed — a positive relationship.
V
Visualize & ModelStudents build a simple two-part model connecting Drop Height to Fall Speed with a positive arrow.
E
Evaluate OutcomesStudents test Low Drop vs. High Drop scenarios and observe the speed difference.
R
Revise & ExtendStudents explore what happens when they drop different objects (heavy ball, light ball, feather) from the same height.

Dropping ModelIt! Into Your Existing Unit

ModelIt! is a supplement, not a replacement for your science curriculum. Each lesson stands alone, but it becomes even more powerful when students revisit their model throughout a unit. Here are easy ways to weave it in:

At the start of a unit Use this lesson to introduce the phenomenon. Students build a “first draft” model based on what they already know. Save it.
After a reading or lab Have students reopen their model and update it: “Based on what you just learned, would you add a component? Change a relationship?” Takes 10–15 minutes.
As a “what-if” tool When students ask questions during class, say: “Great question — go test that in your model!” Students run new scenarios and report back.
For peer discussion Partners compare models: “Why does your model have this arrow and mine doesn’t?” Differences spark scientific debate using evidence.
As a presentation tool Students project their model and walk the class through it live — showing components, running scenarios, answering audience “what-if” challenges in real time.
At the end of a unit Compare Day 1 model to final model. The growth IS the assessment — students see how their thinking evolved.

Slide-by-Slide Facilitation
ModelIt!
Slide 1: Cover2 min
Say: “Have you ever dropped something? What happened? Today we are going to find out WHY things fall down!”
Watch For
Hold up a ball and drop it. Ask: What just happened? Why did it go DOWN and not UP?
Slide 2: What Will We Learn?3 min
Say: “Today we will learn about an invisible force called gravity! Gravity is what makes everything fall down.”
Watch For
Introduce the word 'gravity.' Have kids say it together three times. Do a gesture: hands up, then pull down.
Slide 3: Let's Be Scientists!3 min
Say: “Scientists ask questions and do experiments to find answers. Our big question is: Why do things ALWAYS fall DOWN?”
Watch For
Pair-share: Ask a friend what they think. Give 30 seconds, then ask two students to share.
Slide 4: What Are the Parts?5 min
Say: “Our model has two parts. Drop Height is how high we hold the ball — WE get to decide that! Fall Speed is how fast the ball falls — that happens all by itself.”
Watch For
Use sorting mats. Students place Drop Height on the OUTSIDE and Fall Speed on the INSIDE. Act it out: hold ball high, hold ball low.
Slide 5: How Are They Connected?5 min
Say: “When Drop Height goes UP, what happens to Fall Speed? Does the ball fall faster or slower? That means it is a POSITIVE connection!”
Watch For
Demonstrate with a real ball: drop from knee height, then from above your head. Students point thumbs up for positive connection.
Slide 6: What Did We Find Out?5 min
Say: “We found out that gravity pulls EVERYTHING down! And when we drop from higher up, it falls FASTER. That is gravity at work!”
Watch For
Students say: 'When Drop Height goes up, Fall Speed goes up!' Clap it out like a chant. Fill in the sentence frame together.
Slide 7: STEM Challenge10 min
Say: “Now YOU get to be engineers! Build a tower and test dropping balls from different levels. Which level makes the biggest bounce?”
Watch For
Distribute blocks, cups, and soft balls. Groups build towers with 2-3 levels and test drops. Record with smiley faces (small bounce, medium bounce, big bounce).

Answer Key & Expected Responses
ModelIt!

Component Sorting

  • EXTERNAL: Drop Height
  • INTERNAL: Fall Speed

Relationships

  • Drop Height to Fall Speed: POSITIVE (+) — When you drop from higher up, the ball falls faster because gravity pulls it for a longer time.

Key Discoveries

  • Gravity pulls everything down toward the ground
  • When you drop something from higher up, it falls faster
  • Gravity never stops — it is always pulling things down

Multiple Choice Pre-Assessment Answer Key

1. What happens when you let go of a ball?
Correct: A) It falls down
Yes! When you let go of a ball, it always falls down to the ground.
2. If you drop a rock, which way does it go?
Correct: C) It goes down
That is right! A rock always falls down when you drop it.
3. What do you think makes things fall to the ground?
Correct: B) Something invisible pulls them down
Good thinking! Something invisible called gravity pulls everything down toward the ground.
4. If you hold a toy up high and let go, what will happen?
Correct: B) It will fall down
Yes! The toy will fall down because gravity pulls it toward the ground.

Multiple Choice Post-Assessment Answer Key

1. What is gravity?
Correct: B) A pull that makes things fall down
That is right! Gravity is a pull that makes things fall down toward the ground.
2. When you drop a ball from HIGH up, what happens?
Correct: B) It falls faster
Yes! Dropping from higher up makes the ball fall faster because gravity has more time to pull on it.
3. In our model, which part do WE get to decide?
Correct: B) Drop Height
Yes! WE choose how high to hold the ball. That is the Drop Height, and it is the outside part of our model.
4. Does gravity ever stop pulling on things?
Correct: C) No, it never stops
That is right! Gravity never stops. It is always pulling on everything, even when things are sitting on a table.

Collaborative Learning Protocols
ModelIt!
Why This Matters
ModelIt! is a digital tool, but the deepest learning happens between humans. Computational modeling is most powerful when students build understanding together — debating relationships, challenging predictions, and constructing explanations as a community. These protocols ensure every student’s voice is part of the model. Use at least ONE per lesson.
Model Gallery Walk
When: After students build models (LEVER Phase V)
Setup: Groups post their models on screens/desks. Half the class stays to “defend,” half rotates with sticky notes.
Protocol: Visitors leave 2 sticky notes: one “I notice...” and one “I wonder...” After rotation, defenders read feedback aloud. Class discusses patterns.
Why it works: Students see that the SAME phenomenon can be modeled differently — and that’s how real science works. (Bandura: learning through observation of peers)
Scientist Fishbowl
When: During simulation discussion (LEVER Phase E)
Setup: 4-5 students sit in center circle. Rest of class observes with clipboards.
Protocol: Inner circle discusses: “What happened in Scenario 3 and WHY?” using only evidence from their models. Outer circle records strongest arguments. Swap groups.
Why it works: Students practice scientific discourse without teacher directing. Observers learn to evaluate arguments. (Freire: dialogue as the foundation of critical consciousness)
Component Jigsaw
When: During component analysis (LEVER Phase L)
Setup: Assign each group ONE component to become “experts” on. They research it, discuss it, draw it.
Protocol: Regroup so each new team has one expert per component. Experts teach their component to the team. Team builds the model together using everyone’s expertise.
Why it works: Every student is essential — the model literally cannot be built without each person’s knowledge. Interdependence drives engagement. (Lave & Wenger: learning through legitimate peripheral participation)
Prediction Debate
When: Before running simulations (LEVER Phase E)
Setup: Post a scenario. Students physically move to corners: “Fire Spread will INCREASE a lot” / “INCREASE a little” / “STAY the same” / “DECREASE.”
Protocol: Each corner group has 2 minutes to build their argument using model logic. One spokesperson presents. Then RUN the simulation. Losing corners explain what they missed.
Why it works: Movement + social risk + immediate feedback. Students CARE about the outcome because they committed publicly. Wrong predictions become the best learning moments.
Pair Programming for Models (Digital Collaboration)
When: Throughout model building
Setup: Two students, one device. Driver controls the mouse/keyboard. Navigator directs strategy and catches errors. Switch every 5 minutes.
Protocol: Navigator must verbalize every decision: “I think we should add a negative arrow from Rainfall to Dry Vegetation because...” Driver cannot act without Navigator’s reasoning.
Why it works: Forces articulation of scientific thinking. The model becomes a shared artifact, not an individual assignment. Students who struggle with writing can demonstrate understanding verbally. Borrowed from computer science — where the best code is written in pairs.
Quick Protocol Selection Guide
Short on time (5 min)Prediction Debate — gets bodies moving, generates energy fast
Full period availableComponent Jigsaw — deep collaboration, every student contributes
After simulation dayModel Gallery Walk — peer feedback, multiple perspectives
Advanced discussionScientist Fishbowl — builds academic discourse skills
Every day defaultPair Programming — transforms individual work into dialogue

Common Misconceptions
ModelIt!
Why This Matters
Addressing misconceptions proactively helps students build accurate scientific understanding. Use these as formative assessment checkpoints during the lesson.
Students May Think
“Heavy things fall faster than light things”
Scientific Understanding
In air, heavy and light objects fall at almost the same speed. A bowling ball and a soccer ball dropped together land at nearly the same time. Air resistance (not weight) is what makes feathers float slowly.
Students May Think
“Gravity only works when you drop something”
Scientific Understanding
Gravity is always pulling on everything, all the time — even when things are sitting still on a table. The table pushes back up to keep things from falling through.
